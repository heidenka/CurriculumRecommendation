Academic Information

Content In this unit, students learn about the main private law remedies organised according to their dominant functions. The first part of the unit considers compensatory remedies and in the second part, restitutionary, punitive and coercive remedies are also considered. The unit has a strong practical focus, given the importance of remedies in practice. Students critically examine available remedies and the circumstances of their availability and also consider possible reforms.

Unit Information

Welcome to Remedies!
“A course on civil remedies is extremely important. The starting point to solve a legal problem is almost always to ask what remedy is required. But the dominant pedagogy of law is to think in terms of causative events or causes of action. Courses on remedies close this gap.” 
Justice James Edelman (High Court of Australia)
In this unit, you will learn about the main private law remedies, at common law, in equity and under selected statutes.  The unit is organised according to the dominant functions of private law remedies.  We will begin by considering compensatory remedies, which aim to make good the plaintiff’s loss.  We will look at the different approaches of the common law, equity and the Australian Consumer Law.  We will then consider restitutionary remedies, which aim to strip away the defendant’s gain.  We will look separately at personal and proprietary restitutionary remedies.  Next, we will consider coercive remedies, including injunctions and specific performance, which compel the defendant to do or refrain from doing a particular act.  Finally, we will consider punitive and vindicatory remedies, such as exemplary damages and apologies, which seek to punish the defendant and vindicate the plaintiff.
The content of the unit relates back to, and builds upon, the core units you have already studied in Law School. For this reason, Remedies is sometimes described as a ‘capstone’ unit.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

The Remedies course consists of four hours of teaching per week. The four hours are broken down into (i) three hours of lectures; and (ii) a one-hour tutorial.

Unit Structure

Lectures
There will be three hours of lectures a week.
The lectures will be pre-recorded and uploaded to the LMS platform. 
Felicity Maher will introduce the unit and give the lectures on general principles of compensatory remedies. Nicholas Tiverios will give the lectures on damages for breach of contract, damages in tort, compensation in equity and damages under the Australian Consumer Law. Felicity Maher will then lecture on restitutionary remedies, including gain-based awards for wrongs, restitution for unjust enrichment and proprietary restitutionary remedies.  Nicholas Tiverios will then lecture on the coercive remedies – specific performance and injunctions – and on debt and the limits on debt (including forfeiture and penalties).  Finally, Felicity Maher will lecture on punitive remedies. 
Tutorials
Registration for tutorials is online through CAS. Tutorial groups will remain the same all semester. Tutorials will be held weekly and will commence in the second week of the semester. Tutorials will be taken by Nicholas Tiverios, Rachel Eaton, Rachel Chan, Adam Bond and Bremer Moore.  
If you are unable to attend a tutorial you should contact your tutor in advance explaining your reasons. Your tutor may in exceptional circumstances allow you to attend another tutorial group to make up for the one that you have missed. Please do not attend another tutorial group without having obtained the permission of your tutor and (where they differ) the tutor from the group that you want to attend.
It is Law School policy that those who undertake external work, travel or sporting commitments are expected to plan these around their studies.

Participation

Attendance at all classes is important: there is no substitute for your attendance and participation in class.
A failure to attend a minimum of 8 tutorials may result in a candidate not being able to sit the final examination.  
Further, the use of notes of students from previous years is strongly discouraged. Note-taking is a critical skill for any law graduate. You should therefore listen to the lectures and take the opportunity in law school to develop your note-taking skills by making your own lecture notes.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
