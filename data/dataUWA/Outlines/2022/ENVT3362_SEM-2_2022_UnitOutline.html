Academic Information

Content Decision making for managing environmental systems is increasingly guided by modelling studies in which risks to environmental condition are quantified and future scenarios are assessed. Students are introduced step-by-step to model building of environmental systems drawn from examples such as climate change and land-use change impacts on biodiversity, water and contaminant movement in natural and disturbed landscapes, rivers and also coastal systems. The unit covers issues including the use of models in decision support frameworks, uncertainty and model assessment methods.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

This unit will draw upon processes and knowledge obtained at Level 2 and apply them in the context of understanding "system dynamics". 
The first 7 weeks are hands-on where students focus on “learning by doing”, through pre-recorded lectures, guided in-class (or virtual) seminars with practical activities, and computer-based workshop sessions (denoted "P" and "W" in the schedule below). 
After this period, the class transitions to work on applied projects where students work on problem conceptualisation, and undertake a self-managed process of analysis of a chosen topic using models to explore solutions to environmental policy or management challenges.

Unit Structure

The unit is broadly organised as a series of three sections each including several lectures and associated workshop classes and activities. 
These are summarised as: 
 I. Systems thinking for environmental scientists and managers (2 weeks)
 II.  Modelling environmental systems (5 weeks)
 III. Practical techniques and applications for assisting decision-making (5 weeks)

Participation

Students are expected to attend all lectures and participate in the weekly workshop sessions and computer labs. The labs vary from week to week and include computer-based data-manipulation and modelling activities. Attendance in the weekly lab sessions is compulsory and students must submit completed work each week to as part of Assessment 1. 
For most weeks we follow the sequence:
S: SEMINAR session - this is theoretical content delivered as a lecture/seminar with class discussions and tutorial style tasks, introducing the pre-lab activity and workshop content.
P: PRE-LAB practical activity - this is a self-paced activity to be completed in your own time and prior to the weekly lab.
W: A face-to-face or online WORKSHOP session, working through a computer-based activity.
Should you have any difficulty in completing and submitting work please let either unit coordinator know as soon as possible, preferably before reaching crisis point. Academic staff are sympathetic to genuine problems and will endeavour to assist you to solve them. If you miss course work through prolonged illness please provide the Faculty Office with a medical certificate. Faculty Office staff will then notify the course coordinators

Referencing Requirements

When completing academic work it is essential that students refer to the sources of their information within the text, and professionally adhere to referencing standards. Marks will be deducted for inadequate, or poor quality referencing. Complete details of these works should be arranged alphabetically in a reference list at the end of any document or report you submit. 
The Faculty prefers the use of either the Harvard or the APA reference style. The style chosen must be used consistently throughout any document. These may be accessed at: http://www.studentservices.uwa.edu.au/ss/learning/studying_smarter/jump_start/referencing or http://www.library.uwa.edu.au/education_training_and_support/guides ;
(a) Text referencing 
There are standard procedures for referring to authors and their work, for example: 
  1.Smith and Brown (2006) supported the interpretation…. 
  2.… when recognising two inseparable criteria of experience; continuity and interaction (Clandinin & Connelly 2000). 
  3.When referring to a specific page or pages of a reference: Labaree (2006 p. 17); (Labaree 2006 p.17), Labaree (2006 pp.17-18) 
  4.When referring to more than one work by the same author(s): Fink and Brayman (2004, 2006) 
  5.When referring to more than one work in the same year: Hargreaves (2005a), Hargreaves (2005b) 
  6.When there are TWO authors: (Tilbury & Wortman 2003); Tilbury and Wortman (2003) 
  7.When there are THREE or more authors: (Leithwood et al., 1999) Note: in the reference list, multiple authors should be listed in full. 
  8.When referring to a publication that has no specific author, but which was written by an organization: (UNESCO 1992). 
(b) Reference list 
All work referenced in the text must also be included (in alphabetical order by author) in the reference list at the end of the assignment. 
  1. Book: Diamond, J., 2005. Guns, germs and steel: A short history of everybody for the last 13,000 years. Vintage Press, London. 
  2. Article/Chapter in a book: Fink, D 2000, The attrition of educational change over time: The case of ‘innovative’, ‘model’, ‘lighthouse’ schools, In The sharp edge of educational change: Teaching, leading and the realities of reform, eds N Bascia & A Hargreaves, Falmer Press, London, pp. 29-51. 
  3. Article in a journal: Hargreaves, A., 2005. Leadership succession, The Educational Forum, 69(2): 163-174. 
  4. Government publication: Department of Environment and Heritage 1999, Today shapes tomorrow: Environmental education for a sustainable future, Environment Australia, Canberra. 
PLAGIARISM 
The Faculty of Science has a Policy on Plagiarism that is available from the Faculty and School Offices. It is expected that work submitted for assessment by a University student is the students’ own work, and that any contribution from others (ideas, facts or direct quotations) is given suitable acknowledgement by referring to the source (in a list of references, an acknowledgements section or in some other appropriate way). If such acknowledgement is not given, the writer is guilty of plagiarism. Plagiarism is the use of published work (book, articles, reports) or the work of other students (present or past, at this or any other University), as though it were one`s own. In some cultures, it is regarded as a sign of respect to describe events or ideas in exactly the same words as an acknowledged expert. In the academic tradition to which this University belongs, it is not; it is thought of as mere copying. Note that your project submissions will be assessed via the LMS “TurnItIn” plagiarism detection tool. Special care is necessary in the case of group work. Where a single report is required from a group of several students, it will be assumed that all students have contributed. Students not happy with the contribution of other group members should make appropriate representations to the appropriate staff member. Where each student is required to hand in an individual report based on group research, work of another individual student (eg diagrams, photographs) should be acknowledged. Unfortunately, students have been penalised by many Schools within the University, including this one, for plagiarism. If in any doubt, discuss the matter with your tutor or the unit coordinators. You may also access the plagiarism portal at http://www.studentservices.uwa.edu.au/ss/learning/studying_smarter/jump_start/referencing

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "honest, ethical and responsible scholarship". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
