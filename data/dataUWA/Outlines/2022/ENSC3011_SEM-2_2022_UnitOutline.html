Unit Information

This unit introduces the study of mining engineering. It is an initial exposure to the technologies used to extract valuable minerals from the ground. Topics include the fundamentals of the geology and exploration of mineral resources, typical operational processes and design considerations for open pit and underground mining. A field trip may be made to an operational mine near Perth.
Topics covered in this unit include:
•	Overview of the mining industry
•	Geology
•	Surface mining
•	Underground mining
•	Some specialist topics e.g., blasting rock mechanics, economics
•	Mineral processing
•	Case studies
On completion of ENSC3011 – Fundamentals of Mining Engineering, students should understand the fundamental technologies employed in the development and operation of mining operations; the multidisciplinary nature of mining engineering; and the variety of roles of engineers within these operations.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

At UWA, we are committed to providing you with real-world, authentic learning activities that prepare you for your role as a future mining professional. Learning activities in this unit include lectures and tutorials.
The unit has 5 hours per week of contact time. Lecture and tutorial sessions are face to face unless there are COVID19 restrictions (Refer to ‘Teaching contingency plan’ section for more details).
In addition to face-to-face discussions in the lecture sessions, students are encouraged to make appointments with the lecturer (the unit coordinator) to discuss any of the topics they are unsure of or want more information/clarification for. Refer to the ‘consultation information’ section for more details.
Assessments in this unit consist of quizzes, one major assignment, and a final examination. The quizzes, worth 10% of the student’s grade, will be held on-line and give the students the opportunity to reinforce their understanding of the course. Note that the on-line quizzes will have a time limit associated with them and may differ depending on the type or number of questions. The major assessment, worth 30% of the student’s grade, will give the student the opportunity to gain experience how to write technical reports with associated justifications/calculations related to a mining operation. The final examination will give the student the opportunity to illustrate what they have learnt during the unit.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
