Academic Information

Content This unit examines the role of agriculture in economic development, mainly in developing countries but with relevance to developed countries. Students learn how to apply economic theory and analytical tools to address agricultural development challenges faced by governments, and agents operating in the food and non-food sectors. Thematic areas covered include the economics of farm households, market failure and role of government in markets, food and nutrition security, agricultural productivity growth, technology adoption and impact evaluation, rural land reforms, climate change, and water crisis, agriculture and natural resource conservation, and policy instruments in international trade. A variety of topical questions are addressed, potentially including: what caused the global food crisis? How can we evaluate the appropriateness of government policies, such as fertilizer subsidies? What is the impact of water scarcity on global food security in an era of climate change? Why is land reform a thorny issue in developing countries? Are there opportunities to increase the efficiency of resource-poor producers? What are the effects of international trade and globalization on poor economies? What strategies and policies are needed to promote agricultural development? The unit is issue and problem solving oriented; it requires critical thinking and active participation. Policy implications of the topics are explored are discussed throughout the unit. Students debate and discuss contemporary issues in development, write policy briefs, and estimate empirical models relevant for formulating evidence-based policies.

Unit Information

This unit examines the role of agriculture in development, mainly in developing countries but with relevance to developed countries. The focus is on contemporary challenges in economic development.
The unit is taught in two modules of 6 weeks each. The first module examines agricultural development issues in the food system at and beyond the farm level. The second module examines agricultural development challenges in ensuring sustainability in food and fiber production at the farm level. The two modules are designed to teach students to critically reflect about their understanding of agriculture and economic development as well as be equipped with analytical skills to be able to address policy issues and research in agricultural development. Students will also able to develop their oral and written communication skills in articulating policy issues.
The unit is issue and problem solving oriented; it requires critical thinking and active participation. Policy implications of the topics explored are discussed throughout the unit. Students have the opportunity to discuss issues, design policy briefs, and conduct an empirical analysis to provide evidence-based policy.
Thematic areas include agricultural productivity and growth, food and nutrition security, agricultural technology adoption and impact, food policy instruments, agricultural protectionism, and welfare analysis, farming systems, rural land reforms, climate change, and water crisis, agriculture and natural resources conservation. 
On completion of this unit, the students should be able to:
1. Articulate the important characteristics of agriculture and its role in economic development;
2. Critique various theories of agricultural and rural economic development;
3. Discuss, explain and review current debates in agricultural development such as food security and rising food prices, land reform and property rights, market failure and government interventions, obesity and malnutrition;
4. Analyze alternative agricultural development policies in terms of their potential impact on rural livelihoods, sustainability, equity and economic growth, taking account of different personal, regional and geographical circumstances;
5. Have a conceptual understanding of what can be done to promote agricultural development and environmental conservation through policy interventions; and
6. Develop the capacity to apply economic tools of analysis to problems of environmental management and agricultural development such as food insecurity assessment and evaluation of farming systems.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

This unit is delivered using a flipped classroom format and encourages and fosters collaborative student learning and achievement. Collaborative learning occurs when students work together to achieve a common goal. Pre-recorded lectures arranged by module and weeks following the scheduled timetable are already posted on the unit’s website in the Learning Management System. Students are required to engage with the pre-recorded lectures as scheduled before class. Face-to-face lecture sessions may be arranged on a need basis. All students are required to attend the face-to-face tutorial sessions where a problem-based approach is used to review and master the concepts and principles covered in the lectures.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
