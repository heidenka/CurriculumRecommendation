Unit Information

Unit Information
This unit introduces students to the relationship between employer and employee, drawing on issues, policies and developments within industrial relations. It explores relevant legislation and its impact on the workplace, examining specific areas of regulation including minimum standards, awards, enterprise agreements, dismissal, discrimination, industrial action and safety.
The course provides an overview of the legal frameworks regulating work relationships in Australia.
The course begins with an exploration of the concept of “work”. Different types of work relationships are identified and the legal tests for identifying employment relationships are unpacked. Policy issues concerning sham contracting and labour hire arrangements are discussed.
The course is structured to explore the three (often intersecting) forms of law: contract law, common law (i.e. judge-made law) and statute law (i.e. legislation).
The fundamental basis of an employment relationship, being a contract of employment, is analysed, from formation to termination. Claims for underpaid entitlements, the enforcing of restraint of trade clauses and alleged breaches of confidentiality are examples of contractual law disputes that commonly arise.
After the key principles of contract law are identified and applied to factual scenarios, focus turns to understanding the host of duties placed on employers and employees by virtue of the common law.
Finally, the extensive federal statutory regime regulating work relationships in Australia is examined. This part of the course involves a short discussion of the constitutional foundations of the key federal statute that regulates employment law, the Fair Work Act 2009 (Cth). The Act establishes 11 national minimum standards of employment (including entitlements such as maximum weekly hours of work, leave, notice of termination, redundancy pay), unfair dismissal and adverse action claims, an anti-bullying framework, as well as rights to request flexible working arrangements. The Act also regulates awards and collective agreement-making and establishes the Fair Work Commission, an administrative tribunal that is responsible for, among other things, making awards, conciliating disputes, approving collective agreements and adjudicating unfair dismissal claims. In addition to the Act, several specialised anti-discrimination, equal opportunity and work health and safety statutes are also analysed.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

You will learn in this unit by listening to lectures that discuss case law and legislation, participating in tutorials which tackle hypothetical problem-based questions, and actively reading the textbook and other relevant materials.  You should also use the online facilities on LMS to aid your learning, including links to legislation, commentary and articles.

You may be asked to complete two evaluations during this unit: The Student Perception of Teaching (SPOT) and the Students’ Unit Reflective Feedback (SURF). Unit Structure

Lectures
Students attend two hours of lectures each week. There will be no repeat lectures in this Unit.
Lectures are intended to provide students with an overview. The key legal principles including the sources of law from which they are derived, together with some practical examples will be discussed.
Tutorials and attendance
All students must attend and participate in weekly tutorials. Students attend one tutorial of one hour duration each week. Tutorials commence in Week 2 of Semester 1. Students should sign up for a tutorial using OLCR: http://www.cas.uwa.edu.au/student
As tutorial attendance and participation is assessable (20% of overall assessment), students must attend only their allocated tutorial.
Tutorials are intended to enable students to revise and contextualise the material they have been learning and to develop and practice the analytical skills needed to apply those principles to resolve legal problems.
Readings
Students are expected to read the assigned readings as per the Unit Schedule below before each lecture.
After each lecture, students are encouraged to return to the assigned readings in greater depth and to engage in further independent study of the recommended additional textbooks, cases and materials.

Referencing Requirements

It is important that the referencing of any sources used in your written work is done properly, if only to substantiate the points you are making in your assignment or project. The Harvard style is the preferred used unless otherwise stated by the unit coordinator. Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "honest, ethical and responsible scholarship". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
