Academic Information

Content Animals provide evidence of the many and varied solutions to the challenges imposed on them by their changing environment. This unit builds on knowledge of the diversity of animals and interprets the differences and similarities within the framework of their evolutionary relationships to each other. While sometimes very different, animals provide many fascinating examples of solutions to the problems of gaining nutrients, removing wastes and surviving to reproduce the next generation. The unit provides a broad overview of animal function and structure and integrates this information with that introduced in earlier units of the major. It is a step leading to the units of Level 3 of the major where the underlying fundamental mechanisms and processes are revealed.

Unit Information

Animals are shaped by their evolutionary origins, and by their physical and chemical environments.  Environments, which change over geological time, also shaped the ancestors of extant animal species.  Thus animal forms are often superbly adapted to their environments, but also may be maladapted.  This unit builds on knowledge of the diversity of animals from ANIM2220, and interprets their bauplan form, within the constraints of their current and previous environments, to fulfil the processes that sustain life.  These processes include exchanging gases, gaining nutrients, removing wastes all required for metabolic processes, and surviving to reproduce the next generation.  Although the general requirements for these processes are consistent, specific details may vary dramatically in different environments and for different bauplans.  The unit will focus on animal phyla that have representatives in multiple environments (salt and fresh water, on land, in soil, and in the air), especially vertebrates.  The unit will provide a broad overview of animal form and function, and will integrate this information with that introduced in earlier units of the Zoology major.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

Teaching will be through a blended model of lectures and laboratories, along with textbook reading.  The major learning processes will be theory delivered in lectures, followed by demonstration in laboratories. Students will examine live and preserved specimens, and dissect a diversity of animal species to understand changes in bauplans between and within phyla.
All lectures will be online only.  About half of the laboratory classes will be online only; the remaining half will be face to face, but these too will be available online, should circumstances arise; thus the entire unit may be completed online.  Lecturing staff will be available face to face and online at specified times to assist with learning materials, similar to flipped-lectures.  Lecturing staff can be available for online only students, on a sign-up basis.  Information about changes to classes will be distributed through LMS Announcements in advance. 
For both online and face-to-face classes, it is expected that students will participate all aspects of the unit, watching lectures online, appropriate sections of textbook, prior reading of provided laboratory materials, and willingness to be involved in discussion during laboratories and online discussions. 
The textbook is:  Hickman, R., Keen, E., Larson A. and L’Anson H. 2020.  Integrated Principles of Zoology, 18th edition: McGrawHill

Unit Structure

The unit consist of two lectures (one hour each) per week for the 12 week semester, and one laboratory class (two hours each) for ten weeks during the semester.  Lectures and laboratories will cover:
- essential requirements for basic metabolic processes, and how these requirements differ across major environments, 
- physical and chemical limitations imposed by environmental constraints, 
- relationships between body forms (bauplans) to physiology and ecology, 
- variations of bauplans adapted to each environment within animal phyla, 
- adaptations for different feeding strategies in different environments, for different bauplans, within animal phyla.
- diversity of reproductive processes and development in different environments, for different bauplans.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit
