Unit Information

This unit introduces the history, core concepts and current issues in the fields of philanthropy, fundraising and social investment. It includes an global international historical overview of the emergence of philanthropy from more traditional forms of giving, the recent emergence of social investment and examines social change social, technological and environmental drivers of these changes. An understanding of the different approaches to the contemporary practice of both philanthropy and social investment is provided and is the core of the course with both ethical and technical considerations of good practice in these domains also examined.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

We believe that learning is a dynamic process involving: the instructor; the instructors’ experiences; the subject matter; and the learning styles and experience of the students. Students often hold the wisdom of the subject within themselves. Their experiences are therefore an integral ingredient in the learning experience; they need to be accessed through eﬀective facilitation to enable students to make sense of the materials and enrich the learning experience for others.
In order to enrich the learning process with students’ experiences, we actively encourage students to contribute in group and class discussions and in-class learning activities. All class activities and discussions employ some form of Socratic dialogue to challenge students to seek solutions to both simple and complex problems from a range of perspectives. To enact this approach, we pose problems common to the topic, use guest lecturers and cases to stimulate discussion and critical analysis. We make use of case studies to generate debate and encourage critical and creative thinking in each topic to which students can relate. Thus, to get the most out of this unit, students are required to attend and participate in each lecture having undertaken the required pre-reading and be willing to listen and contribute to class discussions.

Introduction
Welcome to Social Investment & Philanthropy MGMT5518. This course is one of the core units of the Graduate Certificate in Social Impact.
Philanthropy and social investment – the provision of private and investment funds for social purposes – are both enjoying booms in Australia. As increasingly important players in the social sector, getting an insight into what motivates Philanthropists and social investors and what they require of investee organisations is are ever more important. Both these categories of funders  social value and social impact. Both groups collaborate  with government, the community, and the corporate sector to make social change.
The practice of social investment and philanthropy are being changed and shaped by new societal and environmental challenges, new technologies, shifts in the role of government as more of a funder and less a provider of the full range of social goods. In the context of increasing expectations on business for ESG considerations to be addressed there is also a growing role and expectation for corporate philanthropy. Globally, wealth distribution is changing in terms of geography and generation. Two major transfers of wealth are underway, one is the rise of the high net worth community in the East, and in particular China, India and South East Asia, secondly, the greatest intergenerational wealth transfer the world has seen - from the post-war generation (baby-boomers) to their descendents is well underway. 
This course also seeks to provide the opportunity for practitioners and students of the social impact sector to think like investors and philanthropists rather than as social entrepreneurs and social impact leaders. Being a good philanthropist or social impact investor requires as for any investor, skill, information, the right tools and techniques that result in thoughtful due diligence and decision making. All of these can be learned. By encouraging a planned, strategic and businesslike approach to private and corporate giving and investment we can in fact improve social impact. This is a domain which offers career opportunities, challenge and engagement and personal fulfillment to people of all ages and stages of life.
Unit goals:
The course aims to develop a critical awareness of the various stages in the process of philanthropic and social investment, from need identification to evaluation of impact. It also looks at philanthropy and social investment from a wide variety of perspectives including: charity/not-for-profit; donor/investor; recipient; government. The course also aims to equip students with knowledge about the contemporary operating environment and practices in philanthropy and social investment including the legal frameworks and the changes in community thinking about giving and philanthropy.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
