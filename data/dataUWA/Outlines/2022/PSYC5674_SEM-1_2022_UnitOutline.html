Teaching contingency plan (In response to Covid19 restrictions)

We will make every attempt to continue to teach this unit in-person, at the appointed time. However, this might not be possible, such as if I, the lecturer, need to self-isolate; in this case, a short-term solution would be to place as much of the lecture as is reasonable online. In the case that I, as lecturer, become ill, it might mean that there will be some weeks’ workshops/lectures delayed, as given the professional and specialised nature of the postgraduate programs a ‘substitute’ staff is not generally possible to find, especially on short notice. If this happens, I will make arrangements with students to deliver these workshops/lectures as soon as possible after recovering by making arrangements that mutually suit students. Delivering an increased number of lectures in a single week will be considered as the first possibility, as this avoids lectures going past week 12 of semester and final grades being delayed. However, if this is not possible, then these workshops/lectures will be delivered after week 12 of semester. I appreciate that this is not ideal, and see this as a temporary measure put in place to handle this extraordinary circumstance. In the case of other unforeseen events, I will try to bring the most mutually satisfying possibility, knowing that we will still need to ensure that this meets the unit objectives and gets students the intended competencies. In the case of assessments, it might be that in the case of a significant cancellation of class (2 or more classes), due dates are pushed back in-keeping with the length of the delay of the lecture(s). In the case of the presentation assessment, this might need to be delivered by students online instead of in-person, but otherwise no changes are foreseen. Thanks for your understanding and patience as I (and all UWA lecturers) try to bring you the best educational experience that we are able to.

Academic Information

Content Typically, children are referred for assessment by parents, carers, teachers or other professionals working with them. The issues for which they are referred are of concern to the referring party; they are usually, but not necessarily, of concern for the child. Issues that cause most concern vary with the age of the child but typically include problems with emotional development, learning and behaviour. The unit introduces students to the presentation of children with a variety of mental health disorders that typically arise during the developmental years; the role of culture in development, and culturally competent care; assessment tools and techniques; diagnostic criteria and differential diagnosis; communication and consultation with stakeholders; and common evidence-based interventions.

The School of Psychological Science at The University of Western Australia acknowledges that it is situated on Noongar land, and that Noongar people remain the spiritual and cultural custodians of their land, and continue to practise their values, languages, beliefs and knowledge. Children do not self-refer for assessment. This raises a range of ethical and practical issues. Typically, they are referred for assessment by parents, carers, teachers or other professionals working with them. The issues for which they are referred are of concern to the referring party; they are usually, but not necessarily, of concern for the child. For some, this begins to shift early in adolescence, when adolescents begin to seek their own help at times. Issues that cause most concern vary with the age of the developing child but typically include problems with emotional development, learning and behaviour. The unit introduces students to the presentation of children and adolescents with a variety of mental health disorders that typically arise during the developmental years; assessment tools and techniques; diagnostic criteria and differential diagnosis; working with parents; communication and consultation with stakeholders; and common evidence-based interventions.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

METHOD OF INSTRUCTION
The unit will employ a variety of teaching techniques; you will read the literature, attend and listen to lectures, prepare and deliver material to your peers (as current and future colleagues) and participate in class (e.g., through problem-solving and/or role-play opportunities). In some weeks there may be work to prepare for the next class. This may be in the form of preparatory reading, video viewing, or similar forms of engagement with the material.
Teaching will be delivered primarily through problem-based style lectures. This method of teaching involves didactic-style teaching of background information, along with case examples, examples of possible applications of the information that are brainstormed in class, and asking for groups and individuals to problem-solve issues that are relevant to the topic. The instructor will therefore be responsible for providing background information, case examples, and knowledge application examples, and for stimulating problem-solving opportunities for students. Students will therefore be responsible for doing the assigned readings prior to class, reviewing their notes after class and clarifying confusions with the instructor, and participating actively during class.

Participation

Students should come to class; this is not an online unit. If a student cannot attend a class, then please kindly notify the instructor and make plans to cover the material independently.  It is compulsory that students be punctual and attend all coursework and other specified training activities unless extenuating circumstances (such as illness or significant personal crisis) prevent them from doing so. Where students cannot attend (or have not attended) the full required activities or classes, they are expected to notify the unit coordinator to provide documentation (special consideration) explaining their absence or lateness.
A failure to attend without adequate explanation and documented evidence of illness or other impeding factors beyond one’s personal control will result in failing the unit in which the class/activity was missed. Note that even where an adequate explanation and documented evidence are supplied, a student will still fail the unit if he/she has not attended a sufficient proportion of the course. 
A “sufficient proportion of the course” is normally defined as at least 80%; in recognition that 2022 might have impacts on students’ wellness with respect to COVID-19, this will be defined as at least 75% for this year only.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
