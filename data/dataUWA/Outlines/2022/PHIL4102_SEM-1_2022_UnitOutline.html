Academic Information

Content There are diverse areas in social and political philosophy which are of great relevance to current social and political debates. This unit explores one or more of these areas in social and political philosophy. Topics discussed may include, but are not limited to, bioethics, environmental ethics, terrorism, torture, war, social and economic justice, democracy, punishment, globalisation, liberty, equality, multiculturalism and corporate responsibility. While the focus of the unit is on philosophical analysis of these issues, a range of perspectives may be examined. The unit may employ a variety of sources, from academic papers through to films.

Unit Information

Social networks are ubiquitous. While we have come to associate the term with online social media, a social network exists whenever a group of individual people (or animals or other agents in the broadest sense of the word) engage in structured interactions.
Social networks often involve exchanges of information. Classmates may share notes, friends may exchange tips about restaurants or books, children inherit genetic material (which bears information!) from their parents, and so on.
Individual exchanges of information may be more or less successful. The thing you tell me might be true or false, and I may understand and accept the thing you tell me or fail to do so. But moreover, a social network as a whole may be more or less successful from an epistemic perspective insofar as agents in the network tend to succeed or fail at relevant epistemic challenges. For example, Zollman's book (our primary reading material this semester) starts with a case study suggesting that the social network at NASA is ineffective at transmitting information from engineers to managers.
Network epistemology is the name of the subfield of epistemology that focuses on the epistemic relevance of features of social networks. This unit will examine network epistemology from the perspective of Zollman's draft book Network Epistemology, supplemented with relevant articles and book chapters.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

Our meetings are organized as a reading group. This means that there will be little or no lecturing. Rather, our meetings are used to enrich our shared understanding of the reading material through interactive discussion. It is therefore vital that you (a) read the material in advance and (b) come to class prepared with some questions, especially about any points from the reading that were unclear to you.

Unit Structure

We will meet on Fridays (14:00 - 16:00 in seminar room G03 in the Arts building [moved from the originally scheduled time 10:00-12:00 by mutual agreement]). We will proceed at a pace of two readings per week: one chapter from Zollman's book and one related article or book chapter. A detailed unit schedule will be uploaded and updated separately on LMS.
As a general rule, we will use one hour to discuss one reading. For each such hour, there will be one person leading the discussion. Each student in the unit will do this two to five times (depending on final enrollment numbers); I will do whatever remains. To lead the discussion, prepare a summary of the chapter (aim for 20-30 minutes) as well as some discussion questions to get us started. Again, I recommend focusing your questions on aspects of the reading that you would like to clarify your understanding of. Note that leading discussions is a required component of the unit. When it is not your turn to lead the discussion, you are still expected to prepare and participate.
The essay is due on Thursday 19 May. You should write a philosophical essay (i.e., taking a view and defending it through philosophical argument) on a topic discussed in or closely related to the unit, up to 4,000 words. A document with instructions and tips regarding the essay will be made available on LMS.
There will also be an examination. More information on this will be made available in due course.
The standard weighting of assessment items for philosophy honours units applies (I believe this is 60% for the essay and 40% for the test, with a 10% penalty if you were to fail to meet the participation, preparation, or presentation requirements - the honours information that you will have received from the honours coordinator is the authoritative document on this point).
In order to mitigate the effects of implicit biases and other sources of unfairness in marking, the essay and test will be graded anonymously. To make this possible, it is vital that you put your student number but not your name on your essay and test.

Referencing Requirements

In your essay, you should include an in-text reference whenever you are either directly quoting someone else or describing a view or argument in your own words. This reference should give the last name of the author, the year of publication, and a page or section reference - nothing more and nothing less. Example 1: "inequity is the expected outcome of basic cultural evolutionary processes" (O'Connor 2019, p. 3). Example 2: O'Connor's book aims to show that unfairness can be explained through cultural evolution (O'Connor 2019, p. 3). Your essay should include a bibliography that lists all (and only) those sources explicitly referenced in the text of your essay, using a consistent bibliographic style, e.g., Harvard or Oxford.
In the examination and during class discussion, there are no formal referencing requirements. Still, it is important that you distinguish clearly between your own ideas and those you have derived from a scholarly source (e.g., one of the readings). Use phrases like "Zollman claims..." or "Zollman argues" to indicate when the latter is the case. Plagiarism is a very serious issue, and it's in your best interest to avoid creating the impression that you are using others' ideas without acknowledging this.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
