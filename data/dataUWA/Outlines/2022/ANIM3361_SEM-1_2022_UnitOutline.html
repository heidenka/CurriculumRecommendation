In the event of increasing restrictions or a lockdown in response to Covid19, which prevent attendance at face-to-face labs or seminars, ANIM3361 teaching activities will move fully online. There will be no change in the unit objectives, assessment activities or deadlines. Regular announcements will be made throughout the unit to keep everyone informed. Online lab and lecture/seminar activities will be supported by Blackboard Collaborate Ultra sessions at the scheduled times.

Academic Information

Content This unit analyses the way animal populations fluctuate and use resources in space and time. It considers how individuals within populations interact with each other and with individuals in populations of other species, including those interactions that contribute to the determination of the biodiversity apparent in communities today. It covers population parameters such as age structure, birth and death rates and how these are used to construct life tables and population models. Theories and empirical evidence as to how biodiversity has developed over time and is currently maintained are considered along with discussion of how biodiversity contributes to ecosystem function and how that biodiversity should be managed and conserved.

Unit Information

This unit analyses the way animal populations fluctuate and use resources in space and time. It considers how individuals within populations interact with each other and with individuals in populations of other species, including those interactions that contribute to the determination of the biodiversity apparent in communities today. It covers population parameters such as age structure, birth and death rates and how these are used to construct life tables and population models. Theories and empirical evidence as to how biodiversity has developed over time and is currently maintained are considered along with discussion of how biodiversity contributes to ecosystem function and how that biodiversity should be managed and conserved.
Learning outcomes in ANIM3361: students are able to (1) acquire a knowledge of population parameters and use them to produce population models; (2) understand how models are used to manage and conserve animal populations; (3) understand how populations interact with populations of the same and different species and model those responses; (4) use statistics to analyse data either collected during practical sessions or provided from field experiments; (5) articulate the main characteristics of communities and the divergent theories on how diversity is generated and maintained; (6) be able to apply theories of community organisation to conservation and management; (7) use multivariate techniques to analyse community data; and (8) critically assess recent and relevant literature.
Our expectations of students taking this unit are that they:
1. attend all laboratory sessions (face-to-face, when possible).  This is compulsory and attendance will be noted.
2. either attend all scheduled lectures or listen to them on LMS within 24 hours of the scheduled lecture time.
Read at least ONE of the textbook accounts, plus any assigned reading on the lecture topic in conjunction with a review of your notes from the lecture.
Attempt all the self-assessment exercises and check their answers against the solutions posted on LMS.
Complete all the assignments and submit them by the due date.
We also assume that for every contact hour (five per week) for this unit you will spend an equivalent amount of time on private revision, reading and study and completing assignments.  Students who follow these guidelines should have no trouble passing the unit.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

Lectures/seminars are recorded, either before or during the allocated time slot.  Tuesday 9am lectures will be pre-recorded throughout the semester but Thursday 1pm lectures will be "live" giving students an opportunity to ask questions for at least the first half of the semester.  In the second half of the semester, the community ecology seminars will have an assessment component for group presentation and individual contribution to discussions.
Practical sessions are compulsory and involve hands-on participation (face-to-face, when possible). This includes both laboratory and computer-based exercises.  Some practical activities are assessed in-class.  On-line classes may involve scheduled 'Teams' or Collaborate Ultra' sessions.
There is one three-hour field excursion during lab time in Week 9 of semester and one field activity to be completed in your own time.

Unit Structure

There are two lecture/seminars each week: Tuesday at 9am and Thursday at 1pm.  The Tuesday lecture will be pre-recorded, but the Thursday lecture/seminars will involve a face to face opportunity to ask questions in the Jennifer Arnold Lecture Theatre (JALT) in the Zoology Building.  Students must attend one three-hour practical session per week, either at 10am on Wednesday morning or 2pm on Wednesday afternoon in JALT.  Five of these laboratory sessions are held in the Botany annex computer lab.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
