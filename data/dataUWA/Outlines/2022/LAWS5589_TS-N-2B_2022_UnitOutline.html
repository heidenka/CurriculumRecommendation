Academic Information

Content This unit examines the fundamentals of the law in Australia relating to the exploration, development and production of oil and gas and the development and production of wind and solar power. Topics covered include the structure of the licensing and regulatory regimes, the legal nature and protection of exploration and production rights, ownership systems, control of operations, infrastructure and sustainability issues.

Unit Information

This unit aims to give a clear understanding of why oil and gas exploration, production and distribution require specialised legal solutions and how those solutions are shaped in Australia. It compares the legal solutions developed for the commercialisation  and supply of alternative energy sources such as solar and wind and how all those energy sources are responding to requirements for sustainable operations. It provides a working knowledge of  of the Australian legal regime for energy regulation and  the capacity of a critical evaluation of that regime by reference to public policy objectives.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

The unit is taught intensively in workshop mode. Accordingly the proceedings are not recorded on LCS. Students should review materials distributed on LMS well before the start of the intensive workshop. Teaching is through a mixture of plenary and small-group sessions in an intensive workshop. The workshop comprises approximately 9 plenary sessions which review and discuss the materials. These aim to give broad coverage of the key legal materials and concepts. Some sessions will involve Powerpoint presentations which will be distributed after the sessions, if appropriate for future use. Small-group sessions will take up particular problems raised in the earlier plenary sessions for more examination in the light of general guidance given in the plenary sessions. Small-group sessions are held in the afternoon each day and cover the morning's material, giving students the opportunity to test and expand their understanding and analysis of the issues dealt with in the morning. In the small-group sessions students will work in syndicates with industry participants to work up responses to the problems which will then be compared and further discussed in meetings of all the groups together. 
Some of the readings included in the materials are drawn directly from legislation, regulations, and reports of judicial decisions. Context and language may be unfamiliar to students who do not have a legal background, but the lectures will aim to clarify the significance and, where necessary, the meaning of this material. Alongside the intensive sessions the other major element of the unit is a research paper. Students from a non-legal back ground will find the following  helpful: UWA Legal Resources for Postgraduates in Law- introductory materials for students without a legal background. This is on your LMS home page.

The learning outcomes are that students are able to  critically analyse (1) the specialised legal solutions applied in Australia and internationally for the regulation of
exploration, development and production of oil and gas and for the development and production of wind and solar alternative energy; (2) how modern concepts of sustainability are affecting the production of oil and gas and alternative energy; (3) the legal principles underlying the Australian offshore petroleum licensing regime with reference to the principles adopted in selected other jurisdictions and concepts of sustainability; (4)  the differences between the legal principles applicable to the regulation of oil and gas and those applicable to wind and solar
energy; and (5) create clear, concise and persuasive legal arguments using correct and plain English, both verbally and inwriting.

Late submission of Assignments
A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.
For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).
If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.
If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;
Penalty for exceeding word limit
Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
