Academic Information

Content This unit focuses on the most fundamental skills of a geologist in being able to describe and interpret geological material and relationships at outcrop scale. It builds skills in recording and summarising observations, descriptions and interpretations made in the field, and synthesising results to produce diagrammatic and written summaries of an area. The unit comprises laboratory work prior to the field trip to develop skills that need to be applied on the field trip as well as a seven-day field trip to undertake geological mapping (north of Perth). Field work emphasises teamwork and workplace safety.

Unit Information

This unit is an important unit in your progression through the Geology, Geochemistry and Integrated Earth & Environment majors, as well as earth science minors, in focusing on field data collection, representation of field data in graphical forms, and interpretation of geological events through time as shown by the learning outcomes.  Gaining confidence in reading and constructing geological maps and cross-sections is also emphasised by the unit's learning outcomes. The residential fieldtrip is a great opportunity to get to know your classmates and to develop professional workplace skills in appropriate safety and conduct in the field.  Please note that there is no supplementary assessment available for the field trip component of this unit and that the field trip must be completed for this unit. Alongside technical skills, being able to demonstrate scientific communication skills, teamwork, and professional conduct are important to employers of geoscience graduates.  Please review the Objective, Content and Learning Outcomes above.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

This unit utilises experiential learning to support you in building and strengthening your 2D and 3D spatial skills. You will receive your practical manual for this unit in your first class.  We will gradually work through a range of exercises that build depth in your understanding from week to week.  For this reason, it is important and expected that you attend your class each week in this unit.  Preparation for some classes helps to introduce the week's work which may include reading or short exercises to try.  It is important to come to class prepared, including bringing your calculator, pencils, eraser and ruler to be able to successfully complete the work. Some weeks there will be a short presentation to introduce key concepts or background, or feedback from the previous week's work or assessment work and, therefore, you should endeavour to be punctual to class so that you don't miss these important elements.  
Since we will gradually build complexity as we progress through the unit, and in preparation for the fieldtrip, it is important that work is completed and feedback sought, so that you gain confidence in your ability to 'read' geological map relationships to construct cross-sections and geological histories.  In this unit, we will also incorporate learning from Level 1 Geology (e.g. EART1104 or equivalent units elsewhere) and EART2231 Earth Materials with respect to important rock-forming and rock-deforming processes.  Fieldwork is really important for applying conceptual knowledge and practical skills learnt in other units and for highlighting the value and relevance of the coursework in the major.

Participation

It is expected that you come to your practical class each week and complete the work and any associated assessments. You will be given a practical manual which contains the weekly work and related resources at the start of the unit, and you will need to bring this to class with you each week.  Please pay attention to the table of contents and note where short preparatory work needs to be undertaken prior to class. This preparation will help you complete the practical work in class in a timely manner.  COVID impacts may require some teaching activities to shift online as described below.
It is important that you demonstrate sufficient skill development, by attending your classes regularly through semester and completion of practical exercises and assessments, to be able to participate in the fieldtrip and be successful in completing this unit.  Fieldwork can be daunting so our preparation during semester will help you develop confidence in a number of important skills that you will draw heavily on when we are in the field!  Fieldwork will also help you deepen and strengthen your geoscientific knowledge and skills which are directly linked to employment of geoscience graduates.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
