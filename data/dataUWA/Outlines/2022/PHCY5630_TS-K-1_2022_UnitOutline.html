Academic Information

Content This unit continues to provide detailed pharmacology and pharmacotherapy of major disease states and further expands on the concepts and application of pharmacokinetics, which is first introduced to students in PHCY5602 Pharmacy Practice and Pharmacotherapy 1. An emphasis is placed on the student increasing their awareness of the patient as a whole through the concept of patient centred care. Pharmacology and pharmacotherapy is presented in an integrated fashion and will provide an in-depth look at the pharmacists' role in the management of major disease states. Other topics include drug interactions; therapeutic drug monitoring; laboratory testing and monitoring of results; sources of inter-patient variability in pharmacokinetics, clinical case studies and pharmacokinetics of specific drugs. A case-based approach to learning helps student focus is on the key learning objectives. The cases are selected so that they challenge the students' understanding of the topic and provide ground for discussion between the student and the topic tutor. Use of clinically-oriented problems and case studies will enable students to apply principles of pharmacology and pharmacotherapy to real-world examples in preparation for hospital placements.

Unit Information

This unit explores issues related to the pharmacology and pharmacotherapy of major disease states and expands on pharmacokinetic and pharmacodynamic concepts that were introduced in PHCY5602 Pharmacy Practice and Pharmacotherapy 1. 
IPTAPP 3 must be taken alongside the complementary unit PHCY5612 Integrated Pharmacology, Therapeutics and Pharmacy Practice 2 (IPTAPP 2). In both units, emphasis is placed on the student increasing their awareness of the needs of the whole patient through the concept of patient-centred care. 
Pharmacology and pharmacotherapy topics are explored in an integrated fashion with a view to cultivating awareness of the pharmacist’s role in the management of major disease states. Special consideration is given to monitoring the efficacy and safety of treatment regimens such that students gain valuable insight into the development and use of therapeutic management plans. 
A case-based approach to student learning ensures that the teaching focus is closely aligned with the key learning objectives. The cases are chosen to challenge the students' understanding of the topic and provide grounds for discussion between the student and the topic tutor. 
Specific topics include therapeutic drug monitoring; drug-drug interactions; pharmacogenomic sources of inter-patient variability due to inherited pharmacogene variants; clinical case studies and pharmacokinetic studies of specific drugs. Engaging with recent international research studies as well as the use of clinically-oriented case studies will enable students to apply these principles to real-world examples in preparation for hospital placements.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

This unit involves a combination of teaching and learning approaches, including the use of face-to-face in-class lectures, workshops, and tutorials. Lectures are recorded and will be available through LMS following the session. Self-directed learning and group-work sessions will further foster student learning throughout the semester. 
The pharmacology content explores fundamental pharmacokinetic and pharmacodynamic principles while also seeking to cultivate student competence in drug calculations. This knowledge is extended through the application of pharmacology concepts to therapeutics, including an introduction to clinical pharmacotherapy principles that are applied across a selection of major disease states. 
Lecturers will use cases to illustrate their practice area where relevant, while a series of clinical pharmacokinetics workshops will engage students with current international research in emerging fields including pharmacogenomics, drug transporters and drug-drug interactions.

Participation

All sessions are important learning experiences for students and therefore attendance is expected. All sessions delivered by guest lecturers (including the hospital pharmacy sessions) are compulsory. Students must arrive in time to sign-in prior to the session commencing. All tutorials, mentor sessions, laboratories, site visits etc. are compulsory.
If a student is unable to attend a session due to illness or for any other exceptional circumstance, the Unit Coordinator must be contacted as soon as practicable. Absences of more than one day due to illness will require a medical certificate to be presented. Failure to meet this requirement may/will result in a report of Professional Misconduct being made.
Name badges are compulsory for all Pharmacy Practice sessions – the tutors and lecturers use a problem based learning style where you need to participate in discussions in class and it is important that the tutor can refer to you by your name.
Preparation: Many sessions will involve preparation beforehand to enhance the learning experience. Please ensure this preparation is completed. If for some reason the preparation cannot be completed, discuss this in advance with the Unit Co-ordinator. Sessional staff will be asked to report back to the Unit Co-ordinator if preparation is not completed at an acceptable level.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.
