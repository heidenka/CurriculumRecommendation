Academic Information

Content This unit examines the fundamentals of construction law with an emphasis on their use in the context of petroleum and mining projects. Topics covered include the nature of resource project contracting arrangements and construction contracts, common terms in construction contracts, risk allocation in construction contracts, contract formation, common issues including liquidated damages, extensions of time, causation and procedural issues for claims, variations, indemnity and insurance provisions, liability for defects, warranties and indemnities. The unit examines these in the context of a construction project for a mine site, involving either a power station for the mine or a desalination plant. The workshop sessions involve participants familiarising themselves with a standard form of contract widely used in the resources industry in Australia.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

The unit is taught intensively. Teaching is through a mixture of plenary and small-group sessions in an intensive workshop. The plenary sessions are in the form of lectures interspersed with discussion based on the materials. Students will be provided with a draft design and construct (D&C) contract, and this contract will be used for discussion in the plenary sessions and also for the small-group sessions. The first of these will involve an analysis of the risk allocation between principal and contractor in the draft contract.
Some of the readings included in the materials are drawn directly from legislation, regulations, and reports of judicial decisions. Context and language may be unfamiliar to students who do not have a legal background, but the lectures will aim to clarify the significance and, where necessary, the meaning of this material. Students from a non-legal background will find the following helpful: UWA Legal Resources for Postgraduates in Law - introductory materials for students without a legal background (contact Dr Marilyn Bromberg (marilyn.bromberg@uwa.edu.au) for access in LMS). 
The materials for the unit will be released on LMS in stages commencing Monday, 21 November 2022 under the 'Topics' subheading and titled according to the session to which they relate. Students should endeavour to read through the materials relevant to each session prior to attending that session; however, the sessions will cover the topics covered by the materials so pre-reading is not essential.
 The learning outcomes for the unit are that students are able to: 
1. Recognise and describe the fundamental principles of contract law most relevant to construction projects;
2. Recognise and describe the common terms of construction contracts;
3. Apply those principles and terms in hypothetical case studies to demonstrate understanding;
4. Critically analyse the application of those principles and terms in situations relevant to projects in the mineral and petroleum resources sector of the economy; and
5. Create clear, concise and persuasive legal arguments using correct and plain English.

Unit Structure

The unit is taught intensively. Reading materials will be available for access on LMS in stages commencing on 21 November 2022. The intensive workshop commences at 9am on 30 November and runs until 12:30pm on 2 December. Sessions run from 9am to 5pm each day, except for the last which finishes at 12:30pm. 
The venue is LAWS: 106 Law Lecture Theatre, first floor of the Law Building.
Note that the Lecture Capture System is NOT AVAILABLE for this unit. As the unit is run intensively, and students must attend every day of the intensive period, classes will not be recorded for this unit.
The Assessment of this unit consists of:
1. CASE STUDY ANALYSIS
Participants will be assessed on a case study which involves preparing proposed amendments to specific provisions of a principal draft of a D&C contract.  Students will be asked to focus on risk allocation between the parties on several specifically identified areas of risk. The exercise involves reviewing the draft D&C contract, describing the risk from the point of view of the contractor and drafting proposed amendments or risk mitigation strategies in relation to each of the identified areas of risk. 
The list of specific risk areas to be addressed and a qualifications register template to be used for the assessment will be posted to LMS following session 4 of the workshop.
2. TAKE HOME EXAMINATION
A take-home exam will consist of a problem question to which participants will need to apply the terms of the draft D&C contract and the fundamental principles of contract law covered during the course. The take-home exam and accompanying marking rubric will be available to participants on the last day of the intensive workshop. 
Session 8 of the workshop will include a discussion of an example exam question.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
