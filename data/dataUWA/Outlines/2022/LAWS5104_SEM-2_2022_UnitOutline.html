Academic Information

Content This unit explores the nature, function and regulation of companies in Australia, with a view to understanding and being able to apply corporations law principles arising at common law, in equity and under statute. Students engage with corporate governance principles, comparative perspectives and recent developments in the law to critically study the internal structure and governance of the company, placing emphasis on duties owed by directors to the company and rights and remedies available to members. In the context of ongoing debate concerning the role and theory of the corporation, students consider the relationship between corporations and the outside world in the form of corporate liability in contract, tort and criminal law. Through class discussion, students develop appreciation of the ethical issues confronting company directors and their advisers and hone their skills of analysing legal issues, advising as to the application of corporations law to hypothetical problem scenarios and effectively debating policy issues. Students refine their research, analytical and communication skills by preparing a research assignment based on a corporations law problem or policy scenario. They then carry out a critique of their own work to identify how they can further improve these key skills so as to become effective participants in the practice and policy development of corporations law.

Unit Information

There are in excess of 213 million corporations worldwide, including over 2.8 million in Australia (https://www.statista.com/statistics/1260719/global-companies-by-region/; https://asic.gov.au/regulatory-resources/find-a-document/statistics/company-registration-statistics/2021-company-registration-statistics/). The company is the most common entity through which business is conducted. Issues relating to the regulation of companies, and their impacts on and interrelationship with stakeholders and society more broadly, have formed the subject of a spate of recent Royal Commissions and government inquiries, including the Royal Commission into Misconduct in the Banking, Superannuation and Financial Services Industry (2017), Inquiry by the Joint Standing Committee on Northern Australia into the Destruction of 46,000 Year Old Caves at the Juukan Gorge in the Pilbara Region of WA (2021), Australian Law Reform Commission Inquiry into Corporate Criminal Responsibility (2020), Perth and Melbourne Casino Royal Commissions (2021-2), and the Australian Human Rights Commission's National Inquiry into Sexual Harassment in Australian Workplaces (2020).  
Any student who goes into practice as a lawyer or who enters the business community will need, as a minimum, to understand the nature of a company as a potential party to litigation or a contract. Increasingly, students seeking to work in governance, environmental, human rights and broader social contexts will also need understanding of the nature, governance and regulation of companies.  
This unit involves a study of the legal framework governing the nature, operation and regulation of companies in Australia. More broadly, learning in the unit is designed to assist students to:
* acquire an awareness of the historical, political, social and economic policy considerations underlying Australian company law;
* develop the ability to analyse and think critically about current issues in company law and any need for reform;
* develop commercial thinking skills, including an ability to take account of context and client needs in providing legal advice; and 
* critically evaluate their own work by identifying strengths and weaknesses, and engaging with feedback.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

This unit will be taught via a combination of face-to-face lectures and tutorials. Each topic will comprise a series of lectures and readings. Select topics will include guided learning activities (eg quizzes, short questions, sample problems).
Your learning will be reinforced in the relevant tutorials. Tutorials are a critically important part of the unit, as they provide the opportunity to apply your learning to complex fact scenarios. We encourage you to participate as fully as possible in the tutorial programme. 

Unit Structure

The unit comprises three hours of lectures a week, on Tuesdays (1 hour) and Thursdays (2 hours). 
Students are required to check LMS regularly for information and announcements regarding the unit.
Tutorials run weekly, commencing week 2 (week beginning Monday 1 August). Tutorial enrolment is via the on-line Class Allocation System. It is each student's responsibility to ensure they have enrolled in a tutorial for the purposes of the tutorial assessment. You must attend your assigned tutorial to ensure you are assessed for the participation component. Guidance on participation is provided on LMS.
There will be one on-line tutorial each week, running on Saturdays from 10-11am, for students who have been unable to attend their scheduled face-to-face tutorial for reasons relating to Covid19 (ie for students who are unwell or isolating).  Students who need to attend this tutorial should complete the registration form on LMS so that their attendance is registered, and a record can be made of their participation.

Participation

The objectives of the participation mark are to promote: 
* engagement;
* preparation; 
* understanding; 
* reflection; and 
* responsible tutorial membership.
Engagement will most obviously be in speaking but a student might also be engaged whilst listening. This will be important to note for students who are by nature quieter. They may be engaged, though speak less often.
Preparation might be evident in notes the student has come with or in the quality of answers and comments in discussion.
Understanding of the issues raised in tutorials will most commonly be demonstrated though a student's overall contribution to the tutorial discussion, including small group discussions within a tutorial, whether by way of answering questions, asking questions, making observations, or listening and responding to other students.
Reflection on the issues (and preparation) might be evident in the student drawing on material outside the material directly related to the tutorial problem, perhaps from other topics in the course and in questions asked during (or even after) the tutorial seminar;
Responsible tutorial membership refers to the respect given to other student's comments and refraining from attempting to dominate the discussion.
Participation is about the quality not necessarily the quantity of contributions (where 'quality' means considered and reflective contributions -you are allowed to make mistakes!). The aim of including this component is to encourage unselfconscious discussion amongst students, not an atmosphere of assessment.
Your Tutorial Participation mark will be based on your participation in Tutorial 8 (assignment reflections) plus your eight best of the remaining 10 tutorials. Tutorial 8 is scheduled for the week beginning Monday 26 September. Tutorial 8 will involve preparing a critique and sharing reflections on your Corporations Law assignment.  This is a compulsory tutorial, as engagement with the assignment critique is necessary to demonstrate attainment of the unit learning outcomes.

ASSIGNMENT SPECIAL CONSIDERATION 
For students applying for special consideration in relation to their assignment, a maximum extension of 3 weeks will be available in this unit. Students not able to submit their assignment within 3 weeks of the due date may be permitted to withdraw from the unit without academic penalty.


Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
