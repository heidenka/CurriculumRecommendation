Academic Information

Content A large part of the work done by lawyers in practice is determining the meaning and application of words in statutes and contracts. The law of interpretation is the law that deals with the legal principles, aids and method of how to engage in that interpretation. This unit will mainly deal with the law of statutory interpretation, but will also cover key aspects of the law relating to the interpretation of contracts (which is distinct from the law on the formation and enforcement of contracts studied in LAWS4103 Contract). The unit will focus on the primary legislative and common law principles that govern the reading and understanding of the text in its context, and having regard to the instrument's purpose, in order to infer the ‘intention' of the maker. For statutes, other topics covered include legislative drafting, the relationship between statutes and between statutes and the common law, use of extrinsic materials, and delegated legislation. For contracts, other topics covered include use of surrounding circumstances and post-contractual conduct, and interpretation of particular classes of contract (eg: commercial contracts, State Agreements in the resources sector). The unit will highlight the similarities and the differences in the function of interpretation for statutes (public instruments) and contracts (private instruments), and in the approaches adopted by the legislature and the courts to the interpretation of each instrument.

Unit Information

"Many lawyers can get through their busy lives without an intimate acquaintance with many particular subjects of the law ... But none can get very far without a working familiarity with the approaches and rules that govern legal interpretation. Interpretation constitutes the key that unlocks the door to the meaning of law." (Hon. Michael Kirby AC CMG, Interpretation and Use of Legal Sources (Thomson Reuters, 2014) Foreword, vii).
Statutes and contracts are critical to legal practice. They are also important in many other areas of work (such as government policy or administration). There are now very few areas of law that are not significantly regulated by legislation, and contracts are important to many commercial and other legal relationships. To be properly prepared for legal practice, or any work related to law, it is essential that you have a sound understanding of how to read, interpret and apply legislation and contracts, and some understanding of the role that statutes and contracts have in our legal system as a whole. This unit will highlight the similarities and the differences in the function of interpretation for statutes (public instruments) and contracts (private instruments), and in the approaches adopted by the legislature and the courts to the interpretation of each instrument.
This unit can be a challenging one for some students, especially in the early part of the semester. While most of the concepts in interpretation law are not conceptually difficult, there is only a broad framework for the method of use of those concepts. Competence in interpretation of legal instruments requires attention to detail and practice. Tutorials and the assignment are therefore critical for effective learning in this unit.  Note that, for statutory interpretation, it will take us the first few weeks to cover the foundation concepts, which interact and provide the cornerstone of the law. This may mean that the overall nature of the law and the method will not start to 'gel' for you until weeks 4 or 5 of semester.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

The unit will be taught through a combination of weekly lectures and weekly tutorials. You are encouraged to attend lectures in person, though they will be recorded. Tutorials are face-to-face only.
The lectures will address the foundational content. It is essential that students keep up to date with lectures on a weekly basis. Interpretation law consists of concepts that build on each other. Putting together this law is like assembling pieces of a puzzle. Therefore, to eﬀectively study, you must engage with each topic weekly as subsequent areas of study will cumulatively build on prior areas.
Tutorials will primarily take the form of ‘hands on’ sessions. Attendance at tutorials is required and strongly recommended.  Becoming proficient in interpretation requires practice. Tutorials provide students with the most significant opportunity in the unit to enhance and develop their understanding of legal concepts and to put what we are learning into practice through hypothetical problems, case studies and critical analysis. By attending classes, students also have the opportunity to engage with the lecturer/tutor and fellow students.  It is extremely important that you prepare for, and attend, tutorials. Lectures can only provide you with the foundation - they are to guide you in your study, not to provide comprehensive information. 
As the tutorials are interactive, they will not be recorded.

Participation

There are ten tutorials over the semester. It is strongly recommended that you attend ALL tutorials. However, students can miss, without penalty, a maximum of ONE tutorial without applying for special consideration. Students must attend their allocated tutorial group. In accordance with Law School policy (see further under 'Other'), external work commitments, travel arrangements and sporting commitments are not accepted as grounds for absence from tutorials or for swapping tutorials. 
The most up to date information on venues for tutorials is available on the UWA Timetable website:  https://timetable.applications.uwa.edu.au
Students will be given a mark out of 10 for each tutorial. This will be converted to an overall mark out of 10 at the end of semester. Students will be awarded a mark on the basis of their preparation for, and participation in, tutorials in accordance with the following criteria. Please note that assessment of participation is about the quality of contribution, not necessarily the quantity of contributions (where ‘quality’ refers to considered, clear, and reflective contributions – you are allowed to make mistakes!)
HD (8 to 10 marks): Student shows excellent preparation for all tutorials; exceptional standard of participation with contributions being clear, substantive and insightful; student is punctual, involved in discussion, and listens respectfully to others; student shows excellent command of subject matter with articulate, well-constructed arguments.
D (7 to 7.5 marks): Student is well prepared for tutorials; contributions to class discussion are clear, relevant and well considered; student is punctual, involved in discussion, and listens respectfully to others; student shows good understanding of subject matter and arguments are informed and thoughtful.
CR (6 to 6.5 marks): Student is adequately prepared for all tutorials; attends and is punctual, shows interest, contributes to discussion and contributions are generally thoughtful and mostly clear.
P (5 to 5.5 marks): Student is not adequately prepared for most tutorials; attends; contributions to discussion are mostly adequate but reflect little understanding of the issues raised by the tutorials.
F (0 to 4.5): Student attends but may be late, shows little or no interest, contributions or preparation are inadequate; or student does not attend.
Students who do not attend a tutorial will be given a mark of 0 out of 10 for that tutorial unless there is special consideration granted.
NOTE: Students may perform at a level above or below some of the elements of each level descriptor. In that case, the mark will be allocated on an overall weighing of how the student performs.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
