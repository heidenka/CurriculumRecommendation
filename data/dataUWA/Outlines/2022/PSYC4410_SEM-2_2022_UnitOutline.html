Learning And Teaching Strategy

The unit will employ a variety of teaching and assessment techniques. Lectures will be complemented by seminars where students will give brief 3-minute-thesis (3MT) presentations about their Honours projects. Students will also present their thesis work more formally at the annual Honours Conference as well as attend the School of Psychological Science colloquia series and provide critical evaluation of the presentations. 
Learning Outcomes:
(1) Students are able to understand how to present their research in an informative and interesting way to a range of audiences. 
(2) Students are able to critically evaluate oral research presentations.
(3) Students are able to communicate their understanding of the research area in which they will be conducting research to an audience with expertise in this area.

Unit Structure

There will be three lectures in semester weeks 1, 2, and 5. There will be one required seminar (week 4), during which you will give a short 3-minute-thesis (3MT) presentation on your honours research project (i.e., using only 3 slides in 3 minutes). These will be marked via a combination of peer-assessment and marking by the unit coordinator (worth 20% of the grade). Students sign up for one of six available seminars in week 4. Use CAS to select your classes: www.cas.uwa.edu.au
The Honours conference will be held on Wednesday 7 September (during the study break). Here you will give an academic presentation of your Honours thesis research (worth 50% of the grade) which will be assessed by members of academic staff. You are expected to attend the full day conference to learn about the research of your peers and lend them your support. 
You also need to attend at least two of the School of Psychological Science colloquia series and submit critical evaluations of each of them (300 words per evaluation = 600 words maximum total; together worth 30% of the grade). Dates and venues of the colloquia will be announced in lectures and posted on LMS as soon as they are available.

Assessment Details 
3-minute-thesis: You will be required to give a 3-minute presentation on your honours project using only 3 slides. The focus should be on creating interest, so you should explain the project's aims and significance, while only touching on methodological details. You can refer to results if they are available but this is not expected. This presentation should be designed for a general lay audience and will be recorded. You can look at example presentations online (e.g., http://threeminutethesis.org/). The 3MT presentation is worth 20% of your grade. 
Honours conference: You will be required to formally present your Honours project to your peers and academics attending the Honours Conference. Presentations will be marked using a variety of criteria by two markers. Marking criteria will be freely available and will be discussed beforehand in class. There will be a strict time limit (e.g., 12 minutes), plus time for questions (responses to questions will not be marked). Supervisors can only provide feedback once on the Honours Conference presentation—that is, there can only be one "dry-run". Conference talks will be recorded. The Honours Conference presentation is worth 50% of your grade.
Critical evaluation of School of Psychological Science colloquia: Colloquia are research presentations by academic staff and visitors. These are 45 minute presentations, plus 10-15 minutes question time. Students attend 2 colloquia during Semester 2. Students may need to sign-up for specific colloquia, as there will be a quota for face to face sessions, (i.e. a maximum number of students allowed at each colloquium). Details will be provided in lectures if this is required. Questions to the speaker are encouraged. Students evaluate the two attended colloquia on a standardized form, provided by the unit coordinator at the beginning of the semester. Each evaluation should be a maximum of 300 words (= 600 words maximum total) and together, are worth 30% of the grade. APA formatting applies. Dates of the colloquia will be provided in the first lecture. Submissionof this assignment is via Turnitin.


Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "honest, ethical and responsible scholarship". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
