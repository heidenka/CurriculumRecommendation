Academic Information

Content Managing the impact of production processes and human consumption of goods and services on the environment without reducing overall welfare is one of the most challenging issues facing society. This unit introduces students to both current and long-standing issues in natural and environmental resource management and policy. First, it presents basic concepts of natural and environmental resource management. Secondly, it examines economics of mining, fisheries and forestry with an extension to economics of ecosystem services and biodiversity. Thirdly, it examines the economics of pollution control. Finally, it explores non-market valuation techniques in relation to the management of environmental and natural resources. In analysing environmental and natural resource management issues, the unit extensively uses various economic models and valuation techniques.

Unit Information

This unit provides students with an in-depth understanding of the economic principles to optimally manage natural resources and control or mitigate environmental problems. The unit covers advanced topics on welfare economics, resource (renewable and non-renewable) economics, and environmental (pollution) economics from both theoretical and analytical perspectives. The economic models of resource extraction (minerals, fisheries, forests) and pollution control will be examined along with policy instruments. Additionally, a range of non-market valuation techniques will be covered along with their applications.
Students are encouraged to think critically about current management systems and construct alternatives that lead to more efficient management of environmental and natural resources. Different policy options and management strategies generate multiple outcomes (economic, ecological and social) and balancing these outcomes requires an understanding of both use and non-use values of environmental resources. To understand and explore such values, particular emphasis will be given to theoretical and applied aspects of non-market valuation techniques.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

Lecture sessions
The unit consists of two-hour worth of recorded lectures per week. These recorded lectures will be presented in the form of pre-short audio/videos in advance. In the recorded lectures, key learning elements for each topic will be presented and reviewed. It is expected that students read the textbook(s) and assigned readings for better understanding of economic concepts and methods presented in weekly lectures. 
Tutorial sessions
There will be an hour long tutorial session each week. Tutorial sessions aim to develop deeper understanding of the economic concepts covered in the lectures. Tutorial questions/exercises will be posted on LMS in advance for students to prepare themselves for the session. Students will attempt the pre-tutorial task before coming to the tutorial session. During the tutorial session the focus will be on exploring the problems and finding solutions through group work, discussion and problem solving. 
Lab sessions
There will be up to 2-hour long lab session each week. These sessions will provide students an opportunity to explore and analyse data relevant to lecture topics  to reinforce the concepts presented in the lectures. Lab exercises will be closely linked to the Assignments which have emphasis on exploring and analysing data, interpreting the results and discussing the implications.

Unit Structure

• The unit will consist of 2-hour worth of recorded lectures per week (see unit schedule). These lectures are meant to stimulate and assist students’ reading of the weekly class materials. Students are expected to go through the lectures and build their knowledge on presented concepts by reading text book chapters and assigned articles. Assigned text book chapters and resources are made available through LMS (and/or Unit Readings) should be used for in-depth understanding of the topics covered in the lectures.
• The unit includes weekly 1-hour tutorial and up to two-hour long computer labs. In the tutorial sessions, students will critically evaluate and discuss an assigned paper to extend the concepts covered in the lectures or to find solution to given problems. Therefore, students are required to read the assigned readings and attempt the relevant questions prior to each tutorial session.
• Computer labs are designed to learn and apply problem-solving techniques relevant to the topics covered in the lectures. Students are expected to read the required materials prior to coming to the lab sessions for active engagement on lab tasks.
• The materials discussed in the tutorial and lab sessions are integral part of the quizzes. Skills acquired and techniques learned during computer labs will form the basis for lab assignments that carry a significant portion of the unit mark.

Unit Contingency Plans:

  • What will happen to the unit in the event of lockdown, masking, or stringent social distancing, e.g.: moved online; lectures moved online with labs deferred; etc. 

    All lectures are pre-recorded and are available online. Students can watch recorded lectures at a time and location convenient to them. 
Workshops and labs will be move to online mode for those who have enrolled for f2f sessions.


  • What will happen to the unit in the event of staff absence due to COVID-related reasons, e.g.: replacement lecturer; recorded lectures from previous years; etc. 

    If staff are unwell or have to self-isolate*, workshops and labs may be delivered by another member of the teaching team in the originally planned way (f2f). If this option is not viable, workshops and labs will be delivered in a fully online mode and students will be informed in advance.


  • What the implications for students in the unit are if they get COVID or must self-isolate due to close contact. It will be important in this respect to remember the 2 week special consideration policy in play. 

    If students have to self-isolate, they should attend the workshops/labs via the online option. They should inform the unit coordinator to organise the online option for them.
If students are unwell*, they can miss at most two workshops and labs during semester without the need to apply for special consideration. Students are recommended to view the workshop and lab recordings.
If staff are unwell or have to self-isolate*, return of assignment marks may be delayed.

* Note: For students and staff with caring responsibilities, these rules also cover isolating at home with kids.


Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
