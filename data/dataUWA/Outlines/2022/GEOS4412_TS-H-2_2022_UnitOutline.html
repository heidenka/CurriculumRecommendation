Academic Information

Content This unit provides an introduction to the petroleum system concept and how, why and where energy resources accumulate and what datasets and techniques are used to understand their origin and distribution in sedimentary basins. The unit focuses on various methods and data used in geological characterisation of the subsurface to decipher Earth history as recorded in sedimentary basins as well as applied to basin-hosted resources associated with the supply or storage of energy. Emphasis is placed on practical skill development and these skills are also usefully applied to hydrogeological systems and ore mineral systems. Presentations by industry professionals also highlight the relevancy of geoscience knowledge and skills in the global energy transition.

Unit Information

This unit provides an introduction to petroleum systems and energy geoscience more broadly including an introduction to various types of data used in basin analysis for energy exploration, relevant analytical techniques, and how geoscientific data are interpreted. Petroleum systems are a fundamental way to understand the origin and distribution of basin-hosted resources for exploration, and the unit also focuses on key concepts and methods for interpreting geoscientific data in the transition to new energy forms and related topics including carbon storage.  The concepts and practical skills in this unit are highly relevant to fundamental understanding of basin-related processes for deciphering Earth history, and application to other basin-hosted resources including hydrogeological systems and ore mineral systems.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

Emphasis is placed on development of conceptual knowledge and using this knowledge, along with practical skill development, via workshop teaching and learning, with practical activities and assignments.  Practical learning is strongly experiential and authentic to the energy industry including topics such as exploration and consideration of carbon storage.  The practical exercises and assignments help emphasise conceptual understanding and its application to problem solving relevant to sedimentary basin development and energy exploration.  Materials are provided in LMS for each workshop.

Unit Structure

This unit runs for 6 weeks commencing the week of 19 April (Week 16 in CAS) with two workshops per week on Monday and Tuesday mornings, 9 am – 12.45 pm.  Note that the first Monday of the unit is a public holiday (18 April, Easter Monday) and the second Monday (25 April) is the Anzac Day public holiday and UWA holiday.  The unit is composed of two major modules: (i) Petroleum Systems and Stratigraphic Architecture (focusing at basin and basin-margin scale); and (ii) Subsurface Characterisation focusing on reservoirs, reservoir quality and energy/carbon storage.  Reading, class preparation (e.g. videos), revision, and practical work for assessment is expected to be undertaken either as part of workshop time or in non-contact time (up to 6 hours) during the week. Workshops in Weeks 16, 17, 18 and 20 will be split into a F2F component (9 am–11 am) and an online component (11.15 am–1 pm). The workshops in Week 21 may be held fully online but this will be confirmed during the unit. Online interactive workshops are for students who are still overseas and for any Perth-based students who are unwell or isolating and unable to come to class that day/week. Workshop invitations will be sent via Teams.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
