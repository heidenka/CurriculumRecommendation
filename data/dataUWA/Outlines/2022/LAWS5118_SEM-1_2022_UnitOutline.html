Teaching contingency plan (In response to Covid19 restrictions)

COVID 19 Protocols - Updated 24 February 2022. (For active links see LMS Covid -19 page in LMS)


The Law School has developed protocols to deal with those instances where a student/teacher has been in a face-to-face lecture or tutorial and, following the class, isolates and undergoes a COVID-19 PCR test.

1. The student/teacher should inform their Unit Coordinator and the Dean that they are isolating and undergoing a COVID-19 PCR test for any reason - they are symptomatic, are a close contact, were at an exposure site, etc.

2. The student/teacher must advise the Unit Coordinator and the Dean immediately on receipt of the outcome of that PCR test.

3. If the PCR test result is negative, no further action is required.

4. If the PCR test result is positive and the person involved is a student, the student should complete the University's COVID Positive Student Register and will be directed to UWA's COVID-19 website, outlining available support at the University and links to WA Government resources.

5. Others who were in the class with the COVID positive person will be advised, maintaining the anonymity of the COVID positive person.

6. In standard lectures and tutorials (which are typically either 45 mins or 1 hr 30 mins) provided compliance with the mask mandate, under current Government guidelines, others in class will not be considered close contacts. However, they should be encouraged to monitor their health and isolate and get tested if they display COVID symptoms.

7. The WA Department of Health contact tracing team will contact all close contacts.

8. If it is a student that is COVID positive, teaching will continue as normal, with the COVID positive student isolating and attending online classes in all their units.

9. If the teacher is COVID positive, they too must isolate. In this instance, students will be advised of the alternative teaching arrangements.

10. And, finally, please remember that masks are mandatory in all internal areas across the University unless they meet WA Government exemptions. Masks must be worn to securely cover the nose and mouth.

Academic Information

Content In this unit students learn the key aspects of family law: marriage and de facto relationships, separation and divorce, parentage, parenting orders relating to children, property orders and the alteration of property interests and financial support for spouses and children, before progressing to study selected topics in-depth including family violence, family law processes and ethical issues in the practice of family law. The unit develops students' ability to critically analyse family law cases and statutory principles. Students critically examine family law concepts and principles in the context of social, economic and psychological dimensions of family formation and relationship breakdown, including the impact of separation on children.

Unit Information

The unit is taught through interactive lectures, workshops and student focused learning. The first nine weeks of semester concentrate on statutory and case law principles. The final three weeks of semester broaden our focus to topics that include family violence, family dispute resolution and the ethical practice of family law.

Lecture Capture System

In accordance with UWA’s policy on lecture capture all lectures* in enabled venues will be recorded and made available to stream and download by enrolled students via the Lecture Capture System (LCS) for the duration of the unit (including Supplementary and Deferred Examinations period).

* Please note that in some limited circumstances lectures may not be recorded due to:  ethical and/or security related issues; the use of commercially sensitive material; the use of third-party copyright teaching material; or culturally sensitive content. Students will be advised, in advance, when this applies.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

The unit is taught using a combination of lecture recordings with powerpoint presentations and interactive workshops. The lectures for Weeks 1 - 3 will be recorded by Lecture Capture. Lectures for Weeks 4 - 9 inclusive are pre-recorded and will be available by the beginning of each topic in advance of the weekly classes. All lectures will be available in LMS as part of your preparation for weekly classes. 
The lectures beginning in Week 1 on Friday 4 March include interactive activities. The workshops from weeks 4 - 9 will be conducted through a combination of teacher led discussion, student case presentations, small group discussions and whole of class discussions based on set pre-reading, lecture recordings and in-class activities. 
For six weeks of semester there will be one three-hour class (Weeks 1-3, 10 – 12) which is a combination of lectures and workshop activities. All students are expected to attend for the duration of these classes. For the other 6 weeks of semester you will attend one 75minute workshop (allocated in CAS) during weeks 4 - 9 inclusive.  There will be pre-recorded lectures for the weeks 4 – 9 topics and in-class activities to support each topic and the assessment items. All weekly workshops will be led by Robyn Carroll, with guest speakers as presenters for the final three weeks.  

Unit Structure

The topics in this unit are presented in five modules over 12 weeks.
Family Law in Australia (weeks 1-2)
Marriage and De Facto Relationships (week 3)
Parents and Children (weeks 4-6)
Property and Financial Support (weeks 7-9)
Process, Practice and Ethics in Family Law (weeks 10-12) 
The first four modules introduce you to Australian family law with emphasis on the Family Law Act 1975 (Cth), parenting orders and orders for the alteration of interests in property and on the Marriage Act 1961 (Cth). The final module covers selected topics and introduces you to the practice of family law.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
