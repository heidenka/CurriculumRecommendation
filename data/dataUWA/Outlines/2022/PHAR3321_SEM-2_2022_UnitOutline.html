Academic Information

Content In this unit, students are provided with a state-of-the-art insight into how drugs affect physiological and pathological systems. This is achieved by applying experimental pharmacological methods to a range of contemporary topics including clinical pharmacology, clinical toxicology, immunopharmacology, respiratory pharmacology, clinical trials, psychopharmacology, neuropharmacology and oxidative stress.

Unit Information

Students enrolled in Systems Pharmacology Methods PHAR3321 will become proficient in experimental pharmacology methodology, especially as they relate to the topics covered in the co-requisite unit PHAR3320 (clinical pharmacology and toxicology, clinical trials, immunopharmacology, oxidative stress, neuropharmacology, the pharmacology of lipid-lowering drugs, and research innovation and commercialisation.). This is important because much of pharmacology is still in the exploratory stage and therefore mostly experimental – it will also serve you well when seeking employment or further study.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

Laboratory classes and Workshops
Students enrolled in Systems Pharmacology Methods PHAR3321, will complete one session per week. These sessions vary in their format, and consist of a combination of experimental laboratory sessions (6 sessions), workshops (3 sessions), oral presentations (2 sessions) and laboratory tours (1 session). These sessions are scheduled to be held on either Monday, Tuesday or Wednesday afternoons from 1 pm, depending on your lab group allocation in CAS (or as indicated by the Unit Coordinator). Lab groups will be finalised in the first week of semester. The laboratory work has been designed to complement and extend the lecture material covered in PHAR3320 and introduce some of the experimental methodology of modern pharmacology.
Student Seminars
Each student is required to present a 10 minute seminar with a 5 minute question and answer session, and attend and participate in other seminars presented on the same day (only). These seminars will be held in week 5 of semester, between Monday 29th August and Wednesday 30th August, as indicated in the Unit timetable on pages 12 & 13.
Each student will present a seminar that is based on a recently‐published scientific paper. The paper for presentation will be selected by the Unit Coordinator from a pair of papers nominated by the student. That is, by Monday 8th August, each student will need to have COMPLETED THE SEMINAR PAPER NOMINATION FORM ON LMS AND SUBMITTED PDFs OF THE TWO SCIENTIFIC PAPERS they have nominated for consideration via the LMS. During the following week, forms will be returned to students indicating which of the two scientific papers presented for consideration has been selected to be the subject of the seminar presentation. We require submission of 2 papers/student to ensure that each student presents a different scientific paper.
When selecting the two scientific papers, you must choose papers that satisfy ALL the following criteria:
• The paper reports original research (NOT review articles that summarize the findings of numerous scientific papers)
• The paper was published in either 2021 or 2022 (as defined by the copyright date)
• The paper is likely to be of general pharmacological or toxicological interest,
• The paper has been published in a higher quality scientific journal (Journal Impact Factor greater than 3.0)
• The paper has not previously (nor knowingly in the future) been presented/discussed in another unit.

Unit Structure

There is a practical session in each week of semester, with students completing 1 session per week. These sessions vary in their format, and consist of a combination of experimental laboratory sessions (6 sessions), workshops (3 sessions), oral presentations (2 sessions) and laboratory tours (1 session). 
The students will undertake a 5 week project on oxidative stress. There will be 2 student seminar sessions. There will be a clinical pharmacology and a clinical toxicology workshop. Students will have the opportunity to tour medical research laboratories.  Students will also be assessed on their laboratory skills in the first week as well as the last week of semester.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "honest, ethical and responsible scholarship". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
