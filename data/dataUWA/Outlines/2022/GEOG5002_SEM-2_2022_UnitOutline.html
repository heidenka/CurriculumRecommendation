Academic Information

Content Adopting an applied perspective, this unit introduces students to the range of professional contexts engaged in contemporary international development practice. The core focus is on what it means to do development, in a variety of different contexts, and what knowledge and skills a development practitioner needs to have to address real challenges encountered in the field. It introduces students to key actors in international development and the working cultures of large development institutions (e.g. World Bank, UN organisations; non-government development agencies; aid/relief organisations; aid celebrities, and development academics. This exploration is set against theoretical perspectives that inform values, assumptions, rationales and ethics underpinning development practice. Students critically reflect on their own position in development and develop self-reflexive, technical and communication skills required for professionals working in international development, regional planning, and humanitarian aid.

Unit Information

Adopting an institutional and spatial approach to development practice, this unit introduces students to key international organisations, policy frameworks and data that helps understand the institutional, philosophical and political/policy contexts underpinning key international development issues.
The core focus is on understanding the structural and spatial drivers of international development interventions and developing practical data analysis skills and knowledge of international policy frameworks that have national, sub-national, urban/regional and local implications for development practice and practitioners.
Student will be tasked through in-class labs, set academic and/or policy readings and prior knowledge acquired in other units completed to date, to discuss and reflect upon the assumption(s), meaning(s), role(s), value(s), rationalities and ethics that characterise macro- (and micro-) development practice and practitioners.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

Classes for GEOG5002 will comprise a combination of pedagogical modes: (a) interactive lectures led by the unit co-ordinator; (ii) in-class data analysis labs; (iii) academic reading discussions/reflections; and, (iv) student-led presentations.
In-class data analysis labs and academic reading discussion/reflection sessions will generally alternate each week over the course of the semester - see unit schedule for dull details.
Students are required to complete in-class data analysis labs the following week via LMS.
For academic reading discussion and reflections sessions students are required to prepare a 2-page overview of the prescribed weekly reading. This overview should outline the key ideas/concepts/arguments within the reading along with comments about issues/ideas that you are unsure/unclear about, plus several questions for discussion within the class. 
Students should bring their laptops to class and have MS Excel downloaded as the in-class data analysis labs will involve analysing and interpreting large datasets on COVID-19, population, urbanisation, and, the SDGs. The in-class labs are designed to assist you with the student-led seminar presentations and briefing report assignments on the geographies of international development aid funding funding.
Learning outcomes for Semester 2, 2022
At the end of this unit you will be able to:
(1) understand and articulate the role of key international organisations in macro-development practice and international aid;
(2) an ability to analyse and map data that underscores international policy frameworks on macro development practice;
(3) demonstrate an understanding of key theoretical/conceptual and policy perspectives on macro- (and micro-) development practice; and,
(4) develop and enhance written and presentation skills.

Unit Structure

This semester, the class meets face-to-face in Social Science Lecture Room 1.29 and synchronous (at the same time) online via Teams for those students based overseas/interstate and/or unable to attend in-person classes due to COVID-19 or other university valid reasons for non in-person attendance.
Classes are scheduled for 5-9pm with appropriate breaks. 
In general, the class sessions will be split into two basic sessions. Part 1 of the class will entail an interactive presentation led by the unit co-ordinator. Part 2 of the class will entail either (i) a data analysis lab where students will use MS Excel and Datawrapper to analyse and map international datasets; or, (ii) a class discussion/reflection on set readings.
Ideally, as we’re a fairly small class this semester, in-person attendance is preferred as this will foster a convivial learning environment and experience for students and staff.

Participation

Students are required to attend all classes, ideally in-person, so as to engender a convivial learning environment and experience.
Students are required to take part in in-class data analysis labs and complete the lab tasks and submit their assignment by the following Wednesday at 12 noon.
Students are required to read, review and write-up a 2-page overview of set readings as per the teaching schedule. The 2-page overview should include critical comments/thoughts about the key issues raised in the paper, comments on issues that you are unsure/unclear about, and, at least 3 thoughtful (and thought-provoking) questions for discussion amongst the class. 

Referencing Requirements

Students should include all relevant references - academic, policy, organisational etc - in all assignments for this unit.
For in-class data analysis labs, the original data sources for all graphs/tables produced should be noted beneath graphs/tables.
Referencing - academic, policy or otherwise - should be used in the presentation and briefing report for the Geographies of Development Aid Funding assignments. For the presentation, in-text references should be used and full reference details should appear at the end of presentation slides. For the briefing report, student should use in-text referencing, with full details of references included as footnotes on the page that the reference appears.

Other

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "honest, ethical and responsible scholarship". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
