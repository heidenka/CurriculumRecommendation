Academic Information

Content This unit introduces key concepts and methods in the history of architecture and design. It examines how technical, material, cultural, and geographical factors fashioned the ways people build and why they have done so. While including a chronological survey of early modern architecture, the unit makes an adjacent study of specific architectural themes and theories accompanying the political and industrial upheavals that apparently closed the period of the Enlightenment in the West and produced the modern world. Students will investigate such themes and theories by comparing notable case studies that will be researched, documented, and analysed in detail.

Unit Information

ARCT1011 surveys architecture and design up to the 19th century from a cross-cultural and geographical vantage. With an eye to the technical, social, environmental, aesthetic among other developments raised by these early modern contexts, students investigate the many ways they continue to shape the built environment in our times. The unit discusses the key moments that culminate in a history of architecture and also the scholarly domain that studies these moments. However, the unit does not solely focus on the main actors, methodologies, and ideas germane to past architectural endeavour or their influence today in a “globalised” world, but also calls them to task. If we have any hopes of designing solutions to very real, current problems then alternative pasts matter. 
Students learn critical analytical skills, clear thinking and argument, assemble an archive of design precedents, and refine cultural and academic vocabulary basic to the course in architecture.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

Philosophically, the unit aims to cultivate a disposition of interested scepticism in students. This approach is resolutely optimistic. It invokes care as a necessary element of being interested, and takes being interested as a key activity of learning. Students are encouraged to query the taken-for-granted-ness in ways of thinking about the past, what's going on now, and what "might" happen in the future.
Pragmatically, the unit design is firmly educational. Intending to cultivate sustained attention in students, regular meetings with instructors occur face-to-face, and digitally where necessary, in lecture and tutorial settings. Lectures are recorded and made available following live delivery to support integrating content and its application in tutorials and assessment. Learning is further directed by weekly modules on the LMS system, which provide lecture outlines, slides, readings, key examples, and vocabulary list.

Assessment Item 1 
a) One Videoed Oral Defence. The task requires completing the proposal and justification of a comparison, which establishes the foundation for the Written Critical Comparative Analysis (Assessment 2). 
The oral defence is developed by the student in relation to a designated case study (elected via LMS at the commencement of semester) and another project that has been independently located and researched (in consultation with instructor) OR from options provided. It involves a power-point presentation consisting of 5 slides max, which summarises your research in 10 mins max. 
Due no later than Friday, April 01 at 5PM Perth Time, the oral defence is submitted digitally on LMS and uploaded by the instructor to the tutorial blog for viewing and discussion in forthcoming tutorials. 
b) Weekly Responses. The task consists of in-class exercises that address outcomes for the unit and focus discussion in the weekly tutorials.
In-class exercises for tutorials in weeks 3-5 inclusive require students respond to simple activities that prepare them for undertaking Assessments 1 + 2. In-class exercises for tutorials in weeks 6-11 require students respond to a series of instructor prompts related to each group of student oral defences. These prompts are posted on the tutorial blog hosted by LMS on the Friday of the week prior discussion in tutorials. The prompts will also be provided in lectures a week in advance. Attending/watching lectures thus means access to prompts a fraction earlier. 
Students make a self-assessment on the consistency and quality of their weekly responses via the Quiz facility hosted by LMS, due no later than Friday, May 27 at 5PM Perth Time.
Assessment Item 2 
One Written Critical Comparative Analysis of 1500 (+/- 150) words in length + bibliography. (Word count EXCLUDES citations and bibliography.) The task requires the completion of a developed critical comparative analysis, proposed and justified for the oral defence (Assessment 1).
The written critical comparative analysis, which expands on and refines the oral defence, must include accurate citations of appropriate sources. Students may make make relevant and judicious use of images, photographs, plans, perspectives, maps, drawings/paintings etc. to record, substantiate, and enlarge understanding. However, images are not mandatory.
Due no later than Friday, May 20 at 5PM Perth Time.
Assessment Item 3 
Students are required to complete one Final examination (2 hours), which is administered by the University and held some time, unknown to the instructors, determined by the University during the Exam period, June 04--June 18.
The Exam consists of 3 Parts:
Part 1, 20 Multiple Choice Questions (20pts)—the questions for which are drawn from the (optional) weekly quizzes.
Part 2, 10 Slide Identifications (40 pts)—the images for which are drawn from the key examples listed for each week on LMS.
Part 3, 2 short answers (20 pts each). From a pool of questions provided in Week 12 lectures, four will be selected for the exam. You must respond to two of them.

Referencing Requirements

Academic Conduct Requirements
All students are required to submit their work in accordance with scholarly conventions and academic protocols expected by the University. Written work is to be provided for assessment in electronic form via LMS. No other forms or means of dissemination (e.g. email) will be accepted. Students must retain a copy of their work, unaltered prior the submission date. 
Referencing
Every time you quote or use images, text or the ideas of another person in your assessment submission, you must provide a properly formatted reference to the source. For ARCT1011 the only acceptable format for referencing and notation is the ‘Chicago’ academic or citation style. Details of this style are widely available but are easily consulted via the library website and notes which have been prepared by library staff: https://guides.library.uwa.edu.au/Chicago_citation 
The most common type of academic misconduct arises from instances where material is incorporated into text verbatim, without quotation marks, and thus represented as a student’s own words. (This is commonly called plagiarism.) Even where foot- or end-notation referencing is provided identifying where the appropriated material comes from, this is nonetheless misconduct if text is reproduced verbatim. Take care and be sure to paraphrase (put into your own words) such material instead, as well as provide a properly formatted reference to the source. 
As well as plagiarism, contract cheating, collusion, falsifying data and representing work that has already been submitted to the University or elsewhere as an original response to an assignment, academic misconduct may include an additional activity or practice engaged in by a student that violates: "...explicit guidelines relating to the production of work for assessment, in a manner that compromises or defeats the purpose of that assessment. Any such activity undermines an ethos of ethical scholarship.” (From the University's Policy on Academic Conduct.) Such activity or practice includes failure to cite sources at all (not only improperly citing them) even though this is clearly required, failure to cite page number(s) for a foot- or end-notation where required, and citing a range of pages (for example, pp 1-169) from a source when a more precise location is clearly required. (In some case it is appropriate to cite a range of pages, say, where you want to reference the content of an entire chapter or section of a source. Quotations, however, typically appear on one or, at most two pages and these should be indicated precisely.) Whether or not such infractions are investigated as academic misconduct they fail to satisfy unit guidelines and will be penalized by a lower mark or a failed mark depending on their severity.The Unit's LMS page provides additional resources for writing, researching, and referencing to support the completion of a valid submission.
Self-Plagiarism
The submission of a single piece or body of work to multiple units for assessment constitutes self-plagiarism. Self-plagiarism is considered a serious breach of academic integrity and is defined by the university as the recycling, copying, or disguising of previous work for assessment in a unit.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
