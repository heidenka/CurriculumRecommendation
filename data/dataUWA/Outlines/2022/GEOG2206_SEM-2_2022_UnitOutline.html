Teaching contingency plan (In response to Covid19 restrictions)

In the event of the introduction of a lockdown, or stringent social distancing measures, seminars will move online (via Microsoft Teams). Lectures are already pre-recorded and available online. If Sarah (unit coordinator and lecturer) gets COVID and is unable to run the seminar for that week, any necessary independent learning materials will be provided to ensure that students can progress the work required independently, with the opportunity to receive support and assistance at an alternative time.

Lectures are online and available from the beginning of semester so you will be able to attend to lecture materials at their own pace. If you are required to isolate, you will be able to catch up later on any missed seminar via Echo360. Students will have access to the special consideration facility for assessments, which will enable them to receive extensions on deadlines where these are necessary due to COVID or any other illness. 

Academic Information

Content This unit develops and applies key concepts in population geography and planning. It introduces students to the fundamental components of population change and their role in shaping contemporary Australian society. It also examines key policy debates about population growth, distribution, structure and composition in Australia, providing students with the conceptual and analytical tools to explain, critically engage with, and advance, these complex debates. The unit equips students with technical skills to apply their conceptual knowledge of population dynamics to the analysis of socio-economic and demographic data, for the purposes of social policy development and planning. It also emphasises skill development in collaborative learning and professional communication.

Unit Information

This unit, at its core, is about developing your demographic perspective: an analytical lens on population change that will help you make better sense of the world socially, environmentally, politically and economically. Our specific focus in this unit is on population change in Australia. We’ll be examining some of the big population debates that have historically, and continue to, shape the national dialogue about Australia’s identity, its place in the world, and its future sustainability. 
You’ll be introduced to the fundamental components of population change and their role in shaping contemporary Australian society as well as key policy debates about population growth, distribution, structure and composition in Australia. You’ll develop conceptual and analytical tools to explain, critically engage with, and advance, these complex debates. You’ll also develop technical skills to apply your conceptual knowledge of population dynamics to the analysis of socio-economic and demographic data, for the purposes of social policy development and planning.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

This unit adopts a flipped classroom approach to learning. Lectures are pre-recorded and need to be engaged with, alongside additional preparatory materials before each week's face-to-face seminar. 

Unit Structure

This unit is hands-on and student-led, and consists of two main phases. The first phase (weeks 1-6) involves a weekly pattern of individual learning (online lectures and engagement with a curated set of thought-provoking materials) followed by a 3-hour on-campus (COVID restrictions permitting) seminar that consolidates and applies the ‘big ideas’ from your individual learning. You should prepare to spend 2-4 hours each week in individual learning prior to each seminar. 
The second phase of the unit (weeks 7-12) centers around applying the conceptual knowledge derived in the first six weeks of the unit, to the development of a series of technical and collaborative skills in the analysis of real-world population data and debates, culminating in the completion of your final two major assessments. During this phase, preparatory materials will focus on preparing you for the analytical tasks you’ll be engaging in and seminars will be scaffolded around different aspects of analysis that you will complete for your remaining assessments.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
