TEACHING ARRANGEMENTS

UWA Law School continues to value and prioritise face-to-face learning. However, due to the ever-evolving pandemic, protecting our student body and minimising the disruption to student learning, the student experience, and your academic progression is our priority. In light of this, after having considered a range of options, students have been given the option to attend face-to-face OR online tutorials for the duration of this semester.

Students who elect a face-to-face tutorials but are required to isolate for a period pursuant to Government guidelines, must contact the Unit Coordinator to be allocated to an online tutorial for the duration of their isolation.

In the event of future COVID-19 restrictions preventing face-to-face classes, all lectures and tutorials will be delivered online for the relevant period.

COVID PROTOCOLS

The Law School has developed protocols to deal with those instances where a student/teacher has been in a face-to-face lecture or tutorial and, following the class, isolates and undergoes a COVID-19 PCR test.

1. The student/teacher should inform their Unit Coordinator and the Dean that they are isolating and undergoing a COVID-19 PCR test for any reason - they are symptomatic, are a close contact, were at an exposure site, etc.

2. The student/teacher must advise the Unit Coordinator and the Dean immediately on receipt of the outcome of that PCR test.

3. If the PCR test result is negative, no further action is required.

4. If the PCR test result is positive and the person involved is a student, the student should complete the University's COVID Positive Student Register and will be directed to UWA's COVID-19 website, outlining available support at the University and links to WA Government resources.

5. Others who were in the class with the COVID positive person will be advised, maintaining the anonymity of the COVID positive person.

6. In standard lectures and tutorials (which are typically either 45 mins or 1 hr 30 mins) provided compliance with the mask mandate, under current Government guidelines, others in class will not be considered close contacts. However, they should be encouraged to monitor their health and isolate and get tested if they display COVID symptoms.

7. The WA Department of Health contact tracing team will contact all close contacts.

8. If it is a student that is COVID positive, teaching will continue as normal, with the COVID positive student isolating and attending online classes in all their units.

9. If the teacher is COVID positive, they too must isolate. In this instance, students will be advised of the alternative teaching arrangements.

10. And, finally, please remember that masks are mandatory in all internal areas across the University unless they meet WA Government exemptions. Masks must be worn to securely cover the nose and mouth. 

Academic Information

Content This unit introduces students to the principles of equity, which supplements and complements the common law, and the law of trusts. It discusses the development of equity in the Courts of Chancery and the relationship between equity and the common law. In doing so, students examine a number of equitable doctrines including fiduciary relationships, unconscionable bargains, illegitimate pressure, undue influence and estoppel. The unit also introduces students to the nature and the essential characteristics of the trust and the usefulness of the trust in modern society. It examines the formal and substantive requirements for the creation of a trust as well as the principal features of trustees' powers and duties and trustee and beneficiary rights. The class discussions include an examination of equitable remedies, with a key aim of the unit being to explore the relationship between equitable doctrines and equitable remedies and to locate the place of equitable doctrines and remedies within the wider Australian legal landscape.

Unit Information

Welcome to Equity and Trusts!
This unit is an introduction to the principles of Equity, which supplement and complement the common law, and the law of Trusts. It begins with a description of Equity, its development in the Courts of Chancery and its role today as well as the nature and history of Trusts. The unit then examines equitable vitiating factors and estoppel before moving onto a detailed discussion of various aspects of trusts including creation, formalities and constitution, essential characteristics, trustees’ duties and powers and remedies. The unit will conclude with an examination of fiduciary duties. 
Unit learning outcomes
On the completion of this unit, you should be able to:
1)	Identify, state accurately, explain and apply relevant equitable doctrines and principles of Trusts law, both orally and in writing.
2)	Understand and explain, in particular contexts, the relationship between Equity and the common law.
3)	Critically read, analyse and evaluate cases by reference to specified equitable doctrines and remedies.
4)	Use and cite correctly appropriate authorities in written work.
5)	Contribute meaningfully and responsibly to class discussions.
6)	Use effective oral communication skills.

Tutorial Participation

Participation

Tutorial participation  – 10% 
Students will be assessed on their participation in 10 of the 12 tutorials. You can, therefore, miss 2 tutorials during the semester without having to apply for special consideration.
The assessment criteria and the objectives of the participation mark are to promote:
•	engagement, 
•	preparation, 
•	understanding,
•	reflection; and 
•	responsible tutorial membership.
	Engagement will most obviously be demonstrated in speaking although students who are by nature quieter may be engaged, though speak less often.
	Preparation might be evident in notes the student has come with or in the quality of answers and comments in discussion.
	Understanding of the issues raised in tutorials will most commonly be demonstrated though a student’s overall contribution to the tutorial discussion, including small group discussions within a tutorial, whether by way of answering questions, asking questions, making observations, or listening and responding to other students. 
	Reflection on the issues (and preparation) might be evident in the student drawing on material outside the material directly related to the tutorial problem, perhaps from other topics in the course and in questions asked during (or even after) the tutorial seminar.
	Responsible tutorial membership refers to the respect given to other student’s comments and refraining from attempting to dominate the discussion.
	Participation is about the quality not necessarily the quantity of contributions (where ‘quality’ means considered and reflective contributions – you are allowed to make mistakes!). The aim of including this component is to encourage unselfconscious discussion amongst students, not an atmosphere of assessment.
To facilitate the assessment of tutorial participation, it is essential that students attending online tutorials enable their cameras. 
In order to encourage and facilitate student engagement and participation, the use of electronic devices is generally not permitted in face-to-face tutorials in this unit. If you require permission to use an electronic device in tutorials on equity grounds you should see the unit coordinator.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
