Academic Information

Content This unit aims to develop a critical approach to current issues in conservation biology. It examines the following five broad themes: (1) What biodiversity is there? How did it get there (evolutionary history)? Which processes maintain it? (2) Why should we conserve it? (3) What are the threatened genes, species and communities? (4) What are the threatening processes? (5) What actions can be taken to conserve biodiversity? Students are aware of the fundamental linkages between good science and conservation management and the critical engagement between government, universities, business and the community in generating effective conservation outcomes. The unit also introduces students to fundamental science and scientific thinking and approaches to problem solving in ecology, population genetics, species biology and decision theory that underpin modern conservation biology.

Unit Information

This unit is an introduction to the interdisciplinary topic of conservation biology, and requires no prior learning. You will become aware of the fundamental linkages between good science and conservation management and how government, researchers and community organisations engage in generating effective outcomes for biodiveristy. You will also learn about extinction and conservation failures. This unit exposes students to fundamental science and scientific reasoning, and highlights some approaches to problem solving that underpin modern conservation biology.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

This unit covers a broad range of topics related to conservation biology, and while most content is delivered via pre-recorded lectures, students are expected to participate in discussions during compulsary seminars. Much will also be learnt from engaging with conservation agencies and the staff who represent them, and with demonstraors (current PhD students) who all have expertise in conservation research. 
Deeper learning is expected to be developed on a taxonomic group that you will be assigned to work on, and you will be assessed on this learning via two pieces of written work and a presentation. All other unit content is assessed in a final exam, which is a 'failed component'. This means that if you do not pass the exam (i.e. achieve at least 50%) then you cannot pass the unit and will need to repeat it. Consequently we recommend watching ALL online lectures, doing practice quizzes, taking notes in seminars, and delving deeper into topics by reading articles provided in Unit Readings.

Unit Structure

The unit is taught over the full semester, and includes the standard study break over Easter. Broad topics are covered each week in a series of online lectures available each Monday, and many are explored further in Friday seminars. Practical sessions (also Fridays) consist of team work on plant or animal groups assisted by a demonstrator, visits from and to conservation organisations, and student presentations. 
If you have any unavoidable clashes with other units, please let your unit cordinators know so that we can recommend how to compensate for any missed learning opportunities.

Participation

Perth-based students are expected to attend all timetabled face to face activities.

Attendance of team meetings, and visits to conservation-related organisations are compulsory and attendance will be recorded. Further, students will not receive marks for the Red Listing exercise unless they attend the related online workshop.
Participation in SPARK is also compulsory (see the SPARK guide provided in the LMS). If SPARK surveys are not completed by the due date, then individual marks for all team assessments (i.e. a report and a presentation) are multiplied by 0.8, or by the relevent multiplier (RPF) generated for the non-participating student by the SPARK software, based on their team evaluation. If both values are available, then the lower value is used as the multiplier.

Referencing Requirements

The citation and referencing style used in all written work (i.e. case study and Team Report) should be the APA 7th edition citation style.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
