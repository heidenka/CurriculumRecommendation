Academic Information

Unit Information

Welcome to first year Psychology. Regardless of whether you are simply curious about psychology or are already familiar with aspects of it, we will provide you with a well-structured introduction to this remarkable discipline, which we hope will leave you with a lasting fascination.
We introduce you to some of the major issues and discoveries of modern psychology. These range from the study of perceptual systems, through the mechanisms of learning and memory, to considerations of the genetic and environmental contributions to intelligence, and the study of abnormal psychological conditions.
We also introduce you to the principal empirical methods in psychology and the research skills necessary for conducting and understanding psychological science.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

The Psyc1101 unit is available this year in both face-to-face and online timetabled formats. The unit and accompanying activities are arranged around several core topics within psychology.  Each topic takes up a “block” of lectures, and associated laboratories, quizzes, activities, and readings. Each of the unit topics is outlined below.
BLOCK 1: INTRODUCTION
This is the “So, what’s it all about and how does it work?” lecture. 
Topics covered will include: 
• Introductions
• Outline of the course
BLOCK 2: CRITICAL THINKING
Psychology has been referred to as “the Rodney Dangerfield of sciences” (see Stanovich, Ch. 12) – in other words, it does not get the respect it deserves.  Much of the reason for this is because of misrepresentation and misinformation that comes from media, advertisers, politicians, and others who either (i) know little about psychology, or (ii) have much to gain from pretending to know little about psychology.  In these lectures, we will examine some of the basic principles of the scientific method in psychological research, and we will learn how to think critically about information we are given.  Armed with the knowledge of these methods, we will be in a much better position to be able to evaluate psychological claims, and to distinguish science from pseudo-science.
Topics covered will include: 
• Understanding the scientific method (theories, hypotheses, operational definitions, replication, falsifiability, controls)
• Correlations, illusory correlations, and causation.
BLOCK 3: LEARNING
Having gained a basic understanding of the relationship between our physiological and our psychological selves, we now move on to examine how our experiences in the world influence our future behaviour and our ongoing development. In psychological science, the term ‘learning’ is used both in a more restricted, and in a more general sense than in everyday life. The lectures will cover the major theories that explain the ways in which humans and other animals learn from, and adapt to, their environments.
Topics covered will include: 
• Defining learning in its more technical sense. Habituation/sensitisation, classical conditioning, operant learning, and vicarious learning—the applications of these concepts in understanding ‘normal’ and sometimes ‘other than normal’ behaviour.
• Classical Conditioning – Unconditioned v. conditioned stimuli and responses.  Contiguity Theory v. Contingency Theory.
• Operant learning – Compared to classical conditioning. ‘Positive’ and ‘negative’ forms of reinforcing and punishing consequences of behaviour. Schedules of contingent consequences. The use and misuse of punishment.
• Vicarious learning – Learning from observing others. Characteristics of effective ‘models.’ Implications for parenting.
BLOCK 4: GENES & BEHAVIOUR
This series of lectures introduces selected applications of neuropsychology - an understanding of the effects of brain injury on behaviour. Neurobiological factors which can modulate the effects of brain injury on behaviour, such as genetic predisposition, neural reserve, and recovery will be reviewed. Implications of the development of these techniques to diagnose and treat patients who have experienced brain injury will be discussed. The role of genes and behaviour will be described, showing how behaviour shapes and is shaped by genetic influences.
Topics covered will include:
• Behaviour genetics
• Gene-environment interactions
• Evolution, culture, and behaviour
BLOCK 5: PSYCHOLOGY AND THE BRAIN
This series of lectures describes the physiological bases underlying your existence as a human being. It will introduce the immensely complex, biologically based, electrochemical structures and processes that provide the facilities for sensation, perception, learning, memory, motor skills, personality, hopes and dreams.
Topics covered will include:
• The relationship between brain and mind.
• The relationship between our physical and our psychological selves.
• Anatomical and functional descriptions of the nervous system.
• The structure and function of neurons as the basic building blocks of the nervous system.
• The role of neurotransmitters in information processing and in gross differences in personality.
BLOCK 6: COGNITION
This series of lectures examines the ways in which humans process information. In particular, we will examine how we select information from the environment to process and how we pay attention to it. We will review people’s ability to remember things, and we will discover that our memory is subject to surprising distortions and weaknesses.
• How people process information to attend and remember.
• How people’s attention, memory, and decisions can be subject to distortions and biases.
BLOCK 7: PERCEPTION
A large part of our behaviour is determined by the world we sense around us. We detect information from the environment and interpret that information in order to interact effectively with the world. The study of sensation and perception is the investigation of how we do this. It also includes a consideration of how other animals achieve the same ends. Humans live in the world they can detect and understand. Other animals have senses tuned to different ranges of stimulation and sometimes have different senses to humans. They live in a different world.
Topics covered will include:
• Vision: the neural machinery
• Vision: using prior knowledge and learning
• Hearing:- turning soundwaves into neural information
• Taste and Smell:-the Chemical senses
• Skin and Body senses
BLOCK 8: MOTIVATION & EMOTION
Emotional and motivational states are important determinants of our behaviour.  This series of lectures will consider the nature of emotional and motivational states, how they are created and maintained, and how they act to determine behaviour.
Topics covered will include:
• The variety of emotional states and their classification
• Expression and experience of emotion
• Learned and unlearned sources of emotion and motivation
BLOCK 9: CONSCIOUSNESS
What does it mean to be conscious? In this series of lectures, we will explore different states of consciousness, consciousness in the context of sleep, drug use, and hypnosis, and ongoing research related to consciousness.
Topics covered will include:
• States of consciousness
• Drug use, arousal, and sleep

Unit Structure

The Psyc1101 unit incorporates several components, including lectures, laboratories, and self-guided readings and online activities.
LECTURES
Lectures guide you through the major topics and deal with some of the more difficult conceptual issues. You will attend two 45-minute lectures per week.
If you are enrolled in our face-to-face format of the unit, lectures are on Monday and Wednesday, at 10:00am-10:45am - these run all semester unless otherwise noted in the schedule. Lectures will take place in the Octagon Lecture Theatre. If you are signed up for our online restricted format, joining us from Albany, or signed up for our face-to-face format and prefer it, lectures will be recorded and uploaded onto LMS for viewing. To learn how to watch the lectures on your computer - see the Lecture Captures on LMS. Lectures can also be downloaded as Podcasts - instructions on how to do this are provided in the Lecture Capture area of LMS.
LABORATORIES
There are two types of laboratories:
Laboratory meetings, i.e. those you attend at your timetabled time.
Self-guided laboratories, i.e. independent learning for the weeks scheduled.
• Laboratory meetings
There are six laboratory meetings in each semester. The weeks during which there are no lab meetings give you extra time for your assigned reading and completing the self-guided laboratories, quizzes, pre-lab activities, and your lab report. In your laboratory meetings you will learn about psychology in an applied setting.  You will gather data, interpret some elementary statistical procedures, and discuss your readings and other issues in psychology. Demonstrators will foster an atmosphere of discussion that encourages you to ask questions concerning the issues raised in lectures or in your wider reading.
Remember to enrol in a laboratory group before Wednesday 5:00pm of Week 1. You enrol electronically, using the CAS system. You may change your laboratory allocation to another class with available seats up until 5pm on Friday of Week 3. If you change your allocation you must inform your new demonstrator about when your previous laboratory was. You must also remain in the same laboratory group throughout the semester. After Week 3, students are not permitted to swap between different laboratory classes without written permission from the unit coordinators. Students may only swap into a different class for ONE class a semester and only into a designated laboratory class. If you have missed a lab due to a valid reason (e.g. illness, unforeseen emergency), you will be able to request a temporary once-off change of lab via the lab swap portal available on LMS.
• Self-guided Laboratories
There are a series of self-guided, online laboratories available on LMS on weeks when we do not meet for laboratory meetings.
SELF-GUIDED READINGS & ONLINE ACTIVITIES
The textbook was chosen because of its intellectual standard, its coverage of the material, and because it considers other topics that we cannot cover in the lectures. Readings for this unit will not only include the assigned text book but also supplementary articles provided on LMS, as well as textbooks and periodicals available from the library. Relevant reading should be completed before laboratory meetings.
ASSESSMENTS
• Examination: 
The examination will include multiple-choice type questions and will take place during the examination period at the end of semester. Material presented in lectures, laboratories, and readings will provide the basis for the examination.
• Lab Report (1500 words):
The major course work assessment is a laboratory report. You will learn to write psychology lab reports in an online laboratory on LMS. You are also welcome to ask specific questions of your demonstrator or at the demonstrator-supported sessions. For this lab report you will collect and analyse data on a simple psychological experiment in your laboratory meetings, under the supervision of your demonstrator. Further detail about the study will be provided after data collection. Students will obtain a description of the purpose of the experiment, an overview of the method used and help with analysing the data in the laboratories. 
The word count for the laboratory report is 1500 words, and your report must not exceed this word count. The lab report should follow conventional structure. The word count of 1500 excludes the Abstract (150 word limit), Reference List, Tables, Figures, Table and Figure Captions and any Supplementary Materials. The word count given by a word processor is preferable; if counting words by hand, any character or character string preceded and followed by a space or a punctuation mark counts as a word. Markers will stop reading assignments at the word limit and will assign a mark as if the read portion constituted the entire assignment. Written work that exceeds the word limit will not receive credit or be given feedback.
Use American Psychological Association (APA) formatting style for reference citation in the text and the list of references. The UWA library has a guide to APA style, which is available via a link on our unit LMS website.
You will have to read material in addition to your textbook to complete a report. Important references will be placed on LMS but additional references can be found in the library and we strongly recommend you read more widely! Laboratory reports will be submitted ONLINE to Turnitin plagiarism detection software. Turnitin will be opened 2 weeks prior to the deadline for you to check the originality of your draft and make any alterations. At 23:45 on Tuesday 26th of April, the most recent copy of your report that has been uploaded to Turnitin will be accepted as your final submission. See the Lab Report Submission area of LMS for more information.
• LMS Quizzes:
LMS Quizzes: Throughout the semester there are online quizzes available on LMS. Completion of each of these quizzes is will help consolidate the content of each topic. Quizzes will be open throughout the semester and must be completed by 5pm Friday of Week 12. You will be covering the quiz content in the lectures and associated lab meetings and readings.
There are 8 quizzes throughout the semester. Each quiz is worth 1% of your final mark. You will receive the full 1% of the quiz mark if you have answered at least one answer correctly on the quiz.
We encourage you to complete all quizzes as a way to test your knowledge of the unit content.
• Research Participation:
A full and thorough understanding of psychological research can be optimally achieved if you experience each part of the research process. In future psychology units, students will get to experience how to analyse data, and how to design and execute experiments. In PSYC1101, you can experience what it is like to be a participant in psychological research. The School of Psychological Science is continually conducting research, much of which involves human participants. You may earn up to 8% of your final marks by collecting eight (8) experimental credits, which translates to approximately eight (8) hours of research participation across the semester.
Available experiments will be advertised through the ‘Research Participation’ area of LMS (also called SONA). You sign-up on-line for the experiments in which you wish to participate. Please note that we will accept experimental credits for PSYC1101 until 5pm on Friday of Week 12.
Please see our LMS page about how to login to SONA, and more information about the types of experiments that you can participate in.
If for any reason you do not wish to participate in experiments altogether, you may instead choose to submit a 1000 word essay on experimental methods to earn this 8% component of your final mark. You must inform the unit co-ordinators by the end of Week 3 of Semester 1 if you wish to choose the essay option (firstyear-sps@uwa.edu.au).

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
