Academic Information

Content This unit critically engages with global debates and culturally diverse perspectives on gender and politics. Students will deepen their understanding of feminism and gender theories including intersectionality, sexuality, masculinity, and subjectivity. Students consider a range of cases and contexts in examining how gender and sexuality both construct and are constructed by political practices around the world. The unit explores topics such as global feminist movements throughout history and across cultures; gender and public policy; gender, elections and leadership; and gender and political violence.

Unit Information

Gender is a site of political contestation and power struggle. This unit critically engages with global debates and culturally diverse perspectives on gender and politics. Students will deepen their understanding of feminism and gender theories including intersectionality, sexuality, masculinity, and subjectivity. This unit explores key scholarly debates in the field of gender studies through a political science perspective, discussing how these translate into policy, critically examining these issues in the light of contemporary political developments.
POLS 3204 raises awareness of political issues of our time, but it does not teach you what to think about politics and gender. This unit gives students conceptual tools to critically examine politics, gender, and policy, and develop an understanding of the problems we face internationally and in Australia. 
Unit Outcomes:
Students can 1) evaluate the key concepts theories and policy questions around gender and politics; 2) critically appraise key global debates and diverse perspectives regarding gender and gender relations; 3) explain the importance of an intersectional approach to studying and intervening in gender and politics; and 4) communicate advanced knowledge, ideas, analyses and arguments pertaining to gender and politics in different formats.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

The unit will be based on 45 minutes weekly lectures delivered every Monday, with 90 minutes weekly tutorials based on those lecture topics the following week.
Lectures:
A. Lectures provide 1) an overview of a topic that introduces students to key concepts and issues discussed in the unit 2) provides a framework for students to explore these topics, complementing these with recommended readings and tutorial discussions.
B. Lectures for this unit will be interactive with a student focused approach. The constructive approach to learning is well-known for its highly positive student learning outcomes. Please read weekly suggested readings each week before the lectures. This will enhance your lecture experience and help you to engage during interactive sessions of each lecture. 
Attendance to lectures is not compulsory, but I encourage students to attend all lectures. Your and your colleague's learning experience is significantly enhanced when students attend in person. And this is further enhanced when students engage in interactive sections of the lecture.
Tutorials:
Tutorials will be based in a series of activities (e.g. small group discussions) that will aim at interrogating the different plausible perspectives on each issue.
These tutorials discuss the topics covered in each lecture and the required weekly readings. There will be a total of 10 tutorials starting in week 2.
Tutorials can be both a valuable learning experience and enjoyable. But you need to prepare and obtain the maximum benefit from them by doing the allocated readings and preparing to answer the weekly questions during the tutorial discussions.
Tutorial participation is part of your academic development to:
1. respect and comprehend the point of view of others;
2. offer constructive feedback on the contribution of other students;
3. make well organised, well informed, clearly articulated and concise oral arguments and;
4. work within small groups to identify issues, explore alternative approaches, find resolution, and draw conclusions individually and collectively.
Note: absence from tutorials without special consideration will negatively affect your tutorial participation marks (15% of your final marks).

Participation

Tutorial attendance is required and will comprise 15 % of your final grade for this unit. Tutorial participation will be assessed on two key aspects:
1. Your attendance and reflections on the weekly tutorial questions and readings through discussions
2. Your participation in the wider tutorial discussions/ activities assigned by your tutor
Both aspects will assess your preparation in terms of the required readings and lecture materials. Weekly tutorial questions are available in the Unit Handbook (via LMS) under the Reading List.
Note: Absence from tutorials without special consideration will negatively affect your tutorial participation marks.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "honest, ethical and responsible scholarship". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
