Academic Information

Content This unit covers the basic principles of evolutionary theory with special emphasis on the mechanisms that generate the diversity of life. It examines the genetic structure of populations, factors that maintain variation within populations and causes of divergence among populations, the origin of species and methods of phylogenetic analysis. The emphasis is on the integration of fundamental concepts, approaches to solving problems and the application of evolutionary ideas and methods to questions in fields such as ecology, behaviour, conservation biology and systematics.

Unit Information

This unit covers the basic principles of population genetics and the mechanisms of evolution. It is a required unit for the Conservation Biology and Zoology majors and an optional core unit for the Genetics major. It is also relevant to any student with an interest in natural populations of animals or plants.

In accordance with UWA’s policy on lecture capture all lectures* in enabled venues will be recorded and made available to stream and download by enrolled students via the Lecture Capture System (LCS) for the duration of the unit (including Supplementary and Deferred Examinations period).

Learning And Teaching Strategy

This unit is taught using pre-recorded and flipped online lectures, and face-to-face laboratory sessions and discussion groups. There are also online activities including quizzes and report. There is also online laboratory sessions and discussion groups options for students based overseas or based in Albany.

Unit Structure

Lectures
The lectures are the backbone of this unit.  Although there is substantial overlap of content between lectures and the textbook (Ridley, Evolution), the two complement each other.  The content of both the lectures and the specified text references are required and examinable.  The aim is to provide understanding of basic principles.
There will be two pre-recorded lectures per week. For each lecture there will be a face to face tutorial/Q&A session (Monday and Thursday at midday) to review key concepts and/or develop problem solving skills and practice answering questions that may be asked in the exam or quizzes. These sessions will also be recorded using the lecture capture system. Students are expected to watch the pre-recorded lecture prior to attending the online tutorial/Q&A session.
Practical and/or laboratory sessions
One three-hour session per week (Monday at 1pm or Tuesday at 10am, usually in Jennifer Arnold Lecture Theatre − see handout for exceptions). Five of 12 practicals are used to provide the skills necessary to obtain, understand and use the kind of genetic information discussed in the lectures. These include: (a) introductory labs on the derivation of basic models; and (b) a class project involving field collection and analysis of shell banding polymorphism in Theba pisana, which is the basis for an online laboratory report (3 labs, including a problem session). Students are also required to watch an online lecture and complete an online quiz in week 1. The lecture and quiz provide background in fundamental concepts that were covered in Level 1 (or equivalent prerequisite units) and are assumed to be understood by students enrolling in this unit. If you do poorly in the online quiz (<80%) you will need to revise these concepts in order to understand the lectures presented in this unit. Your mark for this exercise is not recorded.
Discussion groups
Five practical sessions are used for discussion groups. The class is divided into groups of up to 15 students, each group developing in depth an integrative topic in population genetics, through discussion of the primary literature; group topics include conservation genetics, applications of genetics in ecology, and marine population genetics. This in-depth approach lets the students use the general principles from the lectures in a novel context. Each student writes a 2000 word essay on a specific topic of their choice, from within their general group topic, so the discussions help to develop each student’s own interests.

DETAILS OF ASSESSMENT
Online quizzes
The online quizzes are designed to test your ability to do population genetic problems. The quizzes will cover content covered in Labs 1 & 2 and Lectures 3, 4, 5, 8, 9, 13-24. These quizzes will be an online assessment using Blackboard and will be run in teaching weeks 2 to 5, 9, 10 and 12.
Laboratory Report
The laboratory report is based on data collected by the entire class. The project is meant to give practice with analysis of real data, exploration of data within the context of guiding questions, and communication of the findings. It will consist of a series of analytical (calculations) and short answer questions. Assessment is based on skill with the methods of analysis, the amount and quality of original thought, and communication.
Discussion Groups
The discussion groups are meant to develop broad topics more thoroughly than is possible in the lectures, and to give you experience in organizing a discussion. This will work only if people read the papers and contribute to the discussion.  To encourage this, 10% of your total mark will be based on your contribution. Half of this comes from the session that you lead, and is based on competence with the material, organization of the session, and success in keeping a substantial discussion going.  The other half is for contribution to each discussion, marked on a scale of 1 for active, substantial contribution, 0.67 for minor participation, and 0.33 for attendance without participation. Note that while discussions will led by several people, and effective leading requires that leaders work as a team, you will each be marked independently. Discussion groups are scheduled during the timetabled slot for laboratory sessions.
Essay
The essay is meant to be a substantial effort, developing over the second half of the semester, and making use of the context provided by the discussion groups.  The essay can be of a subject of your choice, but must be within the general theme of your discussion group. It has a word limit of 2000 words (text only – not including references, tables and figures) and the referencing style is APA 6th edition (http://guides.library.uwa.edu.au/apa). The word count must be displayed on the first or last page of the essay. This is your clearest chance to develop your own ideas on a topic of your choice, and it should show some real individual effort and understanding of the background and conceptual issues.  Do not wait until the last week to begin your essay.  Assessment will be based on intellectual depth, originality, use of the available literature, and coherence of presentation. A breakdown of how marks are awarded is outlined in the document “ANIM3362 essay marking key”, available in the LMS.
The essays must be submitted through Turnitin via the Turnitin assignment tool, within LMS. If you haven’t used Turnitin before there are a number of easy to follow guides to walk you through the steps of submitting an assignment at https://guides.library.uwa.edu.au/textmatching/forstudents. Be aware that you can submit and resubmit up to the deadline to refine your work, BUT that there is a 24 hour delay in receiving text-matching feedback between submissions. Difficulties in meeting submission deadlines requiring special consideration should be addressed to the Science Student Office otherwise late assignments will attract a late submission penalty.
Final Exam
The 2-hour exam is based mainly on the lecture component of the unit, although skills developed in the laboratory are relevant and examinable.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
