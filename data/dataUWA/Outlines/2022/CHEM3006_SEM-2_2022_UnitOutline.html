Unit description and lecture-tutorial content
The unit is designed to introduce the concepts and techniques of chemistry related to real-world applications of physical and analytical chemistry to industrial processes, materials and their effects on the environment.
Lecture Topics covered include:
Prof Rob Atkin
Colloids, Surfaces and Polymers 
Colloids, Surfaces and Polymers underpin key Australian industries in Health and Energy and Environmental, and for applications including: recovery of metals from ores, explosives, paints, coatings, fertilizers, water purification, lubricants and cutting fluids, food, cleaning products, extractions, consumer care products, pharmaceutical delivery devices, and clean energy etc. Companies associated with these applications include Australia’s largest companies and employers of chemists, including but not limited to Westfarmers, BHP, Woodside, Chevron, Alcoa, BASF, Fortescue, Incitec Pivot, iCeutica, Dyno Nobel, Orica, Rio Tinto, etc. This course introduces students to the science of polymeric and colloidal materials and their applications. The relationship between the structure and properties of the materials and their performance will be highlighted. 
Prof Killugudi Swaminatha Iyer
Industrial Nanotechnology
The principle of nanotechnology is the ability to work at the molecular level, atom by atom, to build large structures with fundamentally new properties that are not achievable in the bulk state of materials. Nanoscale materials offer a new paradigm for materials manufacture by submicron- scale self-organization and self-assembly to create entities from the “bottom up” to develop industrially real world applications in information technology, healthcare, therapeutics, diagnostics, environment and energy. The lectures in industrial nanotechnology covers the current cutting edge industrially relevant applications developed using major advances in nanotechnology. The lectures will cover the phenomenon of nanoscale materials and how scale contributes in developing advanced materials for real world applications.  We will focus on the key concepts of surface, size, shape, self-assembly and their significance in nanotechnology using the current industrially relevant case studies. To illustrate this, the lectures will be dedicated to materials that have been a mainstay in enabling key advances in the industry today. We will cover applications of silica, titanium, gold, iron oxides, polymers, quantum dots carbon, platinum nanomaterials and nanoscale imaging.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

The Unit will adopt a blended approach with a combination of online, face to face and practical learning. The lectures will be posted in LMS with learning outcomes expected every week. Workshops will be conducted every week with assignments attached to every workshop in class. Practical learning skills will be acquired with laboratories designed at the cutting edge of surface, colloidal, materials and nanotechnologies.

This unit will be using a blended approach to learning. This means that lecture material will be presented as online videos. The videos are accessible from the units LMS site. Assessment details and schedule
Item: Assignment
1.	In-Class Tutorial Assignments (10% of the unit)
It is expected that you watch the weeks content of videos before attending the tutorial class. In the tutorial class the lecturer will post discussion questions and tutorial-based questions beforehand for you to complete during the class. Towards the end of the tutorial class there will be an in-class assessment (quiz, short answer question) for you to complete and hand in for grading.
2.	Assignment (20% of the unit)
This assignment will be based on the lecture material presented by all lecturers (videos and tutorials). This assignment will involve developing a 8-12 min video presentation on addressing a key concept introduced in this unit. These presentations will be done in pairs who will be randomly grouped by the unit coordinator. The assignment will open at the end of week 31 and is due at the end of week 41. Part of the assignment is to view and comment on at least two other groups videos. This will be done on the LMS discussion forum. You can ask a member of staff in this unit for feedback on your video at any point in semester. Please see details on the LMS for more information on the video assignment.
Item: Laboratory
There are five laboratories in CHEM3006, each are equally weighted and worth a total of 20% of the total unit mark. There are three compulsory laboratories that all students will complete and submit a laboratory report/worksheet for marking on the LMS.
1.	Assessment of the lead contamination in soil samples
2.	Pendant drop tensiometry
3.	Titanium dioxide raspberry solar cell
There are two optional laboratories and you will choose which two laboratories you want to complete out of the following laboratories:
1.	Adsorption isotherm on silica
2.	Coagulation of a Sol-Shulze Hardy Rule
3.	Measurement of the iron corrosion exchange current using the Tafel plot method 
4.	Alcoa Bayer liquor TOC/TA determination laboratory
5.	Synthesis of ruby red colloidal nanoparticles
The laboratory reports/worksheets are due one week after you completed the laboratory. Further details about the laboratories will be made available via the LMS site.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
