Academic Information

Content Ethnographic data and interpretations of it form the core of social anthropology and qualitative sociology. This unit considers how this data is produced. In a seminar setting, students read and analyse productions of the ethnographic imagination. By immersing themselves in the first-hand experiences of anthropologists in the field, students familiarise themselves with the process of ’doing’ ethnography. The unit may address the following issues: (1) the connection of explanatory theories to methods of research; (2) methods of studying and interpreting everyday life—participant observation (everyday life as field research), questioning, recording, note-taking; (3) eliciting and interpreting accounts of social life—semi-structured interviews, questionnaires, genealogies, family histories, participatory social research; (4) observing and interpreting categories and symbols; (5) using information technology resources to source methods and explanations; (6) engaging with innovative and interdisciplinary research methodologies and issues; and (7) how to use ethnography to engage in cross-cultural analysis.

Unit Information

Ethnography is both a set of methods, and a mode of representation, that forms the core of social anthropology and qualitative sociology. Over recent decades, ethnography has become increasingly important for other social sciences as well, including criminology. This unit considers how ethnography is produced, and how its products can be interpreted. Focused around a workshop setting, students will read, and in other ways experience, productions of the ethnographic imagination. By immersing themselves in the first-hand experiences of anthropologists in the field, students familiarise themselves with the process of 'doing' ethnography. In so doing, they will also consider the following issues: (1) the connection of research methods to theory-building; (2) methods for studying and interpreting everyday life, including—participant observation (fieldwork), note-taking, different kinds of interviewing, and multi-modal approaches; (3) interpreting and theorizing accounts of social life; (4) observing and interpreting categories and multi-modal content; (5) using databases, and other resources, to source primary and secondary materials; (6) engaging with inter-disciplinary and experimental research approaches; and (7) how to use ethnography in a range of professional roles.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

LECTURE AND WORKSHOP SESSIONS
The main learning activity for this unit is the weekly two-hour lecture and workshop session. All students are encouraged to attend the whole two-hour session each week, in-person (Covid 19-restrictions permitting).  The session takes place each Thursday, from 9-11am. 
A worksheet for each session is available on LMS, in each topic section. Students should download these worksheets before each session. 
The lecture components of each session will be recorded, and posted to LMS afterwards. 
The lecture and workshops sessions will introduce important concepts and examples, will be a forum for sharing ideas on, and asking questions about, the readings, and will each involve a practical research training component, using multi-modal ethnographic methods. 
Through attending these sessions, you will develop the professional research skills necessary to work as an ethnographer (either in research, or in professional practice), and you will develop your understanding of how to interpret the resulting ethnographic evidence.   
The first lecture and workshop session will be held in Week 1 - on Thursday, March 7.
ONLINE CONTENT
In addition, the unit will include a range of online content each week, made up of a combination of short video segments called 'research notes', ethnographic documentary films, and podcasts. These materials will further help students to develop their understanding of key methods and ideas. 
READINGS
Each week, you will be expected to complete two 'essential' readings, as listed in the topic folders. It is preferable if you could read these before the lecture and workshop sessions, as discussions will centre around engagement with these readings. 
Supplementary readings for each unit are also listed for each topic, enabling students to read more deeply on a given topic. These will be also useful for completion of the assignments. 
All unit readings are available through the UWA Library.

Participation

The weekly workshop discussions and activities are a vital learning activity for this unit. It is very important that you try to attend these sessions having completed the assigned readings, and thought about the relevant workshop question sheet (which are available for each Topic on the LMS site). Workshop discussions and participation are a helpful component of the unit, as an aid to your learning. However, keep in mind that within the workshops, we don't expect you to have complete mastery of the material - workshops are an opportunity to ask questions, especially about concepts or material that you are unsure about. If you didn't understand something, most likely there are other students who didn't quite get it either, and you should be able to use the workshops to work together towards a better understanding. With this in mind, workshops are more about asking questions, and getting engaged in the discussions, rather than about coming up with all the 'right answers' and/or 'doing it correctly'.

Referencing Requirements

It is important to properly acknowledge your sources. In your research essay for this unit, you must properly reference your sources - clearly indicate when you are quoting directly, include citations for all sources that you quote, paraphrase, or otherwise reference, and include a complete reference list comprising all and only those sources that you have cited. Follow the APA citation style as described in the UWA library APA guide (https://guides.library.uwa.edu.au/apa (https://guides.library.uwa.edu.au/apa). Please note that you should include page numbers in your in-text citations when paraphrasing or quoting specific passages from written sources. Refer to the assignment rubrics for more information, available on the LMS site.

Late submission of Assignments
A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.
For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 30 out of 50 (a mark of 5 is deducted per day).
If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.
If an assignment is graded Pass/ Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;
Penalty for exceeding word limit
Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
