The relevant materials for missed practical session(s) will be provided at an alternative time.

Unit Information

Forensic anthropology is the application of the science of physical anthropology to the legal process. Forensic anthropologists apply standard scientific techniques to identify human remains and to assist in the detection of crime. This unit is intended to introduce students to basic ideas and principles in forensic anthropology, including: skeletal anatomy; distinguishing human from non-human bones; biological profiling (estimation of age, sex, stature & ethnicity); assessment of trauma, disease and pathology; interpretation of crime scenes. The unit will also provide practical experience in the correct handling, and analysis, of human skeletal remains. It is envisioned that students will develop an appreciation of how anthropological methods are appropriately applied to skeletal remains recovered from forensic contexts.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

This course is a basic overview of the theory and method applicable to forensic anthropological investigation. Please note that this is an introductory course into what is an extremely broad discipline. As such, we do not expect students to become expert forensic anthropologists. The course is intended to provide students with a basic introduction to the following concepts and principles:
• Forensic recovery of human remains – including the role and importance of archaeological theory and method in a forensic investigation of a crime scene
• Reconstruction and interpretation of events that have occurred at a crime scene
• Skeletal variation
• Distinguishing between human and non-human bones
• Biological profiling (age, sex, stature & ethnicity)
• Trauma, disease and pathology in the human skeleton
• Legislation and ethical considerations relevant to the forensic anthropologist

Unit Structure

LECTURE SERIES
Lecture 1: The modern role of forensic anthropology
This lecture first outlines the structure of the unit and the assessment schedule. The historical development of the discipline of forensic anthropology is then discussed; current application and future directions are also considered.
Lecture 2: Biological profiling I: the estimation of age
Variability in the morphological features used to assess age in the human skeleton progressively increases from birth to old age. Choice of method and accuracy are thus inherently related to whether unidentified remains are those of a juvenile or an adult. This lecture considers the mainstream methods appropriate for age estimation in both juvenile and adult remains. A practical session follows in which students will attempt to determine personal age in both skeletal material and from medical images.
Lecture 3: Biological profiling II: the estimation of sex
The accurate estimation of sex is an important part in establishing personal identity, as it theoretically cuts the possible number of victims by half. This lecture first considers the underlying biology of modern human sexual dimorphism. Various methods for the estimation of sex are then discussed, both metric and non-metric, as applied to the juvenile and adult skeleton. In the practical session students will use a selection of established mainstream techniques to attempt to estimate sex in unknown skeletal remains.
Lecture 4: Biological profiling III: the attribution of ancestry
The determination of ancestry, in conjunction with the aforementioned biological attributes, represent the initial descriptors that reduce the pool of missing persons by excluding individuals who do not share the same physical characteristics. This lecture examines the concept of human biological variability and how it is expressed in the skeleton. The lecture will consider how this concept is applied in forensic investigation and the methods typically employed. The practical session involves evaluating a series of and metric and non-metric techniques for estimating ancestry in the skeleton.
Lecture 5: Biological profiling IV: the estimation of stature
Most methods of estimating stature from the human skeleton are simply based on the underlying principle of a correlation between living height and the length of the long bones. This lecture examines a selection of the mainstream methods of estimating stature in the living and from the complete skeleton and individual bones. The lecture also considers methods for reconstructing original bone length from fragmented remains that are commonly referred to the forensic investigator. In the practical session students will use a variety of methods to determine stature in both the living and from a variety of skeletal elements.
Lecture 6: The identification of pathology and trauma in the human skeleton
In the absence of soft tissues, the forensic anthropologist is primarily responsible for recording skeletal pathology and trauma of known mechanism and etiology (e.g. gunshot wounds, sharp weapon trauma, disease and other illness). Associating such observations with the time of death (ante- or perimortem) is an important factor in attempting to identify the remains and/or circumstances relating to cause and manner of death. This lecture will examine the basic characteristics of various types of skeletal pathology and trauma, in relation to their underlying cause(s). The lecture also considers how these markers can be used to reconstruct both living events and circumstances surrounding death. The practical session involves identifying and interpreting a range of physical expressions of pathology, trauma and other anomalies in the human skeleton. Students will also study the characteristics of bone injuries that identify whether trauma in human bone has occurred ante- peri- or postmortem.
PRACTICAL SERIES
Practical 1: The estimation of age
This laboratory will develop your ability to estimate the personal age of an individual from their skeletal remains. In this session you will apply a selection of techniques for estimating age in the juvenile skeleton; individuals approximately no older than 18 years of age. You will also use a selection of techniques suitable for estimating the age of adults, mostly based on skeletal deterioration.
Practical 2: The estimation of sex
This laboratory will develop your ability to estimate the sex of an individual from their skeletal remains. Although sexing can be performed using the whole skeleton, partial remains are frequently referred to the forensic investigator. So, in this practical session you will examine the morphological variation related to sex in a variety of skeletal elements. You will learn the primary features that assist in distinguishing male from female skeletons, which involves both non-metric and metric evaluations.
Practical 3: The estimation of ancestry
In this laboratory you will examine some of the techniques a forensic anthropologist has available for attempting to discern the genetic background of individuals represented by the skeleton. You will learn the primary morphological features of the human skull that best reflect ancestral affinity; there are two primary methods employed: non-metric (the evaluation of morphoscopic traits) and metric (linear measurement and statistical analysis).
Practical 4: The estimation of stature
In this session you will examine the various techniques available to calculate the living height (stature) of an individual from their skeletal remains. You will learn how to take the measurements necessary to estimate stature on the basis of complete and fragmented long bones, individual bones that contribute to living height, and from footprints and shoeprints. You will also learn to apply adjustments to your computations to account for various factors that affect stature during life.
Practical 5: Identification of trauma and pathology
This laboratory is designed to provide you with some practical experience in the identification and analysis of a selection of physical expressions of trauma, pathology and other anomalies in the human skeleton. In this session you will learn the basic characteristics of projectile and blunt force trauma. The session also includes a brief section on the basics of how to recognise pathological and occupational stress changes (e.g. bone deformation due to disease and arthritis) and other anomalies (e.g. abnormal fusions). You will also study the characteristics of bone injuries that help identify whether skeletal trauma has occurred ante- peri- or postmortem.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
