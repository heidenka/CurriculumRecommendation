Unit Information

This unit provides an intensive study of Australia’s GST law including its current scope and application to domestic supplies, exports and imports of goods and services. While the focus is on the Australian GST legislation and case law, international developments and precedents likely to impact on Australian legislative proposals or judicial decisions.  There is, thus, continual comparisons of the Australian system with those used in other important jurisdictions such as the EU and China.

Both the statute and cases are evaluated from the perspective of benchmark GST policy goals and the administrative and political factors that affect the design of the law.
The key areas covered in the unit include:
- core GST concepts of supply, consideration, enterprise, registered or required to be registered, and connected with the indirect tax zone;
- obligations imposed on resident and foreign businesses and entitlements the law provides to these enterprises;
- approaches to mixed and composite supplies; vouchers, deposits, input tax credits, adjustment events, and other operational measures in the law;
- concessional and technical rationales for input taxed supplies and GST-free supplies; application of the rules to real property transactions, financial, education, health and food supplies;
- application of the law to cross-border supplies and acquisitions, shared economy transactions; and
- specific and general anti-avoidance provisions in the GST law

Learning And Teaching Strategy

The unit is an intensive graduate subject taught over four days. Assessment is based on a short in class presentation and a choice of take-home exam or research paper. At the end of the unit, students will have a thorough knowledge of GST law in Australia and how it compares to comparable systems around the globe.

Unit Structure

The unit is taught in lecture and graduate seminar format in the period Tuesday, 7June 2022 to Friday, 10 June 2022, inclusive. Participants will make short presentations which will be graded for 30% of the unit mark.  For those who choose the take-home exam option, the exam will be on Friday, 24 July.  For those who choose the research paper option, the paper willl be due by 5:00 pm on Monday, 11 July.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.
