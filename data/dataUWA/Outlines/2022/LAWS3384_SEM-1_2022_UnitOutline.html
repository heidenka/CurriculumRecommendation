Teaching contingency plan (In response to Covid19 restrictions)

UWA Law School continues to value and prioritise face-to-face learning. However, due to the ever-evolving pandemic, protecting our student body and minimising the disruption to student learning, the student experience, and your academic progression is our priority.

Given the ongoing Covid-19 restrictions, this unit will be taught online (at least until the study break). Both the lectures and the tutorials will be online. Invitations to join the online sessions will be posted on LMS.

Should face-to-face teaching become possible after the study break, you will be given the option to attend face-to-face OR online for the rest of the semester.

FURTHER COVID PROTOCOLS

The Law School has developed protocols to deal with those instances where a student has been in a lecture or tutorial and, following the class, is required to isolate and undergo a COVID test under Government guidelines.

1. The student is expected to inform their Unit Coordinator/tutor/lecturer that they are undergoing a COVID test for any reason - they are symptomatic, are a close contact, were at an exposure site, etc.

2. All other students/staff that were present in that class will be informed of this fact, ensuring the student's/ identity and circumstances are kept confidential.

3. Under current Government guidelines the other students/staff are not required to isolate or be tested but they may wish to do so.

4. The student is expected to inform their Unit Coordinator/tutor/lecturer of the outcome of the test who will in turn inform others in the class.

5. If the student tests positive, all members of the class must follow government guidelines regarding testing and isolation.

Academic Information

Content This unit introduces students to legal and musicological approaches to examine the place and meaning of music and law in society and to challenge conventional beliefs about law through the lens of music. While law and music are usually understood as very different disciplines, this unit is an invitation to consider how studying one can usefully inform the other. We will, for instance, explore ways in which human interaction has been shaped in these two disciplines and study the impact of ideas related to codification, interpretation, performance and professionalisation. The focus of the unit lies on the development, structure and form of Western law and classical music. However, we will also contemplate other genres and pluralistic approaches that challenge conventional beliefs about law, including the common tendency to ignore its cultural, aesthetic and emotional dimensions.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

This is a relatively small unit, and you are expected to participate actively, both in the lectures and in the tutorials. To facilitate your participation, you should enable your camera.
Please familiarise yourself with the LMS site of the unit as early as possible, and especially with the materials for the first week. You will also be able to sign up for your group presentation at the end of week 1, i.e. once you have a better idea of the content of the unit.
Registration for tutorials is online through CAS. Tutorial groups will remain the same all semester, and I will teach all the tutorials.
The lectures and tutorials will be conducted on the assumption that you have prepared the assigned materials prior to each class. The tutorials will include discussions of assigned materials and presentations by students. 
Grading the ‘performance’ of both jurists and musicians is an inherently subjective exercise, which is why I will endeavour to give you constructive and qualitative comments on your contribution to this unit. In addition to this individual feedback, you will be evaluated as follows:
General participation (15%)
Your active involvement in this unit is crucial. 
I expect that you read and listen to the materials assigned for each week, that you come to class regularly and that you participate in all class activities. 
You will be assessed on your participation in 10 of the 12 weeks. You can, therefore, miss 2 weeks during the semester without having to apply for special consideration.
In addition to attending class, you are expected to:
•	Read and listen to the assigned materials for each week;
•	Contribute clearly and constructively to seminar discussions;
•	Listen to and engage constructively with alternative points of views.
The objectives of the participation mark are to promote:
•	engagement 
•	preparation 
•	understanding
•	reflection and 
•	responsible class membership.
Engagement will most obviously be in speaking but a student might also be engaged whilst listening. This will be important to note for students who are by nature quieter. They may be engaged, though speak less often.
Preparation might be evident in notes the student has come with or in the quality of answers and comments in discussion.
Understanding of the issues raised in class will most commonly be demonstrated though a student’s overall contribution to the discussion, including small group discussions in class, whether by way of answering questions, asking questions, making observations, or listening and responding to other students. 
Reflection on the issues (and preparation) might be evident in the student drawing on additional materials and experiences. 
Responsible class membership refers to the respect given to other students' comments and refraining from attempting to dominate the discussion.
Participation is about the quality not necessarily the quantity of contributions (where ‘quality’ means considered and reflective contributions – you are allowed to make mistakes!). The aim of including this component is to encourage unselfconscious discussion amongst students, not an atmosphere of assessment.
Posting comments on LMS, for instance on your colleagues’ presentations, is strongly encouraged but should not be considered a substitute for participation in class.
Mere attendance and participation in the group exercises, but without any other significant contribution to the class discussions, will be sufficient for the equivalent to a ‘pass’. Occasional to regular participation will earn you the equivalent to a ‘credit pass’ or ‘distinction’. Finally, for a ‘high distinction’, you will have to participate not only regularly but also in an insightful and constructive manner.
In-class presentation (25%) 
Together with a colleague, you will be the ‘conductor’ of one tutorial session. This means that you will prepare a group presentation of 15-20 minutes, in which you will engage in more depth with the theme of the week. What is important is that you go beyond the assigned materials for this topic and that your colleagues benefit from your engagement with additional materials. Moreover, think about questions and perhaps an activity that you think will be useful to stimulate a class discussion following your presentation. 
You can, of course, always contact me in advance to discuss your ideas; and I will remain available to assist the main ‘conductors’ during their ‘performance’ too!
‘Rehearsals’/peer feedback on your research essay topic (10%)
You will be randomly matched with a colleague to discuss your ideas for your final ’score’/essay (see below). This is an opportunity to receive input before you finalise your actual topic and start writing. There will be two ‘rehearsals’:
(1) In the tutorial in week 8, you will present your ideas to your colleague. To prepare for this ‘rehearsal’, you will draft a brief proposal, which should cover your essay question, the main points you want to analyse, and your methodology (i.e. how are you going to develop your analysis?). Moreover, start thinking about the materials that will be useful to write your essay and include a preliminary bibliography of primary and secondary sources. 
Prior to the tutorial in week 8, you will submit your proposal on LMS. You can get up to 5 points for this assessment item.
(2) The following week, in the tutorial in week 9, you will provide each other feedback on your respective topics. In other words, you will have a week to consider your colleague’s proposal and to write up your comments and suggestions (aim for around one page). 
Prior to the tutorial in week 9, you will also submit your feedback on LMS. You can get up to 5 points for this assessment item. 
Please let me know if you encounter any difficulties during this teamwork exercise.
Final ‘score’/essay (50%) 
You will write an essay on a topic of your choice (maximum of 3,500 words). The topic of your essay cannot match the topic of your in-class presentation.  
High-quality papers will be a further and more in-depth reflection on questions raised in class or in the reading/listening materials. You should use the analytical framework proposed in this unit, i.e. legal and musicological approaches aiming to transcend disciplinary boundaries, to explore normative aspects of society. Feel free to think and work creatively! I am ready to discuss your ideas at any time during the semester. 
Note that all words in the text of the assignment (including headings and in-text references) are included in the word count. Footnotes are not included in the word count provided they are used only to give citations or other brief information. If footnotes are used more extensively to provide commentary then these additional words may be counted.
As an annex to your final paper, please include a brief explanation as to whether the comments from your colleague were helpful and how you reflected them in your essay.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
