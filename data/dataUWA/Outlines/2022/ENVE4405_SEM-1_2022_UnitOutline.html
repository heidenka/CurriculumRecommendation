Academic Information

Content This unit serves to introduce the principles of ecological engineering, and apply the knowledge to case studies. A number of case studies from regional, urban and coastal, including natural and built environments, will be used to articulate the ecological engineering principles. The focus will be on the design principles, which will contribute to enhanced ecosystem services delivery in these environments with a particular emphasis on the urban environment and nature-based solutions. Additionally, this unit investigates the local, regional and global context of industries dependent on fundamental ecological processes, with an emphasis on articulating the role of engineers in delivering future sustainable outcomes for both the environment and cities. The content of this unit is mapped to the United Nations Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs).

Unit Information

This unit serves to introduce the principles of ecological engineering, and apply the knowledge to case studies. A number of case studies from regional, urban and coastal, including natural and built environments, will be used to articulate the ecological engineering principles. The focus will be on the design principles, which will contribute to enhanced ecosystem services delivery in these environments with a particular emphasis on the urban environment and nature-based solutions. Additionally, this unit investigates the local, regional and global context of industries dependent on fundamental ecological processes, with an emphasis on articulating the role of engineers in delivering future sustainable outcomes for both the environment and cities. The content of this unit is mapped to the United Nations Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs). 
The unit is intended to develop your ability to:
1. identify and quantify ecological engineering principles and their application to engineering design
2. quantify temporal and spatial scales of importance for ecosystem services in natural and built environments
3. carry out ecological engineering design including appropriate performance assessment methods, and including mapping outcomes and design criteria to SDGs
4. explore nature-based solutions as a way of solving pressing local and global challenges
5. compare and evaluate ecological/nature-based solutions and traditional engineering approaches
6. propose solutions to improve the uptake of ecological engineering solutions

Learning And Teaching Strategy

The content of this unit will be delivered through a collaborative learning model (flipped model), involving activities organised in thematic workshops. The emphasis is placed on practising the ability of integrating information from various disciplines into ecological inspired engineering design. It is expected that all students (participants) take an active role in the deliberations and activities presented in the workshops each week. The participants will be formally introduced to this approach in the first week, therefore it is essential that students attend this session. Anas will act as the main workshop facilitator, supported by invited guest speakers, and two facilitators, Liah and Andreas.
This learning format requires student participation in person. There are no formal lectures to be recorded for this unit. Any materials and additional information, such as movies/clips/material, of relevance will be uploaded to LMS.

Participation

This unit is fundamentally based upon active participation from the students. As such, participants are required to attend all sessions. 20% of the assessment in this course is based upon participants demonstrating collaborative learning, i.e., the student is present for the majority of the session and is actively participating in class activities and discussions. Attendance will be marked, however, simply turning up to class to have you name marked off and leaving shortly after will not count towards your final collaborative learning mark (we have eyes everywhere!).
In exceptional circumstances, special consideration will be given. However, the student must apply for special consideration via the EMS Student Office (located ground floor of EZONE North).
If you are completing this unit online, you will be joining the workshop live and be included in the class discussions as if you were present in person.

Referencing Requirements

The individual assignments and class project take the form of blogs and vlogs. It is expected that students will cite references in these as they would if they were writing a report. Accepted references styles are Harvard and Oxford. In the written take home exam, students are also expected to use these reference styles. Explicit instructions and guidelines will be provided with each assignment.

Essential Textbooks

There are no essential textbooks set for this unit. There may be essential and recommended readings set for this unit available in Unit Readings in the LMS.

Other

1. Individual assignments (2 x 20%) will take the form of blog entries and vlogs, and will be due at the end of the day of the dates prescribed. 
2. A class synthesis project (20%) will be assessed on the basis of a presentation and final group report/blog. This will involve collaboration of all the participants to deliver a synthesis of the learning themes identified in the workshops.
3. Take home exam (20%) will be available for a 10-day period. The take home exam can be started any time during this period, and must be submitted within 24 hours.
4. Collaborative learning (20%) can be defined in many ways depending on the context. In this unit, the aim is to create an environment where the learning happens in groups working together to solve a problem, complete a task, or create a product. It is based upon the idea that learning is a naturally social act. The participants will be assessed on the extent to which they contribute to the development, promotion and delivery of solutions, tasks and products.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
