Academic Information

Content This unit is the first practicum unit for postgraduate clinical psychology students. The aim is to provide a broad introduction to the practice of psychotherapy, and to offer students supervised training and experience in interviewing and assessment. The unit provides an introduction to the key aspects of professional practice and includes consideration of the ethical guidelines and legal responsibilities that relate to clinical practice. The unit also introduces students to assessment, basic counselling skills, interviewing techniques, note taking and report writing.

Unit Information

Students are completing their professional training in clinical psychology and are required to pass placement evaluation reports and submit logbooks of their activities to meet placement competencies as per new APAC requirements. As this is an UP/UF, unit the weighting of the assessments is irrelevant.
This Unit includes a five week module: Ethics & Professional Practice: PREPARATION FOR PLACEMENT (taught by Associate Professer Carmela Pestell) which prepares provisional psychologists (in clinical psychology and neuropsychology) by providing an introduction to the key aspects of professional practice and includes consideration of the ethical guidelines and legal responsibilities that relate to clinical practice. The unit also introduces students to basic counselling skills and interviewing techniques. Trainees will also be taught a decision making model to aid in their application of the APS Code of Ethics and Practice Guidelines. Students will gain an increased understanding of their professional responsibilities, as well as the primary role of AHPRA and the Psychology Board of Australia.
LEARNING OBJECTIVES
Students who complete this module: 1) will be familiar with the national registration act and other relevant legislation; 2) will be familiar with the APS code of ethics and how it guides clinical practice; 3) be able to apply contemporary ethical decision making models when confronted with ethical dilemmas in clinical practice; 4) will have an understanding of the basic elements of effective therapeutic relationships; 5) will be aware of the basic principles of effective interviewing and be able to conduct a clinical interview.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

A range of learning and teaching strategies are used to support students. These include i) Problem-Based Learning: that is, real-world problems within a cross-cultural setting are used as the vehicle to promote student learning of concepts and principles. For example, students work in small groups to generate case formulation, assessment, and treatment plans for de-identified case examples (clinical vignettes) based on actual cases during class. They are taught several contemporary ethical decision-making models and are required to apply them to these vignettes via small and large group class discussions, and by completing online quizzes. ii) Evidence-Based Learning. For example, students are taught an evidence-based approach to risk assessment, with current best clinical practices and health department requirements outlined. Trainees are required to show inter-leaved practice via role-playing a range of clinical scenarios and complete relevant empirical and theoretical readings from a cross-cultural perspective..iii) Simulated Learning is achieved in several ways. For instance, students are provided with clinical assessment tools and therapeutic interventions. These are discussed, questionnaires are completed and scored, and activities are role-played in small groups, video and class demonstrations; or with clients. During clinic meetings, students participate in a mock team meeting in which client issues are discussed within a confidential setting.  iv) Student-centred learning.  Students are also required to complete study guides and quizzes as learning activities. This provides the opportunity to receive feedback on content or ask questions about concepts they did not understand. v) Active student participation: All lectures encourage class reflection and discussion of in-class activities, and the quizzes and additional reading.

Assessment Information
Students will be expected to submit at least two intake and termination reports (re their individual therapy clients) to their clinical supervisor. Formal placement evaluation reports will also be conducted by supervisors at the completion of each individual psychotherapy case, as well as at the completion of all modules. It is also expected that students have developed competencies administering the WAIS-IV, WMS-IV and WISC-V, as this will be a requirement for Clinical Psychology trainees when they attend external placements in the future. There is also an online RiskAware module which must be completed by 30 June 2022.  Risk Aware -Enhancing students' clinical competence in risky environments (www.riskaware.education) is an online education program that enables students to identify and manage placement related risk. Risk Aware consists of: a) Seven online learning modules that provide an academic and theoretical context for student learning; and, b) Authentic simulation‐based learning experiences that allow students to apply their theoretical knowledge and practice newly acquired skills. The seven modules and their content were determined by a literature review, stakeholder interviews and student surveys. The Risk Aware platform also includes downloadable resources, such as the Safe Placement Guide. This resource was designed to guide conversations between higher education providers and their industry partners, promoting student safety through open conversations about risk.

Participation

It is compulsory that students be punctual and attend all coursework and other specified training activities unless extenuating circumstances (such as illness or significant personal crisis) prevent them from doing so. Where students cannot attend (or have not attended) the full required activities or classes, they are expected to notify the unit coordinator to provide an adequate explanation and documentation (such as a medical certificate in the event of illness) explaining their absence or lateness. A failure to attend without adequate explanation and documented evidence of illness or other impeding factors beyond one’s personal control will result in failing the unit in which the class/activity was missed. Note that even where an adequate explanation and documented evidence are supplied, a student will still fail the unit if he/she has not attended a sufficient proportion of the unit. A “sufficient proportion of the unit” is normally defined as at least 80%; in recognition that 2022 might have impacts on students’ wellness with respect to COVID-19, this will be defined as at least 75% for this year only.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
