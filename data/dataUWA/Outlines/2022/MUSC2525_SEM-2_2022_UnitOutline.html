Teaching contingency plan (In response to Covid19 restrictions)

Currently, this unit is planned to be delivered face-to-face. If this is not possible and all teaching must revert to online delivery, the following changes may need to be made to deliver the unit online. These should be read in conjunction with the unit outline published on the LMS. If you have any questions, please contact Dr. James Ledger: james.ledger@uwa.edu.au.

1. The unit objectives will still be met unaltered.

2. The assessment activity will be the same, however:

-Assessments will simply be moved online

-Practical and written exams will be administered via Teams and/or Auralia instead of in-person, and written assignments will be submitted via email

-The 5% "Attendance/Participation" assessment item will be removed, and other assessment items proportionally increased

3. The delivery of face-to-face activities will be moved online:

-Content delivery will remain the same, but simply move from face-to-face to online via Teams + Auralia

-Lectorials will be delivered live on Teams, recorded and uploaded to LMS

-Tutorials will be delivered live on Teams, but will not be recorded

-Attendance at Teams lectorials/tutorials will not be compulsory, but strongly encouraged

4. Actions students needs to take in online mode:

-Students should note that it is in their best interest to attend all live sessions

-In the absence of compulsory attendance, students will need to take responsibility for their learning, ensuring that the lectures are watched each week, assignments are completed, and that they stay engaged with the unit in general

5. Should a student become ill/required to self-isolate, the student will be required to:

-Watch recorded lectures online; complete assignments online via Auralia and/or email written assignments; maintain contact with the lecturer to ensure the student does not fall behind (individual sessions Teams may be organised with the lecturer)

Academic Information

Content This unit is the final in a four-semester sequence that develops core theoretical, musical and perceptual skills. The unit comprises two mutually supportive components—(1) harmony, and (2) aural—in which students will develop an advanced understanding of musical grammar, both on paper and in their creative imagination. Building upon the concepts introduced in Music Language 1-3, this unit deals with more sophisticated harmonic concepts such as post-tonal harmony, and investigates the languages and related analytical tools of post-tonal music. On completion of the four-semester Music Language program, the course aims to produce musically literate students who are able to apply the skills they have acquired in any professional situation. This unit is a core unit in the Bachelor of Music degree, and is also open to students enrolled in other majors and degree pathways who fulfil the prerequisites and who have an interest in music.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

The broader aim of the Music Language four-semester sequence is to support the acquisition of musical fluency and the capacity for independent learning, and to produce musically literate students who are able to apply the skills they have acquired in any professional situation. In this final unit of the sequence, students investigate the languages and related analytical tools of post-tonal music within two mutually supportive components—(1) post-tonal harmony; and (2) aural.
The unit is delivered face-to-face through lectorials, seminars and tutorial formats, supported by online materials. Teaching and learning strategies are practical and interactive, and may include the use of instruments and the singing voice; the use of conducting gestures and clapping; listening analytically to audio and video examples; study of notated examples; the writing of notated assignments both in class and between sessions; and verbal explanations of musical concepts.

Unit Structure

UNIT COMPONENTS
COMPONENT A: HARMONY (50%)
COMPONENT B: AURAL (50%)
Through a weekly 2-hour seminar/lectorial (Harmony) and weekly 1-hour tutorial (Aural), these components build on the concepts introduced in Music Language 1,2 and 3, in which students should have attained a deep understanding of tonal harmony, musical grammar and syntax, phrase/period structure, and analytical techniques. The course now turns to Western music of the post-tonal era (1900 onwards). The course examines post-tonal harmonic theory including set theory and serialism: new approaches to analysis, its application to interpretation of post-tonal music; new approaches to rhythm and duration.
LEARNING OUTCOMES
Upon completion of this unit, students should be able to identify the key characteristics of early post-tonal harmony including, centricity, synthetic scales, polytonality, quartal harmony, complex and irregular  metric & rhythmic devices and patterns.
The student should also be also able to apply skills in tonal & atonal sight-singing and instrumental sight-reading, and be confident in approaching analysis of tonal & post-tonal music through various analytical techniques.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
