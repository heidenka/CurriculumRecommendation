Academic Information

Content Artificial intelligence (AI) is developing at an astonishing speed and will soon influence many aspects of our lives. This unit is an introduction to the fascinating philosophical questions concerning AI: Can machines really have a mind? Are we ourselves just biological machines? What are the risks and benefits of AI? Will there ever be superintelligent machines? Is it possible to survive mind-uploading? Topics may include: (1) the computational theory of mind; (2) Connectionism and neural networks; (3) the nature of computation and implementation; (4) the Turing Test; (5) Searle's Chinese Room argument (6) the extended mind hypothesis; (6) the theoretical prospects for and practical consequences of superintelligent AI; (7) the alignment problem for AI; (8) AI risk; (9) Mind-uploading.

Unit Information

Artificial intelligence (AI) is developing at a staggering speed and will soon influence many aspects of our lives. This unit is an introduction to central philosophical questions concerning AI. It is divided into two parts. The first part explores the nature of mind and consciousness and the possibility for machine minds. The second part focuses on superintelligent AI and the feasibility of mind-uploading.
Part 1: The Nature of Mentality and Consciousness: Can Machines have a Mind?
1. The computational theory of the mind
In order to answer the question of whether there can be artificial minds, it is crucial to get a clear understanding of the nature of our own minds. The Computational Theory of Mind is the most promising account of how the mind works. We will start by getting familiar with the basic tenets of the computational theory of the mind and consciousness.
2. The Classical Model: The Language of Thought
Next, we will look in detail at the classical version of the computational approach to the mind. According to the classical model, the mind works by performing computations on mental representations, i.e. symbols in a language of thought. This theory is appealing because it can explain the systematicity and productivity of human thought. We will focus on Fodor’s version of the classical model.
3. Connectionism and Artificial Neural Networks
In the 1980, a promising alternative to the classical model was proposed: connectionism. This account relies on using neural networks as computational models, which differ in important ways from classical Turing models. We will explore the key features of connectionism and its relation to the classical model. The two are often presented as rival views, but it may be possible to see them as compatible with each other.
4&5. Criticism I: Searle’s Chinese Room Argument
In the 1980, John Searle put forward a interesting argument for the claim that strong AI is impossible — computers cannot really have minds; at best they can act as if they do. We will discuss this argument in detail and lay out the Searle’s alternative to a computational approach to the mind: biological naturalism. We will also discuss the larger philosophical issues the argument raises: the connection between syntax and semantics, and between intentionality and consciousness.
6. Criticism III: Computation & Implementation
In this week, we will look closer at the nature of computation and computational implementation. What is computation and what is it for a system to implement a computation? We will then discuss Putnam’s and Searle’s triviality argument against the computational theory of mind and Chalmers’ reply to the argument.
7. The extended mind
Can your iphone be part of your mind? Andy Clark and David Chalmers have argued that the answer is: yes. We will investigate the intriguing idea that the boarders of your mind may reach beyond what’s in your brain.
Part 2: Superintelligence and Uploading: What are the prospects for and consequences of developing superintelligence AI? Is mind-uploading possible?
1. Superintelligence and Existential Risk
We will consider arguments for and against the claim that we might develop superintelligent AI in the not too remote future. What are the practical and ethical consequences of this? This will lead us into the general discussion of the risk and benefits of AI.
2. The Control Problem and the Alignment Problem
The central questions of this week are the following: Are there ways to prevent a catastrophic singularity from occurring? Could we control superintelligent AI? What can we do to maximise the chances that superintelligent AI’s values will align with ours?
3. Mind-Uploading
Finally, we explore the prospects for mind-uploading. Is it possible to replace our biological hardware with an artificial one, or upload ourselves to a computer? We will look at different forms of uploading and discuss arguments for and against the claim that uploading is possible.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

The course has two chief goals. The first goal is to teach students how to do philosophy, through the development of problem-solving, critical thinking and research skills. Particular skills include:
- Capacity to form arguments. This means not just reporting what famous philosophers have thought, but developing a considered opinion as to whether they are right, where a considered opinion is one that can be backed up with solid argumentation.
- A critical and enquiring attitude to philosophical issues.
- Ability to read high-quality philosophy essays
- Be able to write a better philosophy essay. This is extremely important and has three components. First, you will be able to express your ideas in a clear and precise manner. Second, you will be able to write a more structured essay and, third, you will learn to formulate the sort of arguments you are expected to master at an undergraduate level.
- You will learn to work effectively in collaborative contexts during tutorials. More specifically in tutorials, you will be expected to participate both in small group tasks, such as schematising one of the arguments from the reading, as well as group discussions.
- Be a better researcher. You will be encouraged to use a range of research methods involving a range of information technologies. You will also learn to identify the most important sources for the sort of essay you are writing, so that you can research both more effectively and more efficiently. • You will be encouraged to take responsibility for your own learning via the essay and the exam, each of which will require the synthesising of a range of sources in order to formulate a structured and well-argued paper.
In addition to these broad skills, students will also gain an understanding of the course content. This includes:
- The ability to understand central concepts and arguments in the philosophy of mind, cognitive science, and AI-research.
- An understanding of the computational theory of the mind.
- An understanding of the key concepts and arguments in the philosophical debate about machines minds and artificial consciousness.
- An understanding of a range of models of computational theory of mind, such as symbol system hypothesis and connectionism.
- An understanding of the nature of computation & implementation.
- The ability to assess influential criticisms of Artificial Intelligence, such as Searle’s Chinese Room Argument.
- A familiarity with the key points of contact between philosophy of mind and artificial intelligence research.
- The ability to form considered judgments about the likelihood of the emergence of superintelli- gence.
- The ability to assess the risks and benefits connected to AI-research.
- An understanding of basic accounts of personal identity and the prospects of mind-uploading.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
