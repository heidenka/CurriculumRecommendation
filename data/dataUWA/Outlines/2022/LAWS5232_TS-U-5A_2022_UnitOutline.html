Academic Information

Content This unit examines the core principles of international tax law and is not based upon Australia tax law. It examines these core principles as they apply around the world and the policy issues supporting them. It thoroughly examines double taxation agreements with a focus on the current version of the OECD model. It considers global issues in relation to transfer pricing and thin capitalisation rules as well as the topic of international tax avoidance. It also considers current debates in relation to base erosion and profit shifting.

Unit Structure

This course focuses on the core principles of Double Tax Agreements and International Tax Policy. We have a separate course in the program called BEPS and Transfer Pricing. That course pays more attention to recent developments in relation to DTAs as part of the ‘BEPs’ project. In this course these new developments will be dealt with briefly as the main focus is learning about the long established principles in this area. 
The essential text for this course is: 
Kevin Holmes (2014) International Tax Policy and Double Tax Treaties – An Introduction to Principles and Application (Second Revised Edition). 
The course is built around this text and the text therefore provides your course notes. It is important that you read the text prior to the intensive 4 days class. In addition you should read the relevant OECD model articles and commentary. It is also recommended that you follow up some of the other references made in the text and read the original cases and specific treaty articles. Some additional readings may be put onto LMS as well. 
The text book can be purchased as an e-book at ibfd.org which will allow you instant access if you wish to start working immediately. See: https://www.ibfd.org/shop/book/international-tax-policyand-double-tax-treaties-0 . (It appears that students can get a discount on the book from my reading of this site. This is something for you to investigate if you wish to, please do not ask me how to get the discount as I do not know.) 
I will refer to this book as ‘text’. You will also need to refer to the OECD Model Convention and Commentary and the UN Model. These you can find on the OECD and UN sites free of charge. You will also be referred to a selection of Double tax Agreements along the way. These can generally be obtained from public sites in the relevant countries. 
The text includes valuable practice exercises and examples. I recommend that you engage with these.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
