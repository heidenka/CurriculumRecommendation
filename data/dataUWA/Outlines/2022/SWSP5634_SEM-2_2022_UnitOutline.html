The Australian Association of Social Workers Mental Health Competencies (2014) and National Practice Standards for the Mental Health Workforce (2013) documents provides the broad framework for this series of lecture/seminars. The purpose of social work practice in mental health is to assist to restore individual, family, and community well-being, and to promote principles of social justice. Social works clinical practice occurs at the interface between the individual and the environment; social work activity begins with the individual, and extends to the contexts of family, social networks, community, and the broader society. This unit will examine the two important aspects governing clinical practice in a mental health context. This will include theoretical modalities (knowledge, models of understanding), and clinical intervention strategies (techniques, therapeutic modalities). All the theoretical models examined, where possible, will be evidenced based practice models (EBPs). The philosophical framework governing this unit from the mental health perspective is the biopsychosocial model, which consciously attempts to incorporate all of the significant dimensions in human existence in order to optimally conceptualise our understanding of the human condition in general and mental health issues specifically.

Unit Information

Social Work and Mental Health Practice has been developed to comply with the Australian Association of Social Workers (AASW) Mental Health Competencies. The Australian Association of Social Workers (AASW) Mental Health Competencies document provides the broad framework for this series of lecture/seminars. The purpose of social work practice in mental health is to assist to restore individual, family, and community wellbeing, and to promote principles of social justice. Social works clinical practice occurs at the interface between the individual and the environment; social work activity begins with the individual, and extends to the contexts of family, social networks, community, and the broader society.
This unit will examine the two important aspects governing clinical practice in a mental health context. This will include theoretical modalities (knowledge, models of understanding), and clinical intervention strategies (techniques, therapeutic modalities). The philosophical framework governing this unit from the mental health perspective is the biopsychosocial model, which consciously attempts to incorporate all of the significant dimensions in human existence in order to optimally conceptualise our understanding of the human condition in general and mental health issues specifically. How social work interprets and applies this model in a unique mental health context will be central to the discussions and learning outcomes. These combined approaches will provide the foundation for all the lecture/workshops and will also include the important components of gender, ethnicity and culture. Moreover, these varying approaches will frequently complement each other, although explanatory and conceptual nomenclature may vary.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

The teaching strategy for this unit is a face to face in class delivery.  Small group exercises and class room discussions will characterize the learning environment.  Students need to be prepared to engage in discussion, ask questions and have questions asked of them. 
 Students will in turn conduct a 50-minute presentation to their peers. The presentation will include relevant information that will assist students to better understand and clinically work with clients who are experiencing mental health difficulties.  Important areas to cover in the presentations are: history, diagnostic criteria, issues and controversy’s, biopsychosocial treatment options, social works role, knowledge of the subject matter displayed by all of the presenter(s), presentation skills and keeping to time. All students in their respective groups will be required to participate in the presentation.
After the presentation, the topic will be openly discussed and analysed in the seminar.
Learning Outcomes:
1.	To become familiar with the psychiatric classification process and some of core diagnostic categories used in psychiatry
2.	To become familiar with the biopsychosocial treatment options for mental illness
3.	To have an understanding of the conceptual basis and practical clinical application of the biopsychosocial model of mental illness
4.	To have an understanding of the role of social work in mental health service delivery
5.	To have an understanding of the different philosophical positions in relation to the conceptualisation and treatment of mental health issues e.g. recovery and the orthodox medical model
6.	To know and understand what statutes are relevant to the practice of mental health in Western Australia

Participation

Attendance is compulsory at lectures/workshops as these sessions are an essential part of the professional learning on the course. Furthermore, students have an obligation to their colleagues to attend and contribute. If a student fails to maintain an attendance rate of 80%, it will be deemed they have not satisfied the requirements of the unit unless absences are formally approved in writing by the Head of Division. 
Students unable to attend for unavoidable reasons (e.g. illness) must notify the unit coordinator prior to the class. Absences due to illness must be validated by a medical certificate. If the unit coordinator is not available, a message can be left with the Academic Services Officer.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "honest, ethical and responsible scholarship". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
