Academic Information

Content There are three primary academic objectives of the unit: (1) to provide experiential learning in a research group atmosphere. Students become part of the Centre for Evolutionary Biology (CEB) and bring their ideas to fruition through discussion with the members of the group and their mentor. They participate in fortnightly CEB meetings where established researchers present their data and answer questions from the group. The students see how science is a collegial and constructive process; (2) to learn the generic skills of scientific thinking, hypothesis testing, data handling and scientific communication; and (3) to develop specialised knowledge of the practical considerations of research in their area of study—learning (but not limited to) techniques such as biometry, behavioural analysis, meta-analysis, quantitative/molecular genetics/genomics or comparative analyses. The areas of theory covered depend on the project but could include natural/sexual selection, sperm competition, immunity, sensory ecology, conservation biology and population/quantitative genetics. Projects that focus on a range of study organisms, including terrestrial, marine and freshwater vertebrate and invertebrate groups may be available for practical work.

Unit Information

This unit has three primary academic objectives: (1) to provide experiential learning in a research group setting, where you will work closely with your peer group on a project designed by your group’s mentor (an academic based in the School of Biological Sciences). All students are expected to participate in weekly meetings run through the Centre for Evolutionary Biology, where researchers meet informally to discuss their research progress, share scientific ideas, or hone their presentation skills. Through these meetings, you will see how science can be a collegial and constructive process; (2) to learn the generic skills of scientific thinking, hypothesis testing, data handling and scientific communication; and (3) to develop practical skills relevant to your specific area of research—for example, biometry, analysis of behaviour, practical experimentation, ecological modelling, meta-analysis, phylogenetic analyses, quantitative and molecular genetics, genomic approaches or comparative analyses. The areas of theory covered will depend on the project to which you are assigned, but could include sexual selection, natural selection, community and population ecology, population genetics, behavioural ecology, sensory ecology and conservation biology. Projects that focus on a range of study organisms, including terrestrial, marine and freshwater vertebrate and invertebrate groups, may be available for practical work.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

You will gain a broad understanding of major ideas in evolutionary ecology. Throughout the unit you will develop research skills, including experimental design, data collection, statistical analysis and data interpretation. You will be guided on how to communicate this information clearly and concisely (both through a written scientific paper and an oral presentation). On completion of this unit you should be able to:
1.	Formulate and express scientific ideas in the field of evolutionary ecology
2.	Think critically about how to test evolutionary processes
3.	Demonstrate competency in carrying out practical research tasks
4.	Analyse and present ideas and data with care and clarity

Unit Structure

There are no lectures in this unit but you will be expected to attend a compulsory introductory briefing during the first week of semester. You must also attend all Centre for Evolutionary Biology (CEB) meetings during the semester (see Assessment Tasks below).
You will be allocated to groups of approx. 4 students (depending on eventual class size) and assigned to a research leader (an academic with expertise in the fields of ecology, evolution, genetics, behaviour…) who will act as your group’s mentor throughout the research project. For the duration of your project, your group will be ‘embedded’ within the mentor’s research lab, providing an authentic research experience. 
Your group will be assigned a research topic – a question that you will address over the course of the semester with your peers, either through the collection of novel data, the use of existing data supplied by your mentor, or using library resources to generate the data needed for (e.g.) comparative or meta-analytical work. Please see the LMS site for a list of this year’s projects. In all cases you will use these data/resources to write an independent research paper. Thus, although you will work collaboratively in a group to generate the data for your paper, you will analyse their data independently and produce their own research paper for assessment. The research paper component of your assessment is NOT a group assignment.
There is no set textbook for this unit but you may be guided to relevant literature by your mentor. You will also be expected to use UWA library resources to independently access the primary literature on your research topic.
You will be advised about the timetable of scheduled CEB meetings but note that these usually run every Wednesday afternoon from 1-2pm. These are generally run face-to-face in the Zoology Conference Room (G.06) situated on the ground floor of the Zoology Building. However, the eventual format of these meetings (face-to-face or online) will be communicated to you during the semester. Note that your attendance at each meeting, and contributions, will be monitored and assessed. As a rough guide, you will earn marks if you turn up to all meeting (unless you have a valid reason for not attending) and regularly participate in the question/answer sessions and/or contribute to our short discussions after each meeting.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
