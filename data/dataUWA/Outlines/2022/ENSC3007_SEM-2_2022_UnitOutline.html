Academic Information

Content This unit introduces the fundamental elements of heat and mass transfer. Students investigate heat transfer by conduction, convection and radiation. The analogy between heat and mass transfer is covered and applied in the analysis of convective and diffusive mass transfers.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

Detailed lecture notes are available on the LMS site for this unit and in the form of a course reader.  PowerPoint presentation slides will be used for the lectures, which serve as a summary of the essential and important points of the lecture notes.  Practice questions and their detailed solutions are also available in the LMS site and in the course reader.
The lectures serve to elucidate the essential and important points of the lecture notes and provide additional reinforcing information relevant to the unit.
You will find that the lecture notes include more solved examples than what are presented in lectures.  This will hopefully reinforce your problem solving ability.  Due to time pressure, the lectures will not explain all the derivations of many important equations, rather they focus on the conceptual understanding of their importance and how to correctly apply them to various situations, including new and unfamiliar situations.  The detailed derivations are often available in the lecture notes.  Interested students are encouraged to go through them carefully, so as to strengthen their conceptual understanding. 
The lectures will be conducted in hybrid mode, namely both face to face and online simultaneously.  You are more than encouraged to participate actively, query various points and articulate your concerns or doubts during the lecture, either physically or remotely. Obviously if you choose to attend online, you have to consent to you being recorded during the lecture.  If you do not feel comfortable about being recorded, you can (1) leave the platform, or (2) disable the video feature in Microsoft Teams.  I definitely enjoy interacting with you. 
I have also invited Mr. Paul Tuckwell from Alfa Laval Australia Pty. Ltd. to share his wealth of experience in state of the art industrial heat transfer technologies with you.  This special invited lecture is scheduled on the 19th of September, during the lecture session.  Please note that an online multiple choice question test regarding Paul's lecture contents will be conducted after his lecture.  Note that Paul's lecture and the following online multiple choice question test is an integral assessment, so that 5% weighting is applied to attending Paul's lecture, and another 5% to the online test.  Accordingly, attendance will be taken during the lecture, both physically and remotely.
While the lectures emphasise on the critical understanding of key concepts and share information on current technological and industrial trends, the practice sessions aim to focus on the in-depth understanding of key problem solving skills to particular problems.  You are encouraged to bring with you the supplied detailed solutions, and the facilitator will go through the various engineering and conceptual considerations together with you.  Instead of simply focusing on problem solving, the facilitator will discuss with you in addition the broader background relevant to the problems and, where relevant, the various real life constraints involved. I have provisioned for one online session in Thursday afternoons for those Online Restricted Students.
I have also arranged for you to conduct one lab session. They are arranged to help you gain first-hand experience into real life heat transfer problem as well as the practical difficulty involved in thermal measurements.  It also serves to reinforce what you have learnt conceptually in classes.
The lab involves hands-on experience with two principal types of heat exchanger widely used in the industry, namely plate-type heat exchanger and shell-and-tube heat exchanger.  During the lab session, students will be able to operate these two types of heat exchanger, measure their thermal performance and make direct comparison between these two types of heat exchanger.  Online Restricted Students will be provided with a set of experimental data to complete the formal lab report.

Referencing Requirements

Assignments: Students are expected to work on the two assignments individually. While students can discuss among themselves the general approach regarding the assignments, students must not copy from one another nor adapt the work of another and claim it to be their own. Formal Lab report: Except for those Online Restricted Students, students are encouraged to attend the allocated lab session, conduct the experiment in collaboration with fellow students in a small group setting and collect the experimental data. For those Online Restricted Students, they will be provided with the experimental data to prepare for the individual formal lab report for marking. While the performance of experiment and collection of data are group work, the writing up for the formal lab report is an individual work. In the formal lab report, students must reference all sources from which they obtained information that ultimately appear in the report. This include the lab manual available in the LMS and the course reader. In referencing the sources, students are expected to follow the IEEE referencing style (https://guides.library.uwa.edu.au/IEEE).

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "honest, ethical and responsible scholarship". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.
