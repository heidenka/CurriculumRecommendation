Academic Information

Content This unit focuses on the teaching and learning of science in secondary school. It includes theory necessary to understand the teaching of science such as constructivism, students' conceptions, conceptual change, scientific literacy and the nature of science. The essential, practical aspects of teaching science including science curriculum, planning, teaching strategies, laboratory safety and assessment are covered. The unit explores issues facing science educators today such as the use of analogies and models, motivation, resources, excursions and using information and communication technology (ICT) in the classroom.

ASSESSMENT DETAILS
Task 1: Communicating Scientifically	    Name:
Worth: 10%         
Due: In class session 3, 15 Feb 2022 
This assessment contributes 10% towards your final mark. Your task is to present one scientific concept to the class in a 90 second oral presentation. You will also need to submit one single-sided page of typed information explaining your scientific concept. 
Part A: The oral presentation
The idea is to present a scientific concept accurately in a novel or creative way to ignite students’ interest in learning more about the concept. The 90 second presentation could be used to introduce a new unit of work for students in Years 7-10 or consolidate/reinforce a concept that could be difficult for Year 7-10 students to learn.
You are encouraged to use props or other aids to foster engagement and must keep to the time limit of 90 seconds. Presenters should not use palm cards, notes or PPt during the presentation.
Part B: The A4 page of notes
A succinct, scientifically accurate explanation of the scientific concept (including references) should be submitted in class on a single-sided A4 sheet of paper. The use of diagrams/pictures/charts are encouraged to reinforce content covered in the 90 second presentation.
Task 2: Lesson Planning	                                                                                             30%
Due: Session 5, Thursday March 3, 2022
Your task is to develop three, 60 minute science lesson plans for a mixed ability Year 7, 8, 9 OR 10 class. Your lessons will be a connected sequence as if you were teaching them three days in a row. Your lessons are to be based on one of the four ‘science understanding’ sub-strands of the Western Australian Curriculum. You need to incorporate ‘science inquiry’ and a ‘science as a human endeavor’ outcome in at least one lesson. Your assignment will consist of two parts, Part A and Part B.
Part A will consist of: 
a)	An overview of the lesson sequence that briefly describes the content and how the content links with WA curriculum documents. (i.e., the science understanding, science as a human endeavour and science inquiry skills strands of the WA science curriculum), general capabilities and cross curriculum priorities, where relevant.  
b)	A brief description of the mixed ability students you are planning to teach these lessons to including their year, class size, school resources, school location, time of year. Do not name actual schools.
Part A should be less than 2 pages single spaced, 12-point font.
Part B will consist of the three lesson plans. Each lesson plan must include, as a minimum, information about: 
a)	organisational details - place/date/time/year level/ etc;
b)	title or lesson focus; 
c)	WA science curriculum outcomes written in full;
d)	the specific lesson outcomes/objectives; 
e)	materials and resources required;
f)	a series of lesson steps (including time estimates) with an introduction, teaching strategies, student activities and a conclusion; 
g)	how you will monitor/assess the outcomes/objectives; 
h)	how you will reflect on the lesson.
Lesson Resources 
All lesson resources that will be needed by the teacher to conduct each lesson should be included after each lesson plan in your assignment. This includes referenced worksheets from other sources, activities/readings/questions from textbooks (photocopies), websites (include a copy of main website). You may develop your own resources or select existing resources. 
Use of ICT 
At least one of your lesson plans should incorporate resources that include the use of ICT to support and enhance learning experiences. This can include use of ICT by students or the teacher.
Referencing
There needs to be a comprehensive reference list at the end of your assignment. Use APA 7 style (see library for UWA guidelines).
Information about lesson planning and examples of science lesson plans will be provided and developed during workshops. A generic framework for a lesson plan will be provided electronically (on LMS) so that you can use it as a template and adapt it to your needs. (Note: There is no compulsory format). Lesson plans for beginning teachers are usually about 2-3 pages in length, so it is expected that the three lesson plans (excluding resources and references) will be between 6 and 9 pages, single-spaced, 12 point font. 
More information about planning for teaching and learning science can be found in your textbook (Dawson, Venville & Donovan, Chapters 5 and 6). 
Marking Rubric

Task 3: Learning Program	                                                                                             60%
Due: Session 8, Thursday April 7, 2022
A program is a document that outlines the teaching, learning activities and assessment that are planned for a period of between 2 weeks to a whole year. A program provides clear direction and a time frame to assist with the planning of daily lessons.
 
Programs are written primarily for the teacher, but are important documents of accountability that may be presented to Heads of Learning Areas and other senior staff, the WA School Curriculum and Standards Authority, parents and students. In a school, one teacher may lead the programming for a unit of work and work collegially with their peers in order to develop it. Your program conveys your philosophy and approaches to teaching, learning and assessment, and to students.
For this assignment you will work in a team of 4 people as if you were a lower secondary science learning area team planning a one year program of work for a mixed ability Year 7, 8, 9 or 10 class. Your lecturer will allocate you a year group for your program. While the grade for this assignment will be based on the individual work that you submit, you are expected to work collaboratively with your team as if you were in a school environment. This approach has been designed to be consistent with the way that collaborative planning often happens in schools in order to improve your understanding of the teaching process.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
