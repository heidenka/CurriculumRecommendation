Academic Information

Content This unit is compulsory for all students taking a major in Chemistry. It provides the foundation for more advanced study in all areas of chemistry. Emphasis is placed on core chemical principles and experimental techniques relevant to physical, analytical, inorganic and organic chemistry. Topics include—techniques for separation science such as chromatography, gas–liquid chromatography and high performance liquid chromatography; spectroscopic techniques such as mass spectrometry, nuclear magnetic resonance spectroscopy and infrared and ultraviolet-visible spectroscopy for the determination of molecular structure; methods for determining, interpreting and reporting the uncertainties in experimental measurement; how to write a lab report; general chemical literacy, chemistry societies, sources of reference data, and the publication process; and concepts of thermodynamics of chemical reactions (building on those introduced in CHEM1001 Chemistry—Properties and Energetics).

Learning And Teaching Strategy

Course content includes lectures, tutorial/seminar sessions, workshops and lab classes. Notes, practice problems and other material are provided online (LMS). Students are expected to attend lectures, tutorial/seminar and workshop sessions, and lab classes, execute lab experiments as directed by demonstrators and the lab manual, and do the practice problems provided. Working consistently through the semester and keeping up to date with practice problems will focus your thinking and teach you how to apply what you learn in lectures and labs in problem-solving situations.

Unit Structure

Two lectures and one tutorial/seminar per week (~ 45 mins each). One lab session  (~ 3 h) per week. In semester weeks 7 and 8, the 'labs' are structure elucidation workshops. Three tutorial/seminar slots will contain assessment exercises (tests/quizzes), as indicated in the Unit Schedule below.

Participation

Three tutorial/seminar slots will include quizzes/tests and attendance is compulsory. Students with unavoidable clashes (e.g. a lab for another unit) shoudl contact the unit coordinator to arrange an alternative quiz time. Lab classes are compulsory and all are assessed, either via a worksheet to be submitted at the end of the lab session, a short report submitted via LMS, or a test to be held during one of the tutorial/seminar slots.

Referencing Requirements

Students are expected to adhere to university rules about academic conduct (no plagiarism etc) and to properly cite all sources used for assignments, lab reports, etc. You may use Vancouver referencing style https://guides.library.uwa.edu.au/vancouver (https://guides.library.uwa.edu.au/vancouver). Chemists frequently adopt the referencing styles used by the American Chemical Society or the Royal Society of Chemistry, which are similar to Vancouver. Find a recent paper from an ACS or RSC journal to see how these journals do referencing, and follow that format.

Unit Contingency Plans:

  • What will happen to the unit in the event of lockdown, masking, or stringent social distancing, e.g.: moved online; lectures moved online with labs deferred; etc. 

    Lectures, seminars and workshops will be moved online, either pre-recorded or live-streamed (and recorded) where possible. Laboratory sessions will be deferred (This unit cannot be completed without completing the lab course). The final examination will be online.


  • What will happen to the unit in the event of staff absence due to COVID-related reasons, e.g.: replacement lecturer; recorded lectures from previous years; etc. 

    If well enough the academic may present live-streamed lectures from home using Blackboard Collaborate. If too unwell, the lecturer will be replaced by another staff member, or recordings from a previous year will be made available.


  • What the implications for students in the unit are if they get COVID or must self-isolate due to close contact. It will be important in this respect to remember the 2 week special consideration policy in play. 

    Students who get COVID-19 or are required to self-isolate due to close contact should use the two-week undocumented special consideration in the first instance in line with consultation with the Student Life Team. Students will be able to access this once during the semester; subsequent special consideration requests for absences require normal levels of documentation.

Note that a pass in the laboratory component is required to pass this unit. (Fail the labs, or fail to complete sufficient labs/reports, and you fail the unit).


Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.

Special Considerations

If your study has been adversely affected by illness or other significant circumstances outside of your control, you can apply for special consideration. Significant circumstances may include but are not limited to:

  • serious illness or death of a member of the student's immediate family or household or of a close friend
  • serious injury
  • being a victim of a crime
  • breakdown of relationship
  • sudden loss of income or employment
  • serious disruption to domestic arrangements
