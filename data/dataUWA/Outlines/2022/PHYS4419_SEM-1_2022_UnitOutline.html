Academic Information

Content This unit will provide the student with a broad and thorough understanding of the observed properties of galaxies and the key physical processes affecting their evolution and dynamics. Key topics include (1) galactic structure and morphology; (2) distance indicators; (3) scaling relations; and (4) large scale structures and environmental effects. The content is explored with reference to a range of applications and physical contexts, including a discussion of open problems in galactic dynamics.

Unit Information

This unit consists of two parts: Galaxies - taught by Dr Claudia Lagos - and Galaxy Dynamics - taught by Professor Kenji Bekki. The first part, Galaxies, will take place during weeks 1-7, while the second part, Galaxy Dynamics, will take place during weeks 8-12, plus an exam during exam week. 
The primary aim of this unit is to establish a strong foundation of knowledge of the phenomenology of galaxies, including the properties and characteristics of galaxies in the nearby and distant Universe, the physical interpretation of the various observations, and galaxy dynamics and the various timescales and dynamical processes that shape their formation and evolution. 
Intended Learning Outcomes. At the end of this unit, students will be able to 
1) remember and use the terminology of observations of galaxies and their environments,
2) discuss current research in the field of galaxies, using the language and reasoning at the level of a junior research scientist in the field,  
3) critically analyse current state-of-the-art research on the topic, 
4) present such research to an audience of junior researchers, 
5) derive the effect of environment on galaxy evolution giving reasonable assumptions on the system galaxy/halo, 
6) derive the relevant timescales for the evolution of galaxies and use that to analyse the growth of galaxy components and self-regulation using software and reasoning at the level of a junior research scientist, 
7) analyse data from the SDSS survey, using software and reasoning at the level of a junior research scientist.
8) understand the physics of stellar dynamics, in particular,  dynamical interaction of multiple components of galaxies, such as, stellar disks, central bulges, bars, spiral arms, and globular clusters
9) discuss the formation mechanisms of galaxies, such as galaxy merging, hydrodynamics in interstellar medium, and two body interaction in star clusters
10) try to resolve the unresolved problems of galaxies using simple analytic models of galaxy formation

Learning And Teaching Strategy

Unit will be entirely face-to-face: lectures, labs, student presentations and quizzes/exams are planned to take place face-to-face. Lectures, mathematical derivations and other material will be made available online as well. 

Unit Structure

Below there is a list and duration of the lectures, labs and assignments that make up this unit, split in each part of the unit.
Galaxies:
1) Lectures: 8 lectures of 2 hours duration each. These will take place from week 1 to 5 as per the list below:
- Lecture 1 (28/2/22): Galaxy classification schemes and photometric components
- Lecture 2 (4/3/33): Statistical properties of galaxies and Quiz#1
- Lecture 3 (11/3/22): Distance indicators and Quiz#2
- Lecture 4 (14/3/22): Galaxy clusters, groups and notion of environment and Quiz#3
- Lecture 5 (18/3/22): Environmental effects and Quiz#4
- Lecture 6 (21/3/22): Black holes and active galactic nuclei and Quiz#5
- Lecture 7 (25/3/22): Intergalactic matter, inflows and outflows and Quiz#6
- Lecture 8 (28/3/22): Chemical Evolution and Quiz#7
2) Teaching labs: 4 labs of 1 hour duration each. Two will be devoted to introducing the student to relevant software and databases required to complete assignments, and the other two to student presentations of assigned papers. These will take place during weeks 1, 2, 4 and 5, as listed below:
- Lab 1 (2/3/22): TOPCAT and SDSS tutorials
- Lab 2 (9/3/22): Paper #1 presentations
- Lab 3 (23/3/22): how to write astrobites-like summaries
- Lab 4 (29/3/22): Paper #2 presentations
3) Assignments: two assignments will be carried out by the student during this first half of the unit. Lab 1 will provide the student with the required technical skills to carry out the data analysis part of the assignments. These assignments are due during weeks 3 and 7. The assignments will be distributed as PDF via LMS and the required data to start the assignments will be provided in ASCII format. 
 
Galaxy Dynamics: 
1) Lectures:  The 1st lecture will be presented on 4th April (Monday).  Lectures will be presented on Monday and Friday in April (except for public holidays) and on Monday only in May. 
2) Presentations:  Each student needs to present a talk on the selected topic from the 7 ``unresolved problems". The specific problems and the date of presentation will be informed later.
3) Assignments:  There are two types of assignments. One is a basic theoretical problem and the other is the unresolved problem mentioned above. These are all items for marking.

Participation

Attendance will not be marked. However some assessed items, such as presentations and quizzes take place during lectures, and hence failure to attend may carry out failure to deliver assessment on time. Also, without attendance, students would not be able to solve some of the problems in the assignments (e.g.,  7 unresolved problems in Galactic Dynamics). Therefore, attendance is strongly encouraged.

Referencing Requirements

Students are expected to provide the references of the papers used in their presentations and assignments, following the standard in astronomy (Authors list, year, journal, pages, DOI). Widely used search engine of academic papers in astronomy are http://adsabs.harvard.edu/abstract_service.html and https://arxiv.org/

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
