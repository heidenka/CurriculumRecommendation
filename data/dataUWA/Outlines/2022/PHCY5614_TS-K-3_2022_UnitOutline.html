Learning And Teaching Strategy

The pharmacy research project is hypothesis-driven. Working with research partners under the guidance of a supervisor within a research team, you will gather the relevant background information from a literature evaluation, formulate a hypothesis, design the methodology, organise the resources, perform the study, organise and interpret the data, and communicate your findings in written and oral form for the allocated research project. You may have to conduct the study in a wet laboratory, or at pharmacy practice sites, such as hospitals, community pharmacies, nursing homes, etc. You are encouraged to show personal initiative and independence, and to discuss your input with your supervisor. Supervisors are generally receptive to new ideas and originality because it provides evidence that you have thought more deeply about the project. At the completion of data collection and analysis, you will defend your research to your peers, academic staff and other interested parties. In the assessment of this unit, we will be looking for evidence that you have gained insight into the process of solving a research problem from beginning to end.

Unit Structure

This unit requires you to plan, organise, execute and present a research project under the guidance of a supervisor. You will have to spend the equivalent of 4.5 days per week for 11 weeks to complete your research unit. How, when and where you spend the time to complete the research will depend on the requirements of the research and the directions of your supervisor. This unit requires you to work closely with your supervisor and research team members. The project framework will be established in consultation with your supervisor, but it will be up to you and your team members to develop and execute the research study on a day-to-day basis.

Participation

PHCY5614 PRP is based on block teaching. For 11 weeks, full time students are to commit 4.5 days per week (9 am to 4 pm, Mon to Fri) to the research. Part time students are to discuss your circumstances with group members and principal supervisor and agree on the days on which you will attend to the research in person with the group. 
You are required to attend to all the activities required for the successful execution of the research project. This includes formal and informal meetings with the research group and supervisors, execution of the study, and data analyses. If you are unable to attend, it must be due to an exceptional circumstance, and you must discuss the circumstance and obtain an approved leave of absence with the principal supervisor. 
For you are a full-time student, it is not acceptable to schedule paid/volunteer work or extracurricular activities during the PHCY5614 sessions in your timetable. 
We take a serious view on disruptive behaviour and/or failure to contribute that significantly impacts on the learning experience and academic outcome of team members in the group. Whether you are a full-time or part-time student, you can expect to be allocated a separate project and asked to submit a separate written research report if you persist in unacceptable behaviour, and this action has been taken in the past. 
Attendance at the workshop on writing your research report, the two coordinator’s meetings and the final research seminar is compulsory. Failure to meet the requirement of at least 80% attendance in these classes may result in a report of Professional Misconduct being made.

Ethical Scholarship, Academic Literacy and Academic Misconduct
Ethical scholarship entails the pursuit of scholarly enquiry marked by honesty and integrity. It is reflected both in individual and group approaches to study and assessment tasks, and is part of a broader institutional commitment to maintain and extend robust, defensible and transparent educational standards and practices.
Academic Literacy may be defined as the capacity to undertake study and research, and to communicate findings and knowledge, in a manner appropriate to the particular disciplinary conventions and scholarly standards expected at university level.
Academic Integrity or Academic Conduct means acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research.
Breach of Academic Conduct is any activity or practice engaged in by a student that violates explicit guidelines relating to the production of work for assessment, in a manner that compromises or defeats the purpose of that assessment. Any such activity undermines an ethos of ethical scholarship.
Levels of breaches of Academic Conduct relates to the severity of the breach and are defined as Minor Breach of Academic Conduct (Level 1); Moderate Breach of Academic Conduct (Level 2); and Major Breach of Academic Conduct (Level 3).
1.	Level 1: Minor Breach of Academic Conduct
Examples of minor breaches may include but are not limited to:
•	Minor plagiarism 
•	Minor copying of material 
•	Copying of answers to questions at the end of laboratory practicals.
2.	Level 2: Moderate Breach of Academic Conduct
Examples of moderate breaches may include but are not limited to:
•	Moderate plagiarism
•	Recycling an item of assessment
•	Fabricating or falsifying data
•	Colluding with another student about assessable work and representing that as individual work
3.	Level 3: Major Breach of Academic Conduct
Breaches are deemed MAJOR where the activity may be reasonably judged to be a serious and substantial breach of ethical scholarship and includes (but is not limited to):
•	Cheating in examinations
•	Major plagiarism
•	Fabricating or falsifying data
•	Submitting the work of another person as your own or undertaking work for assessment for another person (ghost writing)
Plagiarism 
Detection software is utilised at UWA to indicate potential breaches related to plagiarism. The following scale has been adopted for the purposes of preliminary classification of plagiarism:
•	Level 1 – less than 10% (minor)
•	Level 2 – 10 – 25% (moderate)
•	Level 3 – more than 25% (major)

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
