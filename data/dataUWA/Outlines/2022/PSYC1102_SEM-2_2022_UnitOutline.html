Learning And Teaching Strategy

As detailed below under Unit Structure, the unit involves five central learning components: lectures, laboratories, reading, assessments, and research participation. Each component is designed to complement the others.
1. Lectures are a chance to explore topics by listening to ideas, opinions, and stories from academics with expertise in a given topic. The lectures are also a chance to ask questions to test and extend your understanding.
2. Laboratories are designed to be immersive learning experiences. We want you to 'get your hands dirty'. This may be by engaging with the lab materials, both in-person and self-paced in the self-guided sessions. This may also be by sharing your ideas and understanding with peers. A major focus of the lab series is building a vibrant community for knowledge sharing and learning, so that you can maximise your learning, university experience, and personal development.
3. Readings are provided for self-paced exploration of topics. Some readings will provide alternate descriptions to those given in the lectures and laboratories, but predominantly provide the opportunity to expand and enrich your knowledge and understanding of a topic. As noted below under Unit Schedule, the chapter readings are listed under Preparation. You are encouraged to complete the readings in preparation for the lectures and laboratories. This will provide you with background to consolidate your knowledge, but also set you up to ask questions - an excellent opportunity to extend your learning.
4. Assessments come in two forms: Quizzes and the Exam. (1) The quizzes are regular knowledge checks for you to test your understanding and track your learning and study strategies. These quizzes are designed to encourage regular engagement with the material which is optimal for learning, and provide practice for the exam. (2) The exam is our final knowledge check to assess your learning in the unit. Exams tend to encourage people to revise and sharpen their understanding.
5. Research participation is designed to give you first-hand experience on what's happening behind the scenes when it comes to the research results and claims that make up the content of the unit. This should deepen your understanding of the literature and concepts. It's a chance for you to ask questions about the experiments, but also learn what a future in research might entail. In addition to the research, we encourage you to ask the researchers about their journey and experience.

Unit Structure

This unit involves five (5) central learning components: lectures, laboratories, reading, assessments, and research participation.
1. LECTURES
Lectures guide you through the major topics and deal with some of the more difficult conceptual issues. There are two face-to-face 45-minute lectures per week.
• Lectures are on Monday and Wednesday, at 10:00am-10:45am. Lectures will be held in the Octagon Theatre - these run all semester.
• Lectures are also recorded for later viewing. Lectures can also be downloaded: instructions on how to do this are provided in the Lecture Recordings area of LMS.
2. LABORATORIES
Laboratories introduce you to the major empirical approaches in psychological science, and will provide a forum for discussion (guided by your demonstrator).
• There are six (6) 1 hr and 45 min laboratory meetings (see timetable below).
• Remember to enrol in a laboratory group before Wednesday 5:00 pm (17:00) of Week 1. You enrol electronically, using the CAS system. You may change your laboratory allocation to another class with available seats up until 5:00 pm (17:00) on Friday of Week 3. If you change your allocation you must inform your new demonstrator about when your previous laboratory was. You must also remain in the same laboratory group throughout the semester. After Week 3, students are not permitted to swap between different laboratory classes without written permission from the unit coordinators.
• There are also five (5) self-guided laboratories available on LMS. These can be completed individually or in groups, but we do not meet formally for these labs (i.e., with a demonstrator).
3. READINGS
Readings are essential. The textbook has been chosen based on its intellectual standard, its coverage of material, and as it considers additional topics that we cannot cover in the lectures. Readings for this unit include (1) the assigned text book, (2) supplementary articles provided on LMS, and (3) periodicals/journal articles available from the library.
4. ASSESSMENTS
Assessments support engagement and retention of the information and concepts delivered in the unit, and allow us to estimate your learning.
Lab Report
The major course work assessment is a laboratory report. You will learn to write psychology lab reports in an online laboratory on LMS. You are also welcome to ask specific questions of your demonstrator or at the demonstrator-supported sessions. For this lab report you will collect and analyse data on a simple psychological experiment as part of the laboratory series. Further detail about the study will be provided after data collection. You will obtain a description of the purpose of the experiment, an overview of the method used, and help analysing the data in the laboratories.
The word count for the laboratory report is 1500 words, and your report must not exceed this word count. The lab report should follow conventional structure. The word count of 1500 excludes the Abstract (150 word limit), Reference List, Tables, Figures, Table and Figure Captions, and any Supplementary Materials. The word count given by a word processor is preferable; if counting words by hand, any character or character string preceded and followed by a space or a punctuation mark counts as a word. Markers will stop reading assignments at the word limit and will assign a mark as if the read portion constituted the entire assignment. Written work that exceeds the word limit will not receive credit or be given feedback.
Use American Psychological Association (APA) 7 formatting style for reference citation in the text and the list of references. The UWA library has a guide to APA style, which is available via a link on our unit LMS website.
You will have to read material in addition to your textbook to complete the lab report. Important references will be placed on LMS but additional references can be found in the library and we strongly recommend you read more widely! Laboratory reports will be submitted ONLINE to Turnitin plagiarism detection software. Turnitin will be opened 2 weeks prior to the deadline for you to check the originality of your draft and make any alterations. See Assessment Items below for the submission date and time. At this time, the most recent copy of your report uploaded to Turnitin will be accepted as your final submission. See the Lab Report Submission area of LMS for more information.
LMS Quizzes
Throughout the semester there are online quizzes available on LMS. These can be taken multiple times. Completion of each of these quizzes will help consolidate the content of each topic. Quizzes will be open throughout the semester and must be completed by 5:00 pm (17:00) Friday of Week 12. You will be covering the quiz content in the lectures and associated lab meetings and readings.
To receive full marks for the quizzes, you need to correctly answer one question per quiz.
Examination
The examination includes multiple-choice questions and takes place during the examination period at the end of semester. The exam is based on material presented in lectures, laboratories, and readings.
5. RESEARCH PARTICIPATION
A full and thorough understanding of psychological research can be optimally achieved if you experience each part of the research process. In future psychology units, you will get to experience how to analyse data, and how to design and execute experiments. In PSYC1102, you can experience what it is like to be a participant in psychological research. The School of Psychological Science is continually conducting research, much of which involves human participants. You may earn up to 8% of your final marks by collecting eight (8) experimental credits, which translates to approximately eight (8) hours of research participation across the semester.
Available experiments will be advertised through the ‘Research Participation’ area of LMS (also called SONA). You sign-up on-line for the experiments in which you wish to participate. Please note that we will accept experimental credits for PSYC1102 until 5:00 pm (17:00) on Friday of Week 12.
Please see our LMS page about how to login to SONA, and more information about the types of experiments that you can participate in.
If you do not wish to participate in experiments altogether, you can choose to submit a 1000 word essay on experimental methods to earn this 8% component of your final mark. You must inform the unit co-ordinator or lead demonstrators by the end of Week 3 of Semester 2 if you wish to choose the essay option (firstyear-sps@uwa.edu.au).

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "honest, ethical and responsible scholarship". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
