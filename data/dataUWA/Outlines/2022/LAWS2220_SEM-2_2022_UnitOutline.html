Unit Information

This unit examines the way the legal system interacts with issues relating to health care, medical research and medical technology. It explores the impact of social, economic, ethical and political influences on the law, and in particular how the law incorporates and responds to these influences. The unit is concerned with how society regulates sensitive and controversial issues associated health care and medical science, and examines how the law interacts with other regulatory methods in relation to these issues. It examines the legal understanding of human bodies, rights and duties associated with health care treatment, and decision-making associated with reproduction and dying. 

The unit is organised around the three themes of Birth, Life and Death. In each topic we will introduce the legal framework and consider the role of law and the way in which the law addresses challenging ethical and social issues. The unit will consider the impact of the law, the limits of legal regulation, and the way in which the law mediates competing interests and values, as well as issues of law reform.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

The teaching and learning strategies for this unit are based on active learning. 

Lectures will take place live on campus – students are encouraged to attend lectures in person if they are able to. Lectures will also be recorded and uploaded to the Lecture Capture System. Lectures will be supported by presentation slides and lecture outlines, which will be made available on the LMS.

Much of the learning in this unit will take place in weekly tutorials, which start in week 1. You will be allocated to a tutorial by the Class Allocation System. You must attend tutorials only for your allocated stream, day, and time. Tutorials will include discussion and activities based on set readings and questions. It is essential that students prepare for the tutorials by pre-reading and addressing the set questions, so that class discussion is focused and engaging. All of the tutors in this unit aim to create an environment in which students are comfortable to extend, explore and test their knowledge and views. We expect discussion to be conducted in a professional way that demonstrates respect for others, accepting that class members may hold quite different views on the subjects arising for discussion. We hope you carefully consider these different perspectives and that you will critique your own views and consider whether they are based on evidence and logic.

You will need to spend an average of 8-10 hours per week on this unit in order to meet the expectations and achieve the outcomes. This includes class time, reading and preparing for lectures and tutorials, reviewing unit materials, researching and writing the assignment, and studying for the final exam.

Unit Structure

Lectures: One 2-hour lecture per week, delivered live on campus each Monday (also recorded and uploaded on the Lecture Capture System). The lectures will introduce the legal framework and will raise social, ethical, economic and political issues relevant to the topic. Classes will include discussion around specific issues and questions, often based on a case study, video, or a reading which students are asked to view or read in advance of the lecture. Students are encouraged to attend the live lectures, then use the recordings only for revision purposes.
Tutorials: One 1-hour tutorial per week. Tutorial classes will be used to explore particular issues in depth. Discussion will be based on readings and questions for each tutorial. Students are required to attend tutorials on their allocated day, time, and stream (however, a weekly online option will be available for students who are symptomatic or otherwise need to isolate due to COVID-19).

Students are expected to participate in classes through active listening and respectful discussion. There is a 15% allocation of the unit's marks for tutorial participation. Students will be assessed on tutorial participation in relation to their best 10 tutorial participation marks.

Referencing Requirements

Students are expected to reference their assignment in compliance with the Australian Guide to Legal Citation 4 (2018). Footnotes are not included in the word count of the assignment, although footnotes must not be used for substantive comment, only for referencing purposes. More information about the referencing requirements for the assignment will be provided when the assignment is released.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "honest, ethical and responsible scholarship". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
