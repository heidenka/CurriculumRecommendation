Academic Information

Content This unit introduces advanced notions and applications of probability and random processes. A selection of topics from the following list are covered: time series, Markov processes, diffusion processes, Brownian motion, point processes, Ito calculus and stochastic differential equations and Martingales. Some of these topics require advanced probability theory which, if necessary, are covered first, and emphasise complex modelling suitable for many application areas (e.g. Black-Scholes option pricing theory in finance) and involves extensive numerical computation.

Unit Information

Fundamental aim of the unit is to introduce you to the mathematics used for   probability modelling of  financial concepts and finding the  fair price of financial products such as options and bonds. 
The subject matter, if treated carefully, involves some very technical mathematics. The approach will be to treat the simpler material fairly carefully and to leave out much of the detail and technicalities of deeper material. A principal aim is to impart a sound intuitive grasp of key concepts and facility with using them in calculation. Doing this will require reinforcement of basic concepts from calculus. 
Assumed knowledge:  You need to understand basic analytic concepts based on limit operations: Limits of functions and sequences, convergence of sequences and series, continuity and differentiability of functions, and the Riemann integral defined as the limit of approximating sums. A non-examinable Chapter 0 will summarize what's required.

Texts: There here is no text covering all of the above topics at just the right level, but there are many texts which cover parts of the syllabus.  Most of the stochastic calculus part  follows the sections in 
C1. T. Mikosch (1998), Elementary Stochastic Calculus with Finance in View. 
The following selection of interesting books  are   segregated into the broad areas which this unit intersects. Many reside in the BJM library as well as many more relevant titles; use OneSearch to find them (Some of them could be  in  external storage sites of the library) 
A. Background & History:
A1. P. Bernstein (1992), Capital Ideas. (A general history of modern finance.) 
A2. P. Bernstein (1996). Against the Gods: The Remarkable Story of Risk.
A3. E. Derman (2004), My life as a quant : reflections on physics and finance.
A4. N. Dunbar (2000), Inventing Money.
A5. R. Lindsey \& B. Schachter (eds.) (2007),  How I became a quant : insights from 25 of Wall Street's elite.
A6. R. Lowenstein (2001),  When Genius Failed. (Dunbar \& Lowenstein relate the financial collapse of "Long Term Capital Management", a hedge fund backed by Merton and Scholes.)
A7. P.G. Zhang (1995), Barings Bankruptcy & Financial Derivatives. (A bit technical in parts.)
B. Probability and stochastic processes:
B1. D.R. Cox & H.D. Miller (1965), The Theory of Stochastic Processes. (Chapters 1 \& 5.)
B2. G. Grimmett & D. Stirzaker (1993), Probability \& Random Processes. (Chapters 7, 8 \& 13.)
B3. S. Karlin & H.W. Taylor (1975), A First Course in Stochastic Processes, 2nd. ed.}(Chapters 1 \& 7.) 
C. Stochastic calculus & diffusion processes:
C1. T. Mikosch (1998), Elementary Stochastic Calculus with Finance in View. 
C2. S. Karlin & H.W. Taylor (1981), A Second Course in Stochastic Processes}, (Chapter 15, a text in itself.)
C3. L. Arnold (1974), Stochastic Differential Equations. 
C4. M. Baxter  A. Rennie (1996), Financial Calculus.
C5. S. Shreve (2004), Stochastic calculus for finance II: Continuous-time models. (A careful and thorough treatment, recommended by some as the `one book you should read'. Once used by the Business School for its honours finance unit, a  very technical account based on measure-theoretic probability.)
C6. P. Wilmott, S. Howison & J. Dewynne (1995), The Mathematics of Financial Derivatives.
(The last three are written by mathematicians about the application of stochastic calculus to finance.  Many similar titles are in the library, but be aware of their enormously varying technical level.)
D. Finance:
  D1. E.F. Fama \& M.H. Miller (1972), The Theory of Finance.} (Material on interest rate models in  Section II.A.5, II.B.1-4.)
D2. J.C. Hull, (1993+), Options, Futures and other Derivative Securities. (A widely used fairly quantitative text. It describes the operation of futures and options exchanges, many types of derivative securities, and provides a low level introduction to stochastic calculus.)
D3. O. de La Grandville (2000), Bond Pricing \& Portfolio Analysis. (A thorough quantitative treatment of its topics but written in a very friendly style by an economist. Good chapters on interest rate models.)
D4. D.G. Luenberger (1998), Investment Science.}(An excellent ``applied maths'' level treatment. Once used for an honours level unit.
 D5. A.G. Malliaris \& W.A. Brock (1982), Stochastic Methods in Economics & Finance. (Chapters 1,2 & 4). A text on stochastic calculus and its application to finance and economics written by an economist.)
D6. D. Ruppert (2004),  Statistics and finance : an introduction.}(Chapters 3, 8 & 9; A very readable text, once used for an honours finance unit.)
D7. J. Stampfli \& V. Goodman (2001)  The Mathematics of Finance: Modeling and Hedging. (Highly recommended for  finance topics, but not so good for stochastic calculus.)

E. Martingales:

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
