Teaching contingency plan (In response to Covid19 restrictions)

All recorded lectures are available online and can be watched at a time and location convenient to the student.

If face-to-face lab sessions are not possible because of Covid19 restrictions, all lab sessions will be moved online.

If students are unwell, they can miss the lab session without the need to apply for special consideration. But students should keep up with the progress of the unit by submitting any required assessments online. If they have difficulty submitting on time, they should let the coordinator know and seek their advice on whether they need to apply for special considerations.

If the instructor for the unit is unwell and cannot teach, then the material will be taught by other staff members.

Academic Information

Content The unit teaches through four modules, reviewing four major themes in science— oceans, the marine environment, and pollution; minerals, soil processes, and land rehabilitation; population processes and disease spread; and sustainable food production. Students learn from the perspective of practising analytical scientist, and are involved in: primary data acquisition; formal analytical model development and specification; using computational approaches to solve analytical problems; and the communication of formal analytical results to a scientific audience. The overall unit focus is on using modern computational methods to specify and then solve analytical problems, and then presenting the results of formal analysis clearly and concisely.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

Lectures are provided as video recordings online. Most lab sessions are interactive, most are face-to-face and happen in computer labs, but at least one lab session is online. 
The lab sessions will be used to discuss concepts and questions related to lecture material and demonstrate implementation of analytical methods using computer software. The lab sessions allow students to practice in an environment where they can interact with each other and with the instructor. 
For a successful learning experience, students need to: 
(1) Make effective use of learning resources provided (recorded videos, provided readings or notes, etc.); 
(2) Come prepared to the lab sessions by doing all required prior reading and preparation; 
(3) Do ALL lab and any homework or take-home exercises; 
(4) Participate in discussions during the lab and through the online discussion boards; 
(5) Seek help by posting questions online in the discussion board or by visiting instructors; 
(6) Invest in software skills early on so that you can enjoy the benefits throughout the semester; and 
(7) Make use of all channels available for learning: tutorials, recorded lectures, discussion boards, and internet resources.

Unit Structure

Each module in the unit is roughly 3 weeks long. The scientific areas of interest are compartmentalized into modules. The same or similar analytical methods can appear in different modules, with different degrees of emphasis. 
Each week, students need to attend only one 2-hour long lab session.
Recorded lectures for each week will appear mostly as a series of short video recording, e.g.six 15 minute long. Some of these short lectures will be on the scientific topic area (context) and the rest will be on analytical methods relevant for those topic areas. 
The teaching resources provided for each week will include journal articles provided as reading materials or information sources. In some cases, these journal articles are easy to follow even for first year students. In other cases, they will be too technical or too advanced and you are not required to understand all the details. However, if an advanced or difficult journal article is included in the folder provided for the week, it simply means either your instructors want you to read the accessible parts of it (usually the front part) for some useful information, or it means they want you to be aware where a particular analytical model or data set comes from.

Participation

Preparation activities (before the lab): Each week you are required to listen to recorded lectures, complete any required readings and attempt any questions provided in the LMS (learning management system) to help you understand concepts or improve your analytical skills. 
Computer lab exercises: Each week there are a set of computer exercises for you to complete. The default option for you is to complete the exercises during your scheduled computer lab session, or in the hours following the session. Once you complete the exercises, you need to upload some aspect of your computer lab work to the LMS or provide answers to related questions online to prove that you have completed the work. The lab instructions will be clear on what you exactly need to do to complete your exercises for the week and demonstrated that you have completed the task. So, follow those instructions.

Other

The unit uses Excel and Python software for lab work and other computer based exercises. There will be a transition period where students will use primarily Excel or Excel followed by Python to do exercises because students are not expected to have prior knowledge of Python. 
It will become clear in the first few weeks, why we decided to teach using a programming software rather than a software application such as Excel where you work (clicking) through all the steps (choosing data, calculating values, inserting charts, etc.) to get the job done, e.g. to plot plastic production against year of production. Programmed software is more efficient once you get over the learning curve and, usually, provides better quality products. And once you learn one programming language, it is easier to learn others as you progress. For example, some statistics units use R software for teaching.
Both quizzes are scheduled for 7 to 9 pm on Fridays to avoid clashes with other activities. Quiz 1 covers the first five weeks of teaching. Quiz 2 the next six weeks (weeks 6 to 11). 
Assignments are due at mid day on Fridays, so that you can seek assistance from learning services if you have problems uploading your assignment.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
