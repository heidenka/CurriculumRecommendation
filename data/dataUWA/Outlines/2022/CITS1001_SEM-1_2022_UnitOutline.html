Academic Information

Content This unit introduces the language structures and techniques needed to write well-structured programs in the object-oriented paradigm using the Java programming language. In particular, the process of developing appropriate classes, objects and methods to solve simple computational problems underlies the entire unit. Core computer programming topics such as the use of variables, primitive and reference data types, expressions, control structures involving selection and repetition, method decomposition and parameter passing are all covered in this context. Algorithmic techniques such as iteration, sorting, searching along with programming practices such as error handling, testing, debugging and documentation are introduced. The unit also covers advanced topics such as association, inheritance and interface. A strong focus is placed on the practical application of these concepts and techniques to produce working programs in computer laboratories. The rationale for using the object-oriented paradigm, and in particular the language Java, is covered in detail. No prior knowledge of computing or programming is assumed.

Unit Information

CITS1001 is a first programming unit that teaches the fundamentals of software engineering in the context of the object-oriented language Java.
No prior knowledge of computing or programming is assumed. However, the unit is designed primarily for students who are planning to major in Computer Science. Students taking programming as a broadening option are encouraged to consider instead the unit CITS1401 Computational Thinking with Python.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

CITS1001 is delivered through a combination of lectures, workshops, and practical laboratories.
Lectures will discuss ideas and concepts in software engineering and programming, and will illustrate these concepts through examples. All lectures will be live-streamed, and both F2F and online students will be able to ask questions.
Labs will involve practical programming exercises which allow students to test their ability to construct solutions to small problems in Java. Support will be provided in lab sessions, and students are encouraged to complete any outstanding exercises outside formal contact time. Solutions will be provided for all lab exercises in due course; where you find difficulty completing an exercise, study the solution carefully and ask questions as appropriate. Learning to read and understand programs is also an important skill! 
Workshops will not introduce any new material; questions will be invited, and lecture and lab material will be reviewed as requested by attendees.
The fastest way to get support in the unit outside these sessions is online. The procedure for asking questions online will be introduced in the first lecture. 

Unit Structure

A typical week in CITS1001 comprises five hours of contact: two lectures, one workshop, and a 2-hour supervised lab session. Students are welcome to attend multiple lab sessions and/or workshops if that would help their progress. 
Students are expected to spend time outside these sessions to review lecture material, to complete any unfinished lab exercises, to read texts and/or other outside sources, and to seek help as needed.

Participation

No rules are enforced about attendance in formal contact sessions, except for the mid-semester test and the end-of-semester exam. 
However, past experience shows that students who attend regularly, who engage with the sessions, and (especially!) who ask questions as soon as issues arise, are more likely to succeed in CITS1001.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
