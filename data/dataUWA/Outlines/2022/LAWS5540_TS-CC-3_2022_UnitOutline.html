Academic Information

Content This unit examines key concepts in environmental protection law in Western Australia, in its national and international contexts. It focuses on the law relating to pollution control and environmental impact assessment. The unit analyses a range of legal techniques and principles applied to environmental protection through six key themes: (1) the key definitions, institutions and objectives, (2) policy and information instruments, (3) controls on particular activities, (4) environmental impact assessment, (5) criminal liabilities, and (6) civil liabilities.

Unit Information

This unit examines key concepts in environmental law in Western Australia, in its national and international contexts.  It focuses on the law relating to pollution control and environmental impact assessment, including an introduction to the new regulatory challenges of reducing greenhouse gas emissions.  The unit analyses a range of legal techniques and principles applied to environmental protection through six key themes or topics: (1) the key definitions, institutions and objectives, (2) policy and information instruments, (3) controls on particular activities, (4) environmental impact assessment, (5) criminal liabilities, and (6) civil liabilities.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

The learning and teaching strategy has three stages, which have evolved from our experience of COVID: (1) from 1 August to 5 September, pre-class guided reading and pre-recorded lectures covering core concepts relating to each of the six topics, and two small assessment exercises; (2) from 6 to 8 September, in-class interactive face-to-face sessions with guest presenters and class discussion covering each of the six topics; and (3) from 9 September, six weeks to research and write an essay on a topic of your choice either suggested or approved by the unit coordinator.  In 2022, 'pre-class' refers to the learning activities before the in-class interactive sessions on 6-8 September. 
Here is a more detailed outline of each of those three stages. 
(1) 1 August to 5 September    The reading guides and assessment exercises will be available on the LMS site from 1 August. We assume no prior knowledge of Environmental Protection Law, so it is invaluable for you to read some core materials, including important legislation and cases, and to learn about central concepts (such as 'emission', 'pollution', 'environmental harm' and the principles of 'ecologically sustainable development'). The pre-recorded lectures will follow the reading guides, and you should read selectively, according to your interest in the releavnt material and sufficiently to enable you to complete the pre-class assessment exercises. The pre-class assessment exercises will focus on some key materials and concepts. 
(2) 6 to 8 September   The in-class interactive sessions will be led by Alex Gardner and explore key issues of contemporary importance and development or reform across each of the six topics, with guest expert presentations providing legal professional, government, industry and community insights. The morning sessions will run from 9.30am - 12.00pm and the afternoon sessions will run from 2.00 - 4.30pm, leaving time each day to prepare for and reflect on the sessions. The program for the six interactive sessions will be available on LMS. There will also be private and public sector non-degree registrants participating in the interactive sessions. They will register separately via an Eventbrite page (https://www.eventbrite.com.au/e/environmental-protection-law-tickets-353526064977) and often bring to the discussions unique practical experience and understanding of the legislation. Degree candidates are automatically enrolled for the interactive sessions by enrolment in this unit. 
(3) 9 September to 24 October    The post-class research essay is the opportunity for you to build on the knowledge gained through stages (1) and (2) by selecting a problem or question that you wish to research.  You will be asked to submit a research proposal within 10 days after stage 2 finishes (by 18 September). In light of the feedback on that proposal by Monday 26 September, you should complete and submit your research paper by the due date, 24 October (see assessment information below). In proposing and preparing your research essay, you are encouraged to aim for publication following completion of the unit assessment. Publication options can be discussed with the unit coordinator after completion of the assessment. 
NOTE  There will be continuing professional development points available for participation in each of the interactive sessions addressing the six topics. 

Unit Structure

The unit is structured in three parts around the three key elements of Environmental Protection Law and the six topic themes. 
Part 1 - Environmental Governance: covering Topic 1 on the key definitions, institutions and objectives of Environmental Protection Law; 
Part 2 - Environmental Regulation: covering Topic 2 on the policy and information instruments for managing cumulative environmental harm, Topic 3 on controls on particular activities causing pollution and environmental harm, and Topic 4 on environmental impact assessment; and 
Part 3 - Environmental Liabilities: covering Topic 5 on criminal liabilities and Topic 6 on civil liabilities for causing pollution and environmental harm.

Participation

Attendance and participation in all of the Stage 2 interactive face to face sessions is mandatory for all degree candidates.
A degree candidate may apply to the Unit Coordinator for exemption from attendance at any session for documented medical reasons or other exceptional circumstances.

Referencing Requirements

There is a strong preference for you to apply the Australian Guide to Legal Citation, edition 4. There is a succinct summary of the AGLC4 provided by the UWA Library: https://guides.library.uwa.edu.au/AGLC4 .
If you believe that you cannot master the AGLC4 footnoting and referencing techniques, you may use the Harvard system of in-text references and bibliography so long as you give a pinpoint page or paragraph citation for the sources of propositions referred to or quoted in your case note or research essay.

Essential Textbooks

There are no essential textbooks set for this unit. There may be essential and recommended readings set for this unit available in Unit Readings in the LMS.

Other

Some students will find it useful to refer to G Bates et al, Environmental Law in Australia, 10th ed, 2019. It is not an essential textbook prescribed for this unit.
The Reading Guides contain hyperlinks to the legislation and most cases referred to. 
The main legislation we study is the Environmental Protection Act 1986 (WA), Environmental Protection Regulations 1987 (WA), Contaminated Sites Act 2003 (WA), Environment Protection and Biodiversity Conservation Act 1999 (Cth), and National Environment Protection Council Act 1994 (Cth). All of this legislation is conveniently available on public websites. 
Case law is available on both public platforms (such as Austlii) and private (proprietary) platforms of legal publishing services accessible through the UWA Library cataglogue. Some of the older cases are included in a unit reader chapters designed for the unit and available through the LMS block for each topic. The unit reader has not been updated since 2012. There are also hyperlinks to most articles referred to, some of which are accessible from open access journals and some from private journal publications accessible by the Library catalogue.


Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "honest, ethical and responsible scholarship". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
