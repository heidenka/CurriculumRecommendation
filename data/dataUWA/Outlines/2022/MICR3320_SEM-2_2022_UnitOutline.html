Academic Information

Content This unit builds on the basic concepts of virology taught at Level 2. It focuses on human and animal viruses associated with disease. The lecture topics which are taught in the unit include features of virus structure; genetics and cultivation; viral taxonomy; the main viral diseases of humans; the replication cycle of the RNA and DNA viruses; genetically controlled interactions between viruses and the host; and the host immune response and the mechanisms by which certain viruses cause disease. The unit also discusses the emergence of new viral diseases, the role of insect vectors and the re-emergence of existing viral diseases together with the surveillance measures which are used to track the movement of viruses locally and globally. The control of viral diseases including the use of vaccines, antiviral drugs and other measures are also presented.

Unit Information

MICR3320 Viruses and Viral Diseases  focuses on human and animal viruses associated with human disease and builds on the basic concepts of Virology taught in MICR2209. 
The main objectives are to familiarise students with: virus structure and taxonomy; isolation and detection methods; viral gene expression and replication; virus-host interactions; viral disease pathogenesis; prevention and control strategies including anti-viral compounds and vaccines; surveillance and public health responses to viral disease outbreaks and epidemics. 
The practical component provides hands-on experience in techniques currently used in diagnostic virology and in research, including cell culture and PCR. In these practical sessions students will learn aseptic techniques and gain experience in culturing cells and safely handling replicating viruses. 
Workshops cover a range of topics including  immunological and molecular methods for quantifying virus;  phylogenetic analysis of virus nucleotide  sequence data; and case studies illustrating diagnosis and treatment of viral infections.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

This unit is taught through a series of lectures and supporting laboratory and workshop/tutorial sessions. To make the most of this unit you will need to undertake any pre-reading provided for the lectures, laboratories and workshops, as well as attend or listen to the lectures via the Lecture Capture System each week.
Lecture attendance in this unit is strongly encouraged and is an invaluable opportunity for networking. We appreciate the difficulties that are being experienced by students as a result of the COVID-19 pandemic. To ensure all students have access to lecture content, all lectures presented in this unit will be recorded. Lecture notes will be available at least 24 hours prior to each lecture and students are responsible for printing their own copy, if desired. It is recommended that students do not rely on the lecture slides as the sole learning resource – please read associated textbook chapters to enrich and enhance your understanding of the content. If possible, we strongly recommend that you attend all lectures in the unit and use the lecture capture system as a recall tool.
Laboratory attendance is compulsory – missed classes will require special consideration approval. Suitable attire (laboratory coat provided), CLOSED shoes and long hair tied back) must be worn to all lab sessions. You are required to keep accurate lab notes during all lab sessions – lab classes are examinable. A laboratory manual including space for note-taking will be provided. Please always act in a sensible fashion when undertaking any experimental procedure. Handle all chemicals and infectious samples with care, and wear gloves and other personal protective equipment supplied. PLEASE BE ON TIME, if you miss the safety information at the beginning of the session you may not be permitted to complete the class. The lab safety induction module MUST be completed before entering the laboratory.
 Workshops are linked to learning outcomes and are compulsory, and examinable – missed classes will require special consideration approval. Workshops provide an opportunity for students to explore concepts in more detail. Academic staff and demonstrators will be available during face-to-face sessions to provide support to students. The workshops are more skills based – data analysis and interpretation – and provide context for the lectures. Please complete all pre-readings and preparations to ensure that you are ready for class. Please bring a mobile device (tablet, laptop), if you have one.
Tutorials are review where all previous lecture, lab and workshop content will be reviewed, and where ongoing progress with the group project is monitored. Attendance is not compulsory but is highly recommended.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
