Academic Information

Content This unit transfers qualitative and quantitative research skills that are essential core competencies for all archaeologists. Topics include: radiometric dating techniques, sampling strategies, geoarchaeological methods, statistics and quantitative research methods, qualitative methods and archival analysis, archaeozoological, botanical and lithic analyses, interview techniques, principles of data management, and lab safety. Experts in the field explain and demonstrate the latest archaeological research methods and participants gain competency in these methods.

Unit Information

Advanced Methods in Archaeology will develop competencies in a range of methods used by archaeologists working in multidisciplinary environments in Australia and more broadly. Each lecture and workshop will provide a brief theoretical background to the archaeological problems and questions that will be addressed (e.g. how to reconstruct people's diet) and then the specific methods and their quantitative and qualitative approaches will be outlined. The course covers topics which will be universal at most site-types in Australia and the Indo-Pacific including a) How did the site form through time? b) How can we date it? c) What plants and animals did people subsist on and where did they collect them from? d) How do we interpret stone tools and their by-products which were used to extract energy or make tools? e) How does symbolic practice such as rock art fit in with economic behaviour such as subsistence, and f) How do we compare open site information, say from artefact scatters, with assemblages recovered and dated from sites covering thousands of years? Structured workshops will allow students to gain competencies and explore the unique insights offered by different methods and material categories. 
Modern archaeology incorporates 2M years of time, has different sub-fields including historic, maritime and Indigenous archaeology, and employs different spatial scales ranging from a single feature, to site complexes through to regional records. Each lecture will discuss the process of studying archaeology at different temporal and spatial scales and emphasise how this can produce different behavioural interpretations.   
Advanced Methods in Archaeology is taught over 6 weeks, beginning in Week 1 of Semester 1. Students must prepare for the Week 1 lecture and workshop. The intensive mode delivery includes a 1 hour lecture by a topic specialist followed by 2 hours of guided workshop exercises. These will build on the required readings, the specialist lecture and guidance during the workshop. The workshops involve discussion of the literature, learning by identifying and addressing problems, the handling of archaeological objects and data, their analysis and synthesis, and discussion of interpretations. A short quiz based on the required readings will be completed before each Workshop. 
Students are required to complete weekly online quizzes, an exercise in the seminar, and to write a fully referenced critical analysis (2000-2500 words) on one or more methods relevant to their proposed Honours research.  There are no exams.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

The format of each session is a one hour lecture followed by class exercises set by the topic specialist. Key issues and research themes will be covered in the lectures, followed by a detailed exposition of the advanced methods use to interpret archaeological sites, their formation and their cultural assemblages including stone artefacts, faunal and botanical remains. There will also be coverage of how these different data sets can be integrated and then interpreted. Both the strengths and limitations of each method will be discussed by the topic specialists.    
A critical discussion of the required readings, case studies and analysis of the samples/collections in the workshops will lead to a) improved understandings of the strengths and limitations of different methods and analyses applied to the matrix and cultural assemblages of sites; b) a more informed approach about what components of a site might yield most information about past lifeways depending on content and preservation, c) competencies in core areas of archaeological investigation, and with special reference to Indigenous and historic archaeology in Australia, and d) an enhanced understanding of new and cutting-edge methods which can revolutionise the way we find, study and interpret archaeological sites.   
Some of these methods may be used by the students in their own Honours research.

Unit Structure

This Unit comprises a 1 hour lecture followed by a 2 hour workshop and covers 6 advanced methods and associated archaeological analysis of different kinds of sites and material categories. They represent some of the core archaeological competencies needed to work in Australia and the Indo-Pacific. There are other competencies such as advanced spatial mapping and advanced statistics not covered here, however the collection and understanding of datasets will be included. At the start there will be a short introductory session (10 mins) by the Unit Coordinator outlining the need for, and benefits accruing from, competency in these advanced methods, the need to have a clear problem identified and the ethics of archaeological research and engagement with communities of interest, including Traditional Owners and Custodians. Each week there will be a quiz associated with the reading material each worth 2% (2% x 6 = 12%), and an in-class workshop designed to be either individually or as a group each worth 8% (8% x 6 = 48%).  At least an hour each week will be provided in-class to complete the assessment for each week.  The final assessment (worth 40%) will be a review of one or more advanced methods relevant to your own Honours topic. 
This unit is designed to be helpful, informative and sometimes challenging but ultimately aimed at aiding the process of thesis preparation and writing.  As an Honours unit, you will be expected to perform in-class and produce some quality work.
The topics for the six classes are: 
1) Geoarchaeology; soils and sediments, different kinds of dating, chrono-stratigraphy, micromorphology and site formation 
2) Archaeobotany / anthracology; the role of plant remains in reconstructing behaviour, diet and palaeoenvironment, fuel preferences  
3) Rock Art; style and form, regionalism, interpretive frameworks, dating and the archaeology of symbolic behaviour and management  
4) Archaeofaunal studies; abundance, prey choice, taphonomy, seasonality and palaeoenvironmental reconstruction 
5) Lithics on the landscape; statistical, sourcing and spatial approaches to open sites, reduction strategies and transport from quarries 
6) Lithics in stratified contexts; reconstructing behaviour from assemblages including mobility, technological innovation and regional adaptation

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.
