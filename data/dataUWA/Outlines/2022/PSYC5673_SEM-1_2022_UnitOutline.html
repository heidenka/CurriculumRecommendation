Academic Information

Content Clinical psychologists and neuropsychologists work with people who experience a range of mental health and emotional well-being difficulties. This unit aims to provide a foundational conceptual framework for the assessment and conceptualisation of common mental health, behavioral and/ or neurodevelopmental difficulties in children/ young people and adults. Students are introduced to psychological assessment and the diagnostic process (using DSM-5 and ICD-10), clinical interviewing and observation methods (e.g., history taking and conducting a mental status examination) and psychometric instruments appropriate for the measurement and monitoring of a wide range of psychopathology in both children and adults. Students also learn how to organise clinical information into a case formulation to guide intervention selection and delivery. Consideration is given to comorbidity and cultural diversity and how these factors may impact clinical assessment and formulation, and clinical practice more broadly.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

This is an Foundational/ Introductory unit for clinical psychologists and clinical neuropsychologists in training. The weekly classes are seminar-style, involving a range of teaching and learning strategies, including didactic teaching, small group and whole class discussions or activities focusing on application of knowledge in clinical work (i.e. case studies, videos, role plays). The classes will be interactive, and students are expected to contribute as well as reflect on the discussions and others' reflections during class. Other learning strategies include readings, assignments (all of which are applied to encourage development of scientist-practitioner thinking and application of new knowledge in case work). A practical/ experiential assignment is also included (psychometric administration video role play), as this is a professional training unit and therefore students are expected to gain knowledge and practical experience.

Participation

Students are expected to attend ALL classes and workshops and must inform the unit co-ordinators if due to extenuating circumstances they are unable to attend a session. 
It is important that students are punctual and attend all coursework and other specified training activities unless extenuating circumstances (such as illness or significant personal crisis) prevent them from doing so. Where students cannot attend (or have not attended) the full required activities or classes, they are expected to notify the unit coordinator to provide an adequate explanation and documentation (such as a medical certificate in the event of illness) explaining their absence or lateness. A failure to attend without adequate explanation and documented evidence of illness or other impeding factors beyond one’s personal control will result in failing the unit in which the class/activity was missed. Note that even where an adequate explanation and documented evidence are supplied, a student will still fail the unit if he/she has not attended a sufficient proportion of the unit. A “sufficient proportion of the unit” is normally defined as at least 80%; in recognition that 2022 might have impacts on students’ wellness with respect to COVID-19, this will be defined as at least 75% for this year only.  
We strongly encourage active participation during the classes through asking questions, participating in small and larger group discussions and contributing to case work discussions, role plays or other related activities.
