Academic Information

Content Humanity has been extracting marine and aquatic food resources for tens of thousands of years by means of fishing, and these days fishing is the single biggest human impact factor on marine animal populations and marine ecosystems globally, followed by climate change and plastic pollution. The impacts of fishing, be it commercial, recreational or for subsistence, can be felt and seen everywhere in the marine environment, from our coastal systems to the global high seas. Fisheries and their impacts are also a major driving force in the global economy, with seafood now being the most widely traded food commodity in the world. Fisheries also have substantial social implications, ranging from increasing poverty and human migrations in West Africa to the emergence of modern piracy in Somalia and elsewhere. This unit will provide students with a solid grounding in the foundations of fisheries science, and identify features, methods and principles of fisheries science used to inform policy and management of both large and small-scale fisheries around the world. In addition to the scientific foundations and principles around fisheries science, this unit will also provide and discuss a range of applied examples from both global and local West Australian fisheries. Core elements will include the foundation principles of fisheries science, the core data needs in fisheries science, introduction to methods used in assessing fish populations, and their application to fisheries data sets at different scales. Finally, clear links will be made to the growing need to align fisheries science with conservation science and strategies and policies, in order to ensure sustainability of fisheries resources for future generations.

Unit basics
Humanity has been extracting marine and aquatic food resources on virtually every coast and in every country for tens of thousands of years by means of fishing, and thereby we have actively driven a large range of changes in the ocean ecosystems. Thus, fishing is the single biggest human impact factor on marine animal populations and marine ecosystems globally, followed more recently by climate change and plastic pollution. The impacts of fishing, be they for commercial, recreational or subsistence purposes, can be felt and seen everywhere in the marine environment, from our coastal systems to the global high seas and the deep oceans. Fisheries and their impacts are also a major driving force in the global economy, with seafood being the most widely traded food commodity in the world. Fisheries also have substantial social implications, ranging from growing social inequity, poverty, food- and nutritional-security, and human migrations in West Africa to the emergence of modern piracy in Somalia and elsewhere. 
This unit will provide students with a broad grounding in the foundations of fisheries science, and identify features, methods and principles of fisheries science used to inform policy and management of both large-scale and small-scale fisheries around the world. In addition to the scientific foundations and principles around fisheries science, this unit will also discuss a range of applied examples from both global and local Western Australian fisheries that highlight spatial and regional differences in management priorities and capacity. Core elements will include the foundation principles of fisheries science, the core data needs in fisheries science, an introduction to methods used in assessing fish populations, and the application of such methods to fisheries data sets at different scales. Finally, clear links will be made to the growing need to align fisheries science with conservation science and strategies and policies to ensure the sustainability of fisheries resources for future generations.

Lab/practical report:
A concise and clear 2000 word (+/- 10%) report (not counting title, author, reference list, tables and figure captions) in a scientific paper format will be required from each student. Each student’s individual report will contain data exploration and evaluation of the fisheries data and associated socio-economic patterns back to 1950 for a specific country being assigned to each student. The associated introduction and discussion embeds this in the context of that specific country as well as the geographic and socio-economic region, and the broader global fisheries context as explored in this unit. Early in the semester, each student will be assigned a specific unique country for his/her lab report by the teaching staff. This is not a group report, even if teamwork by geographical country clusters may take place during lab sessions. If you have a specific personal association with a given country, please discuss this as an option with me during the first lab or after the first week’s lectures.  Submission of reports is via LMS Turnitin by the due date. The lab report is likely due in week 9, two weeks after the mid-semester study break. Details to be advised.
Each report requires the following components (approximate word counts are indicated):
Title: A fully self-explanatory title of around 10-20 words
Author: Student’s name and student number, Unit number
Abstract: No more than 200 words, no references to be cited in Abstract
Introduction: Around 300 words
Methods: Around 300 words
Results: Around 400 words, generally should not contain any references as this is your work, data and analysis. 
Discussion: Around 800 words
References: Complete list of all references cited in the text and tables, as well as table and figure captions. Referencing “style” to be used is for the journal called “Marine and Freshwater Research”, and citation and reference formatting should be consistent with that journal style, and the reference list entry needs to be complete enough for a Google and/or library search to find the exact reference, including volume and page numbers, and where available the DOI number.
Tables and Figures to be embedded within the text, in sequence of first citation. Tables and figures to be sized to present the point being made, but not use up unnecessary space. Each table and figure needs to be cited at least once in either Introduction, Methods and/or Results. Tables and Figures should be placed as close as possible to, but ideally after first citing in text, and each should have a comprehensive, self-explanatory caption. Table captions are integrated into each table as the first row of that table. Figure captions are stand-alone, comprehensive and fully self-explanatory texts below each figure. Tables and figures are numbered consecutively, but independently, i.e., Table 1, Table 2 etc.; Figure 1, Figure 2 etc.
Please use “i.e.” versus “e.g.” in a grammatically correct manner.
Labs during the first half of the unit focus heavily on the data exploration and aspects of the lab report assignment, and much of your lab report data exploration and analysis, material, content and even report writing can be generated by you in the lab sessions during which staff and demonstrator support is available. No support will be provided for lab report preparation or data examination outside of the designated lab time slots, so come prepared to each lab, and maximize your time use during each lab. Do not leave it to the last 1 or 2 lab sessions to ask for help, as that may be too late!

Referencing:
Proper referencing of someone else’s work, findings, thoughts and writings is paramount in science, and is the only ethical, professional way to use, re-use and mention someone else’s work. Direct quotes should be avoided entirely in biological sciences, although they are common in social sciences. In the extremely rare cases where direct quotes are acceptable in biological writing (you are unlikely to encounter this in your writing in this unit), direct quotes always need to be bracketed, e.g., “this text is a quote” (Zeller, 2021), and also need to be referenced. Instead, in the natural sciences, we paraphrase someone else’s findings or thoughts, i.e., we re-write what they say or found in our own words. However, paraphrasing also needs a citation showing where you got the thought, finding or idea from.
Do not plagiarize, as this is cheating and unethical, and will be heavily penalized. Any evidence of plagiarism in your writing will be immediately forwarded to the relevant university committee for consequences and penalization. 
I encourage you to read and digest the relevant UWA resources, e.g.:
https://guides.library.uwa.edu.au/referencinguwa  
I strongly recommend you use a referencing software to avoid mistakes in citations and referencing. My preferred software is EndNote, which is also supported by UWA. For example, your lab report should include EndNote references (or another citation software of your choice such as Mendeley), both as citation inserts in the text, as well as the auto-assembled reference list at the end for your report. If you use referencing software, do not cite any references manually in your text. Please use the journal reference style of Marine and Freshwater Research.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct
