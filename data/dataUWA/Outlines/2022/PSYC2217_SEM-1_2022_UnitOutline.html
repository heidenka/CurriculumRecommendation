Academic Information

Content Cognitive neuroscience bridges cognitive science and cognitive psychology on the one hand, and biology and neuroscience on the other. It is critical to our understanding of the link between the mind and brain. Cognitive neuroscience investigates all mental functions that are linked to neural processes and ranges from experiments performed in the laboratory to computer simulations, and can involve humans and animals. This unit consists of an integrated series of lectures, laboratories, reading and assessment and aims to provide a sound understanding of fundamental concepts and contemporary issues in cognitive neuroscience. It introduces the field and techniques of cognitive neuroscience and covers key concepts in vision, attention, action, memory, audition, language, emotion, development and executive functions.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

This unit is designed to provide a detailed introduction to the methods used to understand the functioning of the human brain, and how these methods inform various fields of psychology. 

Lectures and Readings
Section 1: Methods of Cognitive Neuroscience (Weeks 1-4)
• The first section of this unit concentrates explaining the methods of cognitive neuroscience. Examples of how these methods are used are taken from all fields of psychology, with a focus on person perception. This focus is because (i) most of the methods of cognitive neuroscience have been used to understand this computationally difficult but socially important skill, and (ii) the unit co-ordinator has expertise in the cognitive neuroscience of person perception. Examples from other fields are in the textbook and will be covered in later lectures.
• Lectures 1-4 and Chapters 1-5 of the textbook will cover the methods of cognitive neuroscience, with Chapter 7 covering face recognition (and other aspects of vision).
• A multi-choice test in Week 5 (via LMS) aims to provide early formative feedback, particularly of your understanding of the Methods of Cognitive Neuroscience. Understanding the methods forms a foundation for the rest of the unit.
• Practice multi-choice questions can be found here: https://routledgetextbooks.com/textbooks/9781138490543/quizzes.php
Section 2: Applying the Methods of Cognitive Neuroscience (Weeks 5-12)
• The second section of this unit builds on the first section, examining how core aspects of psychology have been illuminated via the methods of cognitive neuroscience. Readings from the textbook, and other associated readings, reinforce and expand on the material presented in the lectures. 
• Multi-choice tests (via LMS) in Week 9 and Exam Week assess your understanding of the lectures and associated readings. Each test evaluates a 4 week block of the unit. 
Labs
The labs synthesize both sections described above, examining some methods and some topics in more depth. We also use group work to broaden viewpoints and develop skills. 
• The assignment is a lab manual, describing and expanding on the material presented in the lab classes. Each lab manual is submitted individually. 
• A group presentation in the final lab class (Week 11) involves examining cognitive neuroscience in the media. 
This assessment is designed to:
- encourage you to think about what questions in cognitive neuroscience interest you and give you the opportunity to examine one in detail. It will also give you the opportunity to think about the techniques used in cognitive neuroscience. And finally, to examine science communication and mass media.
- think critically when reading research - in both the media and in published papers. Just because something is published doesn’t mean that it is good science or that there are not alternate points of view. Questions that you should think about when reading the paper and writing your presentation include: what question/s were the researchers trying to answer? How was the experiment designed to answer the question? Was it designed well? What did they find? What does this actually mean?
- develop your skills in conveying complex information to others verbally. In addition to giving a presentation, you will also have the opportunity to ask and answer questions and you will also provide written comments on others presentations.
- work with others. Not always easy but a skill which employers value.
Overall, it is our hope that students come to appreciate the role of the brain in psychology!

Group Presentations
Group work is a component of the presentations due in Week 11. Students will be asked to rate their contribution to the project, and that of other group members. 
At the end of the presentation, groups must include a slide which lists who contributed to different aspects of the group presentation. This can include but is not limited to contributions to media article/paper selection, analysis of the paper, additional research, preparing visual materials and slides and/or preparing/delivering the presentation.  Note that everyone in the group will get the same mark, and this information is not considered as part of the marking. This final slide is simply included to make your individual work more visible and make you all more accountable for your overall group success.
Presentations are conducted in the lab class in Week 11. 
To be awarded a grade, students must:
• contribute to preparing the presentation
• attend the lab class in which the presentation is delivered (or seek Special Consideration).  
While we encourage students to practice their presentation skills and contribute to delivering their presentation, it is not compulsory for every student in the group to deliver the presentation. 
In face-to-face mode, the presentations are in person.
In online restricted mode, the presentations are online.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.
