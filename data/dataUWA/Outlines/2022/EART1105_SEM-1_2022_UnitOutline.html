Academic Information

Content This unit focuses on the dynamic processes influencing Earth's surface environment. Several main themes are explored starting with an overarching introduction to Earth systems. The unit subsequently explores subsystems within the context of Earth's surface environment, for example, how plate tectonics interact with climate, sea level, landmasses and the biosphere. Investigations at both global and local scales emphasise how these subsystems are linked and how humans interact with, and are influenced by, these Earth processes. An understanding of dynamic Earth processes allows an informed evaluation of environmental issues that affect society and the biosphere at all levels.

Unit Information

This unit provides students with an overview of: (1) Earth as a system, Earth's internal structure, and Deep Time (Weeks 1-3); (2) Interactions between sea level, the cryosphere, climate and tectonics (Weeks 4 & 5); (3) Plate boundary processes (Weeks 6 & 7); (4) Sedimentary processes (Week 8); (5) Palaeontology (Week 9); and (6) Global ocean processes, beaches and coastal processes (Weeks 10-11). The lecture in Week 12 summarises and integrates all content (Earth as a dynamic system). Lectures and workshops are closely aligned.
By the end of this unit, students should be able to: (1) demonstrate an understanding of the Earth System and its processes at a global scale now, in the past, and in the future; (2) appreciate that the global system has many component systems which are similarly complex (e.g. carbon cycle and the climate system); (3) demonstrate an understanding of the processes and forces that shape the surface of the Earth and result in continuous environmental change; (4) analyse environmental issues affecting humans, by understanding Earth systems and processes.

Unit Structure

This unit comprises:
- Live online seminars, delivered on Mondays at 1 PM via MS Teams;
- Pre-recorded "concept" lectures, uploaded weekly to LMS for self-paced learning; 
- Weekly workshops.
MONDAY SEMINARS
An online seminar on a key topic that is related to the pre-recorded "concept" lectures and workshops for the week.
Each seminar will run for approximately 1 hr, including time to ask questions.
The seminars will begin at 1 PM AWST.
The seminars will be presented live, but delivered entirely online (via MS Teams). Details of how to watch the live online seminar will be provided to students at the start of semester. The seminars will be recorded for online-only students, and slides made available online where applicable. Students should note that the seminar content will be difficult to understand if they choose not to review the pre-recorded lectures provided to them on LMS beforehand.
Seminars begin in week 1, and will occur every Monday on ODD WEEKS ONLY, plus week 12 (i.e. Weeks 1, 3, 5, 7, 9, 11 & 12).
The seminars will be delivered by a staff member of the UWA School of Earth Sciences (SES) with expertise in the subject area, or an invited guest speaker.
DROP-IN HELP SESSIONS
There are 2 scheduled drop-in help sessions with the unit coordinator this semester - 1PM Monday 21st March (Week 4) and 1PM Monday 25th April (Week 8). These sessions will take place online on MS Teams, in lieu of a Monday seminar in Weeks 4 and 8. Details of how to participate in the drop-in session will be provided to students at the start of semester. Students with questions about the unit content are encouraged to attend. Please note this is a Q & A session only, not a lecture.
PRE-RECORDED "CONCEPT" LECTURES
A series of short pre-recorded "concept" lectures will be uploaded to LMS weekly. Students are responsible for viewing this pre-recorded material prior to the weekly Monday seminar. These content lectures will closely follow the material from our assigned textbook.
WORKSHOPS
Workshops take place once a week, starting in week 2.
Use CAS to allocate yourself to a suitable workshop time (http://www.cas.uwa.edu.au/student). Students must attend the workshop in which they are enrolled. No workshops in Week 1.
Students enrolled in the face-to-face mode of this unit will be attending their workshops in person, on campus, unless instructed otherwise during the course of the semester.
Students enrolled in the online timetabled mode of this unit will attend a live online workshop each week (via MS Teams). The online workshop will be scheduled at the same time each week. Further details will be provided to the online students at the start of semester.
PROSH may cause disruptions to Wednesday morning workshops in Week 4 (23rd March).
The format of each workshop will vary from week to week and in general workshop sessions have been designed to support your understanding of the material delivered during the lecture sessions, in addition to providing a pathway to successful assessment grades.
ASSESSMENTS
All assessment items in this unit are due on Fridays at 12:00 noon (i.e. 12 PM, midday, lunch time). Refer to the unit schedule below for further details.
Assessment item 1 (Summative quizzes) test your knowledge and provide you with an indication of areas you may need to review (note that the quizzes in Achieve also do this but they are not a summative assessment in this unit). There are six quizzes throughout the semester. Quizzes 1-5 will be topic-specific, and are spaced equally throughout the semester (weeks 3, 5, 7, 9 & 11; worth 5% each); Quiz 6 will cover the whole unit content (week 12; worth 15%). All six quizzes contribute to your unit mark. Total 40% of your final EART1105 grade.
Assessment item 2 (Earth Science Reports) is split into two separate written submissions, each worth 20% of your final EART1105 grade. Each component is an individual task. Your submissions will be made to Turnitin through a link provided on LMS, and feedback and marks will be available in Turnitin. Work not submitted to Turnitin will not be counted as a submission, so will not be marked. A selection of work will be double-marked by the unit coordinator to ensure that marks awarded by different markers are in agreement, and of the same standard.
Assessment item 3 (Presentation Project) is split into two separate components: your project proposal (5%) and your video submission (15%). Total 20% of your final EART1105 grade. Each component is an individual task. Attending your Week 11 workshop is a requirement of Assessment 3. The best video submissions from each workshop class in week 11 (by popular vote) will be awarded a prize. The unit coordinator will double-mark a selection of presentations, to maintain the standard of marking across all workshops. More information about the specific assessment requirements will be provided on LMS.
There is no final exam in this unit.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.
