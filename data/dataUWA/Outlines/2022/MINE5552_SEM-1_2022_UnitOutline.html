Academic Information

Content The project is a capstone experience in which students integrate and apply knowledge and skills acquired in earlier units to a substantial real-world design challenge relevant to their discipline (mainly focusing on underground mining). Students work in teams, typically with four to six members, and are supervised by an academic and/or industry mentor. Teams are responsible for managing their project as well as reporting against milestones and preparing the necessary design documentation. The academic objectives of the project are to lead students through a systematic engineering design and synthesis process and to better prepare them for professional practice as engineers. The focus of this unit is on teamwork skills and the design process. Even though no field trip is scheduled in this unit, some industry experts in the relevant fields are invited to provide engineering discussions that will be helpful in the decision-making process for the project.

Unit Information

This unit covers the mine design process that includes:
-The size of the operation
-The scale of the operation
-The sequence of the operation (production schedule)
-Equipment selection
-Haulage network design and cycle time calculations
-Mining and processing capacities
-Design of the mine infrastructure for underground mining operations
-Budgeting and basics of mine economics (CAPEX and OPEX)
-Project sensitivity and risk analysis
-Mine closure and rehabilitation
The group project in this unit is a capstone experience in which students integrate and apply knowledge and skills acquired in earlier units to a substantial real-world design challenge relevant to their discipline. Students work in teams, typically with five to six members.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

At UWA, we are committed to providing you with real-world, authentic learning activities that prepare you for your role as a future mining professional. Learning activities in this unit include lectures, tutorials, and project consultations.
The unit has 8 hours per week of contact time. Lecture sessions are face-to-face unless there is Covid19 restrictions (Refer to ‘Teaching contingency plan’ section for more details). There will be other pre-recorded lectures uploaded to LMS, especially for mine design software training. You will be notified when these are available.
In addition to face-to-face discussions in lecture sessions, students are encouraged to make appointments with the lecturer/the unit coordinator to discuss some of the topics relative to their project. In some cases, these project consultation sessions may require the attendance of all group members. 
In this unit, we will be using Whittle, MinePlan 3D, and some other software to complete the weekly engineering tasks. Every week, there will some software training that will help you master each engineering tool. It is imperative that you attend those weekly software training sessions.
Assessments in this unit require the whole group’s participation. This is a project-based unit. Assessments will take the form of progressive presentations (including individual portfolio checks) and final reports. The students are responsible for forming their own project teams (there will be no help from the unit coordinator). Each team will be made up of 5-6 students. Any email communication with the lecturer/the unit coordinator relative to projects must include all members within the group ensuring that everybody is aware of the project progress. Being away from the campus is no excuse for none-involvement with the group project. If there are extenuating circumstances, this needs to be supported by proper documentation. Keep in mind that any email associated with your group work will be ignored if group members are not CCed. When you reply, ensure that you select the “reply all” option.

DESCRIOTION OF THE ASSESSMENTS

ASSESSMENT 1 - Interim Report/Presentation (30% of your final mark)
The purpose of this assessment is to make sure that everyone in each group is learning and practicing the required engineering tools to perform weekly tasks. Each individual check must be submitted in the designated week (at the end of the second lecture session). Each submission contains the work you complete on weekly tasks, which involves the software usage and some engineering decisions on the project deliveries (30% of your final mark). Please check Blackboard as well for details regarding what to submit and how to submit.  
ASSESSMENT 2 - Final Project Presentation (30% of your final mark)
This assessment is designed to measure your presentation skills. Presenting engineering assumptions and designs in a sufficient and easy understandable way is an important task that can lead to stronger relationships with colleagues and professional contacts. 
Each group needs to provide an electronic copy of their Final Presentation before the first scheduled presentation (no exceptions). After this submission, you may not make any changes to the content of your presentation.
Absence from the presentations will only be allowed if there is a medical or extenuating circumstance. This will require documented evidence, e.g. Medical Certificate.
Final presentation will be 30 minutes long. Practicing your presentation is a good exercise for time management. Each student must take part in the presentation. The total number of slides should be no more than 35-40. Try to avoid too much wording on the slides. The room is equipped with projection facilities. However, it is the individual’s responsibility to ensure that the presentation is functioning beforehand. Computer problems will not be allowed to delay the proceedings.

ASSESSMENT 3 - Final Project Report (40% of your final mark)
A written report is the reflection of your engineering skills and your ideas. If you read your own writing, you will have a better perspective about your audience’s understanding on your engineering design and planning. Please restrict yourself to a very concise (to the point), meaningful and relevant discussion. The quality (as opposed to the quantity “i.e. Number of pages or words”) of the content is very important. The number of pages in the report body is limited to about 100 pages. The report will NOT be accepted in excess of the numbered pages. Addition to 100 pages, the appendix section at the end can be used to place some of the long calculations and result tables. However, proper referencing is required in the reporting body to the relative appendix to guide and inform the reader. NO EXCESSIVE WRITING is allowed (i.e. painful long explanations or discussions) in the appendix section. If the appendix section is bigger than the body of the report, your submission will NOT be accepted.
Reports must be submitted with the official UWA statement and all group members must sign the declaration that the work is original, and students understand their rights and responsibilities with regards to academic misconduct, plagiarism and academic integrity. Reports that do not contain signatures of all group members will NOT be accepted.
Student groups are required to submit an electronic copy (in WORD format) via Turnitin – in this case one submission per group by the group leader will suffice. This is for evaluating your report submission in terms of originality. Text matched above 20% will NOT be accepted. You are allowed to submit your report before the due date so that you have a chance if your submission text match is below 20% or not. A printed and bound copy of your final report must be submitted for the evaluation. Quality assurance is required for the printed and bound copy.
You are required to submit your MinePlan 3D projects together with your written report.
Late submissions will, in most cases, receive a zero mark. However, a late submission will be allowed when a deferred deadline has been approved by the course coordinator prior to the due date if there is a medical or extenuating circumstance. This will require documented evidence, e.g. Medical Certificate.

GUIDELINES FOR GROUP PROJECTS
Learning to work effectively as part of a group is a wonderful skill you will frequently use in your career. Do not assume your group experience will be bad. Beginning a group project with a positive attitude will have a favourable impact on those around you. You should not compare this experience to those in the past. This is a fresh start.
Getting Started
1. Get to know one another. At your first meeting, find out each member’s major, job and interests. This will help you be sympathetic to the people in your group and acquaint you with the stresses they have in their lives. Getting to know one another is also an excellent way to get an idea of the talents and skills that will strengthen your group. Find out who types fast, who knows somebody that can be interviewed, who is best at logically thinking through an argument and other similar questions. This knowledge will also be beneficial when dividing tasks. Make sure to exchange phone numbers and set a schedule when everyone can meet periodically. 
2. Team Contract. On a single sheet of paper, put your names and list the rules and expectations you agree as a team to adopt. Each team member should sign the sheet, indicating acceptance of these expectations and intention to fulfil them. Return one copy to your lecturer and keep a remaining copy or copies for yourselves. Refer to Blackboard for an electronic copy of a team contact example.
3. Create a time line. At your first meeting, make a list of everything that needs to be done in order to complete the project. Then organize the list according to priorities and set deadlines for each portion of the project. Your time line will serve two purposes: Firstly you will be organized everyone in the group will know what is due and when. The second advantage is that the project won’t seem so huge when it is broken down into parts. It will seem like a series of short assignments instead of one large one. Refer to Blackboard for an electronic copy of a time table example.
4. Make task assignments. Talk with the group about how to divide the work equally so that everyone is consistently involved over the course of the project. Be the first one to volunteer for an assignment. This will ensure that you get to work on the portion of the project you are best at or most enjoy. When other members of the group see your willingness to volunteer they may be more likely to volunteer for an assignment as well. Make sure the tasks are equal! Don’t let yourself or another motivated person in the group do all of the work.
Working on the Project
1. Do your part! It may not be necessary for your entire group to meet together again, but make sure you attend every meeting for your smaller group. If you cannot attend a meeting, send your assignment (or your findings) to another member so that the group does not fall behind. Remember that any meeting you do not attend means that you don’t have an input into that portion of the project.
2. Use effective communication skills. One of the more important things to remember when working in a group is to be sensitive to other members’ ideas, thoughts and opinions. Part of learning to work in a group is respecting others. There will be times when the work is being done differently to what you think it should be. That is okay. There are times members will disagree with you but you will want them to respect your opinion. Group member differences will make the project interesting, unique and insightful.
3. Uncooperative members. In nearly every group, there is usually one person who may not cooperate. Do not let this person’s attitude affect your attitude or progress as a group. Carefully evaluate if your criticism of a member is merely a personality conflict or if there is a specific problem. If other group members have similar reactions, then perhaps it is best to see the unit coordinator about the problem. This is particularly true if the person is having a negative impact on completing the group project in a timely manner.
The finished Project
1. Get the assignment done a day or two in advance. This will help your group make sure everything is in order. If you have kept to a schedule you should not have to leave anything to the last minute. At least part of the group should do a final edit to ensure the project is presented the way the group had planned. There should be no surprises for anyone in the group about the final project.
2. Routinely evaluate group performances. If the group works together throughout the class or semester, it is important to discuss grades with the group each time you are graded or evaluated. This will give you an opportunity to clarify your roles, express problems the group are having and make changes to improve performance. It will also give you a chance to share positive feedback and successes.
3. Fairly evaluate individual and group effectiveness. The finished product may only be a portion of the grade for your project. Your lecturer may also ask you to evaluate groups and individual performance. Be fair in your assessment. Focus on how well you and other members complete tasks. Do not evaluate someone poorly because you may have disagreed with them or because they do things differently. Be honest with yourself in assessing your performance. If you feel that your final project grade is unfair, make an appointment to see the unit coordinator. Most lecturers are willing to listen to concerns about group projects. Explain carefully to your lecturer exactly why you think the grade is unfair and ask for him/her to reconsider.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
