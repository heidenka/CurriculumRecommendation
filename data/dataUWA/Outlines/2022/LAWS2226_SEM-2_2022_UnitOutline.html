Unit Information

This unit provides an introduction to the history, theory and structure of some of the key international legal institutions in world politics, such as the United Nations, the International Court of Justice, and the Food and Agriculture Organization of the United Nations.
It engages with key institutions and discusses theories and practice in order to understand how the institutions were formed and function. Firstly, students will become familiar with international organisations prior to World War II. Secondly, they will engage in a more extended analysis of the formation and roles of some of the key international legal institutions post World War II. The unit will introduce students to the theories and basic concepts of international law, the sources and subjects of international law. There is also some interdisciplinary engagement with international politics, economics, crime, the environment and food sciences.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

The face-to-face contact for the unit is two hours of lectures per week and one hour of tutorial per week. Lectures will be interactive and so while recorded, it is anticipated that students will have a better learning experience through attending lectures.
The tutorials will involve discussion and analysis of particular issues related to the lecture topics. As with the lectures, students are strongly encouraged to complete the assigned tutorial readings in advance, so that the group discussions are informed and useful. Tutorials will start in Week 2 and will relate to the content delivered in the lecture that week.
LMS will contain tutorial guides with core readings, as well as additional links, podcasts and audio-visual materials for students aiming to extend their learning.
For students applying for special consideration in relation to their assignment, a maximum extension of 3 weeks will be available in this unit. Students not able to submit their assignment within 3 weeks of the due date may be permitted to withdraw from the unit without academic penalty

Referencing Requirements

In preparing for the research essay, students must provide references for all sources that they rely on and use the Australian Guide to Legal Citation (4th edition). You can access the full text of this via http://www.law.unimelb.edu.au/mulr/aglc. The UWA guide to using the AGLC citation style is available at https://guides.library.uwa.edu.au/AGLC4.

Other

Typically this unit is assessed in the following ways: (1) participation assessment; (2) research essay; and (3) examination.
(1) participation assessment (20% of the final grade) will be submitted and assessed via LMS. Marking criteria and further information will be provided in class and on LMS. 
(2) The written assignment (40% of the final grade) will involve the research and writing of an essay (up to 2,800 words) on a choice of questions that will be available on LMS. Modules on how to conduct international law research, including searching for materials, scholarly writing styles and referencing, will be provided by the law library via LMS. Marking criteria and further information on the essay will be provided in class and on LMS.
(3) The final exam is two hours duration, including reading time (40% of the final grade). Students will typically complete a set of short answer, long answer and problem questions. A preparation lecture will be provided in the last teaching week to inform topics, readings and sample questions to better prepare students for the exam. Scheduling of the exam is to be advised.


Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "honest, ethical and responsible scholarship". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
