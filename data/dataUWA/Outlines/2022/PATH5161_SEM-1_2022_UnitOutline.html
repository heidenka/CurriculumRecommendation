Teaching contingency plan (In response to Covid19 restrictions)

This unit includes theoretical and practical knowledge as well a significant skill development. While much of the underpinning knowledge (i.e. lecture material) may be accessed asynchronously through the LMS, a significant face-to-face component (i.e. tutorials and laboratory practical classes) is required for development of critical thinking, problem solving and practical laboratory skill development required by professional accreditation standards. 

Students are expected to attend face to face classes when they are scheduled. Adherence to state and university directions relating to mask use, proof of vaccination, social distancing and all other safety measures are required to attend campus.

To support student theoretical learning during asynchronous delivery, the unit coordinator is available for consultation weekly during the office hours listed above.

In the event of widespread restrictions to campus access or limitations on venue capacity, some face-to-face classes may be converted to synchronous but online activities. However, activities that require interaction of students with laboratory equipment, specialist staff, laboratory reagents or samples, including but not limited to practical competency assessments, cannot be converted to an online activity. Efforts will be made to complete these classes on time with increased protections and / or reduced room occupancy. Where this is not possible, classes may be deferred until restrictions ease, including potential deferral of classes and / or assessments beyond the semester.

Accommodations have been made in anticipation of some staff absences due to mandatory isolation and / or illness to allow scheduled practical classes to continue. However, significant unexpected staff absences may disrupt the planned schedule, resulting in deferral of some classes or assessments. Where this occurs students will be notified by email and LMS announcement of both the cancellation and the new schedule as early as possible.

Laboratory and tutorial classes are not routinely recorded and usually cannot feasibly be repeated or deferred to accommodate individual students. However, students who are unable to attend a required face to face class due to mandatory isolation, illness or other awarded special consideration will be provided class notes and other available resources related to the class and may avail themselves of office hours consultation where further support is needed. Students who miss a scheduled on campus assessment and are awarded special consideration may be offered alternative assessment where feasible, or have their assessment deferred, including deferral beyond the semester.

Academic Information

Content The aim of this unit is to provide relevant knowledge and skills to acquire and interpret haematology laboratory data to reach a diagnosis at a level expected in professional practice. On completion of the unit students are expected to understand and apply the principles of haemopoiesis, haemopoietic disorders and laboratory testing in laboratory haematology. They are expected to be able to interpret laboratory data and make diagnoses. The aim is achieved by increasing the knowledge and understanding of; normal blood and bone marrow (structure, function, cells); haematological diseases (pathogenesis and manifestations); and diagnostic techniques (cell enumeration, morphology, haemostasis).

Unit Information

This unit is a compulsory first year postgraduate unit, forming part of the Master of Clinical Pathology. The unit aims to prepare graduates with the relevant knowledge and skills to acquire and interpret haematology laboratory data to assist a diagnosis  at a level expected in professional practice. On completion of the unit students are expected to understand and apply the principles of haematology, haemtological disorders and laboratory testing in haematology.
To achieve this, the unit will increase your knowledge and understanding of; Normal blood and bone marrow (structure, function, cell types), Haematological disorders (pathogenesis and manifestations), and, diagnostic techniques including cell enumeration, morphology, haemostasis.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

The unit is delivered face to face at the UWA Health Campus. Content is delivered in modules covering Haemoncology, Anaemias, and Haemostasis. Underpinning theory for each learning objective is delivered asynchronously within the LMS, with recorded presentations and written notes by lecture from UWA academics, adjuncts and guest speakers from clinical pathology services. This knowledge is then applied and laboratory techniques are developed through weekly wet laboratories. The results of the lab are analysed, applied and contextualised in weekly tutorials and problem based learning activities.

Unit Structure

The material has been divided into modules, each aligned with a specific topic. Folders for each module can be found on the LMS. Each folder contains a range of materials and activities for you to work through. While some of these activities are "face to face" (e.g. wet laboratories and morphology tutorials), many other tasks are "asynchronous" - meaning you do not need to do them at a specific time or attend a specific class to complete them. Many can be done from home, though some will require access to software or resources only available on campus. Such activities might include watching a video presentation from an expert, reading notes, analysing and interpreting laboratory data using special software, or completing a quiz. The expected time required for you to complete these tasks is highlighted on the description of each module.
Additionally, each module will require you to attend some schedule classes. These are most of wet laboratory classes that run at a specific time. The time that you are required to attend the class is highlighted in the folder description. Be aware that the face-to-face class may require you to have completed some of the activities of the module beforehand.
Despite being asynchronous, you are expected to complete each module in the timeframe indicated. If you do not complete and submit the end-of-module quiz within the expected semester week, you will be flagged "at risk" and will also incur late penalties for submitting late. While it is no problem if you finish all the work for a module and skip ahead to the next week, it is very important that you do not fall behind. This would result in you being unprepared for face-to-face classes as well as scheduled tests and examinations.

Other

This unit includes collecting and working with blood from patients and healthy volunteers. While the activities involved have undergone risk assessment and relevant institutional biosafety approvals, some activities are considered to be exposure-prone procedures (EPPs). As such, it is important that students meet the expectations for Health Care Workers in Western Australia. Under these guidelines HCWs and students are responsible for ensuring—(1) they know their own blood borne virus status; (2) their vaccination status against vaccine preventable diseases is current and they can provide evidence of HBV vaccination or documented evidence of non-responder status; (3) any refusal to undertake recommended vaccinations and/or serology is to be documented; and (4) they adopt infection prevention practices to minimise the risk of occupational exposure (e.g. use of appropriate personal protective equipment and safe handling and disposal of sharps).
To pass this unit, a student must: (a) achieve an overall mark of 50 per cent or higher for the unit; and (b) achieve the requisite requirements(s) or a mark of 50 per cent or greater, whichever is higher and specified in the unit outline, for the practical examinations component.
Supplementary assessment is available by application to the unit coordinator for those students who obtain a mark of at least 50% overall and obtained a mark of less than 50% in one specified component (failed component) of the unit.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
