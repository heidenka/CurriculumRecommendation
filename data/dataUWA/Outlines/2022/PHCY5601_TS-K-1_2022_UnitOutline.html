Academic Information

Content This unit is the first of the integrated pharmacy practice units in the Master of Pharmacy program. This unit begins by introducing students to the role of the pharmacist as a provider of primary health care, and establishes the context for the applied knowledge of pharmacy. It provides foundation knowledge necessary for further pharmacy practice units throughout the course. Students are introduced to the area of pharmacy governance which encompasses regulatory, legislative and ethical frameworks that form the cornerstones of the practice of pharmacy. Consideration is given to the legislation controlling pharmaceutical products and pharmacy practice and the nature and operation of the Pharmaceutical Benefits Scheme and the responsibilities of the pharmacist in relation to this scheme. Students develop communication skills and a sound understanding of the principles of evidence-based medicine that are essential for delivering health care information to patients and others. The use of drug information resources available to the pharmacist is reviewed. The role of the pharmacist as a health care practitioner is exemplified by experiential placements within an aged care facility.

Unit Information

Welcome to Introduction to Pharmacy Practice (IPP). This unit is the first of the integrated pharmacy practice units in the Master of Pharmacy program and is a co-requisite of PHCY5606 Foundations of Primary Care - which means you must complete both at the same time. IPP begins by introducing you to the role of the pharmacist as a health care professional. To help you understand the scope of practice of a pharmacist you will learn about the framework that supports the practice of pharmacy. Pharmacy governance encompasses regulatory, legislative and ethical frameworks that form the cornerstones of the practice of pharmacy.
Competent pharmacists need to be able to access evidenced based information and use it to make current best practice decisions for patients and healthcare professionals. Students learn where to access this information and how to assess the quality of the information. Pharmacists then need to communicate the information they have retrieved and analysed with patients and health care practitioners. The skill of communication in the healthcare context is developed through workshops and experiential placements within aged care and community pharmacy.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

This unit comprises of lecture-workshops, tutorials, small group activities, simulated learning sessions, patient interviews, mentor groups and reflection. The majority of the content of this unit introduces concepts and begins to develop skills in practical ways. Students develop an understanding of the patient perspective and the professional responsibilities of the pharmacist. 
The lecture-workshops provide foundation knowledge that is further developed in tutorials and small group activities. In the simulated learning environment, students practise their communication skills while drawing on knowledge learnt about primary healthcare from PHCY5606. Students can further develop communication skills through interviewing individuals about their medicines and health in a residential aged care facility (depending on COVID-19 restrictions).
Mentor sessions held weekly throughout the semester provide the opportunity to discuss a variety of topics and to practise skills with peers, and to draw on the experience of the mentor who is a recent graduate of the Master of Pharmacy.  
Sessions in this unit are workshops, tutorials, mentor sessions, online modules, simulated learning and experiential learning therefore they are not recorded. Attendance is required.

See the unit timetable for scheduling of the learning activities for IPP. These are generally 2 x 3 hour lecture-workshops and up to 2 hours of mentor sessions each week.
Content 
1. Pharmacy practice introduction
Students gain an understanding of pharmacy within healthcare by exploring the concept of pharmaceutical care. The role of pharmacists and pharmacists’ primary functions in delivering patient centred care are investigated. Students also explore and develop an understanding of the importance of leadership and connection in the context of a student and how this is mirrored when working as a healthcare provider. 
2. Ethics introduction 
The Pharmaceutical Society of Australia’s Code of Ethics is discussed to foster an appreciation of the concept of ethical practice and the role of reflection in a pharmacist’s professional development.
3. Values in healthcare
As healthcare providers, pharmacists must respect the beliefs, values and attitudes of others and work with these to deliver patient centred care. In this session, students examine their own beliefs, values and attitudes in order to provide insight into the diverse people healthcare professionals care for.
4. Forensics
Pharmacists must know and be familiar with the pieces of legislation that govern pharmacy practice and be familiar with professional guidance relating to pharmacy. Pharmacists must practice ethically and also within legal boundaries. Students will learn about this legal framework and how it applies to practising pharmacy. 
5. Communication
Effective communication is a cornerstone of professional practice. If poor communication confuses a patient about how to take their medicine, it can have life threatening consequences. Therefore, developing effective communication skills commences early in the Master of Pharmacy.
6. Rural Health Workshop
Students are offered the opportunity of experiencing the life of a health care practitioner in rural areas through a rural placement instead of a traditional community pharmacy placement. This session provides information about the exceptionally rewarding rural placement experience.
7. Medicines information; Evidence based medicine; Library and referencing 
As students and pharmacists, you will need to know where to find good quality information, how to evaluate the quality of the information you access, and how to reference the source of the information. In these sessions you will develop your skills in these areas to enable you to practice evidence based medicine in the rapidly advancing healthcare setting.
8. Bethanie visits 
Students visit Bethanie Aged Care Facility in Joondanna to practise their communication skills with ‘real patients’. You will spend time with a resident of the facility discussing their health and medicines. This is followed by a group discussion with your peers about the interactions you had.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
