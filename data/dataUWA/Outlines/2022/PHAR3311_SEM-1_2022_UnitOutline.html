Academic Information

Content This unit provides students with state of the art insights into how drugs affect physiological and pathological systems. The practicals in the unit complement the lecture topics in PHAR3310 Molecular Pharmacology to give students mechanistic and experimental insights into receptor structure/function analyses and signal transduction, hypothesis testing and data analysis, computer based approaches to drug structure/activity modelling, drug metabolism and the production of toxic metabolites and genetic differences affecting drug metabolism. In addition, students participate in a seminar series on current pharmacological and toxicological controversies. The seminars are designed to emphasise oral communication skills and student participation in the audience with active listening and questioning.

Unit Information

While the acquisition of facts is a necessary prerequisite for the study of any scientific discipline, it is not the sole aim in level 3 pharmacology teaching and learning. As the companion unit of PHAR3310, Methods in Molecular Pharmacology (PHAR3311) acquaints students with the methodologies routinely used by Pharmacology and Toxicology researchers. Thus students taking PHAR3311 will become proficient in core experimental pharmacology methodologies and subsequent data analysis and interpretation. This is important because pharmacology is an exploratory and interrogative discipline - it is experimental in basis. Should you choose to go on to an honours degree, you will use these techniques in your research project. Alternatively, the practical experience will serve students well for employment in the biotechnology field or other laboratory based industries. 
Lab skills learned during the semester include:
1. Precise and accurate pipetting combined with standard curve analysis
2. Receptor characterisation using living tissue and organ baths
3. SDS-PAGE and western blotting to examine tyrosine phosphorylation 
4. Enzyme-catalysed xenobiotic bioactivation and macromolecular adduction
5. Communication of pharmacological science with an individual presentation

Learning And Teaching Strategy

PHAR3311 is a very hands-on, lab-based unit in which each class has been developed by Pharmacology and Toxicology faculty according to the methodologies used widely in their area of interest. Importantly, the lab content in PHAR3311 is tightly integrated with the lecture content of PHAR3310 and provides students with opportunities to consolidate their theoretical learning with a solid practical understanding of the major topics. Students will also develop critical thinking in data analysis and interpretation as well as in preparation for their seminar presentation where they are expected to evaluate claims and counter-claims of scientific literature. Collaborative teamwork is a very important aspect of this unit as students will be working in groups for most classes.
The overarching learning outcomes for PHAR3311 are:
1. Demonstrate competence in the use of common laboratory skills
2. Demonstrate a capacity to analyse experimental data generated in molecular pharmacology practicals
3. Demonstrate a capacity to synthesise data generated in practicals with molecular pharmacology research literature
4. Communicate molecular pharmacology principles and processes

Unit Structure

There is a lab class in each week of the semester (see below). Practicals make last 1 class (introductory practical),  2 classes (tyrosine kinase practical), 3 classes (drug metabolism and toxicity) or run over a 5 week-series (general receptor project). 
The 5 session project involves the generation of high quality functional and radioligand binding data over 3 practicals followed by a week in which you learn to collate, analyse and graphically present your data. It culminates in the generation and presentation of a scientific poster. The various components of this 5 session, class-based research project will be outlined in week 2 of the semester.
The seminar series aims to develop communication skills as communication of an understanding of the scientific literature is an important aspect of scientific training. Students will give individual presentations based upon an important and potentially controversial topic in pharmacology and toxicology. 
Each practical will normally require a report to be submitted within two weeks of completion of the practical class although timing is adjusted to fit around PHAR3310 summative tests. Several labs also require submission of pre-lab quizzes.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
