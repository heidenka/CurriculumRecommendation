Academic Information

Content This unit provides students with the opportunity to engage in a real-world research based master planning project for an urban or regional location. Students are introduced to key planning theories, concepts and research techniques required to develop a concept master plan. Students study the traditions and philosophies of planning, planning ethics, plan making, and other planning practice issues from the perspective of public and private sectors.

Unit Information

OVERVIEW
Welcome to PLNG4401 (Planning Theory and Practice) which is a core unit in the Masters in Urban and Regional Planning.
For 2022 this unit will be led by Assoc Prof. Paul J. Maginn (Unit Co-ordinator) with additional inputs from a mix of staff and guest speakers that include: (i) Professor Neil Foley (NF); (ii) Andrew Brodie/Tim Parker (Roberts Day); (iii) Rebecca Didcoe (TBB), (iv) Clay Thomas (Peet); and (iv) Chris Swiderski (Flyt).
This unit is fundamentally concerned with students applying the knowledge – conceptual, theoretical, methodological, and, technical – acquired in other units that form part of the MURP programme – and applying this knowledge and technical skills in order to develop an outline concept master-plan.
We will explore general theoretical/conceptual issues in relation to urban planning policy, planning research and plan-making. This will be achieved via a mix of: (i) interactive studio-based classes, (ii) guided fieldtrips; and, (iii) fieldwork. 
Working in groups of 4-6 persons, students will assume the role of a “planning consultancy” and your overall aims/objectives are to develop planning ideas, understand planning policies and collect/analyse data that will help inform your your final outline master plan.
This unit is about three fundamental issues:
•	Understanding and undertaking planning research, the activities and processes associated with collecting and analysing various forms of primary and secondary data and information, that underpin the development and creation of ALL plans;
•	Assembling all data and information and presenting it in a plan, a planning report and a formal presentation so as to convey your overall planning vision and details of your planning proposals; and
•	Providing students with a taste of the realities of professional planning practice and the planning process by working on a real planning/development site.
This taste of planning reality is grounded in the fact that your master plan case study is a real site with real planning challenges/opportunities.  Furthermore, we will be holding 2 studio-based classes at the offices of HATCH Roberts Day, located on 442 Murray Street, Perth, in order to give you a sense of what/how a real planning consultancy looks, feels and operates.  Planning practice and plan-making is a team effort with individual planners (and other professionals) bringing different specialist knowledge and skills together to resolve planning problems.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

OVERVIEW
As a practice- and research-based unit that develops planning knowledge and skills via studio-based learning and fieldwork, plus team working, this unit will be taught primarily in-person. Where possible, classes will also be conducted/recorded for any students who are based overseas/interstate and/or unable to attend in-person due to COVID-19-related issues.
This unit seeks to mimic professional planning practice. As such, students will assume the role of a planning consultancy with each consultancy member responsible for assuming a key role/position and undertaking a variety of tasks within their respective consultancy group in order to develop their own outline concept masterplan.
Planning consultancies will comprise between 4-6 members and each consultancy is required to appoint TWO Co-Directors who will oversee the management of their consultancy. Student consultancies will work individually and collectively on various aspects of the masterplan development process throughout the semester. All students consultancies will work as a single meta-consultancy during the fieldwork component of the masterplan
As professional planning practice and plan-making is a team effort, the assignments for this unit are 100% group-based . There are THREE assignment outputs associated with the unit: 1. Poster (20%); 2. Presentation (20%); 3. Masterplan Report (60%). The poster and presentation are due towards the end of the semester. The presentation will take place on the last day of the teaching semester (Fri 21 Oct) and will be held at the offices of the local council where the project site is based. Posters are due the day before (i.e. Thurs 20 Oct). The Masterplan Report is due a week after the final presentations (Fri 28 Oct).

Assessment
All students are required to complete the major group project assessment – Outline Concept Master Plan – that underpins this unit.
Outline Concept Master Plan: (100%)
As this course is primarily about developing your practical (and theoretical) planning skills and knowledge you are required to complete a major assignment – i.e. an outline concept master plan for a site within metropolitan Perth. This assignment has three integrated components to it:
•	a planning report and plan (60%);
•	a poster (20%); and
•	a formal presentation (20%)
In overall terms, your task is to develop a strategic planning vision underpinned by robust and comprehensive data collection and critical analysis that seeks to create a diverse, vibrant and sustainable town centre for local residents, workers and visitors.
You will need to develop a set of cogent, inter-related and justifiable planning and planning-related visions, proposals and/or policies for the overall study site and discrete areas within the town centre.
The outline concept master plan exercise and all assignment outputs is a 100% group-based assignment. Students are to assume the role of a planning consultancy with responsibility for fulfilling the following simple brief:
•	develop an overall planning (and/or design) vision for the site;
•	prepare a suitably scaled and zoned planning map and any other  relevant precinct plans/maps to illustrate the location, size and nature of key land uses (e.g. housing, retail, commercial, industrial, transport, open space, and so on) within the project site and immediate surrounds;
•	prepare a planning report that sets out in general detail the broad set of conceptual ideas, proposals and/or policies – e.g. physical, social, economic, environmental and/or infrastructural – that reflect your planning vision for the site and take into account relevant planning (and non-planning) policy frameworks and analysis of data on, for example, demographic, housing, retail, economic, environmental issues pertinent to the site;
•	utilise appropriate graphics and imagery, ideally your own, to help illustrate your vision and proposals; and
•	provide a clear rationale and justification for your vision and broad conceptual proposals throughout the planning report.

The aims/objectives of this brief need to be reflected in your:
•	Plan: This should comprise: (i) a suitably sized, scaled and zoned plan(s)/map(s) that illustrates the key land-use proposals (e.g. residential, commercial, community, open spaces, transport etc) that you envisage for the site in overall terms; and (ii) suitably sized, scaled and zoned precinct/neighbourhood plans for the project site. All plans are to form a part of the final report.
•	Report: The report should outline the rationale and justification of the vision and specific proposals contained in your concept plan. In other words, the report is the textual representation of your analytical research and interpretation of the planning and non-planning policy frameworks underpinning your outline concept master plan. As such, this will necessitate conducting research, analysing data and writing up your findings/interpretations on a number of fronts, including, but not limited to, the following items:
o	site analysis and wider spatial context analysis,
o	key federal, state and local government policies and legislation,
o	demographic and socio-economic analysis,
o	property market analysis – residential, commercial, and industrial;
o	public life surveys
o	land-use surveys
o	built environment assessment
o	infrastructure and services,
o	landownership issues, and
o	public services and community facilities audit.
The report should also present illustrated visualisations of your planning and design vision for the area in terms of any new proposed space/places, buildings, facilities, open spaces, traffic and pedestrian networks and systems, and streetscapes.
•	Poster: This should be an informative and creative visual and textual summary of the overall vision and key aspects of your master plan proposals and report. The poster should be A0 in size and matt laminated.
•	Presentation: You will be required to present your master plan and proposals to a panel of judges made up of practising planners.
Both the poster (20%) and presentation (20%) will be assessed by a panel of professional planners comprising up to 4-5 people from the local council and HATCH Roberts Day. The poster is due on Thursday 20th October and should be submitted to Assoc. Prof. Paul Maginn. Your presentation is due on Friday 21st October.
A poster exhibition and formal presentation of your master plan(s) will take place on Friday 21 October. In addition to the panel of judges, other staff, including local councillors, from the LGA may sit in on final presentations. Other professional planners and/or academic staff may also be in attendance.
You should see the opportunity to present in front of a panel of your professional peers as a chance to demonstrate to prospective employers your planning knowledge and skills.
The planning report (60%) is assessed by Assoc. Prof. Paul Maginn and should be submitted by no later than Friday 28th October. This is to provide you with sufficient time to make any revisions to your draft report following the feedback received from the panel of judges.

Planning Consultancy and Project Management:
Each student group MUST adopt a formal name for their planning consultancy. This name must be confirmed by Week 2.
Each planning consultancy MUST also appoint a Project Manager and Deputy Project Manager who will be responsible for the overall strategic management of the team and master plan project. Being appointed to these 2 roles does not mean that your sole task is a managerial one. You should establish your various skill sets and interests as an initial step in the process of allocating roles and responsibilities and individual tasks. A good division and specialisation of labour should help ensure that you complete various tasks on time and to a high standard.
Weekly Duties/Tasks
Each group MUST keep a log of all meetings, attendees and key decisions and items for actioning at all meetings.  Ideally, you should draw up a meeting schedule in advance so that every group member is well aware of the dates/times/locations of meetings. You are required to submit this schedule of meetings by Week 3. You should also use MS Teams as the primary means of communication within your respective teams.
Each group must also keep a log of activities/tasks assigned to individual/ sub-group members so that you can all monitor and track your performance. The log will also be monitored by Assoc. Prof. Paul Maginn throughout the semester and a final detailed log must be submitted along with your final report. A draft template for the log is attached at the end of this document for you to use or amend as you see fit.
Each team will be collectively responsible for deciding upon and allocating individual/sub-group tasks/activities. Each individual team member is responsible for confirming their performance in completing set tasks within agreed timeframes with the Project Manager/Deputy Project Manager and wider group.
All project team members are to actively participate in the development of their master-plan. Moreover, all teams members are to be afforded the opportunity to participate equally in their master plan.

Peer Review
Real world planning practice is dependent upon team work. Hence, for this assignment it is imperative that ALL individual members do their utmost to contribute fully and equally to the workload of each group. This means, amongst other things, attending all scheduled group meetings, conducting fieldwork and/or analysing relevant data, producing written and/or graphical work on time and to a satisfactory quality. In order to award an individual mark to each student, a student-based peer review process will be conducted at the end of the semester – the activity log will be extremely helpful here for each team in determining the contribution of each team member over the course of the semester.
The peer review process requires each student assessing, quantitatively and qualitatively, as well as with honesty and integrity, the overall contribution of each member (including oneself) to their group master plan project. The peer review process is designed to do two things: (i) recognise and reward individual effort and contribution; and (ii) eradicate and penalise any free-riding. 
Ultimately, the peer review process may result in some students’ individual marks deviating, positively or negatively, from the overall group mark awarded to each master plan.  Individual marks may fluctuate by a maximum of 30% points above/below the overall group mark. Any student deemed via the peer review process, observations throughout the semester and/or concerns raised by any group to have not contributed fairly/equally to their group project face the prospect of scoring a fail grade - hence, the importance of keeping the weekly activity log to record each group members' contribution.
In the event that any group experiences difficulties in terms of one or more team members not contributing fairly/properly/equally to the project (e.g. not attending meetings regularly, not delivering outputs in a timely manner or to an agreed quality/standard/format), then please alert the unit co-ordinator as soon as possible so that any issues/problems can be resolved promptly. 
DO NOT DELAY IN REPORTING ANY ISSUES/PROBLEMS WITHIN YOUR GROUP. IT’S BEST FOR ALL THAT ANY ISSUES ARE DEALT WITH AND RESOLVED PROMPTLY.

Unit Structure

OVERVIEW
Classes for PLNG4401 take place on Fridays and Mondays and will be delivered via a hybrid teaching mode - i.e. in-person and online via MS Teams for those students in the class who are based inter-state/overseas and/or are unable to attend in-person due to COVID-19-related restrictions. Friday classes are delivered via 2 intensive blocks of teaching over the course of the day.
Block 1 (9-12.00/12.30 morning session) This block will generally be used for interactive lecture/studion-based sessions. Lectures conducted during this session are designed to be interactive and reflective in character in order to illustrate the linkages between planning/design theory and planning practice so as to bridge the so-called professional gap that exists  between planning theory and practice.
Block 2 (2-5.00 afternoon session) This block will generally be used for more practical studio-based sessions (on- and off-campus) plus dedicated time and space for students to work in their ‘consultancy teams’ on the outline concept master plan project. Particular emphasis will be paid to helping you improve some of your urban design skills, planning policy knowledge and planning research skills so that you can present your master plan proposals in a professional manner via your plan, report, poster and  presentation. 
Monday classes take place from 2-4pm and will focus mainly on research design and strategy issues related to conducting fieldwork activities including: (i) online HH/Business Survey; (ii) Public Life surveys, (iii) land-use surveys, and, (iv) demographic/economic/social data analysis.
There will be at least 1 organised fieldtrip and 2-3 fieldwork days/sessions:
(a)	an initial guided tour of the project site on Friday 25th July (10.00-12.00) with representatives from the LGA that the project site is located as well as planners/urban designers from HATCH Roberts Day is located; and
(b)	Between 2-3 fieldwork visits will be required to (i) distribute e-survey invites to local residents and businesses and (ii) conduct Public Life surveys in order to collect various forms of primary data to help you make sense of the site and inform the development of your masterplan . There will be 2 full days of fieldwork on Friday 26th and Saturday 27th August.
Student groups will also need to conduct their own additional site visits to the actual project site and adjacent land areas in order to develop a more nuanced understanding of the local built environment, business activity, traffic and congestion, parking, infrastructure, sense of community and vibrancy and the wider spatial contexts that surround and impinge upon the project site.
Classes marked Outline Concept Master Plan Group Project Session in the schedule below have been set aside for you to come together in your ‘consultancy teams’ so that you can work and focus on your master plan projects. Students will also be required to meet on their own time to discuss and progress their master plan. Hence, you will need to develop a meeting schedule for your groups.
All student groups are required to deliver a draft presentation/ overview of their emerging draft masterplans.  This is to see how well you are progressing towards completing your project and also an opportunity to get feedback on your proposals and ideas.

Participation

The studio-based approach to learning, extensive research design and fieldwork activities, and group-based assignments that underpin this unit necessitates that students attend ALL classes - ideally in-person - on Mondays and Fridays. There are 2 full days of fieldwork - Fri 26 Aug (8.30-5.00pm) and Sat 27 Aug (9.00-5.00pm) - that all students are required to attend.
Students will work in their consultancy groups throughout the semester. The overall aim is for you to build rapport and close working relationships with one another so that everyone is clear about the role and responsibilities and the need to work for the greater good of the group. 
Time has been set aside within the teaching schedule for students to come together and work in their consultancy groups. In addition, students will need to come together to work on their masterplan projects outside of normal teaching times, especially towards the end of the semester when you need to submit your poster, deliver your presentation and write-up and submit your final report.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
