Academic Information

Content This unit investigates the rapidly changing conceptions of “Matter” or material innovation in design, through digital fabrication and 1:1 prototyping. Within this context, students will engage and apply new digital or robotic fabrication methods and technologies, prototyping methods, and experimental material development, culminating in 1:1 prototypes or assemblages exhibiting bespoke properties. Students will research the relation between material properties and specific prototyping methods (CNC, 3D printing, robotic fabrication, etc.) and apply this research towards ‘learning by making' to design, develop and prototype functional or “responsive” material assemblies. Project development and outcomes will explore and apply contemporary topics relating to material craft, digital fabrication, and emerging technologies such as robotic fabrication and smart and responsive materials. The overall academic objective of the unit is to develop a critical rethinking of “Material,” towards a new approach to design and making, combining both analog and digital craft. Material innovation and prototyping topics and projects provide a critical and practical foundation for students to engage twenty-first century design practices, whereby the designer is involved in the development of new tools, processes, materials and prototypes.

Unit Information

This unit investigates the rapidly changing conceptions of “Matter” or material innovation in design, through Robotic + Digital fabrication and 1:1 prototyping, with applications in architectural design.
Within this context, students will engage and apply new digital or robotic fabrication methods and technologies, prototyping methods, and experimental material development, towards 1:1 prototypes or assemblages exhibiting bespoke properties. 
Face to Face learning + making will take place primarily in the [Clough] Robot lab on the UWA main campus, with additional tools and processes available through the School of Design workshop. 
Intuitive digital workflows will utilize Rhino + grasshopper for control of UR5 robot arms, and custom tools to facilitate material exploration design. Students will not need to know any code or scripting, however the unit is both digitally intensive and requires the ability to work in the lab or workshop to accomplish group + individual prototyping of material assemblages.
New in 2022, we  will also explore the potential of “Mixed-Reality or AR assisted fabrication, as a new way to combine Intuition and Process as a way of making.
Students will research the relation between material properties and specific digitally controlled prototyping methods (Robotic Stacking / Bending / Weaving / Material Deposition, etc.) and apply this research towards Learning by Making, to design, develop and prototype material assemblies with architectural applications.
Project development and outcomes will explore and apply contemporary topics relating to material craft, complex geometries, and the relation between intuition and procedure, improvisation, and precision.
The overall academic objective of the unit is to develop a critical rethinking of “Material,” towards a new approach to design and making, combining both analog and digital craft.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

Teaching + Learning Strategy Note No. 1: Research
Primary Research Materials may include reference textbooks available in the UWA Library via unit readings, Journals available in the EDFAA library, or specific online resources suggested by the unit coordinator.
Students are expected to analyse and graphically interpret research material using your own diagrams, drawings or models. Merely copying and pasting online images, etc. or re-presenting online image search results are not considered acceptable forms of documenting research sources.
Teaching + Learning Strategy Note No. 2: Robotic Crafting
The use of 6-Axis (UR5) Robot Arms for student project development, will be required, with access provided for the CLOUGH lab in the UWA School of Engineering. Alternative Tools + Processes may be assigned contingent on the capacity of the lab to support specific project outcomes. Material-Process workflows and associated lectures, workshops, etc. will be adjusted accordingly, to support project outcomes, based on limitations regarding the availability of the robot arms during the semester.
Students using the robot lab will be required to bring a laptop with Rhino 6 or 7 (Windows version preferred, MAC version only if necessary) and associated plugins (Robots, etc.). We have a single Lab Computer available as a shared resource, however this is not a substitute for your own computer. Please contact the unit coordinator if this presents a problem, to consider possible alternatives.
Teaching + Learning Strategy Note No. 3: Digital Fabrication
Students may need to utilise the School of Design Workshop,  to support secondary processes (Casting, Spraying, CNC Milling, 3D Printing, etc.) that are not possible in the Robot Lab. 
However these are considered additional or alternative resources to be approved in consultation with the unit coordinator; the primary tools for this unit are the UR5e Robot Arms + Custom tools.
Teaching + Learning Strategy Note No. 4: Lean Innovation
Students are expected to understand and apply basic methods and principles introduced in tutorials and workshops, and develop additional, individual expertise (both digital and material-process) as part of the project development, extending and developing basic methods or instruction towards processes that are developed and refined as needed to prepare initial prototypes and final assemblage outcomes.
Learning objectives will require the ability to work both Independently and in Designated Teams, to accomplish the following project related outcomes:
• Research of contemporary Digital + Robotic Crafting methods, examples and case studies, and associated software, hardware or material requirements.
•Robotic Crafting of Material, combining a variety of tools and digital + physical workflows, minimising the necessity for manual craft, and emphasising economy of material in the development process, to produce initial prototypes.
• Integration and Refinement of Digital + Material Processes and initial prototypes, towards precise, well-crafted final assemblies and outcomes, based on an approved procedure or workflow.

Participation

* Face To Face Attendance is essential for this unit, as the primary means of instruction and participation in all tutorials and workshops.
This is the primary means of unit content delivery, collaborative skill-building, access to required equipment and lab, and consultation with your Unit Coordinator and Project Team.
Use of the the Robotics Lab and related equipment during scheduled class times, is essential for building the skills and knowledge necessary for completion of assessments!
Additional time in the Robot Lab (and/or School of Design Workshop) outside of weekly scheduled classes will be necessary, to support project development and completion.
* Active Participation during scheduled class times requires students to be prepared during attendance, with relevant soware or materials, completed readings, completed safety inductions, etc. in preparation for in-class activities and project development. Lack of participation or preparation will be recorded and will be considered as a component of the assessment process.
* Team Coordination of Group activities, assignments or assessments require that each team member both coordinates with others, and takes individual responsibility for preparation & development of projects at each class meeting. This includes time- sensitive coordination with team members to order materials, share digital files or models with team members, develop workflows, etc. to make effective use of scheduled (workshop) time.
Unexcused Absences:
Do not expect lectures or content missed through unjustified absence to be delivered or revisited by the instructor outside of the scheduled class time.
Students who miss a tutorial or workshop session will be responsible for obtaining any missed content, handouts, project briefs, assignments, etc. to prepare for the following class.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "honest, ethical and responsible scholarship". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
