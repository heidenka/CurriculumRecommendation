Academic Information

Content This unit examines major social, political and economic developments that have taken place in twentieth century Korea, and the challenges and opportunities that these have created for the two Koreas. The unit investigates national identity discourses and ideas of ethnic nationalism in a divided (and increasingly multi-ethnic) country. Special attention is also given to conceptualisations of gender, body and sexuality that draw on both 'traditional' and more recent neo-liberal discourses of the same, and how these inform such increasingly commonplace bodily practices such as cosmetic surgery, as well as cultural representations of gender and subjectivity in popular culture. The unit will also examine how culturally specific social structures, such as generation, kinship and perceived social status, affect patterns of interpersonal communication and relations. Students evaluate how the combination of indigenous philosophical traditions, the legacy of Japanese colonialism, the presence of US military forces on the peninsula, the civil war and the influx of competing Cold War ideologies have shaped contemporary South and North Korean societies. Throughout the unit, students are encouraged to actively engage in class through interactive interactive workshop tasks and design a short in-depth research project on a topic of their choice but relevant to the themes covered in workshops. This unit is taught entirely in English, and no prior knowledge of Korean language is assumed.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

This semester's unit will consist of a mixture of activities with both face-to-face or online options, online learning activities (assessed online tasks, videos to watch before class), and things to prepare before coming to class so that we can focus on active learning approaches. Note that the seminars are not recorded because of our focus on active and 'flipped classroom' learning. If you cannot attend, please engage with the online learning materials to keep up with what you're expected to learn.
Please check each week's learning tasks well in time before your allocated workshop. Some of the tasks (short recorded lectures, videos for discussion and required readings) must be completed before each workshop for you to make the most of each week's learning tasks in class. Others have a deadline by when you need to complete them (see assessments).
For assignment advice and guidance, please see the assessment section in the LMS site for this unit. If you are unsure about any aspect of the assessments please do not hesitate to contact me.

Unit Structure

This unit is taught primarily in class workshop format with both a face-to-face and online option available. In addition, there is a recorded lecture  to watch every week in advance of the workshop. Refer to the Unit Schedule for topics and dates.

Participation

Seminar participation in discussions (15%)
Students will be assessed on their attendance at and participation in the workshops over the course of the semester. Particular attention will be placed on evidence of reading and critical reflection on the set questions and references; as well as on how well you have prepared in advance for the tasks set in the above course outline. You will be expected to have read the ‘Required Readings’  for each week and it is assumed that you will be familiar with the materials at the start of each seminar.  Participation is not the same as attendance – an HD mark for this component can only be achieved through active participation in discussions and through showing evidence of prior preparation. Passive participation with an occasional input is likely to attract a ‘C’ grade for this component.
Marking criteria for the online tasks is clearly indicated in the rubric under the Assessments tab on LMS, but if still in doubt, please don't hesitate to ask for clarification.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "honest, ethical and responsible scholarship". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.

Special Considerations

If your study has been adversely affected by illness or other significant circumstances outside of your control, you can apply for special consideration. Significant circumstances may include but are not limited to:

  • serious illness or death of a member of the student's immediate family or household or of a close friend
  • serious injury
  • being a victim of a crime
  • breakdown of relationship
  • sudden loss of income or employment
  • serious disruption to domestic arrangements
