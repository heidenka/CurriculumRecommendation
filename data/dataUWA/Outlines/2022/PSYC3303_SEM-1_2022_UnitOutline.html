Academic Information

Content Real-world problems can benefit greatly from applying psychological research to their solutions. Yet policymakers have not always made use of this research and psychologists have not always communicated it in a usable form. This results in less effective solutions and under-utilised research. This unit consists of an exploration of how psychological theories, methods, and evidence can be used to assist in understanding and solving contemporary social problems. It is also designed to enable students to develop the skills necessary to present this information to policymakers. The unit introduces students to some of the major social problems in contemporary society. The topics vary from year to year as key issues and debates in society and in the field of psychology change but may include climate change, gender inequality, social conflict, and educational disadvantage. In addition to lectures on these modern social problems, students are also be encouraged to identify and explore some real-world problems that interest them through the tutorials and assignments. The unit prepares students for further research in psychology with an applied focus, and for employment in organisations - including business, government and the non-government sector - whose work requires the application of psychology to policy development. It will also ensure that students understand the limits of such research. Although a lot is now known about human behaviour from fundamental, often laboratory-based research, there is still relatively little evidence about how these research findings can actually be applied to influence the behaviour of communities and populations.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

This unit takes a blended approach, with lectures, online content, and tutorials. 
Lectures and online content will deal with a range of social problems to which psychological theories, methods and research findings are relevant. The content will focus both on a description and broad analysis of the selected social problems, as well as the application of psychological science to these problems. The aim of the lectures and online content is to advance understanding about complex social problems, help you appraise the key psychological aspects which contribute to this problem, and help you judge which of these are relevant to solving or relieving the problem. Overall, this content will show how scientific psychological investigation can assist in resolving societal debates.
In the tutorials, you will discuss a social problem in-depth, and identify the psychological constructs   that are relevant to understanding the problem and/or to intervening to change the problem. Through interactive dialogues in these  tutorials, you will learn to communicate in a way that translates psychological theory into practical application for both lay and professional audiences. In and outside of the tutorials, you will work together in small groups to design an intervention based on psychological research and theory to solve a social problem chosen by the group. The group interventions will then be translated to presentations that will be given in the last tutorial. This group assignment will teach you how to collaborate in problem-focused think tanks to devise solutions to real-life problems.

Unit Structure

LECTURES
Lectures will deal with a range of social problems to which psychological theories, methods and research findings are relevant. For each social problem addressed, the first half of the lecture content will focus on a description and broad analysis of the problem, while the second half will explore the application (and lack thereof) of psychological science to these problems. 
Most of the lectures will be given by Dr Notebaert, but guest lecturers will also contribute. These guest lecturers will highlight some of the research and applied work that is conducted at the School of Psychological Science in specific problem areas, and/or will share their experience in working in the field of policy making. 
Although the lectures will be recorded and made available on LMS, it is strongly recommended that students attend. Previous years’ results indicate that students who attend lectures in-person consistently achieve better results than those who do not.
TUTORIALS
Tutorials will be held most weeks (see unit schedule below). In the majority of the tutorials, you will discuss a social problem in-depth, and identify the psychological constructs that are relevant to understanding the problem and/or to intervening to change the problem. You will also get to practice communicating how psychological research can help address complex social problems through interactive dialogues. In the last tutorial, students deliver their group presentations to their fellow students.
ASSESSMENTS
Individual Policy report (30%):
Report writing is an essential skill for all psychology graduates. This assignment is designed, in part, to give you the experience of writing a report based on a selected problem area and making it suitable for a non-specialist audience seeking to comprehend the processes important to understanding and altering the identified behaviour.
The policy report is different than the lab reports you have written for other units, as it is designed for a lay audience, rather than an academic audience. Requirements for the content of this report, suggestions for its structure and format, as well as the marking guide, are provided on LMS. 
Presentations (30%): 
The group presentation assessment is designed to enable you to learn and practice the skills necessary to work in teams to produce policy proposals suitable for presentation to government, business or other interested parties. The emphasis is on translating relevant psychological theory and research into a format and language that can be easily understood by such groups without doing violence to scientific accuracy and integrity.  
Requirements for the content of this presentation, suggestions for its structure and delivery, as well as the marking guide, are provided on LMS.
Final test -Take Home Assessment (40%):
The final test will consist of a case study which will be released to you through LMS. You will have several days to provide a written analysis of this case study, which will need to be submitted trough Turnitin. The case will be a description of a modern world social problem, or a policy proposal/intervention. You will be asked to comment on this case by answering a set of questions in a way that conveys your grasp of the learning outcomes of this unit. This can include, but is not limited to, conducting a problem analysis, researching the contribution of psychological constructs, proposing a policy recommendation, and/or critiquing an existing policy. 
Information on how to prepare for the final test can be found on LMS, as well as an example of a previously used case study.

Participation

TUTORIALS
Participation in the tutorials encourages greater in-depth processing of the course material, and application of learned material in a variety of ways. In addition, active participation in the tutorials will teach you the communication skills for translating psychological theory and research into practical application.
Tutorial attendance is not mandatory, however attendance will add substantially to your learning. This unit is different to all other Psychology units, as it requires a different way of thinking and communicating. The tutorials are designed to be interactive and give you the opportunity to practice this way of thinking and communicating, which will improve your performance in the assessments in this unit. In addition, tutorial attendance will help you learn through observing others and reflecting on their performance. Moreover, by attending tutorials you will be able to get to know your tutor better, as well as your fellow students who you will be working with in your group project. For all these reasons, tutorial attendance is strongly encouraged. After all, it is clear to us year after year that those who participate actively in totals obtain better grades in this unit.
Dyadic discussions: Each tutorial requires you to come prepared to speak about at least one psychological construct relevant to understanding and/or ameliorating a part of the social problem for that week. In each tutorial, students will be randomly selected to serve as interviewers and interviewees. The interviewer will ask the interviewee to explain the social issue, describe a relevant psychological construct, and outline how it can be targeted to help address the social issue. These will be brief, informal discussions, facilitated by the tutor. There are no marks associated with these discussions, as the aim is to practice this style of communicating in a low-stakes environment.  
Group discussions: For some tutorials, scenarios are listed on LMS that describe a particular challenge relevant to the social problem that is examined that week. You will need to come prepared to take part in the discussion on how to respond to that challenge. Prepared students will refer to the previous week’s lectures and also to relevant papers/readings. Formulating responses to these scenarios will also help prepare you think critically about these social problems and the way we can address them through policy, which is a critical learning outcome needed for this unit’s assessments. 
GROUP WORK
By week two you will be assigned to a group in your tutorial. In and outside of the following tutorials, you will work together to design an intervention based on psychological research and theory to solve a social problem chosen by the group. You can choose to present this intervention in your Individual Policy Report, or you can choose a different social problem. The group interventions will then be translated to presentations that will be given in the last tutorial. To help navigate this group work assignment successfully, please refer to the SPS Guide to Working in Groups which can be found on LMS (in the Tutorials folder).
This Guide to Working in Groups will help you prevent any problems in the group work process, or will enable you to tackle problems early should they arise. However, sometimes issues occur which cannot be resolved without assistance from the Unit Coordinator. If this is the case, you need to report this to the UCs at least <<<10 days>>> before your scheduled group presentation (email psyc3303-sps@uwa.edu.au), to ensure there is sufficient times to resolve these issues. Keeping records of commitments and meetings, as suggested in the Guide to Working in Groups, will be invaluable to working towards a satisfactory solution of group problems. 

Referencing Requirements

POLICY REPORT
For this report we do not restrict the way in which references need to be done to one particular pre-defined way, because policy reports vary widely (and wildly) in how their referencing is done, and we want you to have this flexibility as well. It means you can use any referencing style that you think suits the lay-out of your report. As such, there will only be two things we care about when we look at your references: 
1.	References are done in a consistent manner throughout the text, and
2.	The source of the information can be clearly identified somewhere in the report (e.g. when referencing a journal article, somewhere -whether in a footnote or in a list at the end- the reference needs to include more than just the author(s) and year). 
This means you can use a referencing style such as APA, Vancouver (a numbered style), a system where you put references in footnotes. In general, we encourage a reference style such as Vancouver (https://guides.library.uwa.edu.au/vancouver), because your text will not be cluttered with names and dates which could be distracting for your readers.
FINAL TEST
References in the final take-home assessment need to be in APA7 (https://guides.library.uwa.edu.au/apa).

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
