Academic Information

Content Students learn how the theory of plant and animal breeding and biotechnology is applied in practice through analysing real- world cases. This unit improves communication skills and analytical skills and is taught in modules. Each module or case is taught in blocks of two weeks. Experienced professionals with special expertise in an area of genetics and genomics relevant to breeding and biotechnology deliver the lectures. Only revolutionary, successful and applicable cases in breeding and biotechnology are chosen and presented in the unit. The lecturer responsible for each module gives students a recent publication in a contemporary area of development in breeding and biotechnology and a three-hour lecture/discussion, clearly contextualising the focused area in the big picture of breeding and biotechnology. Students read the paper, participate in the lecture/discussion, and find two related papers in the same area from the literature. Based on the materials/information given and found, students write a one page commentary critically analysing the case, including the genetic foundation, process of development and successful application in the industry. Students may also comment on what they have learned, critical issues and possible future directions. Students also have a chance to give a five-minute oral presentation for each module.

Unit Information

The unit is taught in modules. Each module or case is taught in blocks within 2 weeks. Experienced professionals with special expertise in areas of genetics and genomics relevant to breeding and biotechnology deliver the lectures. Only revolutionary, successful and applicable cases in breeding and biotechnology are chosen and presented in the unit. It is expected that the lecturers responsible for each module give students a recent publication in a contemporary area of development in breeding and biotechnology and a 2-3 hour lecture/discussion to students, clearly illustrating the focused area in the big picture of breeding and biotechnology. The lecturer will highlight a “focus paper”.  After reading the focus paper and participating in the lectures/discussions, students are expected to find two related papers in the same area. With appropriate in-text citation of the focus paper and two related papers, students must write a one page commentary critically analysing the case, including the genetic foundation, the process of development and the successful application in industry. Students may also comment on what they have learned, critical issues and possible future directions. Students are also expected to give a 5 minute talk at the end of each module. The commentary and the 5 minutes talk are marked separately. It is proposed that the following five modules will be given in 2022.
1.	 Genetic diversity, the basis of natural and human-directed evolution.  Traditionally animal and plant breeding has relaid on crosses between domesticated forms and selection leading to the erosion of genetic diversity, making new genetic gains difficult. In this module we will discuss current approaches to evaluating and increasing genetic diversity, their advantages and limitations. Students will then work on their (animal, crop or microbe) species of interest and research its adaptive eco-geographic distribution, the status of the relevant germplasm resources, or its genetic diversity in both cultivated and wild forms, and formulate opportunities for its improvement. – Dr Judith Lichtenzveig 
2.	Breeding in the post-genomic era. DNA sequencing technologies have radically changed routine approaches in breeding and biotechnology. The achievement of the first human genome took ca. 10 years and substantial investment by several countries; today it takes about a day and one millionth of its original cost. Once we have sequenced a species genome, do we now know it all? Is there a point to genetic studies in this context? What skills do future breeders, researchers, or biotechnologists are likely to require? Students are encouraged to work on their (animal, crop or microbe) species of interest and evaluate existing genomic and other ‘omics’ databases, match these to existing germplasm resources and propose an improvement strateies for key traits of agricultural interest – Dr Judith Lichtenzveig
3.	Breeding better animals by the understanding of interactions between host and microbial communities in the gastrointestinal tract Animals supply us with important products such as meat, milk and eggs. They grow and develop using the energy and substances from what they eat. There is a large community of microorganisms in the gastrointestinal tract, which breaks down the feed, either promoting or limiting animal growth, especially in ruminant animals. It is well demonstrated that the best combination of the host and microbial communities at the genetic and genomic levels can facilitate animal production – Professor Phil Vercoe
4.	Genomic selection in animal and plant breeding. This is a module about a new approach for improving quantitative and qualitative traits in large animal and plant breeding populations that uses whole‐genome molecular markers with high density and high‐throughput genotyping and phenotyping – Professor Wallace Cowling and Dr Renu Saradadevi
5.	Genetically modified organisms – science and impact in agriculture. This module will start from basic foundations of GM to the successful use of GM animals for production efficiency, GM crops for insect resistance and herbicide tolerance as well as golden rice. A big picture of the past, present, and future of GM and its limitations will be discussed – Professor Guijun Yan

Learning And Teaching Strategy

Two half-days every second week will be allocated to the Unit. The first half-day will be used for the introduction of the Unit, including the purpose of the unit, some background foundations, and the suggested approaches in learning the Unit; and the second half-day will be used for distribution of the paper, lectures, and discussions of the first module. Students can use the rest of the time to search for papers and read the papers given and found. For the subsequent weeks, the first half-day will be used for the summary of the finishing module and the second half-day will be used to start the next module i.e. distribution of the paper, lectures, and discussions of the next module. The final half-day will be used for the overall summary, SURF, and SPOT tests.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
