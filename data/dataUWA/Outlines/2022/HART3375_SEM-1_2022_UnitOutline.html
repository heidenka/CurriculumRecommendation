Academic Information

Content This unit provides students with the unique possibility to engage with art that is relevant to their lives today by exploring art of the twenty-first century. Particular focus is devoted to contemporary practice and thought about art that has formed within a technologically rich context. Attention is given to discourses surrounding artistic responses to the world today and to how particular ecological, political, social and technological concerns of the twenty-first century inform artistic practice. The unit provides essential tools for researching and recognising alternative art histories to identified dominant narratives. It incites an awareness of the key debates surrounding the intersection of art and the twenty-first century world. Students also develop the general ability to conduct independent research in novel areas and an ability to think creatively and clearly construct and express new ideas and arguments.

Unit Information

This unit explores the ideas, practices, discourses and concepts relevant to art, curation and art theory occuring in the twenty-first century. Instead of subscribing to one preferred narrative or historical term (such as 'Contemporary Art'), the approach we will be taking on in this unit will be a relativist and at points suspicious position toward the putatively continuous grand narratives of the twentieth century: of (post-)modernism, (neo-)liberalism, globalism, technological utopia. In the twenty-first century, artists have ostensibly taken on the role of 'cultural mirrors', where major events and discourse are taken not only as a subject for artistic practice, but as a motivation to intervene on mainstream forms of mediation and representation. 
Across our classes, we will explore three broad topics — The current state of tension between images and objects; Ecology and the 'Anthropocene'; The Internet, data, control and surveillance. While these topics do not exhaust what can be said on art in the twenty-first century, it is our contention that these major themes will work to answer the question of narratives and their survival into the new millennium, and will draw out the importance of art history as a tool to respond to a world of cultural and political acceleration. 
This unit aims to guide students through the material, political, historical and conceptual complexities that are maintained and produced across art in the twenty-first century. To enter into the debates and discourse surrounding the contemporary art world is to enter into a multi-disciplinary dialogue ranging widely across the humanities and the sciences. Critical theory, philosophy, environmental science, technology, politics and economics all come into play in this unit. The art historical methodology cultivated in this unit will be advocated for as a unique vantage position in the contemporary era of dense visual politics.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

Classes will be delievered face-to-face on campus, and online via MS Teams.

Unit Structure

The Unit Structure is as follows:
Lecture (two hour): the lecture will draw out and explore the ideas, references and concepts according to the week's assigned content. Lecture notes and the slides will be available via LMS after it has been presented.
Tutorial (one hour): The tutorial session will be used to host group and individual presentation and discussion on the unit content, following the lecture. Each week, the LMS will be updated to provide suggestions for presentation topics. However, students are encouraged to present on topics of their choice, so long as it remains relevant to the unit content. You can be marked down for presenting or leading a discussion that is irrelevant to the week's topic and content, although tangential and contextually relevant but conceptually diverse topics are certainly encouraged. It is a good idea to consult with your unit coordinator if your are unsure on your presentations.

Participation

Weekly preparation for classes include reading and research based on content posted on the LMS (see 'Announcements'). Group work and presentations will be carried out in each tutorial. Students are marked on participation in these presentations across the entire semester. 
This includes attending tutorials, leading discussions, presenting ideas and responding to other students. In the LMS, students can post brief written comments on the unit content prior to tutorials as an alternative form of participation. 
If you are unable to make it to class - either online or in person - please email your unit coordinator. If you would like to arrange an alternative assessment, please email your unit coordinator as soon as possible.

Referencing Requirements

It is important to reference consistently, no matter what style guide you are working from. See the Library's online guides for further information: https://guides.library.uwa.edu.au/referencinguwa/styles
The School of Design prefers the Harvard style, but for this unit we are happy for you to go with your own preference, so long as it is consistent across your submission. You will be marked down for inconsistent or incorrect referencing. 
Use sources from traditionally non-academic / not peer-reviewed sources sparingly: an overuse of such references will reduce your mark. It is encouraged that you find peer-reviewed and standardised sources to produce your research from. We will cover this in class, but please email your unit coordinator if you have any questions or concerns.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
