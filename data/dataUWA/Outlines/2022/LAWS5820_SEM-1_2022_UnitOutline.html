PANDEMIC TEACHING ARRANGEMENTS 

UWA Law School continues to value and prioritise face-to-face learning. However, due to the ever-evolving pandemic, protecting our student body and minimising the disruption to student learning, the student experience, and your academic progression is our priority. In light of this, after having considered a range of options, students have been given the option to attend face-to-face OR online tutorials (via Microsoft Teams) for the duration of this semester.

In the event of future COVID-19 restrictions preventing face-to-face classes, all lectures and tutorials will be delivered online for the relevant period.

COVID PROTOCOLS

The Law School has developed protocols to deal with those instances where a student/teacher has been in a face-to-face lecture or tutorial and, following the class, isolates and undergoes a COVID-19 PCR test.

1. The student/teacher should inform their Unit Coordinator and the Dean that they are isolating and undergoing a COVID-19 PCR test for any reason - they are symptomatic, are a close contact, were at an exposure site, etc.

2. The student/teacher must advise the Unit Coordinator and the Dean immediately on receipt of the outcome of that PCR test.

3. If the PCR test result is negative, no further action is required.

4. If the PCR test result is positive and the person involved is a student, the student should complete the University's COVID Positive Student Register and will be directed to UWA's COVID-19 website, outlining available support at the University and links to WA Government resources.   

Student Register: https://uwa-advocate.symplicity.com/care_report/index.php/pid182774?

UWA COVID-19 website: https://www.uwa.edu.au/students/Supporting-you-during-COVID-19

5. Others who were in the class with the COVID positive person will be advised, maintaining the anonymity of the COVID positive person.

6. In standard lectures and tutorials (which are typically either 45 mins or 1 hr 30 mins) provided compliance with the mask mandate, under current Government guidelines, others in class will not be considered close contacts. However, they should be encouraged to monitor their health and isolate and get tested if they display COVID symptoms.

7. The WA Department of Health contact tracing team will contact all close contacts.

8. If it is a student that is COVID positive, teaching will continue as normal, with the COVID positive student isolating and attending online classes in all their units.

9. If the teacher is COVID positive, they too must isolate. In this instance, students will be advised of the alternative teaching arrangements.

10. And, finally, please remember that masks are mandatory in all internal areas across the University unless they meet WA Government exemptions. Masks must be worn to securely cover the nose and mouth.

Academic Information

Content This unit gives students an understanding of how legislation is made and, once in force, how it is to be read, understood and interpreted. The unit has an emphasis on interpretation in practice. The topics discussed include the legislative process, drafting, common law and legislative interpretative principles, the relationship between statutes and the common law, how to use extrinsic materials, the effect of amendments, the relationship between a statute and its delegated legislation, and comparisons to the interpretation of non-statutory instruments, such as contracts.

Unit Information

Statutes and statutory interpretation law are critical to legal practice. There are now very few areas of law that are not significantly regulated by legislative provisions. To be properly prepared for legal practice, or any work related to law, it is essential that you have a sound understanding of how to read, interpret and apply legislation, and of the role that statutory interpretation plays in our legal system as a whole.
Many eminent members of the legal profession have highlighted the importance of this area. For example:
-->Hon. Robert French (former Chief Justice, High Court of Australia): “It is of fundamental importance that all law graduates, whether going into practice or other forms of law job, should be aware of the central significance of statutes in the Australian legal system...…They should be able to apply the provisions of a statute to a particular fact situation and be able to make inferences about rights, powers, duties and liabilities which may arise out of that application.” [‘Bending Words: The Fine Art of Interpretation’ (Speech delivered at Legislation in Practice Guest Lecture Series, Law School, University of Western Australia, 20 March 2014, 17].
-->Justice Leeming (Court of Appeal, Supreme Court of New South Wales): “What is commonly thought of as ‘common law’, namely, the various bodies of judge-made law…taught in law schools and written about in law books is and always has for the most part been sourced in statute and is unintelligible without reference to statute.” [‘Theories and Principles Underlying the Development of the Common Law – The Statutory Elephant in the Room’ (2013) 36(3) UNSW Law Journal 1002, 1004].
-->Hon. Michael Kirby (former Justice, High Court of Australia): “Interpretation…is an activity that most lawyers are regularly engaged in. If you don’t enjoy resolving problems about the meaning of words, you should probably think of a career swap…law is expressed in words…words and their meaning constitute the essence of it.” [Foreword, P Herzfeld and T Prince, Statutory Interpretation Principles (Thomson Reuters, 2014) v]
This unit addresses statutory interpretation law. In the context of learning about the three foundational concepts of text, context, and purpose, and more specific and controversial interpretative principles, the unit also highlights the broader role of statutory interpretation in our legal system. This is because, as well as being pivotal to legal practice, the function of statutory interpretation is critical in determining the boundary between the judiciary, the legislature and the executive.
This unit can be a challenging one for some students, especially in the early part of the semester, for three main reasons. First, it necessarily approaches the law in a way that may seem different to the legal studies you have engaged in so far, which often focus on formulating the principle of a judicial decision. Second, while most of the concepts in statutory interpretation law are not conceptually difficult, there is only a broad framework for the method of application of those concepts. Statutory interpretation competence requires practice at weighing and balancing of interpretative factors. Third, it will take us the first few weeks to cover the foundation concepts, which interact and provide the cornerstone of the law. This may mean that the overall nature of the law and the method will not start to 'gel' for you until week 4 or 5 of semester.

LECTURES
It is essential that students keep up to date with lectures on a weekly basis. Statutory interpretation law consists of concepts that build on each other. Putting together this law is like assembling pieces of a puzzle. It is not like some other areas of law where there are discrete and separate topics. Therefore, to effectively study, you must engage with each topic weekly as subsequent areas of study will cumulatively build on prior areas.
You are encouraged to attend lectures in person, as being able to participate in practice questions, ask and listen to questions and answers, and hear directly from the lecturer will enhance your learning.
If you do rely on a lecture recording, you must remember that you are not permitted to:
i. pass any lecture recordings to any other person by any means.
ii. publish, distribute, broadcast or sell any lecture recordings in any form (this includes, but is not limited to, the internet and hard copy publication).
Student behaviour of this type is a breach of the University’s copyrights and constitutes misconduct.
TUTORIALS
Please note that tutorials commence in the first week of semester. Please ensure that you have been allocated a tutorial group through the class allocation system (CAS) by the start of semester. It is important that you attend the tutorial group that you have been allocated by CAS. If, due to an exceptional and unforeseen event or circumstance, you need to attend another tutorial group for one tutorial then please contact the Unit Coordinator to arrange this.
Tutorials will primarily take the form of ‘hands on’ workshops applying the law addressed in lectures. There will also be opportunities to discuss some particular principles in depth, given their importance and relevance to our system of government.
It is extremely important that you prepare for, and attend, tutorials. Lectures can only provide you with the foundation - they are to guide you in your study, not to provide comprehensive information. The tutorial readings and exercises are to enable you to engage with the law and ideas we address in the lectures more meaningfully and in greater depth. Tutorials provide students with the most significant opportunity to put what we are learning into practice through hypothetical problems, case studies and analysis of principles and theories. Becoming proficient in statutory interpretation requires ‘doing it’ and practising that ‘doing.’
To facilitate the assessment of tutorial participation in online tutorials, students attending online tutorials must enable their cameras.

Participation

There are six tutorials over the semester. Although usually students would be expected to attend ALL SIX tutorials, due to the pandemic the Law School policy for semester one 2022 is that  students can miss, without penalty, a maximum of ONE tutorial where tutorials are held fortnightly.  Students won’t need to apply for special consideration or inform unit coordinators/tutors of their absence and the final tutorial mark calculation will ignore that ONE missed tutorial. 
In accordance with Law School policy (see further under 'Other'), external work, travel or sporting commitments are not accepted as grounds for swapping tutorials.
Students will be given a mark out of 10 for each tutorial. This will be converted to an overall mark out of 10 at the end of semester. Students will be awarded a mark on the basis of their preparation for, and participation in, tutorials in accordance with the following criteria. Please note that assessment of participation is about the quality of contribution, not necessarily the quantity of contributions (where ‘quality’ refers to considered, clear, and reflective contributions – you are allowed to make mistakes!). 
HD (8 to 10 marks): Student shows excellent preparation for all tutorials; exceptional standard of participation with contributions being clear, substantive and insightful; student is punctual, involved in discussion, and listens respectfully to others; student shows excellent command of subject matter with articulate, well-constructed arguments.
D (7 to 7.5 marks): Student is well prepared for tutorials; contributions to class discussion are clear, relevant and well considered; student is punctual, involved in discussion, and listens respectfully to others; student shows good understanding of subject matter and arguments are informed and thoughtful.
CR (6 to 6.5 marks): Student is adequately prepared for all tutorials; attends and is punctual, shows interest, contributes to discussion and contributions are generally correct and mostly clear.
P (5 to 5.5 marks): Student is not adequately prepared for most tutorials; attends; contributions to discussion are mostly adequate but reflect little understanding of the issues raised by the tutorials.
F (0 to 4.5): Student attends but may be late, shows little or no interest, contributions or preparation are inadequate; or student does not attend.
Students who do not attend a tutorial will be given a mark of 0 out of 10 for that tutorial unless there is special consideration granted.
NOTE: Students may perform at a level above or below some of the elements of each level descriptor. In that case, the mark will be allocated on an overall weighing of how the student performs.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
