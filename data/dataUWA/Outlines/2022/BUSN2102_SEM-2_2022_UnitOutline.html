Academic Information

Content This unit highlights how innovation and entrepreneurship are solving major global social and economic challenges such as hunger, poverty, homelessness and intergenerational disadvantage and unemployment. It considers the rise of social entrepreneurship, the shift to a focus on collaboration, and the role of corporate social responsibility, philanthro-capitalism, social finance and impact investing in creating new options for positive social impact. New tools for social change in a digital and social media world (e.g. Kiva, Kickstarter, Twitter, Facebook) are considered and trends in the measurement and demonstration of social impact are examined. The aim of the unit is for students to gain an evidence based and rich picture of what works in driving social change in the contemporary world.

Unit Information

The Business School at the University of Western Australia cordially welcomes all students enrolled in BUSN2102. Professor Paul Flatau will be the Unit Coordinator.
The vision of this unit is for students to feel empowered to affect positive social change armed with a robust understanding of today's and tomorrow's most complex social problems plus the skills to design and implement innovative and effective solutions through business.
This immersive unit will encourage and enable students to think purposefully, creatively, and even provocatively about global and Australian social, economic, and political issues. Students will gain an understanding of the theory, practice, and tools available to address these issues, and will be able to put their training into practice with the business proposal component of the unit. As an interactive unit, students will develop their ability to “report, respond, relate, reason and reconstruct”, and will involve active flipped content. This unit will allow students to develop their own pathways to change the world. Through partnerships with agencies seeking to achieve social change in many of the grand social challenges facing Australia, students will be directly engaged in community action particularly in the development of their group project.
Unit content
The subject matter in this unit covers both social problems and challenges and tools and concepts to drive social change. Included in the former are ending entrenched disadvantage, ending extreme poverty globally, closing the gap in Aboriginal outcomes and achieving sustained social progress. Among the tools and concepts we explore to drive social change are social innovation, social finance, social enterprise, social impact measurement, and theories and methods of leadership and collaboration.
Unit goals
The unit aims to increase understanding of many of the profound world and Australian social challenges and of ways to address these problems focusing on the concepts and areas of social innovation and design, entrepreneurship, and finance. The unit also seeks to develop the capacity of students to develop innovative projects in a service learning environment and to develop communication skills.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

Course content will present relevant theory, tools, and concepts, and then discuss problem-based examples and include instructions for individual and collaborative exercises. The unit is designed so that the student learning is "flipped". Content is delivered online in videos of lecture content, in sets of powerpoints, and in other material. Workshops will be face-to-face but also live streamed via teams. Tutorials will be conducted in both face-to-face mode and in interactive online sessions. Regardless of mode tutorials require active participation and application of content. Your tutors will be able to discern whether you have engaged with the online content by judging the quality of your participation. 
You may be asked to complete an evaluation during this unit: the Student Evaluation of Learning & Teaching (SELT). The SELT is optional and enables you to provide feedback about the teaching and the unit content. You will receive an email to complete three surveys for your tutor (1), the lecturer (2), and the unit overall (3). We encourage you to complete the forms as your feedback is extremely important and can be used to make changes to the unit or lecturing style when appropriate. This feedback is very valuable, is accepted without prejudice and has been instrumental in improving teaching and learning in this unit.

Unit Structure

Lectures
Student learning is "flipped" such that Lectures and learning materials are provided online, each week, for students to watch and review in their own time.
Discussion Board
The discussion board is designed to assist students with reviewing the unit content to ensure that they are prepared for synchronous activities. Each week, a discussion board activity will be released based on the lecture and learning materials. The discussion will remain on LMS for students to review when completing assessments. Additionally, please post all questions about the unit to the "Assignment Questions" and "Content Questions" discussion boards. This ensures that all students have access to the same information about the unit.
Workshops
The weekly workshop includes practical application of lecture content. In some weeks, guest representatives from social enterprises and other community organisations are invited to present on panels and answer questions submitted by students. Please note that the workshop order may deviate slightly from the unit schedule based on the availability of guests.
Tutorials
The weekly tutorial enables students to demonstrate critical thinking and ask questions. Tutorials will cover material relating to each week's content and require active participation in discussions, formal debates, quizzes and collaborative activities. Tutorials will allow students to scaffold and receive feedback on their dialogue, business concept, business plan, and pitch throughout the semester.
Dialogue
Students will conduct their own research on a social issue and present a solution that utilises business to address that issue. In place of a traditional essay, the individual assessment will be written in the form of a dialogue. Usual academic standards such as scholarship and referencing is expected.
Group project
The unit includes a group project. Groups are formed in tutorials.  The group project involves groups developing their concept of a social enterprise to meet social needs and issues covered in this unit together with a business plan, and pitch their plan in person. A pitch event will be held in each tutorial at the end of the semester. 
Please refer to the Class Allocation System (https://www.cas.uwa.edu.au/) page for the workshop and tutorial timetable.

The APA 7 style is the style used by the Business School. Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "honest, ethical and responsible scholarship". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.

If your study has been adversely affected by illness or other significant circumstances outside of your control, you can apply for special consideration. Significant circumstances may include but are not limited to:

  • serious disruption to domestic arrangements
