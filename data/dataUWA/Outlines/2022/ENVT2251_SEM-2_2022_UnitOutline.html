Academic Information

Content This unit provides a holistic treatment of the field of hydrology with a focus on process understanding in relation to the catchment water balance and topics relevant to water management. A focus is given to understanding and quantitative descriptions of the surface and subsurface components of the hydrological cycle allowing students to appreciate the fundamentals of the disciplines of both surface hydrology and hydrogeology. Students gain an understanding of a range of biophysical processes through exposition of fundamental theories and process descriptions, combined with analysis and interpretation of observed data. Contemporary topics related to water management such as managing pollution, water resource allocation and river management are introduced. In addition to lectures, assignments and computer-based data analyses, field and laboratory work exposes students to practical applications and approaches to hydrological measurements.

Unit Information

This unit provides a holistic treatment about water science. This includes a specific focus first on learning core concepts in the field of hydrology to develop a process understanding of controls on the catchment water balance, and then explores a range of application-specific topics relevant to water management. A focus is given to understanding and quantitative descriptions of the surface and subsurface components of the hydrological cycle allowing students to appreciate the fundamentals of the disciplines of both surface hydrology and hydrogeology. Students gain an understanding of a range of biophysical processes through exposition of fundamental theories and process descriptions, combined with analysis and interpretation of observed data. Contemporary topics related to water management such as managing pollution, water resource allocation and river management are introduced.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

The unit is structured as a series of sequential modules, with one running each week. The first 5 modules set the foundation by providing the fundamental underpinnings for understanding the water cycle, with accompanying quiz and practical report assessments (Items I and II below). Once students have mastered these core concepts, the remaining 7 modules look at more context-specific water management areas and develop the students critical thinking and problem solving ability (Item III below). In these later modules, contemporary topics related to water management such as managing pollution, water resource allocation and river management challenges are introduced. 
Each module is made up of essential theoretical content and example practical exercises to help students master concepts and develop awareness and experience with common techniques used by environmental consultants and catchment managers. In addition to lecture content, assignment-based activities, computer-based data analyses, field and laboratory work exposes students to practical applications and approaches to making hydrological measurements and assessing water-resource related problems.  
In general, students are expected to under-take self-directed preparation (e.g. online video and/or reading material) each week, prior to attending the Monday workshop session. Within this workshop session, we undertake a hybrid between a traditional lecture and a hands-on tutorial / activity session. Students must attend these each week to be able develop the required level of skill in assessing concepts and relevant environmental data. As skills and knowledge are built upon from module to module, it is essential that students keep up to date with the content and assessments each week. Most weekly modules also include a practical activity in the allocated Wed session, which is either a hands-on activity, a field visit, or a data-based activity.

Unit Structure

The unit is organised as a series of three thematic areas, each including a series of lectures, labs and workshop/tutorial classes, and an assignment. These are: 
 I.          Catchments & Rivers:  Hydro-climatology, catchment hydrology and surface water flows 
II.         Sub-Surface Hydrology:  Below ground hydrological processes in soil and groundwater

Referencing Requirements

REFERENCING: When completing academic work it is essential that students refer to the sources of their information within the text. Complete details of these works should be arranged alphabetically in a reference list at the end of the report/assignment. The Faculty prefers the use of either the Harvard or the APA reference style. The style chosen must be used consistently throughout any document. These may be accessed at: http://www.studentservices.uwa.edu.au/ss/learning/studying_smarter/jump_start/referencing or http://www.library.uwa.edu.au/education_training_and_support/guides Some examples as a guide: (a) Text referencing There are standard procedures for referring to authors and their work, for example: 1. Smith and Brown (2006) supported the interpretation…. 2. … when recognising two inseparable criteria of experience; continuity and interaction (Clandinin & Connelly 2000). 3. When referring to a specific page or pages of a reference: Labaree (2006 p. 17); (Labaree 2006 p.17), Labaree (2006 pp.17-18) 4. When referring to more than one work by the same author(s): Fink and Brayman (2004, 2006) 5. When referring to more than one work in the same year: Hargreaves (2005a), Hargreaves (2005b) 6. When there are TWO authors: (Tilbury & Wortman 2003); Tilbury and Wortman (2003) 7. When there are THREE or more authors: (Leithwood et al., 1999) [ Note: in the reference list, multiple authors should be listed in full.] 8. When referring to a publication that has no specific author, but which was written by an organization: (UNESCO 1992). (b) Reference list All work referenced in the text must also be included (in alphabetical order by author) in the reference list at the end of the assignment. 1. Book: Diamond, J., 2005. Guns, germs and steel: A short history of everybody for the last 13,000 years, Vintage, London. 2. Article/Chapter in a book: Fink, D., 2000. The attrition of educational change over time: The case of ‘innovative’, ‘model’, ‘lighthouse’ schools, In: The sharp edge of educational change: Teaching, leading and the realities of reform, N Bascia & A Hargreaves (Eds), Falmer Press, London, pp. 29-51. 3. Article in a journal: Hargreaves, A., 2005. Leadership succession, The Educational Forum, 69(2): 163-174. 4. Government publication: Department of Environment and Heritage, 1999. Today shapes tomorrow: Environmental education for a sustainable future, Environment Australia, Canberra. PLAGIARISM : The Faculty of Sciences has a Policy on Plagiarism which is available from the Faculty and School Offices. It is expected that work submitted for assessment by a University student is the students’ own work, and that any contribution from others (ideas, facts or direct quotations) is given suitable acknowledgement by referring to the source (in a list of references, an acknowledgements section or in some other appropriate way). If such acknowledgement is not given, the writer is guilty of plagiarism. Plagiarism is the use of published work (book, articles, reports) or the work of other students (present or past, at this or any other University), as though it were one`s own. In some cultures, it is regarded as a sign of respect to describe events or ideas in exactly the same words as an acknowledged expert. In the academic tradition to which this University belongs, it is not; it is thought of as mere copying. Special care is necessary in the case of group work. Where a single report is required from a group of several students, it will be assumed that all students have contributed. Students not happy with the contribution of other group members should make appropriate representations to the appropriate staff member. Where each student is required to hand in an individual report based on group research, work of another individual student (eg. diagrams, photographs) should be acknowledged. Unfortunately, students have been penalised by many Schools within the University, including this one, for plagiarism. If in any doubt, discuss the matter with your tutor or the unit coordinators. You may also access the plagiarism portal at http://www.studentservices.uwa.edu.au/ss/learning/studying_smarter/jump_start/referencing. Please note that plagiarism detection software may be used to check assessments.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "honest, ethical and responsible scholarship". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.
