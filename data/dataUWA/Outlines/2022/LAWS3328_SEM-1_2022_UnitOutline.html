Unit Information

This unit is an introduction to the legal framework for international trade and explains the relevant international and domestic laws which affect that activity. Emphasis is placed on the treaties and conventions which are crucial to international commercial transactions. The major areas of study are international sale of goods; settlement of commercial disputes; carriage of goods by sea; and payment in international trade. Students will gain a better appreciation of the legal complexities associated with international transactions. Students who intend to work in any area involving international trade or commerce would find this unit very useful and relevant.
Upon successful completion students will have a basic knowledge of (1) the issues that are raised when two parties from different countries enter into an international transaction for the sale of goods; (2) the laws that are applicable in relation to the contract for the sale of goods between two parties from different countries; (3) the laws that can ensure a proper resolution of disputes between parties from different countries; (4) the different methods of resolving a dispute; (5) the rules relating to transportation of goods by sea; and (6) the rules relating to payment in international trade. Students will  gain skills in (1) identifying the issues that arise when entering into a transaction with a foreign party; (2) understanding and using the relevant Conventions and laws in resolving disputes that can arise between parties involved in an international transaction such as buyer and seller,  carrier and banks involved in an international transaction; (3) legal method and writing; and (4) oral communication (through participation in tutorials on fact-based questions and 2 quizzes held during the semester.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

This unit will be taught through a combination of formal lectures and weekly small group meetings (‘tutorials’). The formal lectures will outline the key legal principles applicable to each of the listed lecture topics and will also indicate the sources of law from which those principles are derived. Students should supplement the lectures by reading the materials to be listed in Unit Readings, and any written notes that are provided by the lecturers on LMS. In tutorials, students will spend time discussing a number of hypothetical legal problem questions. The purpose of tutorials is to enable students to revise and contextualise the materials they have been learning and, at the same time, to help them develop the ability to construct principled arguments as to how those materials can be applied to the facts of a given legal problem question.  The problem questions that students learn how to analyse and answer in tutorials will be very similar to the problem questions that appear in the Final Exam.

Participation

The objectives of the participation mark are to promote:
• engagement,
• preparation,
• understanding,
• reflection; and
• responsible tutorial membership.
Engagement will most obviously be in speaking but a student might also be engaged whilst listening. This will be important to note for students who are by nature quieter. They may be engaged, though speak less often.
Preparation might be evident in notes the student has come with or in the quality of answers and comments in discussion.
Understanding of the issues raised in tutorials will most commonly be demonstrated though a student’s overall contribution to the tutorial discussion, including small group discussions within a tutorial, whether by way of answering questions, asking questions, making observations, or listening and responding to other students.
Reflection on the issues (and preparation) might be evident in the student drawing on material outside the material directly related to the tutorial problem, perhaps from other topics in the course and in questions asked during (or even after) the tutorial seminar;
Responsible tutorial membership refers to the respect given to other student’s comments and refraining from attempting to dominate the discussion.
Participation is about the quality not necessarily the quantity of contributions (where ‘quality’ means considered and reflective contributions – you are allowed to make mistakes!). The aim of including this component is to encourage unselfconscious discussion amongst students, not an atmosphere of assessment.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
