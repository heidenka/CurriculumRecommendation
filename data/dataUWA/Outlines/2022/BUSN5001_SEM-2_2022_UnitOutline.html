Academic Information

Content Blockchain is a foundational technology enabling a more efficient, secure and transparent economy. Blockchain is a particular type of a shared database, which is a subset of distributed ledger technologies (DLT). The technology allows for decentralised creation and distribution of digital assets, like cryptocurrencies and Non-Fungible Tokens (NFTs), execution of smart contracts and operation of Decentralised Autonomous Organisations (DAO) - the backbone of Decentralised Finance (DeFi) and much more. Blockchain-based innovations open enormous opportunities for new business models, entrepreneurship and employment. However, if compromised, the technology can bring opposite results than intended. The origin and the essence of blockchain is inseparable from Bitcoin (2008) and other open public blockchains, like Ethereum. Open public blockchains that are not owned and controlled by any single commercial entity, or a government offer unique properties of decentralisation, high security, and immutability. These qualities are usually compromised to achieve industrial scalability by commercially motivated entities providing ‘blockchain' (i.e. DLT) applications for business. This interdisciplinary, non-technical and introductory level unit offers comprehensive knowledge of blockchain and critical evaluation of distributed ledger technologies in business. The unit combines sound theory with practice and the latest updates from practitioners working with the technology.

Unit Information

This  interdisciplinary unit introduces students to blockchain technologies, their business applications and broader socio-economic implications. Students learn what blockchain and distributed ledger technologies are, how they work and what major problems they solve (why they came to be). Students become familiar with applications and use cases powered by blockchain technologies, including: Bitcoin and cryptocurrencies, Decentralised Finance (DeFi), Decentralised Autonomous Organisations (DAO), metaverse and tokenised digital assets including Non-Fungible Tokens (NFTs) and digital credentials among others. While the appreciation of entrepreneurial mindset and the creation of decentralised Web 3 solutions powered by open public blockchains are highly valued, students also learn about corporate and government-centred applications of private blockchains in provenance tracking, digital identities and Central Bank Digital Currencies. 
The unit balances the understanding of relevant theoretical frameworks with the development of practical skills and the exposure to real use cases presented by invited guest speakers from the industry. Students also gain introductory-level knowledge on how to safely acquire and use cryptocurrencies, use crypto wallets, test the inner working of blockchain nodes, design their NFTs, explore the metaverse and other emerging Web3 applications. Students may be asked to download and use approved mobile and desktop apps to access the Web 3 ecosystems. 
The unit is offered to non-technical audience across disciplines with no programming prerequisites. The teaching staff in the unit stands for inclusion and diversity and promotes these values in their teaching practice.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

The teaching in this unit is delivered via blended (F2F) and online learning modes. In cases of requested lockdowns due to the pandemic or other emergencies teaching will be carried out in an online mode and students are required to frequently check the LMS announcements for relevant updates. 
This unit allows students to meet the required 150 hours of learning time needed for a 6-credit UWA unit. Self-study time and work on a team project outside classes are essential for successful completion of the unit. Traditional readings are accompanied by interactive multimedia (movies, podcasts, weblog, social media) as well as short video-lectures and video interviews with professionals from the B&DLT industry.
Students will be required to engage with some of the learning material and complete their introductory profile/video before classes start. More detailed content and instructions are to be available on the LMS. Student will also participate in a novel form of assessment, which involves (decentralised) peer marking.

Unit Structure

The unit is structured around three key modules. First, students are introduced to the technical foundations and relevant theoretical frameworks behind blockchain technology (no technical skills are required). In the second module, students learn about the diverse applications of blockchain technologies and start developing their practical start-up-focused business project in teams. The final third module, deals with broader socio-economic and ethical implications of blockchain technologies and offers students an opportunity to broaden their perspectives when anticipating the evolution of the technology.
Students will be encouraged to form teams in the first seminars and start working on their self-directed project systematically as we progress through the content of different unit modules.

Participation

Participation in class, whether it is listening to a lecture or getting involved in other activities, is an important part of the learning process, therefore it is important that you attend classes. Practical activities, new skills and discussion of key readings will be conducted in class. Some assessments will also be conducted in class.  More formally, the University regulations state that ‘to complete a course or unit students shall attend prescribed classes, lectures, seminars and tutorials’. 
Regular and timely participation of all students in team work for the completion of the team assignment is vital. Students are encouraged to keep any evidence (e.g. emails, communication on social media, submitted contributions) of their regular participation and contribution to their team project throughout the semester.  The team project is a self-managed activity, i.e. the teaching staff does not intervene or assist in the team formation and team work. Students' ability to manage teamwork and resolve potential issues will be assessed. However, any sustained absence in participation or a misconduct of any team members should be timely reported to the unit coordinator by any of the other team participants. 
Where a student, due to exceptional circumstances, is unable to attend a scheduled class or timely complete an assessment, they are required to apply for special consideration at the student office. Omissions of this rule will negatively affect student's marks.

Referencing Requirements

Referencing and formatting It is important that the referencing of any sources used in your written work is done properly, if only to substantiate the points you are making in your assignment or project. The Harvard style is the preferred used unless otherwise stated by the unit coordinator. There are some notes for guidance which have been prepared by the library staff: ‘Citing your Sources Harvard Style’ (http://guides.library.uwa.edu.au/harvard). The EndNote software package is a really good system for building up a database of references. Not everyone will want to invest the time in using this system but you should consider it if you intend to build up resource materials or plan to undertake extensive research in a particular area. The library staff have also developed a tutoring package: ‘A Quick Guide to Using EndNote’ which provides the basics for using EndNote with an essay http://guides.library.uwa.edu.au/endnote 
Please use standard font size 12 and 1.5 line spacing in your written assignments. Make sure text is evenly adjusted to right and left margins. +/- 5% of word count difference (excluding references) is accepted for major written assignments. Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
