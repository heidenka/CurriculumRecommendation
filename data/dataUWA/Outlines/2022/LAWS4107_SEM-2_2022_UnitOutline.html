Academic Information

Content This unit considers in detail the Torrens System of land title registration including (1) the concept of indefeasibility of title; (2) the exceptions to indefeasibility; and (3) the nature and role of caveats and unregistered interests in the Torrens System. It explores the law regarding land transactions including mortgages, leases and easements. The unit fosters a critical and analytical approach to examining land law principles which are revealed in the case readings and in legislation. It identifies, where appropriate, ethical issues that arise in land law. It seeks to advance the core legal skills of problem solving; statutory interpretation; effective oral communication skills; and clear, concise and persuasive written legal arguments. The assessment seeks to examine these core legal skills and further provides students with the opportunity to hone their skills in case law analysis; statutory interpretation and independent learning. In addition, students participate in a reflective feedback exercise in which students are asked to reflect on an item of assessment submitted in the first semester property unit [LAWS4104 Property (formerly LAWS5104 Property)].

Unit Information

This unit fosters a critical and analytical approach to examining land law principles which are revealed in the case readings and in legislation. It identifies, where appropriate, Indigenous perspectives and ethical issues that arise in land law. The unit seeks to advance the core legal skills of: problem-solving; statutory interpretation; effective oral communication skills; and clear, concise and persuasive written legal arguments. The assessment is designed to examine these core legal skills and also provides students with an opportunity to learn material independently of formal class tuition. In addition, students are assessed on a reflective feedback exercise in which students are asked to critique their own performance in the first semester exam for Property Law - a pre-requisite unit for Land Law.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

As noted below under Unit Structure, in 2022 Land Law is structured as follows: one 2-hour lecture and one 2-hour workshop for each student per week. The Land Law unit adopts a number of teaching and learning strategies, however, three warrant particular mention: (1) robust in-class discussion which is facilitated by -  interactive lectures, weekly 2-hour workshops and extensive in-class problem solving exercises; (2) independent learning of statutory material and cases which is facilitated through the assignment; and (3) self-reflection of each student's personal exam performance in the first semester Property law exam.

Unit Structure

(a)	Lectures 
There is a 2-hour lecture each week from 2-4pm on Monday in the Social Sciences Lecture Theatre. These lectures will be recorded and will be available on LMS.  
(b)	Workshops
The Land Law group is split into four workshop groups and students are allocated to one of these groups. Each group meets once a week for a two-hour workshop. The Discussion Problem questions, found at the end of the Learning Guide and on LMS, are incorporated within the workshops. 
All of the workshops are held in the Moot Court and the times are as follows: 
(1) Wednesday 1-3pm;
(2) Wednesday 4-6pm; 
(3) Thursday 9-11am; and
(4) Thursday 1-3pm.
As the workshops are interactive, they will not be recorded.
(c)	Attendance, Participation and Note taking
Attendance at all workshops is highly recommended.  By attending classes students have the opportunity to engage with the lecturer and fellow students.  It is also essential that students prepare their own notes and do not rely on notes from previous years. The Land Law unit may be taught differently from year-to-year and the content of the unit may also change to take account of developments in the law. Note taking is a critical legal skill and it is important that accurate notes for the current year are developed by each student.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "honest, ethical and responsible scholarship". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
