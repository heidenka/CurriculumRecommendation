Unit Information

The core components of this unit are biomedical sciences, clinical skills, foundation of optometry and analysis of the literature.  
In this unit, students will gain foundational knowledge in the physical and biomedical sciences underpinning optometric practice. Students will engage in problem-based learning cases drawn from common optometric presentations, particularly those associated with developmental and refractive disorders of vision and basic pathophysiology. Team-based learning seminars will support the teaching of PBL cases. Alongside this, students will gain knowledge and skills in simple history taking and patient examination, in fulfilment of the OCANZ list of clinical competencies. The clinical skills practice sessions will assist students to form constructive relationships between vision science and clinical practice. 
Learning Outcomes
At the time of successful completion of the unit, the student should be able to:
1.	Describe the different refractive conditions associated with the human eye, and demonstrate the skills to measure these refractive errors and the dispensing of associated optical appliances;
2.	Collect, communicate and safeguard data relating to the structure and function of the eye using appropriate clinical techniques and tools; 
3.	Discuss the basics of pharmacology and microbiology, as relevant to the eye, and their uses in ocular diagnoses and therapeutics; 
4.	Correlate the basic optical principles that are relevant to light transmission and image formation by lens systems to the correction of refractive errors; and 
5.	Develop and apply skills necessary for working within a team, and for communicating and collaborating effectively with peers and colleagues.
The specific topics to be addressed in this unit include:
-	Describe the gross and fine structures of the globe and related structures of the eye and explain their role and significance in physiological function.
-	Discuss the basics of pharmacology and microbiology, as relevant to the eye, and articulate the role of common ophthalmic drugs in ocular diagnosis and therapeutics
-	Describe basic optical principles that are relevant to light transmission and image formation by lenses, lens systems and the eye
-	Articulate and explain the technical and clinical applications of basic optical principles in the production and function of ocular appliances and ophthalmic instruments
-	Develop and apply skills necessary for working within a team, and for communicating and collaborating with peers and colleagues
-	Retrieve information from the basic and clinical science literature, analyse its content and apply the knowledge gained to advance personal and group learning
-	Demonstrate an understanding of, and proficiency in operating, basic clinical tools that are used to assess the structure and function of the eye
-	Describe the range of appearances and the different refractive conditions associated with the normal eye
-	Collect and communicate data regarding the structure and function of the eye using basic clinical tools
-	Compare and contrast the clinical data collected with findings reported in the clinical and scientific literature
-	Describe the basic principles of optical dispensing and explain how these are applied in an optometric practice

Learning And Teaching Strategy

OPTM4108 is delivered in a blended-learning environment, which links pre-recorded lecture material with face-to-face PBLs, TBLs, and clinical skills classes. The materials for the unit will be provided on a weekly basis in LMS. 
In 2022, lecturers and teaching staff will facilitate and support fully online sessions in the instance that face-to-face sessions are not able to be conducted. 
Students will, on average, spend 150 hours a semester undertaking the teaching, learning and assessment activities associated with this unit. This includes:
•	6 hours of pre-recorded lectures each week.
•	Around 20 x 2-hour practical classes covering clinical skills.
•	Around 12 x 2-hour Problem Based Learning classes covering the lecture content.
•	Up to 6 x 2-hour seminar classes consolidating and integrating learning from biomedical sciences with learning from foundation of Optometry, PBLs and clinical decision-making assessments undertaken in class.
•	Around 5 hours of other clinical skills assessments.
•	Students are expected to dedicate a further 12 hours a week to private study of subject matter contained in this unit, including weekly readings, private revision and completing take-home assignments and assessments.
Attendance at all sessions is an important learning experience for all students and therefore attendance is expected. In addition, external experts are invited, and a full audience is their expectation. All sessions delivered by guest presenters are compulsory. All PBLs, TBLs, practical and seminar sessions are compulsory; with 100% attendance rate required. Students must arrive in time to sign-in prior to the session commencing. Please note it is considered professional behaviour to attend all sessions on time. 
Most face-to-face sessions involve preparation beforehand.  Please ensure this preparation is completed.  
If a student is unable to attend a session or complete their preparation, they will need to discuss this with the Unit Coordinator, Elisse Higginbotham. If a student is unable to attend a session due to illness or for any other exceptional circumstance, the Unit Coordinator must be contacted as soon as practicable, and the Clinical Skills Supervisor, PBL Tutor or TBL lead cc’ed in on the email covering their sessions. Absences of more than one day due to illness will require a medical certificate to be presented. Failure to meet this requirement may/will result in a report of Professional Misconduct being made.
Special consideration for missed assessments and absences of greater than two days from scheduled classes must be sought in line with UWA Policy.
Name badges are compulsory for all face-to-face sessions – the tutors and lecturers use a problem-based learning style where you need to participate in discussions in class and it is important that the tutor can refer to you by your name. Laboratory coats are required for all practical sessions.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
