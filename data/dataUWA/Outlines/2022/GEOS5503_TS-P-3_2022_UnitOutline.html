Unit Information

Successful exploration and production of oil and gas fields, and conventional oil and gas and other energy resources requires an advanced level of knowledge of the geological environments and geophysical attributes of geological materials and technology available to monitor and model reservoirs and other elements of petroleum systems. 
This unit follows GEOS4412 Petroleum Systems and aims to develop more specialized skills relevant to petroleum exploration and production. The unit is especially valuable to students actively seeking employment in the energy sector after graduation. The unit teaches advanced geological and geophysical concepts and techniques relevant to the exploration, development and production of hydrocarbons. Students will use state-of-the art technology and world class research facilities including computer software and datasets provided by the industry. This is an activity-based unit where students will be asked to interpret well core, well log, and 3D seismic datasets as part of several group and individual projects.
The unit comprises hands-on or, depending on COVID restrictions, virtual core workshops (held at the Core Library of Department of Mines and Petroleum of Western Australia) where we shall discuss the sedimentology and 3D architecture of the reservoirs from giant gas fields of the North West Shelf, and some individual exercises from logging and interpreting core. Applications to subsurface reservoir modelling, carbon sequestration and hydrogen storage will also be covered.
You will also study 3D seismic interpretation, and depending on COVID restrictions this will be hands-on or virtual practical training in structural and stratigraphic interpretation of 3D seismic data and interpretation of seismic attributes and amplitude anomalies. Students will be asked to complete a group exercise using a high-resolution 3D dataset.
The unit includes the participation of industry scientists who have contributed online lectures, videos and hands-on practical exercises.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

Students will work mainly in a tutorial or workshop mode, with focused practical exercises, punctuated by lecture material, and
supplemented by recommended reading.
The learning outcomes will be that students should be able to:
1) demonstrate understanding of key geoscientific concepts related to exploration and production of petroleum resources including subsurface carbon sequestration and gas storage;
2) develop practical skills in applied data analysis and interpretation related to petroleum systems and;
3) integrate geological and geophysical datasets to solve problems related to exploration and production, subsurface carbon sequestration and gas storage.

Unit Structure

Emphasis is placed on both theory and practical skill development. Whenever possible this unit is taught via workshops that are composed of presentations / discussion of concepts and their practical application usually via practical exercises during face-to-face workshops, or depending on COVID restrictions, online virtual workshops.
Classes will start as online for the first three weeks as we cover background content, and seismic interpretation workshop exercises (online due to COVID restrictions in the G105 laboratory). The last three weeks will be run in blended mode; held offsite in workshop mode at the Core Library at 37 Harris St. Carlisle, WA, 6101. Note that this is also subject to COVID restrictions (WA Govt. facility access conditions will apply) but we can also run this entirely online for those that cannot attend F2F (the core has been photographed and short videos prepared, so those attending online can still complete the work to meet all the learning outcomes). 
The exam will be online only.

Participation

You are expected to attend and participate in every class each week whether F2F or Online at the times designated between 9am and 4pm Perth time (else watch the recordings if this is not feasible for those doing online only in different time zones). Missing the live online and F2F workshops will create some problems for you because in the case of the face-to-face core workshop exercises the drill cores will only be laid out once and not repeated, and you will be expected to catch up on any missed work prior to the next class including the hand-in work. Nevertheless, for those doing Online ONLY we have made exercises for you to do that will mimic what we are doing or would do in the F2F mode, including access to lots of short videos of the core for you to review as if you were present.  Completion of the practical work, reading and project assignments during the week (between classes) is expected so please be prepared. Naturally, if COVID restrictions are applying throughout the semester, then all will be online. Your active participation is needed in the Q&A sessions.

Referencing Requirements

Students are expected to work both individually and as part of a group. Original personal observations and interpretations are required, and use of published materials must be fully referenced. Use only the original published journal or refereed conference papers (avoid derivative papers and web-based articles).

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
