This unit examines the factors that influence psychological functioning in a social environment.  The unit considers some of the latest theories, research and applications in areas such as social cognition, social influence, group dynamics, altruism and cooperation.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

This unit involves three key learning components: Lectures, Laboratories (Labs) and Readings.  Each component is designed to complement (not duplicate) the others.
LECTURES guide you through the major topics and deal with some of the more difficult conceptual issues.  You attend one 1-hour 45-minute lecture per week. Lectures are on Tuesdays from 2:00pm-3:45pm in the Octagon Lecture Theatre - these run all semester (i.e., one per week for 12 weeks).  Lectures are recorded for later viewing.  You may attend in person and/or watch them on your computer (see Lecture Capture in LMS).  Lectures can also be downloaded, with instructions on how to do this provided in the Lecture Recordings area of LMS.  The material covered in the lectures is critical to the successful completion of the 2 assigned Laboratory Reports and the final Exam.
LABORATORIES introduce you to the major topics and empirical approaches in social psychology, and will provide a forum for discussion (guided by your demonstrator).  There are 5 laboratory meetings, each 1-hour and 45-minute.  Attending the laboratories is essential to the successful completion of the 2 assigned laboratory reports, and to the content covered in the exam.  Remember to enrol in a laboratory group - you enroll electronically, using the CAS system.
READINGS are essential too.  The textbook has been chosen because of its intellectual standard, its coverage of the material, and because it considers other topics that we cannot cover in the lectures.  Readings for this unit will include the assigned text book and supplementary articles provided on LMS, as well as textbooks and periodicals available from the library.

Unit Structure

Lectures & Laboratories: There are 12 double lectures (1-hour and 45-minutes per lecture) and 5 lab classes (1-hour and 45-minutes per lab).
Formative Feedback: Students will be given the opportunity to receive performance feedback in the unit by completing online quizzes.  Feedback on the quizzes is immediate (i.e., upon the student submitting their responses online).  The quizzes are not worth anything, but the questions are similar to what can be expected in the multiple choice component of the final exam.
Laboratory Reports: Students will complete two Laboratory Reports.  The word count for Laboratory Report 1 is 1000 words (worth 15% of overall unit mark) and the word count for Laboratory Report 2 is 2000 words (worth 35% of overall unit mark), excluding the Reference Section and the Abstract (150 words).
Exam: The final exam is composed of 6 short answer questions and 60 multiple choice questions.  It is worth 50% of the overall unit mark.

Participation

To successfully complete the two laboratory reports it is crucial that students attend the labs.  This is because the lab report instruction and content is only provided in the labs.  In addition, the content covered in the labs is also examined in the final exam.  The labs are designed to be fun and interactive with an emphasis on developing data processing, data visualisation and data analysis skills.  These skills are highly transferable and are much sought after in the modern workplace.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "honest, ethical and responsible scholarship". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
