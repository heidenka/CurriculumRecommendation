Academic Information

Content This unit provides students with basic knowledge, and practical experience, in the following concepts and principles: (1) basic knowledge of the growth and anatomy of the human skeleton (bones and teeth); (2) applications of radiography in forensic odontology; (3) the use of rugae, lip prints and facial superimposition in the identification process; (4) different ageing methods used by the forensic odontologist; (5) the role of the forensic odontologist in disaster victim identification (DVI); and (6) bite mark analysis.

Unit Information

The application of forensic anthropology requires an intimate knowledge of human skeletal anatomy relative to variation concerning age, sex, ancestry, species, trauma, preservation and taphonomic alteration (amongst other pertinent factors). The basic underpinning of the discipline is thus largely grounded in a sound anatomical knowledge of the anatomy of the human skeleton, including the dentition. Casework requirements will vary and are often unpredictable, therefore being able to identify a complete and intact skeleton (whilst important) is not enough – a competent anthropologist must be able to identify small bone fragments, reconcile commingling, determine human from non-human animal origin, reconstruct fragmentary remains, and understand appropriate mensuration and documentation methods.
This unit is intended to introduce students to basic ideas and principles in forensic osteology, including: basic knowledge of the embryological origin of the human skeleton; basics of bone growth and development; dental anatomy, charting and odontometrics; regional anatomy of the human skeleton (skull and teeth; thorax and pelvis; vertebral column; and the appendages); bone measurement and morphological quantification; repair and reconstruction; casting and three-dimensional scanning. The unit will also provide practical experience in correct handling techniques and methods for casting and reconstructing of human skeletal remains. It is envisioned that students will develop an appreciation of the importance of a sound anatomical knowledge of the human skeleton as it relates to requisite casework referred to the forensic anthropologist.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

This course is a basic overview of human osteology and odontology. Please note that this is an introductory course into what is an extremely broad topic. As such, we do not expect students to become expert osteologists, rather we are equipping you with the basic knowledge required to successfully train within forensic anthropology. The course is intended to provide students with a basic introduction to the following concepts and principles:
• The basics of human osteology and aspects of forensic dentistry
• Embryological origins of the human skeleton
• Skeletal growth and intramembranous and endochondral ossification
• Fetal osteology and juvenile skeletal anatomy
• Appropriate bone handling techniques and methods of skeletal documentation
• Regional anatomy of the human skeleton

Unit Structure

LECTURE SERIES
Lecture 1: The language of anatomy and introductory osteology
In this session an overview to the unit is first provided. Thereafter the first part of the lecture discusses the origins of anatomical terminology and its importance in modern forensic practice. Relevant directional and sectional terminology is also introduced. In the second part of the lecture, skeletal anatomy and concepts relating to the post-mortem alteration of bones are examined. Basic principles relating to correct bone handling procedures will be discussed. 
Lecture 2: Juvenile osteology
The purpose of this lecture is to introduce concepts relating to the embryological origins of the human skeleton. Aspects of skeletal growth, specifically intramembranous and endochondral ossification are also considered. The anatomy of the fetal skeleton is introduced and thereafter a regional approach is taken to explore the morphology of the juvenile skeleton – birth through adolescence.
Lecture 3: Overview of forensic odontology
This is the first of two lectures presented by the state forensic odontologist, Dr Stephen Knott. In this lecture, Dr Knott will explain the importance of dental evidence as it relates to forensic investigations. The teeth hold evidentiary value not only in personal identification, but also in linking (or eliminating) suspects to a bite-mark left in a victim or at a scene of a crime, as a repository for molecular material suitable for genetic identification, a record of health during life, and as a marker of personal age. Dr Knott will explore the discipline by drawing on his many years of service in broad applications ranging from routine casework through to deployment in response to mass-death scenarios (e.g., disaster victim identification).
Lecture 4: Human identification
This is the second of two lectures presented by the state forensic odontologist, Dr Stephen Knott. In this lecture, Dr Knott focuses on the role of the forensic dentist in relation to personal identification. The teeth are one of only a small and restricted number of biological indicators of identity that are deemed admissible for legal purposes. The lecture will consider the methods applied to establish identity based on the dentition. In addition, the lecture will also explore the specific reasons why establishing personal identity is so important (e.g., closure to families; settlement of estates; amongst others).
Lecture 5: Dental anatomy
The purpose of this lecture is to specifically consider the development and anatomy of the human dentition. The embryological origins of the teeth are explored and their growth and development thereafter from birth through to early adolescence is discussed. The relevant nomenclature used to describe the jaws and teeth within is explained, as are the various methods available for dental charting. The interpretation of orthopantomograms (OPGs) is introduced, as are concepts relating to the measurement of the dentition (odontometrics), either digitally or in physical specimens. The lecture concludes by exploring variations in the dentition that relate to pathology or developmental anomalies, and the methods used to quantify attrition.
Lecture 6: Documentation of human skeletal remains
The documentation of human skeletal remains by the forensic anthropologist is one of the most fundamental tasks performed; it facilitates most of the analyses and interpretations that follow. The purpose of this lecture is to consider the multifaceted types of documentation performed and explore the most appropriate methods for doing so. When skeletal remains are referred to the anthropologist, one of the first requirements is inventorying. The latter is considered, as are aspects relating to the stabilization of fragmentary remains. The specific methods, and the specialized equipment, used to measure human skeletal remains are discussed. As most material referred to the anthropologist will at some point be returned to the investigating authority (e.g., for subsequent burial), the lecture will conclude by considering the importance of alternate methods of  documenting remains that facilitate further ‘virtual’ analysis of those remains should the need arise.
PRACTICAL SERIES
Practical 1: Introductory osteology
This laboratory will facilitate your knowledge of individual bones and important features of the human skeleton. In this session you will learn the appropriate techniques for handling bones, in addition to the primary skeletal features necessary to understand and apply the methods of analysis presented in subsequent lectures (e.g. formulating a biological profile; age, sex, stature and ancestry).
Practical 2: Regional anatomy
This laboratory is split into three weekly sections: 2.1 – the skull; 2.2 – thorax, vertebral column and pelvis; 2.3 – the appendages. Each is allocated a specific weekly practical session time designed to develop your detailed knowledge of regional anatomy. In these sessions you will apply a selection of techniques that will be built upon in advanced units for the assessment of skeletal remains and the formation of a biological profile.
Practical 3: Juvenile anatomy
This laboratory will develop your ability to identify bones in the human subadult skeleton and thus achieve an understanding of growth and development of human remains. In this session you will seriate juvenile bone by size and developmental stage, through the quantification of physical specimens and medical images. Identification of fetal bone is not only important for estimating age, and understanding growth and development (both normal and anomalous), but also in differentiating human from non-human bone. 
Practical 4: Forensic odontology
This laboratory will develop your dental anatomy background, including an understanding of morphological variation, changes with age and development, and dental pathology. In this session you will apply a selection of techniques for the study of teeth in forensic and physical anthropological contexts. 
Practical 5: Measurement and recording
This laboratory will develop your ability to create a permanent record of the human remains being analysed. In this session you will apply a selection of techniques for acquiring three-dimensional representations of skeletal and/or dental elements. You will also learn how to take basic skeletal measurements using the appropriate landmarks, definitions and equipment.
Practical 6: Fragments and burnt bone
This laboratory will test your understanding of skeletal and dental anatomy by identifying elements that have undergone post-mortem modifications such as fragmentation and burning. You will be required to identify landmarks on, and attempt to reconstruct fragmented bones as well as identify fragments of further anthropological interest.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
