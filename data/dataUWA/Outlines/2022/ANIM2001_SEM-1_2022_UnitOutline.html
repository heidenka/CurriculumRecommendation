Academic Information

Content This unit will emphasise the global impact that Darwin's theory of evolution has had on a range of human endeavours. The unit provides an engaging environment for understanding how evolutionary principles have shaped contemporary human populations and global human endeavours, such as the need to communicate through language, to feed ourselves, fight disease, design our everyday products, conduct business transactions, and fuel our artistic talents. The unit will also explore the darker side of Darwinism, tackling unsettling topics such as ‘scientific racism', colonialism, and the basis for human conflict. In doing so the unit explores the variation in culture and religious belief that is so important in developing an objective understanding of cultural diversity. Students will gain an understanding of how Darwinian principles can be used to help us understand our own actions, but also to respond to some of the biggest challenges facing humankind, including the loss of biodiversity, climate change and disease. Overall, students will gain a broad understanding of what it is to be human, in the context of evolution, in a culturally diverse and environmentally challenged world..

Unit Information

The Darwinian Revolution unit covers a very broad swathe of disciplines in order to understand what evolution means to us as humans. The unit has content from expert agriculturalists, anthropologists, artists, biologists, economists, historians, lawyers, medics, musicians, nutritionists and philosophers – all drawing on their expertise to show how evolutionary thinking (for better or worse) has affected their sphere of expertise, or how their subject has, in turn, affected evolutionary thought. The unit comprises five modules:
1. Principles of Evolutionary Theory: Explores the history and principles of evolutionary thinking.
2. Humans, Evolution and Culture and Society: Develops an understanding of what it is to be human, with details of human evolutionary history revealed in our genes. Brings together insights and evidence of adaptation, of our sex lives, language, music, conflict and culture.
3. Darwin’s Dangerous Idea: Explores how those seeking to justify racism and genocide have misappropriated evolutionary principles. This section also tackles the challenges that an evolutionary understanding bring to topics such as human conflict, the rights of apes, and the notion of how moral thoughts themselves might have evolved.
4. Evolution and Human Endeavour: Explains how evolution has been important in agriculture and domestication and how the evolution of resistance threatens health and agricultural systems. Explains how our knowledge of evolution sheds light on medicine and disease, how the field of economics has contributed to evolutionary thought, and how engineers and technologists have drawn inspiration from naturally selected solutions to nature’s problems.
5. Evolutionary Hangovers: We face an extinction crisis – how can we address this in an evolutionarily objective way? Humans are adapted to living in small groups, in a rural environment so where does this leave us in a crowded couch-dwelling modern life full of evolutionarily novelties such as digital media? And how do we rein in our evolved yet self-destructive preference for carbohydrates?

Learning And Teaching Strategy

The Darwinian Revolution incorporates a 'blended' approach to student learning, involving face-to-face classes, 'flipped' and interactive classes, and an online component. All of these approaches are designed to stimulate questions and discussion among students and staff and to equip students to understand how Darwinian principles have shaped our lives and how we, in turn, have shaped the theory that underpins these principles.

Unit Structure

Lectures and ‘flipped' classes
There are two lecture slots each week. These slots will be filled either with formal lectures or interactive ‘flipped’ classes. Where we conduct flipped classes, a recording of the formal lecture will be made available to you one week prior to the scheduled class – there are instances where we have lecturers who are overseas or interstate and these in particular will be of this format. When we run flipped classes, you must access recorded lectures and any associated reading material before each class. The process will not work if you have not seen this material beforehand. Flipped classes provide an opportunity to think more deeply into the topics covered in the formal lecture, discuss key topics in an informal and friendly environment, receive and provide feedback from/to peers, and interact with your lecturers. Note that flipped classes are NOT recorded as they rely on discussions that mainly take place among students. We therefore strongly urge you to attend interactive flipped sessions but also encourage attendance at regular lectures, as these also provide ideal opportunities to interact with your lecturers and classmates. Lecturers are always happy to answer questions, so please interrupt us if you do not understand something or require further information on a topic.
Online activities and quizzes
The unit is divided into five modules. During each module, online activities and quizzes will be posted. The online activities are designed to engage you with the material covered in the lectures and develop questions for the Q&A activity. The activities will take a number of different forms and will usually involve you thinking, reading, watching, listening to or doing something. Alongside these activities there will be a small set of questions relating to the content of the lectures and/or the activities. These activities are about engagement and thought and are designed to be interesting and stimulating and will keep you up-to-date with the course. The opening and closing dates for the activities are given under Assessment Tasks; these activities will help you to track your progress through the semester and means that you will avoid a heavy workload (cramming) at the end of semester.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
