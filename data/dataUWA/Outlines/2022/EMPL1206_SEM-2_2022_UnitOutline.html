Unit Information

Welcome to Social Relations of Work! We hope that you will enjoy the unit and find it useful and interesting.
Work plays an important role in structuring our daily lives and our sense of self. It provides a structure for how we should define ourselves in relation to others and how we should behave. It is a source of fun and enjoyment, but also a source of alienation and conflict. This course will examine some key theories of social psychology and sociology, and apply them in the context of work and employment. The unit is divided into three sections. We start with a brief overview about the meaning of work to people, as well as the role of context and culture. The second section looks at the social self, or the individual - how we understand the self, how we perceive others, and how we develop stereotypes, prejudice and discrimination. Finally, the third section looks at human groups, particularly social relations at work. In this context, we will examine group processes, conflicts, social exchanges, and will bring it all together by looking at how psychological contracts, emotional and aesthetic labour develop in workplaces. 
As you go about developing your understanding about these concepts, you will have the opportunity to exercise reflection, judgement, and critical thinking.
Please contact me if you have any queries regarding the unit.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

This unit is organised as a combination of pre-recorded lectures and weekly tutorials (available as F2F or online). Only lectures in Week 1 (Introduction to the unit) and Week 12 (Reflection and preparation for exam) will be conducted in F2F mode. Online students will be able to join these 2 lectures via MS Teams. 
All lecture and tutorial materials will be uploaded onto the LMS. Students are expected to listen to the pre-recorded lecture material (Weeks 2 - 11) and to do the weekly readings in their own time. Students will also be expected to attend a 1-hour tutorial every week (Weeks 2 - 11), during which time they will be working on assessed in-class activities. Attendance to tutorials is essential. In this unit, the lecture is only one part of the learning process: students' participation is crucial. Additional readings, weekly preparatory work, and completion of assessment exercises will also contribute to students' understanding and consolidation of knowledge.
Teaching and Learning Evaluation
You may be asked to complete two evaluations during this unit: the Student Perception of Teaching (SPOT) and the Students Unit Reflective Feedback (SURF). The SPOT is optional and is an evaluation of the lecturer and the unit. The SURF is completed online and is a university wide survey and deals only with the unit. You will receive an email from the SURF office inviting you to complete the SURF when it is activated. We encourage you to complete the forms as your feedback is extremely important and can be used to make changes to the unit or lecturing style when appropriate. This feedback is very valuable, is accepted without prejudice and has been instrumental in improving teaching and learning in this unit.

Unit Structure

As outlined above, the structure of the unit consists of three sections:

- Part II: The social self

Referencing Requirements

It is important that the referencing of any sources used in your written work is done properly, if only to substantiate the points you are making in your assignment or project. The APA 7th Referencing Style is preferred in this unit, unless otherwise discussed with the unit coordinator. There are some notes for guidance which have been prepared by the library staff: https://guides.library.uwa.edu.au/apa. The EndNote software package is a really good system for building up a database of references. Not everyone will want to invest the time in using this system but you should consider it if you intend to build up resource materials or plan to undertake extensive research in a particular area. The library staff have also developed a tutoring package: A Quick Guide to Using EndNote which provides the basics for using EndNote with an essay http://guides.library.uwa.edu.au/endnote

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
