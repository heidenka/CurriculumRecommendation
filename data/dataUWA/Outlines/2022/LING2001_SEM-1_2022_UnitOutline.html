Academic Information

Content What do we know about the form of the expressions in our language? How can we describe this knowledge? This unit will equip students with the theoretical and practical knowledge needed to analyse linguistic data and produce grammatical description. We will study the key linguistic concepts needed to understand and compare morphological and syntactic structures across the world's languages. We will also learn how to apply these concepts to identify patterns in data sets from individual languages.

Unit Information

Languages are complex systems that human beings create in order to communicate an infinite array of subtle meanings. These systems are extremely sophisticated and strongly rule-based. Our goal is to begin to understand how these systems work - that is, to explore the unity and diversity of structure that lead to the amazing complexity we see in the world's 6-7000 unique languages. 
In this unit, we will study the morphological and syntactic structures of human languages: the patterns that languages are built on top of, the features that tend to recur across them, and how much variation is possible. Our study will be theoretical, as we will search for ideas and approaches that can unify our findings at a higher level. It will also, however, be practical, as we will use the concepts we learn to identify patterns in data sets from real languages. At the end of the semester, students will be equipped with some of the knowledge needed to analyse linguistic data and produce grammatical descriptions.

Participation

I very much encourage you to attend all lectures and tutorials. (If you are overseas or otherwise unable to come to campus, you can watch the lecture recordings after class is finished.) You will not be able to do well in this unit without this essential learning and practice time.
Before each tutorial: 
1.) Review the content of the previous week's lectures. You will need this information during your tutorial. Bring your notes with you to your tutorial.
2.) Complete and submit your tutorial preparation assignment ("tute prep"). These are due by 3:00 pm on Wednesdays of tute weeks (regardless of which tutorial group you're in).
     a.) To complete your tute prep, first download the tute prep assignment from LMS ("Tute preps & Submission Links"). Each tute prep will become available on the Thursday of the week before it is due.
     b.) Do the exercise, then submit a copy of your results using the designated Turnitin link for that prep (also under "Tute preps & Submission Links"). Any format that is readable on a PC is great — PDF, DOCx, or TXT. (‘Pages’ files are not readable on PCs, unfortunately. Please do not submit in this format.)
     c.) Bring a copy of your completed tute prep to your tutorial so we can all discuss everyone’s answers. It can be on paper or on screen.
Attendance and participation are not marked, but again, you will not be able to master the material if you do not turn up.

Referencing Requirements

In this course, use APA referencing style (https://guides.library.uwa.edu.au/apa/introduction_apa7). 
Most of the examples shown on the website above don't include page numbers, but I find this super annoying. Please DO include page numbers. For example, "the corresponding plural form ju-lu was not used by any speakers" (Bavin & Shopen, 1987, p. 171).
If you use exact words from any source (one of the textbooks, a website, a TV show, a podcast, etc.), you must put these words in quotation marks and acknowledge the source via an APA style reference (see link above). If you paraphrase an idea from any source, you must still acknowledge that you are paraphrasing, and state where you got your idea from (using the APA style referencing system again).

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
