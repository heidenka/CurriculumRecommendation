Academic Information

Content Delivering a relevant and reliable customer experience is critical to the overall business performance. Customer experience management is increasingly concerned with the long-term evolution of customer experience in service industries such as airlines, telecommunications, travel and tourism, hospitality, retailing and entertainment. Firms must make a concerted effort to provide consistent and frictionless customer experience. This unit provides students with skills to build and create memorable consumer experiences. The emphasis is on designing the ultimate customer experience in the phygital (i.e. a space where physical and digital mediums merge to render services) world. The dominant logic of this unit is on consumer/experience-centric logic.

Unit Information

Delivering a relevant and reliable customer experience is critical to the overall business performance. Customer experience management is increasingly concerned with the long-term evolution of customer experience in service industries. To navigate the experience economy, firms must make a concerted effort to provide a consistent and frictionless customer experience in the phygital world (i.e., a space where physical and digital mediums merge to render services). 
In this unit, you will learn various theories and concepts on customer experience and contemporary practices in designing memorable experiences. Experiential marketing differs from traditional goods/service-focused marketing in many aspects. It is, therefore, important that you are able to recognise these unique complexities and the implications for marketing strategies. As this unit has a strong practical emphasis, real case studies of different firms and industries are featured in the lectures, tutorials and assignments.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

Strategy
You will complete a series of learning activities each week (i.e. readings, lecture, case study, tutorial) and work on a team project throughout the semester. These activities and assessments support the development of broad marketing knowledge and skills by facilitating hands-on learning experiences and reflection. The final examination allows you to review the unit material and make connections between different topics, hence a comprehensive understanding of the subject.

The Student Experience of Learning and Teaching (SELT) survey enables us to seek student feedback on their experience of learning and teaching in the unit. You may be asked to complete evaluations of the unit and your instructors' teaching (lecturer and tutor). You will receive an email inviting you to complete the SELT survey when it is activated. We encourage you to complete the survey as your feedback is extremely important and can be used to make changes to the unit or teaching style when appropriate.

Unit Structure

This unit is based on the delivery of weekly two-hour lectures and one-hour tutorials.
Lectures (face to face @BUSN-G89 / live online via Teams)
Lectures deliver important content that introduces and explains the key concepts of experiential marketing, with current examples from industry or the media. You will also participate in short activities and discussions during the lectures to enhance your learning. Each session, you can choose to participate in person or online or catch up by watching the recording. An in-person/online drop-in consultation session follows the lecture. Please refer to the LMS for detailed information on the lecture topics and how to access them online.
Tutorials (face-to-face / online via Teams)
Tutorials complement the lectures with case studies, hands-on activities, and discussions. Your preparation and participation levels will be assessed each week, so it is important you always come to class prepared, having completed the assigned tasks (i.e. readings, lecture, and case study). Please refer to the LMS for tutorial activity details and to the Class Allocation System (https://www.cas.uwa.edu.au/) for your tutorial timetable.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
