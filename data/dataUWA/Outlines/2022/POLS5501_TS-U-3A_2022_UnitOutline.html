Learning And Teaching Strategy

This subject is comprised of two main modes of online teaching and learning:
-	Seminar-style presentations by the coordinator, during which there is space given for questions, discussion and short tasks aimed at quickly embedding and understanding new content.
-	Longer pieces of groupwork, as part of pairs and groups or as individuals, that aim to extend understanding of core concepts and their application to real-world public policy.
During the online class, students will be asked to engage with a range of content, including:
-	Academic articles and sections of books
-	Discussion papers and proposals from interest groups and think tanks
-	News articles, visual media and audio discussing relevant policy topics
While many topics and examples discussed will be from or relevant to Australia, the course will also utilise a comparative and internationalist view of public policy. As such, students are strongly encouraged to discuss concepts and examples from other contexts that they are familiar with, including from non-Western or non-democratic political systems.

Unit Structure

This subject is taught as four intensive days of online class, from 9am to 4pm. Each day will consist of four 90-minute sessions, with two short breaks and a longer lunch break. Each day will have a distinct focus (see Unit Schedule), but the topics are sequenced that knowledge can be built upon as we progress through the four days.

Participation

Students are required to attend all four days of Online class. If any student is unable to attend all or part of any of the classes, they are asked to contact the subject coordinator as soon as possible. 
There are several ways in which students can get the most value out of this class:
a) to join the class having looked closely at the required readings, with a view to identifying core concepts to use in discussions and classwork
b) be prepared to participate in class discussions and group activities to the best of your ability, regardless of whether you have a background in public policy or not. 
c) to engage with your peers in good faith

ASSESSMENT ONE:
Several position papers will be available for download via the LMS. Before class begins, it is your task to familiarise yourself with these papers. You do not need to read the entire paper - some are rather long - but at least skim the introduction so you get a sense of what they are advocating for.
You will then be split up into six groups, with each one allocated a position paper. Each group is required to prepare a 20 minute presentation.
Each group member is required to present, and each person will also be required to submit a 500-word executive summary of the group's presentation (worth 20% of grade), via the LMS.
The presentation must at least touch each of the following questions:
A.	Which organisation produced the position paper? How might their background/organisational mission inform their position?
B.	What is the argument being made in the position paper? What are the policy implications?
C.	What evidence is being used in paper to justify this position? How persuasive is it?
D.	If a change is being proposed, how would it be implemented? What potential barriers exist to change? What would be required to create the conditions for change? If the paper advocates against change, how might they help protect the status quo?
E.	In your view, is the proposal good policy? Why or why not? Is it good politics?
NOTE: Not all of these questions need be given equal attention in terms of time. Neither do you have to structure your presentation in the way outlined here.
You will be marked on the following criteria:
1.	Clarity of communication by presenters (including visual aids such as slides) - weighting: 10% of grade
2.	Evidence of understanding of the issues raised by the position paper - 20% of grade
3.	Use of evidence to support claims - 20% of grade
4.	Strength of analysis regarding value of proposal and political prospects - 20% of grade
5.	Equal participation among group members - 10% of grade
You will be given time in class each day to work on your presentations. However, you will likely also need to begin thinking about these papers and the presentation prior to the beginning of the intensive week, and to do some work individually outside of class during the week.
I will address the presentation in more detail on the first day of class.
ASSESSMENT TWO & THREE:
The overarching goal of these two assessments is to build upon what you have learned from the literature covered in the seminars and readings to undertake rigorous research on the subject matter in a real-life situation. 
You will be asked to select ONE policy area which you believe to be in (or soon to be in) a 'window of opportunity' for change (see Kingdon reading). 
This can be from any country or policy area that you are interested in or familiar with. The window of opportunity may relate the a) shortcomings of existing government policy, b) a problem which is not addressed by government policy at all. 
NOTE: This topic must be distinct from the topics addressed in the group presentations (whether from your group or others). 
Do not choose very broad topics (e.g., the environment), which are difficult to handle within the word limit. It is better to focus on one specific aspect of the policy or topic. 
You are expected to produce two pieces of assessment:
A policy memo (Assessment Two) and a more detailed report (Assessment Three). 
More specific details will be provided on the LMS prior to the beginning of the subject. However, note that the policy memo will ask you to focus on the nature of the policy problem and the window of opportunity for change, while the report will ask you to draw upon key concepts and literature from the course in proposing a potential response or solution.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
