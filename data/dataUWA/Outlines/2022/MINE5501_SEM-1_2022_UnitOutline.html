Academic Information

Content This unit deals with the theory of exhaustible resources; project appraisal; government policies; royalties; export controls; and economics impact analyses. It also covers the Mining Act and Mines Safety and Inspection Act; duties of care; roles and responsibilities; environmental and native title issues; and occupational health and safety.

Unit Information

This unit covers theory and applications in the economics of natural resource management. It builds on the basic theory of welfare economics, Pareto optimality and resource allocation efficiency to develop the economic principles required for the analysis of resource and environmental management issues, with particular emphasis on minerals.
Specific topics covered include determining optimal pollution flows and policies to achieve them, the economics of climate change, economic impact modelling of mining projects, optimal extraction of minerals under varying mining conditions and market structures, the taxation of mineral extraction and valuation of the natural environment, and the modelling of the economic impact of mining projects.
Natural resources have particular characteristics that influence the application of economic theory to resource and environmental management. Environmental and ecological concepts are used alongside the economic theory in order to develop effective policies.
The major objective of this unit is to further your understanding of the fundamental contribution that microeconomic analysis can make to solving natural resource and environmental management problems relevant to mining.
Most natural resource management problems arise because the sum of the competing demands for the various environmental and consumption services that natural resources can supply exceeds the capacity of these resources to provide the required services over time. Seen like this, it is the classic microeconomics problem: how best to allocate scarce natural resources between competing uses.
However, it needs to be recognized that the management of natural resources poses some unique problems and microeconomic theory has evolved to account for these. Some natural resources, like canyons and old growth forest, are irreversibly damaged through use and are not replaceable. Other natural resources like fisheries can be sustainably harvested, whereas for resources like minerals, harvesting depletes the resource. Environmental pollution is an interaction between production and consumption activities and the assimilation capacity of the environment, which is itself a natural resource that can be damaged by over use.
An understanding of the way microeconomic analysis has evolved to deal with these complex issues and how this informs the establishment of appropriate policy frameworks will be the major outcome of the course.

DESCRIPTIONS OF ASSESSMENTS
Assessment 1 (In-class Quizzes): Refer to LMS for details
Assessment 2 (Essay): The nominated essay topic is designed to allow you to undertake an evaluation of a natural resource management policy. The evaluation will take the form of a literature review and requires you to assemble relevant theoretical and applied literature to assess the economic merit of policies being implemented in relation to a specific policy issue. The focus is the application of relevant economic theory to the policy problem. Marking will be based on how well you have mastered the application of economic theory to the nominated natural resource problem and how well the theory is integrated into the analysis. 
The essay will require you to;
-Select an applied topic from a nominated list and choose the appropriate theoretical construct for its analysis.
-Demonstrate why the chosen theory is most appropriate.
-Demonstrate how the theory informs policy for dealing with the nominated problem.
-Document actual policy solutions that are being implemented for dealing with the nominated problem.
-Assess the success of these policies and how well they correspond to the theory.

RECOMMENDED BOOKS
There are several excellent books in Resource and Environmental Economics. They operate at various levels of technical and mathematical complexity. Some focus on graphical analysis and basic principles whereas others favour mathematical analysis. The following is a list of very good backup books which cover relevant material for the course. You should scan these texts and seek one that suits – a little more graphical/descriptive or a little more mathematical depending on your background and use in tandem with the main text..
Perman, R. et al Natural Resource and Environmental Economics, 3rd ed. London: Addison-Wesley. 2003 or 4th ed. 2011 - A very comprehensive and rigorous exposition of the field. We will work from the 4th edition, but the 3rd will also suffice.
Field B and Field M, Environmental Economics London: McGraw-Hill 8th edition and earlier  - Excellent exposition of many of the topics we cover. Good examples and mostly graphical. Any edition 6th through 8th will suffice.
Pearce D.W and Turner R K, Economics of Natural Resources and the Environment, London: Wheatsheaf, 1st or 2nd editions. Excellent and comprehensive – mostly graphical –chapters on environmental economics and theory of sustainability are very good.
Hartwick, J.M. and N.D. Olewiler, The Economics of Natural Resource Use, New York: Harper and Row, 1998. - Especially good on natural resources – fisheries economics, minerals economics and optimal mineral depletion with mineral resources of different sizes and qualities and taxation.
Tietenberg, T. Environmental and Natural Resource Economics, New York: Harper Collins, 9th edition. - Not as analytical as the others. Comprehensive and widely used. Especially good for an overview/policy perspective on a range of topics. Any edition from the 6th onwards will suffice on most topics.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
