Academic Information

Content This is an introductory unit that explores prokaryotic and eukaryotic cell structure and function at the physiological and molecular levels. A strong emphasis is placed on applications in biomedicine and biotechnology. The unit builds on concepts introduced in BIOL1130 Frontiers in Biology (formerly BIOL1130 Core Concepts in Biology) and ANHB1101 Human Biology I: Becoming Human. The first part of the unit covers DNA, RNA and protein structure, DNA replication, gene expression and its regulation, and recombinant DNA technology. The second part of the unit deals with the cell cycle and cell differentiation, cell structure and compartmentation, the structure of biological membranes and strategies used to move molecules across these membranes, and intercellular communication. Applications of cell and molecular biology in microbiology, disease diagnosis and therapy, and genetic engineering are discussed in the final part of the unit. Practical classes illustrate fundamental techniques in cell and molecular biology.

Unit Information

This is an introductory unit that explores prokaryotic and eukaryotic cell structure and function at the physiological and molecular levels. A strong emphasis is placed on applications in biomedicine and biotechnology. 
The unit builds on concepts introduced in BIOL1130 Frontiers in Biology (formerly BIOL1130 Core Concepts in Biology) and ANHB1101 Human Biology I: Becoming Human. 
THEORY - the theory content of the unit is divided into seven themes: (1) Basic concepts; (2) The central dogma of molecular biology; (3) Proteins and recombinant DNA technology; (4) Regulation of gene expression; (5) Cytoskeleton and cell membranes; (6) Cell growth and communication and (7) Applications in biotechnology and biomedicine.
Through study in this unit, it is intended that students will: (1) be able to define structural and functional characteristics of living cells and macromolecules; (2) explain and compare basic genetic mechanisms of prokaryotic and eukaryotic cells, including DNA replication and transcription; translation of proteins, mutation and repair; (3) explain and relate methods used in recombinant DNA technology and biotechnology; (4) explain how cells communicate, move and divide.
PRACTICALS - The unit has three hands-on practical sessions that illustrate fundamental techniques in cell and molecular biology. In practical 1, students will isolate and quantify DNA and learn how to evaluate and present data. In practical 2, students will examine how restriction enzymes digest double-stranded DNA and how to analyse restriction enzyme digested DNA. Students will further study how chemical agent can cause DNA mutations. In practical 3, students will study the movement of uncharged and charged solutes and water across cell membranes. At completion of the three practical classes, it is anticipated that students will be able to generate, record and interpret data to demonstrate competence in using standard laboratory techniques and apparatus.
PLEASE NOTE:  Students will have to pass a compulsory online Biosafety module before the completion of semester week 4. This module teaches students safe work practices associated with biological hazards, particularly microbiological organisms. Upon passing this module, students will receive a certificate that is valid for three years and allows the participation in further Biosafety modules.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

The unit is taught using a blended model of learning and teaching, made up of online and face-to-face activities.
LECTURES - The theoretical component of the unit is delivered via face-to-face lectures. All students will have access to recordings of face-to-face lectures (see also "Lecture Capture System" above for important information). The lectures are presented by a cross-disciplinary teaching team. Students are expected to listen to and work through the lecture content and to consult textbooks to complement the lecture content. Online practice questions and assessed quizzes, published on LMS at regular intervals, will allow students to engage in revision and self-reflection of their theory learning.
PRACTICALS - The practical component of the unit is delivered via three face-to-face classes. The three practicals are held in three different venues across campus. Students are expected to attend the practical classes prepared and on time. The practical sessions are composed of three parts, a preparation part, the laboratory work and a data interpretation and evaluation part. Study material will be available prior to each practical via LMS. Students are expected to read and work through the material and through pre-lab recordings prior to taking a pre-lab quiz for each of the practicals. Students will do experimental work, record and interpret data. This will be assessed via post-lab assignments (practical 1 and 3) and an in-class worksheet (practical 2).
BIOSAFTEY - Students will have to pass a compulsory online Biosafety module before the completion of semester week 4. This module teaches students safe work practices associated with biological hazards, particularly microbiological organisms. Upon passing this module, students will receive a certificate that is valid for three years and allows the participation in further Biosafety modules.

Unit Structure

Students are required to check dates and times of theory (lectures) and practical sessions on the Class Allocation System (CAS): https://www.uwa.edu.au/students/My-course/Class-timetable. Failure to attend a practical session on time may lead to exclusion from that practical.
THEORY - The unit has 36 lectures (3 per week, 45 minutes each) that are delivered face-to-face by a multidisciplinary teaching team. The lectures are organised in seven themes (see "unit information" for details). As described under "Learning and Teaching Strategy", students are expected to engage in revising the lecture, consulting textbooks for complementary readings and by working through additional material available via LMS (e.g. videos) on a regular basis.  Students are expected to use revision questions (approximately one Multiple Choice Question – MCQ - per lecture) published on LMS at regular intervals for self-reflection of their theory learning. Knowledge of the theory content will be assessed during semester via online quizzes (approximately one MCQ per lecture) published at regular intervals (18% on unit marks) and the final exam composed of 80 Multiple Choice Questions (50% of unit marks). 
PRACTICALS – NOTE Student must pass a mandatory online Biosafety module before the end of semester week 4 and provide evidence of successful completion by submitting a certificate via LMS. Failure to successfully complete the Biosafety module may result in exclusion from practicals 2 and 3. Successful completion of the Biosafety module will be rewarded with 2% of unit marks and a certifcate that is valid for three years and allows participation in further Biosafety modules.
The unit has three practicals (3 hours each) focussing on: the isolation and quantification of a DNA as an example of a macromolecule (practical 1); the use of restriction enzymes and agarose gel electrophoresis to analyse DNA and to study the use of chemical agents that can cause DNA mutations (practical 2); and the movement of water and solutes across cellular membranes (practical 3). To prepare for each practical, students must work through material provided via LMS and take a pre-lab quiz composed of MCQs. The unit has three pre-lab quizzes with a total value of 9% of unit marks. During the laboratories, students will do experimental work, practice the safe use of standard laboratory techniques and apparatus and record data. Following the experimental work, students will interpret data and/or demonstrate knowledge of the experiments done. This will be assessed via post-lab assignments (practicals 1 and 3) or a worksheet during the practical (practical 2). These assessments have a total value of 21% of unit marks.
