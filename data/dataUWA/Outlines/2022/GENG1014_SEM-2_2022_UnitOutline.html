Academic Information

Content Through this unit, student engineers will be able to recognise and explain the interconnectedness between the natural and built environments and society, as a foundation to design sustainable engineering solutions and to be responsible stewards of the Earth. This unit provides an introduction to the physical, geological, hydrological and ecological processes for earth systems engineering, and a holistic systems approach to how these processes respond and interact with each other. The unit covers theories and quantitative techniques to understand interactions between water, air and soil, and the ecosystem response to these interactions.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

The content of this unit will be delivered through lecture and workshop sessions, organised into topics/themes each week. Workshops each week will also dedicate time to ensuring students develop the skills required to complete the assignments (e.g., technical report writing, debating, and multimodal presentation/blogging and vlogging). It is expected that all students take an active role in the deliberations and activities presented in the workshops each week.
Any materials and additional information, such as movies/clips/material, of relevance will be uploaded to LMS.

Unit Structure

This unit runs every week of the standard Semester 2. Each week you will attend one two-hour lecture (Monday) and one two-hour workshop session (on Tuesday, Thursday or Friday); venues are as listed on the timetable for this unit. As noted in the Unit Schedule below, not all weeks will have a workshop session. There is a class field trip on Friday 19 August 2022, 10am-2pm. Study break is after Week 6 of semester.
The outcomes of this unit will be assessed through individual and group assignments, as well as participation and collaborative learning. There are eight assessment items in total: three individual quizzes, three individual assignments, participation/group reflection, and collaborative learning. More information on the assessments in this unit can be found in the section 'Other'.

Participation

This unit is fundamentally based upon active participation from students. As such, participants are required to attend all sessions. 16% of the assessment in this course is based upon participants being present and demonstrating collaborative learning, i.e., the student is present for the majority of the session and is actively participating in class activities and discussions. Attendance will be marked, however, simply turning up to class to have you name marked off and leaving shortly after will not count towards your final collaborative learning mark (we have eyes everywhere!).
In exceptional circumstances, special consideration for class participation will be given. However, the student must apply for special circumstances via the EMS Student Office (located ground floor of EZONE North or via email).

Referencing Requirements

The three assignments for this unit are in different modes, but it is expected that students would cite references in all of these as they would if they were writing a report. Accepted reference styles are IEEE, Harvard or Oxford.

Other

Collaborative learning can be defined in many ways depending on the context. In this unit, the aim is to create an environment where the learning happens in groups working together to solve a problem, complete a task, or create a product. It is based upon the idea that learning is a naturally social act. The participants will be assessed on the extent to which they contribute to the development, promotion and delivery of solutions, tasks and products.


Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "honest, ethical and responsible scholarship". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
