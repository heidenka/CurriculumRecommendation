Unit Information

This unit examines the role of Islam in the political process at state, regional and international levels. It looks at the main precepts of the Islamic religion, the notion of the Islamic state and its relationship to notions of democracy. In addition, the unit deals with the issue of Islamic revivalism in such countries as Algeria, India, Iran, Pakistan and Saudi Arabia. Given that a number of secular and non-Muslim states host a significant number of Muslims, the question is raised of the role of Islam in the relationship between Muslim immigrants and host states. From the perspective of the interaction between domestic and international politics, the unit examines the emergence of Islamic militancy and the linkages established between Islamic groups across state boundaries, and assesses their implications for state, regional and international stability.
The unit is premised on an understanding that Islam's role crosses international boundaries, with implications for the functioning of international and regional institutions. Questions have  been asked about the 'inherent nature' of Islam and Muslims across the globe. Does Islam encourage violence in the name of Jihad? Does Islamic internationalism always pose a threat to global stability? Does a clash exist between Islam and the West? What roles do Muslims living in predominantly non-Muslim and liberal democracies play? Can they be 'trusted'? What policies, if any, should be adopted towards Muslims and countries undergoing Islamic revivalism (in its different manifestations)? Unlike the 1970s when Muslim countries were also subject to intense international scrutiny, Islam has occupied a central place in contemporary discussions and debates among Muslims as well. These discussions mirror, and sometimes extend beyond, the questions raised by non-Muslims. They centre on the place of religion in the lives of Muslims, the connection between sacred texts and socio-cultural contexts, the relationship between Islam and politics, and the rights and responsibilities of Muslims across the world. In short, Islam's role in global politics is attracting unprecedented attention in the new millennium.
The unit is divided into three parts. The first part concentrates on the basic concepts of Islam as a religion and the divisions between groups concerning the relevance of these ideas. It discusses these differences in light of historical experiences and views on the relationship between text and context. Particular attention is given to the concept of Caliphate in Islam, how Muslims view the nature and responsibilities of a state vis-a-vis Muslims, and ideas about Islam's relationship to the non-Muslim world. The second part deals with the phenomenon of Islamic revivalism. It looks at the role played by societal and state-sponsored Islam in selected Muslim majority states. Questions are raised about the implications of Islamic revivalism for democracy and presence of alternative views in these countries. This part also analyses the effect of Islamic revivalism on women and religious minorities, and ideas about foreign policy. The third part investigates the role of transnational Islam and developments related to Islam and Muslims since the terrorist attacks on the US in September 2001, including linkages among Muslim diaspora, the rise of the so-called Islamic States, Islamophobia, home grown militancy, and their implications for Muslim minorities living in non-Muslim societies, especially Australia.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

Teaching and learning in this unit is based on the premise that we learn best when we enjoy the subject, have the freedom to ask questions, conduct independent research, collaborate with others and ensure that we learn techniques of research which could be policy relevant. As such, the unit consists of the following:
1. Face-to-face one hour lecture per week
 The lectures are designed to introduce students to the basic ideas about Islam, interpretational debates among Muslims on selected issues, political Islam, global linkages between Muslim states and societies and the shifts in how Muslims and Islam have interacted with a globalised world. My approach is to use lectures as a space in which students can focus on what is being communicated, develop their listening skills, and be able to ask questions about a dimension they are unclear about. So I would allocate 5 minutes towards the end of each class to let students ask questions. Additional questions could be asked on Discussion Forum if you wish.
I would also provide relevant readings at the end of each lecture for those interested in additional information.
2.Two hour long tutorials
The two hours long tutorials are designed to enable students to deepen their understanding of the role played by Islam and Muslims in contemporary international system. Each student is allocated a question from the tutorial topics identified in the unit program. The students are required to do the following:
a) prepare a 1,000 words written presentation (like a mini-essay, with full references and bibliography). The word count does not include references and bibliography. 
b) prepare a powerpoint presentation to use in the class to make a 10 minutes presentation to other students. 
Once all presentations for the day are given, students are expected to ask questions to the presenters, and share their own views on the issues being discussed. Each student who is NOT presenting on a given tutorial topic is expected to have read at least two recommended readings for the week to participate in the discussion.
Students are also guided to explore other related issues in smaller groups in each tutorial group.
So, if you think two hours is too long, rest assured you would wish there was more time!
3. Simulation Exercise
The unit includes a simulation exercise in which students are allocated roles as representatives of selected countries. They are expected to work in teams collaboratively to develop a position paper for their assigned country, and then use it as the basis for presenting their arguments and suggestions in an hypothetical summit meeting dealing with the selected issue.
This exercise is designed to give students the opportunity to learn about specific issues, conduct research in smaller groups, and learn the art of negotiation and deliberation in international meetings. Their ultimate aim is to find a resolution to the specified issue...or at least attempt to find one.
4. Self Reflections
Each student is expected to write a summary of what they have learned during the week and upload it as Journal Entry by 4pm Friday. Please note it is part of both formative and summative assessment. Students are required to reflect on knowledge and experience acquired in the unit at the end of the semester in the Final Examination. The weekly entries  assist with this process and help your tutors to monitor your learning as the semester progresses.
5.Final examination
The final examination will consist of two questions that will assess students' learning and ability to integrate information acquired during the semester.  This would include 
1. a Multiple Choice Quiz ,and 
2. a reflective essay (1800-2000 words) that would draw upon your reflections contained in Journal Entries.

Participation

Tutorial Participation:
Students are required to attend all tutorials and actively participate in the discussions following the Tutorial Presentations. Please note active participation is based on the understanding that you would be reading at least two of the recommended readings each week, demonstrate that knowledge willingly and also work actively with other members of the smaller in-class groups to answer questions identified during the course of the discussion. 
Participation in Simulation Exercise:
The Simulation exercise requires all students to work collaboratively in small groups and prepare Group Reports on the allocated country roles. Each small Country Group would be required to identify a Representative who would summarize their opinion on radicalization in their assigned country and how the issue could be addressed globally and locally. 
During the two Simulation Exercise Meetings, students are required to listen attentively, keep notes on the positions of other countries, confer with their own Country members in the tutorial on how to respond to the positions taken by other countries, conduct independent research when required, and develop strategies for collaboration with other countries. 
The Exercise requires students to identify potential partner countries, work with them, and develop draft resolutions. They are also required to critically analyze other draft resolutions and strive to find some common grounds to reach a resolution.
Please note, it is ESSENTIAL that students do not miss the Simulation exercise (including the preparation of the Group Report and developing draft resolutions). Failure to do so without a valid reason (approved through Students Office Special Consideration) would mean losing ALL marks for the Simulation Exercise.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
