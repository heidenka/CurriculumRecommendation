Academic Information

Content This unit explores the origins and definitions of life, and the processes that led to today's incredibly diverse array of organisms, from viruses to vertebrates. Unifying patterns and concepts are demonstrated, such as the structure and function of cells, how cells reproduce, how traits particular to a species are passed to new generations, and how the inheritance of DNA is the basis of evolution and the formation of species. The unit appeals to students wishing to begin their training as a biological scientist and who are interested in learning about topics that are challenging and fascinating our leading researchers.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

This unit adopts a blended model with face to face classes, online learning, extensive online support materials, workshops and dedicated lab classes. Students are expected to actively participate in all components of the Unit. 
For students studying remotely, all material will be available online with recorded lectures, online practicals and online workshops held via MS Teams. The only difference between online only and face to face students is the delivery of the practicals. The lab preparation is the same. 

Unit Structure

Theory
The theory is taught through the online textbook  (Biology, Raven, 12th edition, ISBN 9781260565959) with the help of online exercises  and guided text book revision (LearnSmart). The lectures associated with the online learning, will support the textbook, but will also visit and explain other important concepts you will need to learn as you become a biologist, such as statistics, report writing and an understanding of how research evaluates and tests information. The first lecture (Introduction to BIOL1130) is held live (face to face) in the Octagon Lecture Theatre on Monday 28th February at 11 am. The other recorded lectures (from 2 to 13) are available on the home page of the BIOL1130 LMS site (Lecture Capture System link) – see below for more details. 
Research forums 
Research forums provide real life applications of the learning topics and information of how to use this knowledge. Research forums are held live (face to face) in the Octagon Lecture Theatre on Mondays at 11 am. They are recorded and available on the home page of the BIOL1130 LMS site (Lecture Capture System link) – see below for more details. 
Labs
Wet labs, practicals and Q&A sessions are designed to teach core practical skills and report writing skills. Wet labs and practical sessions are compulsory and are held at Crawley and Albany campuses. In Crawley, wet labs will be conducted in the first-year laboratories in the Biology Wing of the Botany Building at the southern side of campus. Wet labs will begin in Week 2. Your laboratory room allocation will be determined by the Class Allocation System (CAS). Check the CAS website for your allocation during the first week of classes. Once allocated, you are expected to remain in the same practical session for the remainder of the semester. However, any students with difficulties related to laboratory allocations should contact Ms Lindie Watkins (student-sbs@uwa.edu.au) who can assist you with clashes and changed enrolments, etc.
There will be weekly Q&A sessions for all face to face students, held via MS Teams, at 12 pm on Fridays (on weeks where laboratories are scheduled). 
For those students studying remotely (online students), the practicals will be delivered through online resources and there will be online interactive Q&A sessions held at 1 pm on Fridays (on weeks where laboratories are scheduled).

Participation

Attendance of lectures and research forums is optional, but strongly recommended. Participation in the wet labs and practicals is compulsory, as this will teach you the practical skills that will enable you to progress in your degree (there will be one wet lab and one practical for each of the four modules). Participation in the Q&A is optional and is recommended if you have questions you need answering. 
Online only students will be exempt from the labs, but will be required to work through the online lab component. 
The four (4) module exams are based on the online learning, the lectures, and the research forums. 

Referencing Requirements

Students will use the Journal of Experimental Biology referencing style. Guidelines on how to do this will be provided throughout the course through lectures, workshops and information on LMS. Further information is available at https://jeb.biologists.org/content/manuscript-prep#3.

Scientific report 
There will be two (2) practice reports prior to the final report that you will submit for wet lab 3 (40% of unit mark). We will detail how to write the reports in the practical sessions. The aim of the reports is to assist you in learning how to write reports and understand where to find and where to report different parts of the information associated with scientific experiments. The rubric that you will use for practice reports will be the same as for the final report that you submit. The practice reports are not assessed, but they are essential for your learning. It will guide you in what makes a good and bad report and the process will expose you to examples of how to structure a report. We will be providing our marking for these reports after you have completed the reports. 


Unit Contingency Plans:

  • What will happen to the unit in the event of lockdown, masking, or stringent social distancing, e.g.: moved online; lectures moved online with labs deferred; etc. 

    In the case of a lockdown or stringent social distancing, all lectures, research forums, wet labs, and practicals will take place online. For Crawley and Albany face-to-face students this will mean that face-to-face lectures, research forums wet labs, and practicals will be transitioned online. For online-only students, there will be no change. 

Students will be required to wear masks in line with government restrictions in all face-to-face classes including research forums, lectures, wet labs, and practicals.


  • What will happen to the unit in the event of staff absence due to COVID-related reasons, e.g.: replacement lecturer; recorded lectures from previous years; etc. 

    Lectures and research forums may be delivered by an alternative lecturer. If this is not possible, research forums and lectures will be replaced with recordings from previous years.

If lab/practical demonstrators are sick for COVID-related reasons alternative demonstrators may be used. If this is not possible, labs/practicals may be replaced by online labs/practical sessions utilising pre-recorded content developed for online students and interaction with available lab demonstrators through Microsoft Teams.


  • What the implications for students in the unit are if they get COVID or must self-isolate due to close contact. It will be important in this respect to remember the 2 week special consideration policy in play. 

    Students who get COVID or need to self-isolate as a close contact will be allowed 2 weeks extension on any assignments due in that period. They will also be provided with online material for any research forums, lectures or wet labs missed.


Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
