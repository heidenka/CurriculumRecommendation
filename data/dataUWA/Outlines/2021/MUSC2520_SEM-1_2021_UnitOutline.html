Teaching contingency plan (In response to Covid19 restrictions)

Due to COVID-19 we have faced unprecedented changes to the way we deliver teaching. Currently, this unit is planned to be delivered face-to-face. If face-to-face teaching is not possible and all teaching must revert to online delivery, the following changes may need to be made to deliver the unit online. These should be read in conjunction with the unit outline published on the LMS. If you have any questions, please contact Dr Jonathan Fitzgerald, jonathan.fitzgerald@uwa.edu.au.

1. Will the unit objectives still be met unaltered?

-YES

2. Will the assessment activity be the same?

-YES, assessments will simply be moved online

-Practical and written exams will be administered via Zoom instead of in-person, and written assignments will be submitted via email

-The 5% "Attendance/Participation" assessment item will be removed, and other assessment items proportionally increased

3. What activities will replace face-to-face activities?

-Content delivery will remain the same, but simply move from face-to-face to online

-Lectorials will be delivered live on Zoom, recorded and uploaded to LMS

-Tutorials will be delivered live on Zoom, but will not be recorded

-Attendance at Zoom lectorials/tutorials will not be compulsory, but strongly encouraged

4. Actions the student needs to take in online mode:

-Students should note that it is in their best interest to attend all live sessions

-In the absence of compulsory attendance, students will need to take responsibility for their learning, ensuring that the lectures are watched each week, assignments are completed, and that they stay engaged with the unit in general

Academic Information

Content This unit is the third in a four-semester sequence that develops core theoretical, musical and perceptual skills. The unit comprises two mutually supportive components—(1) harmony; and (2) aural. These components comprise an integrated whole in which strong interconnections are defined in both content and assessment. The broader aim of this four-semester program is to support the acquisition of musical fluency and the capacity for independent learning. This unit extends students' understanding of musical grammar, both on paper and in their creative imagination. It deals with sophisticated harmonic concepts and chromaticism. On completion of the four-semester Music Language program, the course aims to produce musically literate students who are able to apply the skills they have acquired in any professional situation. This unit is a compulsory unit in the Specialist Music Studies major in the Bachelor of Arts degree. It is also open to students enrolled in other majors and degree pathways who fulfil the prerequisites and who have an interest in music.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

MUSC2520 comprises two equal, complementary and integrated components: (1) Harmony and (2) Aural. The assessment requirements within this unit are designed to ensure that students develop a satisfactory grasp of each component. A firm understanding of the language of music is essential, supporting all musical development in performance, composition and musicology.
The unit is delivered face-to-face through lectorial, workshop and tutorial formats, supported by weekly assignments and online materials. Teaching and learning strategies are practical and interactive, and may include the use of instruments and the singing voice; the use of conducting gestures and clapping; listening analytically to audio and video examples; study of notated examples; the writing of notated assignments both in class and between sessions; and verbal explanations of musical concepts.

Unit Structure

UNIT COMPONENTS
COMPONENT A: HARMONY (50%)
Through a weekly 2-hour lectorial and 1-hour tutorial, this component builds on the concepts introduced in Music Language 1 and 2, in which students should have attained a deep understanding of tonal harmony, musical grammar and syntax, phrase/period structure, and analytical techniques. We will now examine more sophisticated harmonic concepts (including new developments in the nineteenth century which pushed the boundaries of tonality); how musical structures combine to create logical, coherent and goal-oriented large scale forms; and the implications for composition, analysis, and performance.  
Learning Outcomes:
Upon completion of the Harmony component, students should be able to (a) identify and construct the following chromatic harmonies: Neapolitan bII, augmented sixths, chords of mode mixture, common-tone diminished sevenths and common-tone augmented sixths, altered dominant sevenths; (b) identify and construct diatonic and chromatic modulations; (c) identify tonal ambiguity, including the expansion of mode mixture, plagal relation, reciprocal process, semi-tonal voice leading, double tonality, enharmonic reinterpretation, and symmetrical sonorities; (d) construct basic reductions of semi-tonal voice leading; (e) outline the development of tonal paths from the eighteenth century to the late nineteenth century; (f) identify and label chromatic sequences and sequential progressions; (g) demonstrate an understanding of large-scale formal structures including binary, ternary, rondo and sonata forms; (h) demonstrate an understanding of compositional trends and developments in the 19th century and their impact on the tonal system; (i) demonstrate a basic understanding of post-tonal compositional aesthetics & procedures.

COMPONENT B: Aural (50%)
Through twice weekly 1-hour tutorials, this component builds on the concepts introduced in the Aural components of Music Language 1 and 2, in which students should have attained the ability to confidently cope with music in major and minor keys, and in simple and compound meters. We will now incorporate non-diatonic pitches into the major and minor framework established in previous semesters, and develop a fluidity with rhythms in not only simple and compound meters, but also mixed and asymmetrical meters.
Learning Outcomes:
Upon completion of the Aural component, students should be able to (a) transcribe and perform melodies that feature non-diatonic pitches; (b) transcribe and perform rhythms in simple, compound, mixed, and asymmetrical meters; (c) transcribe chord progressions that feature diatonic and non-diatonic harmonies; (d) use solfège as a tool to cope with modulating melodies; and (e) use solfège as a tool to cope with modal music.

Participation

Participation is an essential component of the teaching and learning of these fundamentally practical elements - both Harmony and Aural are taught in a manner that requires practical responses and musical interaction. Therefore physical presence in the classroom is essential, and students are partly assessed on the quality of their participation in class. Students will be expected to prepare between sessions in order to participate fully, effectively and expressively in preparation for assessment.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.
