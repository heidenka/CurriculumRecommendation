Academic Information

Content This unit provides students with an understanding of the governance structure of planning systems in Australia, and in particular Western Australia. It introduces students to the political and bureaucratic frameworks that underpin planning; the role of different tiers of government—federal, state and local; the links between planning, the business sector and non-government organisations; the dynamics of power, conflict, and decision-making in democratic planning systems; and the complex interplay of social, political and economic processes within planning systems and policy decision making more broadly.

Unit Information

Welcome to PLNG5403. Planning and Governance, a core unit for the Postgraduate Diploma/Masters in Urban and Regional Planning, will be led by Dr. Paul J. Maginn in Semester 1 of the 2021 academic year.
Whilst planning is a technocratic profession, all planning (re)actions (e.g. research, problem identification, policy-making, policy implementation, and policy evaluation) take place within some form of political context, if not multiple contexts - personal, professional, political and/or community. As such it is imperative that professional planners, whether they work in local/state government and/or the private sector as statutory and/or strategic planners need to have an appreciation and understanding of the theoretical and practical facets of the political and institutional landscapes that characterise planning and policy-making.  PLNG5403 provides a foundational introduction to the complex interplay of structural processes, institutions and organisations, and, policy and political actors that characterise planning and governance systems as the democratic basis of urban and regional development. 
A core aim of this unit is to enhance students’ appreciation, appetite and understanding of the policy and political contexts of planning as a technocratic profession. Hence, three core concepts lie at the heart of not only this unit but professional planning practice: Power; Policy; and, Politics. 
This unit blends theory and practice about ‘real world’ planning and governance via a mix of academic-based lectures, student-led seminar presentations, and guest lectures from professional planners and policy advocates. In addition, students will be directed to additional materials (e.g. podcasts, documentaries, films, TV shows) to provide supplementary insights into planning and governance.
Students completing this unit will gain critical understanding – theoretical and practical - of the policy and political machinations of planning/policy processes by surveying key concepts and theories within urban planning, politics, governance, and decision making. Planning and policy decisions are informed to varying degrees by technical expertise and value judgments expressed by democratically elected officials, planners, and a myriad of other stakeholders such as local communities, social movements (e.g. BLM, Save Our Suburbs, the development industry and environmental groups. 
PLNG5403 exposes how the actions and interplay of these different actors are influenced by wider structural processes – globalisation, capitalism, patriarchy, racism and class – and ideological beliefs. Through guided and independent self-directed study, students will be able to understand the ‘bigger picture’ about planning and governance and the significance of politics within professional planning practice.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

To develop critical thinking and professional capacities in understanding the governance, political and policy landscapes that characterise planning decision-making systems, institutions, and processes in Australia, especially Western Australia.
This is to be achieved by:
1.	Introducing students to the political and bureaucratic frameworks that underpin planning; the role of different tiers of government - federal, state and local – and non-governmental institutions in planning and policy-making; and the complex interplay of social, political and economic processes within planning systems and policy decision-making more broadly. 
2.	Creating sensitivity to the tensions between planning as a technical profession and a political activity within the dynamics of governance within and beyond the spheres of government.  
Unit Learning Outcomes 
At the unit end, you will be competent in your ability to demonstrate the following outcomes:
1. Critically distinguish key theoretical underpinnings of planning and governance (Assignments 1 and 2);
2. Develop an appreciation of the practical and operational facets of the ‘institutional, policy and political landscape’ of planning and governance within WA/Australia (Assignments 2 and 3); and,
3. Be prepared for planning practice by exposing you to the political and bureaucratic machinations of planning decision-making and governance at the state and local government levels (Assignments 1, 2 and 3).

Unit Structure

The unit is taught primarily via face-to-face learning – subject to any restrictions as a result of COVID19. Lectures will be broadcast and recorded via MS Teams for those unable to make it on to campus.
Classes take place on Mondays from 10-12pm and 1-3pm. See the teaching schedule for full details of content and structure of all class sessions. Please note that these may be subject to change/cancellation due to illness or other relevant reasons.
PLNG5403 is delivered via a series of (i) content-based classes that will focus on key conceptual and theoretical aspects of planning and governance; (ii) a series of presentations by professional planner and policy advocates on the strategic, practical and operational aspects of planning and governance, and (iii) student-led seminar presentations. 
There will also be several fieldtrips (virtual and in-person) to real planning and governance spaces (e.g local council committee meeting, JDAP meeting, WAPC public meeting)  that students will be advised/encouraged to attend in order to see planning and governance in action. 
A range of materials – ‘Readings’; ‘Listenings’ and ‘Viewings’ - will be used throughout the semester. Required and recommended reading materials, especially those relating to Assignment 1 and Assignment 2 will be advised during class and available via  in the LMS.
As postgraduate students independent self-directed learning and exploration is expected and strongly encouraged. Hence, you are required to source and review additional reputable reading/listening/viewing materials to help enhance your intellectual and professional understanding of planning, policy-making and governance.  
Time Commitment
Over the course of a semester students are expected to commit at least 150 hours of study towards this unit.  Students who commit their time wisely and demonstrate a commitment to developing their knowledge and understanding of the theoretical, practical, political and professional aspects of planning and governance are, based on past experience, likely to perform better.

Participation

Students are strongly encouraged, where possible, to attend all classes in-person for PLNG5403. In-person attendance provides for a richer and more dynamic learning experience with your student peers and with academic staff. Moreover, it also allows you to develop social networks and other people skills by interacting directly with other students.
The first teaching session (10.00am-12.00pm) in PLN5403 is primarily content-driven and led by the unit co-ordinator, Dr Paul Maginn. These classes will be broadcast and recorded so you can view lectures at a later time. Although these classes will be content-driven they will also be interactive in the sense that students will be invited/encouraged to ask questions and/or respond to questions posed during class.
The second teaching session (1.00-3.00pm) in PLNG5403 largely includes a mix of student-led seminar presentations and guest lectures by professional planners from the DPLH/WAPC, and policy advocates from the Committee for Perth, and, the Urban Development Institute of Australia (WA). 
ALL students are required to attend ALL students-led seminar presentations, the fieldtrip to the DPLH/WAPC and the two guest lectures as outlined in the class schedule. Furthermore, ALL students are required to read the designated core readings for each student-led seminar presentation. Students leading each seminar presentation are required to supplement the core readings with additional readings so as to be able to deliver an informed presentation. Students leading the various presentations are encouraged to share whatever additional readings they find with other student via the discussion board on the LMS.

Student Complaints Protocol 
The Department of Geography and Planning operates a carefully designed protocol with respect to student complaints. These apply to the delivery of any unit with the prefix GEOG or PLNG. All matters are followed up, whether these are made in person, via email or in writing. Strict confidentiality and anonymity is applied at all stages. 
Student complaints relating to administration, teaching, supervision or the conduct of either staff or students should be directed to the relevant Head of Teaching and Learning within the Department. Student complaints relating to any other matter should be directed to the Responsible Officer as detailed at https://www.web.uwa.edu.au/university/complaints/students/complaint-policy-and-procedures
The Departmental Heads of Teaching and Learning are:
Dr Sarah Prout Quicke (sarah.proutquicke@uwa.edu.au) for undergraduate units; and,
Dr Linda Robson (linda.robson@uwa.edu.au) for postgraduate units.
In the event that the complaint involves either Head of Teaching and Learning, the complaint should be directed to the other Head. Any complaint from a student will be acknowledged in writing within 2 working days of receipt. 
Complaints received by the Head of Teaching and Learning will be followed up by an invitation to the student to meet with the relevant Head of Teaching and Learning, together with the Head of Department (currently Dr Julian Clifton). This discussion will be an opportunity to explore the background to the complaint and discuss it in more detail, not to determine the validity of the complaint. The student is welcome to bring along a friend or other supporting individual if s/he wishes. Staff may take notes during this meeting but these records will be strictly confidential and not divulged to anyone outside the meeting.
The Head of Department and the Head of Teaching and Learning will then hold a separate meeting with the member of staff identified within the complaint. This will explore the staff perspective on the complaint. The identity of the complainant will not be revealed, nor will any material be used which could identify the complainant. 
Both of the above meetings will be held within 10 working days of acknowledging the complaint.
Within five working days of the above, the Head of Department and the Head of Teaching and Learning will reach a decision on the complaint and communicate this via email to the student and member of staff involved. The student retains the right to appeal this decision. 
Further information on the official complaint procedure is available at https://www.web.uwa.edu.au/university/complaints/students/submit

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.
