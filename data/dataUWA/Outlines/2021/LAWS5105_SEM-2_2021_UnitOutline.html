Academic Information

Content In this unit, students learn about the main private law remedies organised according to their dominant functions. The first part of the unit considers compensatory remedies and in the second part, restitutionary, punitive and coercive remedies are also considered. The unit has a strong practical focus, given the importance of remedies in practice. Students critically examine available remedies and the circumstances of their availability and also consider possible reforms.

Unit Information

In this unit, you will learn about the main private law remedies, at common law, in equity and under selected statutes.  The unit is organised according to the dominant functions of private law remedies.  We will begin by considering compensatory remedies, which aim to make good the plaintiff’s loss.  We will look at the different approaches of the common law, equity and the Australian Consumer Law.  We will then consider restitutionary remedies, which aim to strip away the defendant’s gain.  We will look separately at personal and proprietary restitutionary remedies.  Next, we will consider coercive remedies, including injunctions and specific performance, which compel the defendant to do or refrain from doing a particular act.  Finally, we will consider punitive and vindicatory remedies, such as exemplary damages and apologies, which seek to punish the defendant and vindicate the plaintiff.
The content of the unit relates back to, and builds upon, the core units you have already studied in Law School. For this reason, Remedies is sometimes described as a ‘capstone’ unit.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

The unit content is delivered by way of three hours of face-to-face lectures per week (also recorded for students who cannot attend) and a one-hour face-to-face tutorial per week.   

Unit Structure

Lectures
There will be three hours of lectures a week as follows:
Monday 11.00 – 12.45 (Alexander Lecture Theatre)
Wednesday 10.00 -10.45 (Murdoch Lecture Theatre)
Felicity Maher will introduce the unit and give the lectures on general principles of compensatory remedies. Nicholas Tiverios will give the lectures on damages for breach of contract, damages in tort, compensation in equity and damages under the Australian Consumer Law. Felicity Maher will then lecture on restitutionary remedies, including gain-based awards for wrongs, restitution for unjust enrichment and proprietary restitutionary remedies.  Nicholas Tiverios will then lecture on the coercive remedies, specific performance and injunctions, and on debt and the limits on debt (including forfeiture and penalties).  Finally, Robyn Carroll will lecture on punitive and vindicatory remedies.

Participation

There is a 10% tutorial participation mark which will be assessed by reference to your performance in your 10 best tutorials.
The objectives of the participation mark are to promote:
•	attendance, 
•	engagement, 
•	preparation, 
•	reflection; and 
•	responsible tutorial membership.
Engagement will most obviously be in speaking but a student might also be engaged whilst listening. This will be important to note for students who are by nature quieter. They may be engaged, though speak less often.
Preparation might be evident in notes the student has come with or in the quality of answers and comments in discussion.
Reflection on the issues (and preparation) might be evident in the student drawing on material outside the material directly related to the tutorial problem, perhaps from other topics in the course and in questions asked during (or even after) the tutorial seminar;
Responsible tutorial membership refers to the respect given to other student’s comments and refraining from attempting to dominate the discussion.
 
Attendance is expected at all 11 tutorials over the course of the semester. We appreciate, however, that from time to time students may be unable to attend due to illness or some other reason. With this in mind, if a student misses 1 of the 11 tutorials this will not affect the tutorial participation mark. The mark will be based on the student’s performance over the remaining 10 tutorials.
Participation is about the quality and not necessarily the quantity of contributions (where ‘quality’ means considered and reflective contributions – you are allowed to make mistakes!). The aim of including this component is to encourage unselfconscious discussion amongst students, not an atmosphere of assessment.
All students are expected to prepare for, and to contribute to class discussions in, all tutorials. Attendance and participation is the key to effective learning.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
