Academic Information

Content This unit is designed to inspire pre-service science teachers to make science exciting, interesting and relevant for high school students. It provides them with opportunities to develop theoretical knowledge needed to interpret the curriculum and make appropriate judgements and decisions related to the teaching of science. The focus of the unit is on specific learning outcomes, implementation and assessment strategies associated with ATAR and General science subjects in Years 11 and 12 in Western Australia including physics, chemistry, biological sciences, human biological science, earth and environmental science, integrated science and psychology. Students develop a repertoire of teaching skills and strategies for engaging adolescents in meaningful learning that leads to a powerful understanding of science concepts.

DETAILED ASSESSMENT REQUIREMENTS
Assignment 1: Critique of contemporary science education issue                40%			~2000 words
Assignment 1 consists of two parts. Part A is a five minute presentation and a five minute discussion and Part B is a written critique.
Part A (10%)
Present an oral presentation to your classmates about a contemporary issue in science education. 
•	Prepare a 10-minute discussion about an issue that is important in secondary school science. Select from: 
o	Ability streaming in science classes
o	Single and mixed sex science classes
o	Argumentation and explanation in science
o	The use of socioscientific issues 
o	Critical and creative thinking in science
o	Teacher use of ICT in science
o	Student use of ICT in science
o	Developing literacy in science
o	Differentiation in science
o	Integration of STEM
o	Safety in science labs
o	Indigenous perspectives in science
o	Overcoming pseudoscience 
o	Explicit instruction versus Inquiry based teaching
o	Promoting science careers
o	Informal science education (eg museums, science centres)
o	Other (check with lecturer)
•	Aim to generate some discussion in the class about this aspect of science teaching and draw on the other pre-service teachers’ experiences. You should aim for approximately 5 minutes presentation and 5 minutes discussion (10 minutes in total).
Part B (30%)
For Part B of this assignment, you are required to provide a written analysis of the presentation topic.  The following are suggested headings for your assignment: 
Introduction: Briefly introduce and summarise your assignment. (approx 150 words)
Theory: You should explain how and why this topic is important for teaching and learning science. Develop a definition or description of the topic.  Explain the theory/philosophy that underpins the topic. Outline the findings of any research that has investigated the outcomes of the topic. Include references to your textbook, lecture notes, and other literature (approx 500 words). 
Analysis of Topic: Provide examples from the literature and your own experience of how the topic can be implemented in the classroom. Discuss the advantages and disadvantages of the topic from the teacher and the student perspectives and with different types of students/schools. Reflect on how you would use this topic to improve student learning with different groups of students. (approx. 1100 words)
Conclusions and Implications: Give a brief summary and overview of your assignment and the implications of your learning and your future teaching (approx 250 words).
References: Include a list of all references using the APA style. (not included in word count)
Format: Please state word count on cover page, page numbers, use headings and subheadings
Assignment 2: Probing Science Understanding 60%			 ~3000 words
Regardless of teaching approach, finding out students’ prior knowledge is crucial in science teaching. Research suggests that students have many alternative conceptions about science concepts that teachers need to take into consideration when planning. This assignment requires you to collect data from your fellow students, friends and family or other adults to find out what they already know about a science concept of your choice that will be taught in Year 11 or Year 12 Biology, Human Biology, Chemistry, Physics, Earth and Environmental Science, Psychology or Integrated Science.
Suitable topics can be found in the SCSA syllabus documents and textbooks. Topics might include genes and DNA; atomic structure; chemical bonding; Newton’s first law of motion; gravity; or the difference between heat and temperature. 
You must collect data to present in your assignment. The data you collect will depend on the method that you choose to probe understanding of the topic. 
Method of probing students’ understanding
Suitable places to look for resources to help you with finding out what the adults know about the concepts include the following:
o	American Association for the Advancement of Science (AAAS) hosts more than 600 assessment items (http://assessment.aaas.org/pages/home)
o	Two tier multiple choice items provided in this unit
o	Journal articles provided in this unit
o	SCSA past examinations and assessment items
o	Scootle (www.scootle.edu.au)
You can also create your own method of probing understanding using diagrams, photographs or a demonstration. You can also ask them to draw pictures, create a concept map or mind map or to write a paragraph about the topic. If you choose to collect data in this way, make sure you justify your choice by referencing literature. The purpose is to find out what the adults know. You need to be careful about how you do this. If you simply ask “what do you know about equilibrium? It is likely they may respond, “I don’t know anything.” Please note that multiple choice questions alone usually are not suitable for probing  understanding, unless they  are then asked about why they gave particular answers. You may need to trial your method of probing understanding with one or two adults and then refine it before you collect the final data for this assignment. 
You can collect your data during this unit. Other suitable approaches might be to get the adults to complete a short test, problem, quiz, POE (predict-observe-explain), concept map, concept cartoon, or diagram about the concept. Then, based on the responses to this activity, you could select 3-5 adults to interview one-to-one for five minutes so that you can ask them more probing questions about what they think about the concept and why they gave those answers. It is recommended that you collect the data for the assignment early in the unit.
The following subheadings are suggested headings for your written assignment.
Introduction: Briefly introduce and summarise your assignment (approx. 150 words)
Overview of the science concept (approx 200 words)
In this section provide a scientific explanation of the concept that will be the focus of your assignment. Refer to science textbooks or other resources from where you gathered this information and include these in the references section of the assignment.
Links with the upper secondary subjects (approx 150 words)
This section will consist of a description of where this topic is found in the Year 11 and 12 syllabus documents. You also need to describe where the concept has been developed in the year 7-10 curriculum. Reference this section with the relevant syllabus and text books and remember to included them in the references section of your assignment.
Literature review (approx 500 words)
In this section provide a literature review of research that has been conducted about students’ or adults’ prior knowledge about the concept. A variety of journals, books and websites are available online and through the library that focus on research in science education. You must include all references in the reference section of your assignment.
Methods of data collection and analysis (approx 500 words)
In this section include a clear and succinct description of the methods and procedure you used to probe understanding of the concept. Full copies of any interview protocols, quiz questions or problems used to probe understanding are placed in an appendix. 
Describe the way you analysed the data, that is, how you changed/manipulated that data so that it could be presented in the findings section (below) in a succinct and informative manner. Read the marking rubric carefully and note where trialling your data collection techniques is introduced.
Findings: Prior knowledge/alternative conceptions about the concept (approx 1000 words)
In this section you will describe your findings about the adults’ understandings of the concept. Data can be best presented in graphs, tables, short summaries and succinct examples of student work or interview excerpts to give an overview of what you found about the adults’ understandings. The findings should be linked with the literature review by explaining how your findings are similar to, or different from, those in the literature. 
Refer to the detailed data in the appendix to support and provide evidence for the assertions you make about the adults’ understandings. An example follows:
Four of the five adults did not make any connection between DNA and protein synthesis (see interview transcripts with Person B, C, D and E in appendix). Only one person, A, was able to do make a connection between DNA and proteins. A said, “the code on the DNA is to tell the cell how to make proteins” (Interview transcript with Person A in appendix).
Implications for teaching and learning (approx 500 words)
In this section discuss the implications of your findings about the adults’ understanding of the concept for teaching and learning. That is, based on what you found out about their understandings, how would you go about teaching these ideas to your future science students? What would it be necessary to do to convince students that some of their ideas are not correct? How would you introduce and consolidate the concepts so that conceptual change is likely to take place? Make sure strategies are linked to the literature.
References
Include a list of all references using APA 7 style. (not included in word count)
Appendices
Include examples of data collection techniques and all original raw data collected in appropriate appendices.
IMPORTANT: You must not identify the adults whose work you include in your assignment and do not use any information collected for any other purpose. You should remove the names of adults on all work samples and refer to them with pseudonyms, numbers or letters or in a de-identified manner such as Person A etc.

RESUBMISSION RULE
Failure to pass an assessment item within a unit (MTeach)
Students must pass all components of a Master of Teaching unit to pass the unit overall. Students who fail an assessment item in a unit may resubmit it if—
(a) this is their first request for a resubmission in the unit;
(b) they contact the unit coordinator by email within 5 University working days of the release of the result and formally request a resubmission.
(c) A resubmitted assignment that is deemed to be a ‘fail’ by the unit coordinator, will receive the original failing mark for the component.
(d) Where resubmission is approved, the reassessed mark is capped at the assessment pass mark, unless an application for mitigation is approved in accordance with the University Policy on Assessment: Special Consideration (UP11/23).
(e) A resubmission is normally due one week after being approved by the unit coordinator.
(f) Assignments that are failed on the grounds of lateness or academic misconduct will not normally be considered for resubmission.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
