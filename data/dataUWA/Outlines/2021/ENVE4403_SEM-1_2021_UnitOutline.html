Academic Information

Content The aim of this unit is to gain a quantitative knowledge of the fluid dynamics of both freshwater and marine environments. Topics covered include (1) physical laws governing fluid motion and exact solutions, an introduction to scaling as a means of simplifying complex equations; (2) introduction to turbulence—Reynolds-averaged Navier-Stokes equations, basic statistical descriptions and concept of eddy viscosity, molecular versus turbulent diffusion; (3) mixing in rivers—boundary layers, velocity profiles, hydraulic jumps, transport equation, shear dispersion; (4) mixing in a density-stratified fluid—models of mixing, concept of mixing efficiency and mixing across density interfaces, applications to estuaries, the coastal ocean and lakes; and (5) turbulent jet, plume and buoyant jet dynamics for wastewater discharge design.

Unit Information

The aim of this unit is to gain a quantitative knowledge of the fluid mechanics of environmental systems, and the processes that transport material in these systems.
The topics covered in 2021 will be:
(1) The physical laws governing fluid motion and an introduction to scaling as a means of simplifying complex equations.
(2) An introduction to turbulence — a basic statistical description of turbulence, incorporation of turbulence into the governing equations and the concepts of eddy viscosity and turbulent diffusivity.
(3) Open channel flow (as a turbulent, unstratified flow) — bulk descriptions of the flow (changes in depth and flow energy, hydraulic jumps) and understanding vertical variation (velocity profiles, effects of bed roughness).
(4) Mixing and dispersion in rivers — models for describing the transport and mixing of dissolved species, particles and heat.
(5) Turbulent, density-stratified flows — flows with vertical density differences, and models of mixing in these systems; mixing across density interfaces; applications to estuaries, the coastal ocean and lakes.
(6) The dynamics and dilution of turbulent jets, plumes and buoyant jets.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

Relevant material will be presented to the class during the lecture periods. A strong emphasis will be placed on providing relevant examples to environmental engineering in the lecture material. Furthermore, emphasis will be placed on teaching the techniques which need to be applied to compete the assessments. Students will need to keep up with the lecture material in order to make effective use of the weekly practical classes. Students are encouraged to work collaboratively on the assignments so as to learn from each other. Obviously, each individual must present their own solutions that they understand. Remember that you will need to complete your exam independently, so you are strongly encouraged not to rely on your peers to the detriment of your own understanding!
The total number of contact hours per week is 5 hrs, on average. It is expected that you will spend an additional 5 hrs per week studying the material, preparing assignment solutions and writing laboratory reports.
Please ask me lots of questions in class and during office hours.

Whereas the two other practical sessions will centre around the class working through particular problems with the tutor, the practical session on Monday 11-12 will serve as an unstructured drop-in session where the tutor will answer any questions that students have about any aspect of the unit. Instructions for the two laboratories will be posted on LMS.

There are 2 laboratory sessions (scheduled through CAS):
1. Open-channel flow (Week 7)
2. Tilting tube (Week 10)

You are required to participate in both laboratory sessions and to write up both laboratory reports. The laboratory reports should be submitted electronically on LMS.

Online timetabled students will be asked to watch video recordings of the two laboratory sessions, and complete laboratory reports based on their analysis.
For all students: Your lab report should include an Abstract, Results and Discussion section. The Discussion section should include the answers to the questions in the lab instructions. Please see "Guidelines for lab reports" on LMS for further details.

Each of the assignments should be submitted electronically through LMS. Scanned handwritten copies are acceptable, but only if they are clear and legible! Assignments that cannot be read cannot be marked. After submission, you will be provided with full solutions and a comprehensive marking scheme to each assignment.

Final exam
The final exam will be a 24-hour take-home exam and will cover all unit content.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
