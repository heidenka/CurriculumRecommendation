Academic Information

Content How do our brains interpret inputs from, and govern our interactions with, the world? This unit examines the theoretical, functional and biological bases of perception. To do so, the unit explores the psychological and neural basis of sensory perception, attention and the impact of brain damage on perceptual behaviour. It considers classic questions about the degree of functional localisation and specialisation present in the brain and also shows how behavioural and physiological measures may be used to study both brain function and the impact of the environment on that function. In the associated practical classes, students become acquainted with contemporary theory, phenomena and methodology in perception and sensory neuropsychology.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

The unit will use a lecture format to deliver critical content. There will be opportunities to ask questions in the middle and the end of the two hour lecture sessions. There will also be laboratory classes where you will be introduced to tools used for assessing sensory performance in a range of sensory modalities, in addition to collecting the data to be used for the laboratory report. The lectures and laboratory classes will be supported by the recommended textbook.
At the end students will be able to (1) appreciate the importance of the scientific method in advancing psychological knowledge; (2) describe the basic functioning of multiple sensory systems in both normal and abnormal populations; (3) understand and use research tools for studying sensory and perceptual functions relevant to psychology; (4) analyse, interpret and present data from different contemporary research techniques; and (5) present written summaries of scientific research.

Unit Structure

The unit will be presented using 12, 2- hour, lectures and 5, 2-hour, laboratory classes.

Participation

The unit presents a broad range of material that is best understood by actively reading the set chapters prior to the lectures and ensuring that you keep pace with the unit at is unfolds.
The laboratory classes compliment the lectures and are intended to provide hands-on experience of research in the topic area and assessment tools that might be used in this area. You will gain the most by attending all sessions and using these tools. Your contribution to the data collection for the laboratory report will both, help you write the report accurately and also ensure we have sufficient data to answer the research questions in the report.

Referencing Requirements

The lab report is a written report of the experiment which is to be conducted during the laboratory classes. The report should follow the conventional structure outlined in the laboratory classes and include a word count of the body of the assignment, excluding the Abstract and Reference List. Use American Psychological Association (APA) formatting style for reference citation in the text and the list of references. The UWA library has a guide to APA style at the following URL: https://guides.library.uwa.edu.au/apa

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.
