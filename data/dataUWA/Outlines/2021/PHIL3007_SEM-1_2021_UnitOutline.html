Academic Information

Content This unit introduces students to mathematical logic: the study of formal languages and their mathematical properties. The first part of the unit focuses on classical first-order logic and its metatheory: we discuss the interpretation of first-order logic and how to construct proofs within it. We then show that any such proof yields a valid argument (soundness) and that there is a proof for every valid argument (completeness). The rest of the unit discusses some more advanced topics in logic. These may include non-classical logics (e.g., modal logics, multi-valued logics, fuzzy logics, intuitionist logics, relevance logics, second-order logic), model-theoretic features of first-order logic (e.g., the compactness and Löwenheim-Skolem theorems), as well as an introduction to computability, recursion, and Gödel's incompleteness theorems.

Unit Information

This unit provides an introduction to mathematical logic, which is the study of formal languages. As noted above, the first and largest part of the unit concerns classical logic (propositional logic and predicate logic). But whereas in PHIL2002 the focus is on using these languages to construct and evaluate particular arguments, in this unit we take these languages as a whole as our object of study. In particular, we will ask the question whether some of the proof systems we construct for these languages are apt, in the sense that only valid proofs can be constructed ("soundness") and that all valid proofs can be constructed ("completeness"). 
The second part of the unit will consider some applications of these ideas. First, we will look at some basic model theory, which provides insight into what sorts of mathematical structures predicate logic can and cannot distinguish between. And second, we will look at a brief introduction to computability and incompleteness, arguably the most famous application of predicate logic. 
It is worth noting that this unit proceeds on the assumption that an informed philosophical discussion of logic requires an understanding of the mathematics used in logic. As such, the vast majority of the content of this unit is mathematically focused. That said, philosophical questions will occasionally be touched upon in the lectures, and students are welcome to bring up philosophical questions in class or in consultation.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

We will have two lectures and a tutorial each week. The lectures cover the theoretical material. The purpose of the tutorial is to answer any questions you may have and work through practice problems, which will prepare you for the weekly problem set. The weekly problem set for the previous week is due at the beginning of the tutorial. The idea is that each week you should split your time between on the one hand digesting the lectures for that week and trying your hand at the practice problems (which the tutorial will help you with) and on the other hand finishing the problem set which focuses on the material from the previous week. 
There are several learning strategies open to you. To become familiar with the theory, there are the lectures (available through the Lecture Capture System and face-to-face), the lecture notes which cover the same material in written form, and the textbook and handouts (available through Unit Readings). It is up to you how to use these: you may prefer just using the textbook or the lecture notes (e.g., if you like reading the material at your own pace), or just watching the lectures (e.g., because you find the combination of watching and listening more effective than just reading), or using both to get slightly different, but mutually reinforcing perspectives.

Unit Structure

The two weekly lectures have been pre-recorded and can be watched through the Lecture Capture System. If there is appetite for doing so, I will deliver these in person as well on Wednesdays and Fridays. The lectures are relatively short (almost all are around 30 minutes rather than the typical 45 minutes, with just two lectures being a bit longer at around 40 minutes). The accompanying lecture notes are available through LMS. 
The tutorial will be on Thursdays. As of the time of writing this, these are scheduled to take place at 16:00 in Arts 161 (but double-check this with your timetable!). We will discuss the practice problems for that week (see the unit schedule below) and any questions you may have about the lectures or anything else. 
The weekly problem sets are due in hard copy at the beginning of the tutorial. Please make sure to arrive on time. If you arrive late to the tutorial your problem set will be marked with the usual late penalty. If this presents a problem for you, you can hand in your problem set during an earlier class or during my consultation hour. Use this option, for example, if you know you cannot come to a particular tutorial, if you think you might need to arrive late, or if you have worked ahead. 
All lectures, lecture notes, practice problems, and problem sets are available on LMS from the beginning of the unit. You should feel free to work ahead as far as you like. This can be an especially useful strategy if you know another unit will take a lot of your time at certain points in the semester. If you require little or no help, you could conceivably complete all the work for this unit in the first few weeks (apart from the mid-term test and the exam).

Participation

Attending and actively participating in the tutorials is highly recommended. Homeworks are to be submitted in hard copy and in person at the start of the tutorial. Students who are absent or late to a tutorial will incur an automatic late penalty on the homework that is due that day, unless they had submitted that homework in hard copy and in person at a previous lecture, tutorial, or consultation.

Referencing Requirements

Homework should be handed in at the beginning of the tutorial on the day it is due. Homeworks can be printed or handwritten (but not emailed, hard copies only!). 
In order to mitigate the effects of implicit biases and other sources of unfairness in marking, all written work will be marked anonymously. To make this possible, it is vital that you put your student number but not your name on all written work (homeworks, mid-term test, and exam). 
Late work will be penalized 5% of the total available marks per day it is late (rounded up! e.g., five minutes late counts as one day late), as per university policy (UP15/5 in the UWA Policy Library https://www.uwa.edu.au/policy/home). If you think you may qualify for an extension you need to contact Student Services. More information is available at https://www.uwa.edu.au/students/need-help/special-consideration Outside of this process, late penalties will be applied without exceptions. 
Discussing your work with fellow students is allowed and in fact encouraged. However, any submitted work should be written solely by you. If you relied on extensive collaboration, please make note of this fact in your answers where relevant (without naming names to preserve anonymity). As always, make sure you properly cite your sources when relying on others' ideas in your written work. 
Please talk to your unit coordinator if you have any questions about the standards of academic integrity. The consequences of failing to abide by these standards can be severe. Please refer to the University's guidance on academic conduct here: https://www.student.uwa.edu.au/learning/resources/ace or the University's official policy on such matters (UP07/21 in the UWA Policy Library https://www.uwa.edu.au/policy/home).

Other

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
