Academic Information

Content This is an introductory unit that explores the molecular basis of life at the cellular level. The theoretical content of the unit is divided into four major themes: (1) DNA, with a focus on recombinant DNA technology; (2) information flow from DNA to RNA, with an emphasis on how this is regulated at the transcriptional and post-transcriptional levels; (3) the protein life cycle, involving an examination of protein synthesis and catabolism pathways; and (4) proteins as functional molecules, where the fundamentals of enzyme action are highlighted. Detailed content includes (1) role of DNA polymerases in DNA replication, tools of the molecular biologist, cloning, genetic recombination and DNA sequencing; (2) transcriptional regulation of genes, post-transcriptional processing and methods to quantify mRNA transcripts; (3) protein synthesis in prokaryotes and eukaryotes, control of protein synthesis, post-translational modifications, protein targeting and protein catabolism; and (4) proteins as enzymes, enzyme kinetics, enzymes as tools of discovery, proteolytic enzymes and antibodies as tools of discovery. The laboratories introduce students to modern technologies used in commercial and research laboratories, and include fundamental techniques, DNA hybridisation, polymerase chain reaction (PCR) and enzyme kinetics.

Unit Information

Understanding molecular mechanisms is fundamental to understanding how cell function can be manipulated (e.g. biotechnology) or how cells are dysfunctional (e.g. disease). This is an introductory unit that explores the molecular basis of life within the context of the cell. The content of the unit is divided into four major themes: (1) DNA and recombinant DNA technology; (2) the regulation of gene expression at the transcriptional and posttranscriptional levels; (3) the mechanism and regulation of protein synthesis (4) the catalysis of biological reactions by enzymes. Laboratory classes introduce students to the technologies and techniques required in modern biochemistry and molecular biology laboratories.
The theory course focuses on the molecular basis of life at the cellular level. At the completion of this course, students will:
1. Develop a broad knowledge of the fundamentals of Biochemistry and Molecular Biology.
2. Be able to understand the key principles in Biochemistry and Molecular Biology.
3. Be familiar with the chemistry, structures and functions of biological molecules in a cellular context.
4. Be familiar with the “language” of Biochemistry and Molecular Biology.
The laboratory course focuses upon Biochemical and Molecular Biological techniques that are currently used extensively in both research and commercial laboratories. At the completion of this course, students will:
1. Demonstrate in-depth knowledge about selected key techniques in biochemistry and molecular biology.
2. Be able to integrate and apply existing knowledge to solve complex scientific problems.
3. Apply appropriate laboratory skills to answer questions of relevance to the discipline of biochemistry and molecular biology.
4. Demonstrate effective critical thinking skills.
5. Demonstrate effective quantitative reasoning skills.
6. Effectively communicate scientific information in written and oral forms.
7. Engage collaboratively and independently in scientific processes.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

Lectures
2 per week on theory only. We strongly recommend that you attend all lectures in the unit and use the lecture capture system as a backup. Studies show that students who attend lectures regularly generally do better than students who do not attend lectures.
Laboratories
6 x 3 hr lab sessions, 4 x 1 hour post lab sessions.
BIOC2001 has four laboratories, with each laboratory running over three to four weeks. Pay attention to the dates. In some weeks, you will be completing the discussion for one laboratory while preparing for the next laboratory. Lab sessions are held in 1.42 of the Bayliss Building. Unit Structure

Theory
The theory component (lectures) is divided into 4 themes. At the completion of each theme, an online quiz will test your knowledge of the theme, with about 1 question per lecture. Each online quiz is worth 5% of your final mark. For each theme, there is also a practise quiz (formative assessment) to assist with your revision of the theme.
At the end of the semester, there will be an exam (40% of final mark) which will consist of multiple choice questions and short answer questions. A practise exam with the same structure as the theory exam will be released towards the end of semester. Check LMS for an announcement nearer exam time for revision material and specific exam information.
Laboratory Practicals
Laboratory Practicals and Discussion Sessions in alternate weeks. Note: An On-Line Safety Quiz must be completed in order to gain access to the first Pre-lab quiz.
Week A – Pre-laboratory (You do not attend the laboratory). Listen to the lecture covering the theory and information about the laboratory (on the laboratory LMS site). Test yourself with the practise pre-laboratory quiz online (formative assessment) and then complete the assessed pre-laboratory quiz online. Note that the quiz closes before the first practical session.
Week B – Laboratory practical (You must attend the laboratory – 3 hours). You will need to download the experimental instructions from LMS. Complete the practical component and fill in the relevant results sheet. 
Week C – Post-laboratory discussion and quiz session (You must attend room 2.15 Bayliss Building – 1 hour of the 3 hour time slot allocated to laboratories). During this session, you will also complete a quiz on the laboratory.
Each laboratory is worth 9% of your final mark. Each student does 1 presentation during semester and this is worth 4% of your final mark.
See the document titled: “Unit guide - Information about laboratories” on the LMS website for further details.

Other

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
