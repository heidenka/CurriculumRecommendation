Academic Information

Content In this specialist unit, taught in French and designed specifically for ex-beginners, students are introduced to some of the most important writers and thinkers in French and francophone literature in the twentieth and twenty-first centuries. The weekly lectures focus on providing the cultural and historical context to the authors studied and the tutorials focus on the close analysis of a specific literary extract. The classes allow students to extend their knowledge of famous literary figures, practice pronunciation and begin to discuss literature in French. They also focus on extending writing skills in French, teaching the necessary vocabulary and allowing students to build their written repertoire from short paragraphs to more extended compositions. This unit is designed to help students towards achieving Level A2 of the Common European Framework of Reference for Languages (CEFR). Students are equipped with the necessary language and research skills to be able to study more advanced Level 2 and Level 3 specialist French units.

Successful language learning can only be achieved by regular practice of the language in all its forms. For this reason, it is highly recommended that students complete all the activities for each class uploaded to LMS.  It will help improve your command of the French language.

Unit Structure

ALL CLASSES START IN WEEK 1
1 x 45-minute recorded lecture & 1 x 90-minute tutorial per week for 10 weeks.
The weekly lecture will focus on providing the cultural and historical context to the texts we study each week as well as information on the relevant author.  The tutorial will focus on specific extracts from the set texts as listed in the programme and will allow you to extend your knowledge of these three important writers of contemporary francophone literature, practice pronunciation and discuss literature in French.  It will also focus specifically on extending your writing skills in French.
Learning outcomes:
Students are able to (1) achieve aural comprehension and oral expression skills in the French language at an intermediate level through the use of French in class and as a result of structured in-class activities; (2) achieve reading comprehension skills at an intermediate level by studying the passages in the set text; (3) achieve written competency at an intermediate level through regular exercises in the composition of standard grammatical structures; (4) attain an increased understanding of French and francophone cultures by reading and analysing texts in their cultural and historical context; (5) attain an enhanced mastery of English and an increased understanding of how language works in general; (6) achieve highly developed interpersonal skills including the ability to work effectively in pairs and small groups; and (7) develop formal and independent learning skills, resulting from sustained effort and regular practice in class and at home with the textbook.

Referencing Requirements

In addition to the general remarks about academic conduct below, please note that it is not permissible to seek help from a private tutor or native-speaker for proof-reading, proof-editing or correction of work submitted for assessment and grading. This would be considered collusion and could attract severe penalties. When preparing for your assessments, you may use online or other translation tools to help you with specific words or phrases, but not to translate big chunks of text. There will be no value to you if you write your composition in English and then translate it into French. You will be judged on written French that is appropriate to your level and not at the level of an advanced speaker. Bonnie is always happy to look at drafts and you can email them at any time for formative feedback.

Other

Details of assessments:
•	Composition 1 (week 4 tutorial time, 45 mins, 20 marks): your first test, a short composition in French, will be held in the first 45 minutes of our tutorial in week 4. You will need to write 1-2 paragraphs on one of the 3 extracts we have studied in class so far (Apollinaire, Césaire or Camus) in which you say what the text is about, its main themes, important philosophical ideas related to the extract and what you think of it. You can use your dictionaries but not your textbook and I will provide copies of the 3 extracts. You can choose which one you write on. You will be marked half on the correctness of your grammar and half on your ideas. 
•	Composition 2 (week 7 tutorial time, 45 mins, 20 marks): The second composition will follow the same format as Composition 1 except that you will need to choose 1 extract out of Sartre, Beckett or de Beauvoir.
•	Final test (week 10 tutorial time, 90 mins, 40 marks): this test will comprise three parts.  In part one you will need to answer 10 true-false questions in French that refer to the different extracts/authors/intellectual movements we have studied in the course (10 marks);  in part two you will have a choice of answering specific questions on one of two extracts we have studied in class (15 marks);  in part three you will need to answer two general questions in which you will need to demonstrate your understanding of the course’s principal themes (15 marks).  All questions are to be answered in French. Dictionaries are permitted, but the textbook is not.
•	Tutorial participation (ongoing assessment + video submission Friday 5pm week 11, 20 marks): this mark includes an assessment of your attendance (5 marks); preparedness for class, participation in group discussions and general contribution to the class learning atmosphere (10 marks) and a short video uploaded to LMS in which you recite one of the extracts we have studied in class and speak about it for 1-2 minutes in French.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
