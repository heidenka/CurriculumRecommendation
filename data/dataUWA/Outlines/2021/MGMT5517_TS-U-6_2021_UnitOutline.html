Introduction
Welcome to MGMT5517: Leadership for Social Impact.
“You must be the change you want to see in the world.” M.K. Gandhi
Leadership for Social Impact is a core unit for the Graduate Certificate in Social Impact and an optional unit for the MBA program. This unit provides students with an introduction to the challenges and opportunities of leading organisations with the purpose of improving social outcomes. Students are able to explore the role of leadership in achieving social outcomes across private, public and non-profit contexts; examine emerging trends in how different forms of organisations are contributing to progress on social outcomes; understand and critically evaluate theories and concepts of leadership; and identify their own leadership values, strengths and weaknesses and gain insight into intrapersonal, interpersonal, group and organisational dynamics important for leadership effectiveness.
Unit content
This unit covers current challenges in achieving social benefit in organisations and programs you lead; innovative forms of organisation; the challenges and opportunities of leadership; models of leadership and adaptation, suggesting ways in which they might intervene more effectively to create positive social outcomes; and diagnosis of leadership and governance challenges.
The unit will cover:
The context for leadership in the social economy
Leading collaboration
Leadership and corporate social responsibility
Leadership theories and models for the third sector
Leading social change and transformation
Leading self
Leading teams and organisations
Empowering and engaging communities for collective impact
Leading social programs and social innovation

Learning And Teaching Strategy

“Through learning we re-create ourselves. Through learning we become able to do something we never were able to do. Through learning we reperceive the world and our relationship to it. Through learning we extend our capacity to create, to be part of the generative process of life.” (Senge 2010). The teaching and learning philosophy underpinning this course is based on these principles.
An effective learning orientation requires self-directed double-loop learning (Argyris, 1982). To do this, the learner needs to move beyond single-loop learning based on their own reasoning and beliefs to a deeper, reflective analysis which challenges the underlying assumptions, beliefs and values inherent in organisational problems and allow curiosity and feedback to change their behaviour and alter goals and beliefs. David Kolb (1984) talks about learning as a cycle of four stages; this process is active and passive, concrete and abstract. Concrete experience (doing) is followed by observation and reflection (thinking and feeling). This leads to the formation of abstract concepts and generalisations (connecting) then to hypotheses to be tested in future action (deciding). Finally, this leads to new experiences. This learning cycle is continuously recurring. Our concepts in experience are continuously tested and modified as a result of our observation of the experience. This requires an open mind, open heart and open will (Scharmer 2018). The learning experiences and assessment in this unit are designed to facilitate such experiential and action learning.
This unit uses a variety of learning strategies including:
Reflective exercises
Self assessments
Case studies
Class discussion and debate
Group activities
Simulations
Lectures
Syndicate work
Independent study
All these approaches are designed to provide a stimulating learning environment that accommodates most learning styles. This approach to teaching relies heavily on the willingness of students to participate. unit facilitators will guide you through the course material through presentations and facilitating classroom discussions and activities. They will provide examples and evidence from research to link theory with practice. They will also create a safe learning environment, which encourages dialogue, participation and interaction among learners. Your responsibilities are to do the prereadings and complete any pre-work before coming to class, to participate in discussions, to respect all comments from others during class and to complete all assignments on time.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
