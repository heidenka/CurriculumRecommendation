Teaching contingency plan (In response to Covid19 restrictions)

Due to COVID-19 we have faced unprecedented changes to the way we deliver teaching. Currently, this unit is planned to be delivered face-to-face. If face-to-face teaching is not possible and all teaching must revert to online delivery, the following changes may need to be made to deliver the unit online. These should be read in conjunction with the unit outline published on the LMS. If you have any questions, please contact Dr Jonathan Fitzgerald, jonathan.fitzgerald@uwa.edu.au.

1. Will the unit objectives still be met unaltered?

-YES

2. Will the assessment activity be the same?

-YES, assessments will simply be moved online

-Practical and written exams will be administered via Teams instead of in-person, and written assignments will be submitted via email

-The 5% "Attendance/Participation" assessment item will be removed, and other assessment items proportionally increased

3. What activities will replace face-to-face activities?

-Content delivery will remain the same, but simply move from face-to-face to online

-Lectorials/seminars will be delivered live on Teams, recorded and uploaded to LMS

-Tutorials will be delivered live on Teams, but will not be recorded

-Attendance at Teams lectorials/seminars/tutorials will not be compulsory, but strongly encouraged

4. Actions the student needs to take in online mode:

-Students should note that it is in their best interest to attend all live sessions

-In the absence of compulsory attendance, students will need to take responsibility for their learning, ensuring that the lectures are watched each week, assignments are completed, and that they stay engaged with the unit in general

Academic Information

Content This unit is the second in a four-semester sequence that develops core theoretical, musical and perceptual skills. The unit comprises two mutually supportive components—(1) form and structure; and (2) aural. These components comprise an integrated whole in which strong interconnections are defined in both content and assessment. The broader aim of this four-semester program is to support the acquisition of musical fluency and the capacity for independent learning. This unit extends students' understanding of musical grammar, both on paper and in their creative imagination. It deals with larger units of musical structure. On completion of the four-semester Music Language program, the course aims to produce musically literate students who are able to apply the skills they have acquired in any professional situation. This unit is a complementary unit in the Music Studies major (degree-specific major) in the Bachelor of Arts degree. It is also open to students enrolled in other majors and degree pathways who fulfil the prerequisites and who have an interest in music.

Unit Information

This unit is the second in a four-semester sequence that develops core theoretical, musical and perceptual skills. The unit comprises two mutually supportive components—(1) harmony; and (2) aural. These components comprise an integrated whole which reflect skills essential for success as a professional musician; a passing mark must be achieved in both harmony and aural components to pass the unit. 
The broader aim of this four-semester program is to support the acquisition of musical fluency and the capacity for independent learning. This unit extends students' understanding of musical grammar, both on paper and in their creative imagination, and deals with larger units of musical structure. On completion of the four-semester Music Language program, the course aims to produce musically literate students who are able to apply the skills they have acquired in any professional situation.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

The Unit comprises two equal, complementary and integrated components: Harmony (50%) and Aural (50%). The assessment requirements within this unit are designed to ensure that students develop a satisfactory grasp of each component;  students must achieve a mark of at least 50% in both the Harmony and Aural components to pass the unit. A firm understanding of the language of music is essential, supporting all musical development in performance, composition and musicology.
The unit is delivered face-to-face through lectorial/seminar, workshop and tutorial formats, supported by weekly assignments and online materials. Teaching and learning strategies are practical and interactive, and may include listening analytically to audio and video examples; the study of notated examples; the writing of notated assignments both in class and between sessions; the use of instruments and the singing voice; the use of conducting gestures and clapping; and verbal explanations of musical concepts.

Unit Structure

UNIT COMPONENTS
COMPONENT A: HARMONY (50%)
Through a weekly lectorial/seminar and tutorial, this component builds on the theoretical concepts introduced in MUSC1321. Students will gain a deeper understanding of tonal function through the study of the phrase model, examine the logic and hierarchy of larger musical structures such as musical sentences and the period, become acquainted with chromaticism in the form of applied chords and tonicizations, and ultimately be better equipped to make convincing interpretative phrasing decisions in their own repertoire. 
Learning outcomes: 
Students will be able to (a) demonstrate a thorough understanding of tonal function through second level analysis, identifying the function of any harmony within a phrase; (b) demonstrate an understanding of the various kinds of periods, sentence structure, and their implications for interpretation and performance; (c) recognise and label any diatonic sequence; (d) recognise and label any applied chord; (e) demonstrate an understanding of tonicization and diatonic modulations; (f) identify and label any variant of binary form.

COMPONENT B: AURAL (50%)
Through twice weekly tutorials, this component builds on the skills developed in MUSC1321. Students will gain fluency in hearing and interpreting four to eight-bar melodies through developing dictation and sight singing skills. These melodies will reinforce concepts introduced in the harmony component by adhering to classical sentence and period phrase structures. Students will gain an increased sensitivity to rhythm and awareness of harmonic structure by: singing and dictating harmonic progressions; group rhythm work; and hearing, performing and improvising upon harmonic cycles in popular songs.
Learning outcomes: 
Students will be able to hear, dictate, interpret and perform: (a) eight-bar melodic structure in major and minor keys; (b) diatonic harmonic progressions in major and minor keys; and (c) cyclic diatonic harmonic progressions in popular songs.

Participation

Participation is an essential component of the teaching and learning of these fundamentally practical elements - both Harmony and Aural are taught in a manner that requires practical responses and musical interaction. Therefore physical presence in the classroom is essential, and students are assessed on the quality of their participation in class. Students will be expected to prepare between sessions in order to participate fully, effectively and expressively in preparation for assessment.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.
