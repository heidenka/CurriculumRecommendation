Academic Information

Content The unit examines key issues relating to international criminal law, enabling students to examine the historical and theoretical developments, sources, fundamental principles and objectives of international criminal law as well as the main characteristics of international crimes (such as genocide, crimes against humanity and war crimes). The unit has a research focus, developing the independent research skills of students who explore a variety of primary and secondary materials in the field of international criminal law.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

Lecture capture is not used as this this unit is taught intensively through workshops rather than lectures. 
The unit also has an important online component. It is therefore imperative that you familiarise yourself with the LMS site of this unit as early as possible. Our seminar sessions during the intensive period will be conducted on the assumption that you have read and prepared at least the essential readings prior to each class. The seminar sessions will include discussions of assigned materials and presentations by students. Pre-reading lists for each topic are available on LMS.
As mentioned, the seminar sessions will be held via Zoom. To limit the danger of collective Zoom-fatigue, and to make the most out of the synchronised component, I will pre-record introductory lectures for each topic. The online sessions will then run over four hours on the days of the intensive period. 
Preparatory quiz (pass/fail)
To ensure that you are equipped with the knowledge needed to engage in meaningful debates in class, you will complete a short preparatory quiz focused on the introductory materials covered on the first day of our face-to- face seminar sessions. The questions will be made available on LMS on 20 September 2021. You must post your answers by 27 September 2021. Once you have posted your answers, you will be able to see and comment on your colleagues' answers. While this online quiz is not assessable, its completion is compulsory and considered a unit pre-requisite.
Participation (15%)
In addition to attending the seminar sessions, you are expected to:
•	Read and prepare the prescribed readings prior to the seminar sessions;
•	Listen to the pre-recorded materials, including the presentations of your colleagues; 
•	Contribute clearly and constructively to seminar discussions;
•	Listen to and engage constructively with alternative points of views.
Moreover, posting comments on LMS, for instance on your colleagues’ presentations, is strongly encouraged but should not be considered a substitute for participation in class.
Mere attendance and participation in the group exercises, but without any other significant contribution to the class discussions, will be sufficient for the equivalent to a “pass” in this activity. Occasional to regular participation will earn you the equivalent to a “credit pass” or “distinction”. Finally, for a “high distinction”, you will have to participate not only regularly but also in an insightful and constructive manner.
Presentation (25%)
You will be working in pairs on your presentation. You can decide to work closely together, or to consider two different aspects. In any event, consulting with your co-presenter to decide what you will be focusing on is essential. 
You will record and post a presentation of altogether 20 minutes (so roughly 10 minutes per person) based on one or several additional readings. You will then facilitate the ensuing class discussion via Zoom. While you should plan to make a brief, general introduction to the topic, you should go beyond the readings that are required for everyone and focus on – and make your colleagues benefit from – what you have read in addition to the required readings. For instance, you can engage with the main arguments of a particular text, or compare the approaches of two different authors. You must upload your presentation by 29 September 2021. 
The list of topics and details of additional readings will be provided on LMS at the beginning of the pre-reading period. You will be able to sign up for a topic on LMS (sign up by 15 September 2021 at the latest).
Assessment of this activity will depend on the level of analysis and the quality of the presentation, including the way in which the materials are presented to the class. Better presentations (equivalent to a “credit pass”) will not only summarise a few substantive points of a particular reading but also stimulate and facilitate a constructive class discussion afterwards. Very good or excellent presentations (equivalent to a “distinction” or “high distinction”) will both synthesise and engage in a critical way with the materials through a very close reading of the particular text and/or by drawing on additional materials.
Research paper proposal / peer feedback (10%) and research paper (50%)
You will prepare a research proposal for your final research paper. The research topic must be related to one of the themes covered in the Unit but cannot correspond to the topic of your seminar presentation. The research proposal must cover your research question, main arguments, methodology and basic structure and preliminary bibliography of primary and secondary materials. The due date for the research paper proposal is 18 October 2021. As part of this exercise, you will also provide constructive comments on one of your colleagues’ proposals. The due date for this feedback is 25 October 2021. In your feedback, aspire to be not only critical but also respectful at all times. 
You will then write a paper of not more than 4,000 words on the topic outlined in your earlier research paper proposal. This paper will require independent research and analysis. Follow the Australian Guide to Legal Citation (fourth edition) and include a bibliography (not part of the word count). In accordance with the Law School's assessment policy, footnotes are not included in the word count provided they are used only to give citations or other brief information. If footnotes are used more extensively to provide commentary then these additional words must be part of the word count. The paper is due on 19 November 2021. 
Note that if you want to graduate in December, you will have to submit your paper a week earlier, i.e. by 12 November 2021 (and I will do my best to submit your final marks in time).

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
