Unit Information

This unit covers attention, executive functioning, principles and dissociations of human memory, information updating, skill acquisition, problem solving and reasoning, judgement and decision making, applied cognition including forensic analysis, eyewitness testimony, expertise and human error.
With the help of lectures, labs, and independent reading, students acquire an in-depth knowledge of cognitive psychology. Students explore the basic methodologies involved in cognition, they gather an appreciation of complex theoretical concepts, and they learn about the applications of cognitive psychology outside the laboratory. The applications include examples from forensic psychology (e.g. eyewitness testimony) and the psychology of human error.
Learning outcomes: Students will be able to:
(1) explain the key ideas and theoretical concepts in cognitive psychology covered in lectures and assigned readings;
(2) demonstrate an understanding of experimental design and research methodology in cognitive psychology;
(3) critically evaluate published psychological research papers in cognitive psychology;
(4) present written summaries of scientific research and draw conclusions from scientific data.

Unit Structure

Lectures
COVID restrictions allowing, there will be twelve 2-hr face-to-face lectures, Wednesdays 12-2pm in the Octagon Lecture Theatre. Lectures are identical for PSYC2215 and PSYC3315. Lecture slides will be made available on LMS in pdf format on the day of the lecture or the day before. Attendance at lectures is non-compulsory but strongly encouraged. Lecture recordings will be available via the Lecture Capture System (link on LMS). If we are forced to have online lectures, recordings will be made available via the LMS and a weekly online video chat session will be used to answer questions.

Labs
There will be five 2-hr labs, in semester weeks 3, 4, 5, 9, 10. There are different labs for PSYC2215 and PSYC3315. Lab attendance is not compulsory. However, it is highly recommended that lab classes are attended—this is where you will participate in many demonstrations, ask questions, and where you will receive all the information needed for the lab report.
Lab sign-up is via https://www.cas.uwa.edu.au/student. Please note that unit coordinators have no influence on class allocations. Information on how to permanently swap lab streams can be found on LMS. If you need to attend a different lab as a one-off, e-mail psyc3315-labs-sps@uwa.edu.au and provide information on your original lab and the lab you want to attend before the lab session to get an OK from the relevant demonstrators (do not just turn up to another lab please).  For students unable to attend classes on campus, an online lab stream will be available, irrespective of CAS class assignment. 
Please note that Prosh is scheduled for Wednesday, March 24 (semester week 5), COVID restrictions allowing. The lecture will run as usual, as it is in the afternoon; however, there will be no morning lab classes. If you are in a Wednesday AM lab, please use the Prosh Replacement Class Sign Up on LMS to move to a different lab in week 5.
Additionally, as part of the lab component, students are asked to participate in an online experiment by the end of week 3 (March 14). The experiment forms the basis for the lab report (see below for details).

Quizzes (15% of total; due each Tuesday, 11.45pm)
There will be weekly online quizzes relating to lecture content, accessible through LMS. Each will comprise two multiple-choice questions. Quizzes open right after each lecture (Wednesday, 3pm), and remain open until the following Tuesday, 11:45pm. You have only one attempt but unlimited time. Quizzes will be graded, and are worth 15% of the overall grade (1.25% each week).

Written Examination (50% of total)
There will be a two-hour written examination during the end-of-semester exam period. This is scheduled to be an invigilated exam conducted on campus only. If you are currently not in WA, please be aware that we cannot guarantee availability of invigilated online exams, and that no alternative assessment may be available to complete the unit.
The exam will comprise 20 multiple-choice questions (each worth 1.25% of the exam; total 25%), 4 short-answer questions (each worth 12.5% of the exam; total 50%), as well as an essay question (worth 25% of the exam; “essay question” basically means “long-answer question”: no formal essay structure or referencing is necessary). All topics covered by the examination will be touched on in the lectures. Additional reading (textbook; articles on LMS) is encouraged. There is no access to previous exam papers.

Assignment: Laboratory Report (35% of total; due Wednesday, March 31, 11.45pm)
A laboratory report @ max. 2,500 words. The topic of the lab report will be provided and discussed during the lab meetings. Some independent reading is expected for the report; you need to cite original sources (peer-reviewed journal articles and scholarly book chapters); the reading list (see Other information below) contains some articles to get you started. The lab report will be based on an online experiment (< 1 hr) that you are asked to participate in (also see Participation below).

General Notes on Assignments
Assignments are submitted online through Turnitin (link on LMS). You can revise and resubmit your assignment as many times as you wish before the submission deadline. By submitting the assignment, you are declaring that the work is your own and that it conforms with the School’s and the University’s principles of academic conduct. Turnitin will give you an ‘originality report’ on your assignment, which will let you avoid unintended plagiarism of other work. Initially, this originality report will not consider submissions of the same assignment (i.e., submissions by your classmates), but be aware this will be updated before marking. Please also see note below regarding plagiarism. Assignment coversheets are no longer required.
Written assignments must be submitted by the due dates as specified below. A penalty for late submission will apply to all assignments received after the deadline without an extension. Late submissions will be penalized by 5% of the available marks for each day the assignment is late (this includes weekends; there is a 7-day limit for late submissions). Work submitted on the due day but after the specified submission time will be recorded as one day late. If you already submitted a draft version before the deadline, you can no longer submit an updated version after the deadline, and the draft submitted before the deadline will be marked.
All word limits should not be thought of as benchmarks to aim for, but as absolute maxima. Good academic writing means including everything that is relevant while being as concise as possible. Text in excess of the word limit will not be marked.
Like all assignments in this School, your work must conform to APA style and formatting guidelines (7th edition of the Publication Manual of the American Psychological Association). Penalties may apply if you fail to write to APA standards.

Plagiarism
The University of Western Australia takes very seriously the matter of academic misconduct by students and has policies in place that define misconduct (including plagiarism) and the penalties that apply. The consequences for misconduct can be severe, including exclusion from the university.
All students are required to make themselves aware of the definitions and policies relating to academic misconduct, found at the websites below.
http://www.governance.uwa.edu.au/procedures/policies/policies-and-procedures?policy=UP07%2F21
http://www.student.uwa.edu.au/faculties/faculty-of-science/Psychological-Science-students/academic
Our personal advice on plagiarism:
• Never copy-and-paste anything.
• You need to use your own words; slight rephrasing (e.g., replacing "for example" with "for instance") is not sufficient.
• Your writing also needs to reflect your own thoughts: "Copying" a text by making the exact same points in the exact same order is not OK even if you change the words.
• Be aware that Turnitin will pick up plagiarism even if you plagiarize a classmate’s work that is submitted at the same time. It will not show up in your Turnitin report, but it will show up in ours.
• There is no magical percentage on Turnitin up to which it is “OK” to plagiarize. The number is just one indicator.

Participation

It is expected that students participate actively in the labs and lectures, and complete the experiment associated with the lab report (10% of the lab-report mark is awarded for participation in and completion of this experiment by the end of week 3, Sunday, March 14). You can participate in your own time with your own device (i.e., a laptop or desktop computer; we cannot guarantee it will work with tablets/phones); if required, a computer room can be booked in the School for the experiment. A link to the online experiment (as well as a link to book a room in the School if required) will be posted on LMS. Students who do not wish to participate in the experiment can opt out of the lab report and will be given an alternative assignment (a 3,000-word essay); students wishing to complete the alternative assignment need to notify the unit coordinators via e-mail by the end of week 2 (March 7). Students who do not wish to participate in the experiment can also forego the participation credit (this is explicitly not recommended, as participation helps with the lab report), or request an alternative lab session with a unit coordinator or senior demonstrator to run through a demonstration of the experiment (by the end of week 3; March 14).

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
