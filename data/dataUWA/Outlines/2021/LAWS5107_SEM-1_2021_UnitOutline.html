Academic Information

Content Evidence is about the adversarial trial which is our society's primary, ultimate mechanism for determining disputed facts for the purpose of assigning legal liability. The study of the rules of evidence is an examination of procedure and substance. The unit looks at a set of rules which determine the process of a trial and which embodies theories of knowledge and morality—How best do we arrive at a truth? How best do we balance truth and fairness? At a trial, two conflicting stories are told and after hearing the stories, a fact-finder (jury, judge or magistrate) must say 'yes' or 'no' to the story told by the prosecution/plaintiff. It matters, in determining that 'yes' or 'no', what information the fact-finder is allowed to hear, what use they are allowed to make of what they hear, and even how sure they need to be in order to say 'yes'. It is the rules of evidence which determine these things. Students learn the major rules of evidence governing a trial in Western Australia and make comparisons with rules from other jurisdictions. They extend skills of case analysis, legal reasoning and statutory interpretation by considering hypothetical scenarios—Is certain evidence admissible? If so, how can it be used and by whom? Is there enough evidence? Can it be countered? Students extend skills of legal reasoning and critical analysis by exploring the concept of a fair trial as it appears in specific principles and underpins the system of evidence rules as a whole.

Unit Information

All societies have a variety of structures and institutional resources for resolving disputes. For example, in Australia, Aboriginal societies have for thousands of years resolved disagreement through organised structures and principles of law, and continue to do so, alongside non-Aboriginal institutional structures. The law degree you are undertaking is primarily a study of Australian Western law, and this is the case in this course too.

Non-Aboriginal legal structures for resolving disputes range from relatively informal mechanisms such as Negotiation and Conferencing processes to relatively formal mechanisms such as Arbitration and the trial. The adversarial trial is non-Aboriginal Australia's most formal – its ultimate – dispute resolution mechanism. It provides a means of determining disputed facts in order to assign legal liability, which is then the basis for an exercise of State (societal) power. The Evidence course is about this adversarial trial. 

The study of the rules of evidence is an examination of both substance and procedure. The unit looks at a set of rules which determine the process of a trial and which embody theories of knowledge and morality. Key questions include 'how best do we arrive at truth?' and  'what is the relationship between truth and fairness?'. At a trial, two conflicting stories are told and after hearing the stories, a fact-finder (jury, judge or magistrate) must say 'yes' or 'no' to one of the stories – that of the prosecution/plaintiff. It matters, in determining that 'yes' or 'no', what information the fact-finder is allowed to hear, what use they are allowed to make of what they hear, and even how sure they need to be in order to say 'yes'. The meaning of stories can differ significantly depending on what information the fact-finder is allowed to hear or see. 

It is the rules of evidence which determine these things. In this course you learn the major rules of evidence governing a trial in Western Australia and make comparisons with rules from other jurisdictions. You extend skills of case analysis, legal reasoning and statutory interpretation by considering hypothetical scenarios. Is certain evidence admissible? If so, how can it be used and by whom? Is there enough evidence? Can it be countered? You extend skills of legal reasoning and critical analysis by exploring the concept of a fair trial as it appears in specific principles and underpins the system of evidence rules as a whole. 

The course will provide students with an understanding of the common law of Evidence and the main pieces of legislation dealing with the law of evidence. The main Act is the Evidence Act 1906 (WA). Reference will be made to the 'Uniform Evidence Acts', which have been enacted in most Australian jurisdictions. However, although it is likely this uniform model will be enacted in the near future, it is not currently the law in Western Australia. Therefore, emphasis in this course is on the common law, Evidence Act 1906 (WA) and various other Acts that together are the source of evidence law in this state. 

To date, in your other law subjects, you have generally looked at the elements of an offence or cause of action. In Criminal Law you considered whether the elements of an offence (usually created by Criminal Code (WA)) have been satisfied. The elements may seem to be satisfied on the facts in your problem but in Evidence we go one step further. We look at the types of Evidence that can be adduced at trial, for example, real evidence, documentary evidence and oral testimony. 

Now, just a word of warning. Evidence can be tricky. It is a large subject. There is a lot of information to take in. At times you might feel a little overwhelmed. It is one of those subjects that can be compared to a large jigsaw puzzle. All through the semester we will try to put the pieces together and it may not feel as though you are getting the full picture. However, suddenly (hopefully) at the end of the semester everything should fall into place. 

On the brighter side, Evidence is really very interesting, topical and, dare we say, an exciting subject. Students typically enjoy the unit a great deal, and find that it adds another dimension to their understanding of legal processes. We hope that this is true of your experience in this unit too.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

The teaching and learning strategies for this unit are based on active learning. Lectures will take place live on campus – students are encouraged to attend lectures in person if they are able to. Lectures will also be recorded and uploaded to the Lecture Capture System. Lectures will be supported by presentation slides and lecture outlines, which will be made available on the LMS. 

Much of the learning in this unit will take place in weekly tutorials, which start in week 1. You will be allocated to a tutorial by the Class Allocation System. You must attend tutorials only for your allocated stream, day, and time (you cannot switch between tutorials). Tutorials will include discussion and activities based on set readings and questions. It is essential that students prepare for the tutorials by pre-reading and addressing the set questions, so that class discussion is focused and engaging. All of the tutors in this unit aim to create an environment in which students are comfortable to extend, explore and test their knowledge and views. 

You will need to spend an average of 8-10 hours per week on this unit in order to meet the expectations and achieve the outcomes. This includes class time, reading and preparing for lectures and tutorials, reviewing unit materials, researching and writing the assignment, and studying for the final exam.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
