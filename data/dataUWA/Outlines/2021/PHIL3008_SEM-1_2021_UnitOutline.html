Academic Information

Content Just about any decision we make is made under conditions of uncertainty. This goes for common everyday decisions, such as whether to bring an umbrella or whether to cross the street, where we face uncertainty about rain and traffic accidents respectively. But it also goes for big life-changing decisions at the personal level, e.g., choosing a university degree or life partner, and at the policy level, e.g., choosing a policy to address climate change or income inequality. What should we believe in the face of uncertainty? And how can we take uncertainty into account when choosing what to do? This unit will introduce you to rational choice theory, which aims to answer such questions. The basic ingredients of most views in rational choice theory are probabilities, which are used to represent the decision-maker's beliefs about how likely different possibilities are, and utilities, which represent the decision-maker's preferences. We will spend the majority of this unit discussing and criticizing these central notions. More specifically, we will look at the interpretation of probability, expected utility theory as the dominant view in decision theory, the distinction between risk and uncertainty, paradoxes in decision theory, Dutch Book arguments, the recently popular epistemic utility theory, and game theory.

Unit Information

In this unit we study decision theory, which concerns individual decision-making under uncertainty and is fundamental to various other fields, including economics, public policy, and statistics. As noted in the content overview above, decision theory addresses all kinds of decisions ranging from the banal (such as whether to carry an umbrella or crack an egg) to the sophisticated (whether to purchase this stock, whether to implement this policy) and the existential (as in the choice of career or life partner).
Expected utility theory is the canonical view in decision theory. It holds that our beliefs can be represented by probabilities, our desires can be represented using utilities, and an act is rational just in case the expected utility of that act is maximal among the available options.
As this is a philosophy unit, we will spend most of our time studying, questioning, and criticizing this foundational view. More specifically, we will look at the interpretation of probability, the operationalization of utility, the distinction between risk and uncertainty, paradoxes of expected utility theory, Dutch Book arguments, and epistemic utility theory. The last two weeks of the unit will consist of a brief excursion into game theory.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

Our meetings are organized as seminars. Among other things, this means that there will be ample space for discussion. Discussion may happen both in response to specific discussion questions and impromptu based on questions or comments raised by students. The main goal of the seminars is to come to a shared understanding of the reading assignment for that meeting and/or a shared understanding of some mathematical tool commonly used in decision theory.
Since the goal is a shared understanding, attendance at and preparation for the seminars is essential. Students are expected to have done the required reading for a given week in advance of the first seminar (which is on Wednesday).
Because discussion is central to a successful seminar, most of these will not be recorded. Only the Thursday seminars, which will focus on the mathematical tools used in decision theory, will be recorded. Students are still welcome to raise questions and start discussions during recorded seminars, but are advised that these may be heard on the recording.

Unit Structure

We will meet on Wednesdays, Thursdays, and Fridays. These seminars are currently scheduled to take place from 12:00 to 13:00 in Arts 1.62 (also known as ALR 10), but double-check this with your timetable. There are two study breaks scheduled: one for the week of 7 April (this is a university-wide break) and one for the week of 28 April.
Seminars are usually led by the instructor, but not always: each pair of students will lead part of a seminar at some point in the semester. This involves giving a short presentation summarizing a reading assignment (or a key argument from a reading assignment). The mark for this presentation constitutes a quarter of the participation mark for the unit (i.e., 5% of the final mark). The remainder of the participation mark is determined by attendance, preparedness, and participation in discussions. If you are uncomfortable speaking up in class, please mention this to the instructor early on so we can discuss alternate ways to show active participation.
Formal methods, especially those relating to probability and statistics, are an important part of the decision theorist's toolkit. Thursday's seminars will tend to focus on these. There will be three marked problem sets testing students' understanding of these topics.
The final marked component of the unit is the essay. Detailed requirements are available on LMS. Please note that there are two deadlines: an outline of the essay is due on Monday 10 May and the complete essay is due on Monday 31 May.
In order to mitigate the effects of implicit biases and other sources of unfairness in marking, written work (problem sets and both versions of the essay) will be marked anonymously. To make this possible, it is vital that you put your student number but not your name on all written work.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
