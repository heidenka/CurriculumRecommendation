Academic Information

Content The aim of this unit is to gain a quantitative knowledge of hydrologic processes across a range of space and time scales. Topics covered include (1) physical laws governing the catchment water balance at large scale, catchment energy balance and conversion to mass balance, calculation of evapotranspiration and estimation from satellite data; (2) introduction to runoff processes—the flood hydrograph and flood frequency analysis, variable source area runoff concepts and calculation; (3) unsaturated zone hydrology—Richards equation, unsaturated flow and transport parameter identification, scale issues and preferential flow; (4) groundwater flow—general principles and estimation of recharge and discharge, groundwater-surface water interactions, identification and quantification of fluxes in groundwater-dependent ecosystems; and (5) introduction to ecohydrology and quantification of vegetative feedbacks in hydrologic processes.

Unit Information

This is an introductory course on hydrology, the study of water. 
Learning Goals:
1.   To be able to quantify water in different phases and environmental states.
2.  To use conservation of mass to derive governing equations for water flow and storage in different states.
3.  To base engineering design criteria on an understanding of the variability of rainfall properties and their translation into variable runoff properties.
4. To individual guided design projects that quantify, apply mass balance, and explore the variability of water resources and their consequences for society.
5.  To communicate design outcomes in an engaging and professional way.
Expectations and Pre-requisites:
Introductory Fluid Mechanics is a pre-requisite course.
Proficiency in Excel, Matlab or an equivalent mathematical programming tool (e.g. R, Python).  You can use Excel for much of the class, and assessments will not require that you use a coding platform.  However, some worksheet items will be difficult to complete with Excel.  These are not assessed but represent important explorations of ideas in the lectures.  You can work with friends in class or online to complete these exercises so that students who are not confident in using a coding platform can work with peers who are more comfortable doing so.
Many of the concepts in this course have close analogues to topics covered by a basic course in dynamics (e.g. displacement-velocity-acceleration relationships).  If you have not taken a course of this nature, you might want to consult with me (or take a dynamics course before taking hydrology).

Learning And Teaching Strategy

Mode and Expectations:
In 2021, this class is approximately 50% face to face students and 50% online students.
It will therefore run in a flipped mode.  
ALL lecture material has been pre-recorded and can be viewed on LMS under the "Class Materials" tab.  This is broken up by week.  There is a separate set of 1-5 pre-recorded modules for each week.  Also provided for each week are the powerpoint slides, a worksheet that is intended to assist you in taking notes and practicing the material from the week's lectures, and a summary of the key concepts and formulae.  Workshops each week will provide you with a chance to (i) ask questions, (ii) seek clarification on the key ideas, (iii) work on the questions on the worksheet individually or with your peers, (iv) obtain help on difficult worksheet questions.  Some of our workshop time will be taken up with specific Guided Design workshops which are intended to prepare you for 3 assessment items.  Two of our scheduled lecture/workshop timeslots will also be devoted to field trips to sites in the City of Perth, and others may also incorporate guest speakers to introduce you to water issues around Perth.  We are scheduling a separate set of workshops for offshore/remote students to avoid the complications of operating in hybrid mode.
You are expected to:
(i) View all lecture material prior to class that week
(ii) Review the worksheet, start to complete it, and bring to class each week the questions on the worksheet you are finding difficult (for discussion, work with peers and to seek help from the instructor).
(iii) Use the summary sheets to guide you in studying for the midterm and final exams.
To access this material for any given week, you will need to get 100% in a 5 question review quiz based on the material from the previous week.  These quizzes are available on LMS as the "Weekly Quizzes" under "assessments". You can take the quizzes as many times as you like (until you get 100%).  I will not see the results.  

Unit Structure

Classes:
There is one face to face class each week, 10-1pm on Thursdays in G17 Maths.
An online workshop will be scheduled (timetabling is taking place now).
Field trips are scheduled for April 12 and May 10th.  These trips will take us into the City of Perth, a short bus ride from UWA Campus.  You are expected to make your own way to a rendezvous point (confirmed closer to the date) and back.  We expect both field trips can be completed within the 3 hours scheduled for class.  We do not have an effective way to offer the field trip to remote students, and the field trip content will not be assessed.
Assessments:
You will have 3 kinds of assessment, and 5 assessments overall:
3 Guided Designs (30%)
These assessments will start with a workshop in which you will be guided to think about design criteria, equations/models, site specific issues and to develop a strategy to formulate a design to solve a particular water related problem. You will be provided with Excel based tools to assist you with implementing technical analysis.  You will then have 1-2 weeks and be required to write a professional, 1-2 page design report.  This report is individual, and you will be assessed on the design and your writing.  
1 Mid Semester Exam (15%)
This will be a multiple choice online exam with approximately 10 questions, taken alone and in your own time.  
You will have two opportunities to complete the exam.  Your grade will be  the higher of the 2 exams you submit.  You will know what your grade would be after the first opportunity, but you will not receive information about which questions you got right/wrong.  Some of the questions may change in detail (e.g. different numbers in quantitative questions) between the two attempts.   
1 Final Exam (55%)
This will be a conventional written examination which I am likely to administer as a "take home" over a 24 hour period, in order to accommodate online-only students.

Other

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
