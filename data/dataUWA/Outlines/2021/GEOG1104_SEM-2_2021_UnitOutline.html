Academic Information

Content This unit focuses on physical disasters including earthquakes, hurricanes, tsunamis, epidemics, and those related to climate change including fires, droughts and floods. It provides a comprehensive understanding of the environmental, physical and geological and social triggers of these events from an environmental science viewpoint and the social vulnerabilities and failures that, together, result in slowly unfolding disasters or rapid-onset catastrophic events. The unit uses a variety of domestic and international case studies to examine how, in each case, physical vulnerabilities, environmental hazards and risk management intersect to produce disasters that threaten lives, livelihoods and ecosystems across a range of biophysical and socioeconomic contexts and scales. The unit is delivered via a one-hour lecture each week which focuses on overarching themes and concepts that are further explored through a three-hour tutorial each week. The concepts include vulnerability, hazards and governance as understood through physical geography and human geography. Students apply these concepts through an investigation of a real-world disaster in tutorials to unravel the layers of complex interactions that result in disasters. Tutorials are designed with a strong emphasis on practical and group work to foster skills in multi-layered analytical thinking that encompasses patterns, processes and interactions which are fundamental to Level 1 learning goals.

Unit Information

This unit investigates the physical and social drivers of disasters including earthquakes, hurricanes, tsunamis, epidemics, and those related to climate change including fires, droughts and floods. It provides a comprehensive understanding of the social, environmental, physical, and geological triggers of these events through the lens of social vulnerabilities and governance failures that, together, result in slowly unfolding disasters or rapid-onset catastrophic events. The unit uses a variety of domestic and international case studies to examine how, in each case, multidimensional vulnerabilities, environmental hazards, and risk management intersect to produce disasters that threaten lives, livelihoods and ecosystems across a range of biophysical and socioeconomic contexts and scales.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

At the end of this unit, students will be able to: 
(1) distinguish between a variety of environmental and geological hazards, including their origin, pace, frequency and intensity [assessed by: online quizzes, individual essay]; 
(2) understand the environmental, physical and governance factors through which an environmental hazard becomes a disaster [assessed by: online quizzes, individual essay]; 
(3) appreciate the multiple layers that produce vulnerability in societies, including differences between low- and high-income countries and inherent disadvantages of particular populations [assessed by: online quizzes, individual essay]; 
(4) gain knowledge of and experience in applying core concepts and analytical skills in human and physical geography, environmental sciences and planning [assessed by: online quizzes, individual essay, group poster & presentation]; 
(5) debate and design strategies for better risk management to reduce harm from disasters [assessed by: Tutorial discussions, group poster & presentation].

Unit Structure

This semester, the unit is delivered face-to-face and online, via two 45min pre-recorded lectures each week that focus on overarching themes and concepts that are further explored through a two-hour interactive tutorial each week. The interactive face-to-face tutorials all happen in the Geography & Geology Building, 1st floor, room GGGL 1.23 (1st year Geography Lab).
The concepts include vulnerability, hazards, and governance as understood through physical geography and human geography. Students apply these concepts through an investigation of a real-world disasters in tutorials to unravel the layers of complex interactions that result in often catastrophic outcomes. Tutorials have a strong emphasis on practical and group work to foster skills in multi-layered analytical thinking that encompasses patterns, processes and interactions which are fundamental to Level 1 learning goals.

Group posters due Monday 18 October at noon via LMS; Presentations during tutorial sessions (worth 30%): 

In groups of five to six people, you are asked to assess two contrasting case studies of a single type of disaster in terms of Vulnerability; Environmental Hazards; and Risk Management (prevention/mitigation; the preparedness; the response and the recovery).

Each group needs to select one specific disaster and two case studies of this type of disaster to compare and contrast. These may not be examples you have already used in the course.

We ask you to produce a visually striking, analytical poster of the two selected disaster case studies covering all three themes (Vulnerability, Environmental Hazard and Risk Management) of the disaster under investigation. The poster must clearly convey the core drivers of the differential outcomes of the two case studies. It must also address any specific advice that you as a group, would want to give the Prime Ministers of these countries to mitigate the impacts of future disasters?

The poster is to be digitally created (Power Point) and virtually displayed for the final poster presentation during the tutorials. Each group will have 10 minutes to verbally present the poster and their main findings.

Participation

Each student  will sign up for one out of eight tutorial slots and keep this slot for the semester. Each tutorial will run over 2 hours as a face-to-face or live online Zoom activity. SUBMISSIONS
In accordance with UWA’s assessment policy, the standard late penalties apply for submission after the deadline. A penalty of 5% of the possible mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays), after which the assigned work is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due. For example, if you submit an assessment item late by three days, and it was scored a mark of 45 out of 50, you would receive an actual mark of 37.5 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day as a penalty). 
Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached. The same rule applies for an assessment under the required word range.

STUDENT COMPLAINTS PROTOCOL 
The Department of Geography and Planning operates a carefully designed protocol with respect to student complaints. These apply to the delivery of any unit with the prefix GEOG or PLNG. All matters are followed up, whether these are made in person, via email or in writing. Strict confidentiality and anonymity is applied at all stages. 
Student complaints relating to administration, teaching, supervision or the conduct of either staff or students should be directed to the relevant Head of Teaching and Learning within the Department. Student complaints relating to any other matter should be directed to the Responsible Officer as detailed at https://www.web.uwa.edu.au/university/complaints/students/complaint-policy-and-procedures
The Departmental Heads of Teaching and Learning are currently Dr Sarah Prout Quicke (sarah.proutquicke@uwa.edu.au) for undergraduate units and Dr Linda Robson (linda.robson@uwa.edu.au) for postgraduate units. In the event that the complaint involves either Head of Teaching and Learning, the complaint should be directed to the other Head. 
Any complaint from a student will be acknowledged in writing within 2 working days of receipt. 
Complaints received by the Head of Teaching and Learning will be followed up by an invitation to the student to meet with the relevant Head of Teaching and Learning, together with the Head of Department (currently Dr Julian Clifton). This discussion will be an opportunity to explore the background to the complaint and discuss it in more detail, not to determine the validity of the complaint. The student is welcome to bring along a friend or other supporting individual if s/he wishes. Staff may take notes during this meeting but these records will be strictly confidential and not divulged to anyone outside the meeting.
The Head of Department and the Head of Teaching and Learning will then hold a separate meeting with the member of staff identified within the complaint. This will explore the staff perspective on the complaint. The identity of the complainant will not be revealed, nor will any material be used which could identify the complainant. 
Both of the above meetings will be held within 10 working days of acknowledging the complaint.
Within five working days of the above, the Head of Department and the Head of Teaching and Learning will reach a decision on the complaint and communicate this via email to the student and member of staff involved. The student retains the right to appeal this decision. 
Further information on the official complaint procedure is available at https://www.web.uwa.edu.au/university/complaints/students/submit

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
