Academic Information

Content Global agriculture needs to feed an increasing world population while also facing climate variability, increased input costs and environmental sustainability issues. In this unit students learn how precision agriculture technology can be used in rainfed cropping systems to meet these challenges. This concept is based on observing, measuring and responding to variability in crop growth and yield in the field. The unit consists of lectures, practicals and a full-day field trip.

Unit Information

The objective of this unit is to give students an understanding of precision agriculture technology. The aim of precision agriculture is to measure and manage variability in the agricultural system (e.g. the crop field). The unit comprises a series of lectures on soil/crop biomass/yield measurement and mapping, remote and proximal sensing (EM, radiometric, NDVI etc.) and the concepts of site specific crop management and variable rate application of inputs to improve productivity and profitability. 
The learning outcomes are: Students are able to (1) articulate how precision agriculture can improve production and environmental outcomes; (2) demonstrate an understanding of the principals, applications and limitations of precision agriculture; (3) transform spatial data into useful maps; (4) synthesise spatial data and farm information into agronomic decisions.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

The unit consists of a series of online lectures as well as face-to-face computer workshops/lectures (where farm precision agriculture data will be analysed/mapped) a field day and a practical exercise. The field day will include a visit to a farm practicing precision agriculture, for discussions and interaction with the farmer and a precision agriculture consultant. The knowledge and skills learned will be assessed through a test and two assignments, involving the processing of real farm data.

Unit Structure

The unit consists of a series of online (pre-recorded) lectures, face-to-face computer workshops/lectures and a field day. 
There are seven days with face-to-face workshops/lectures, which run on Monday and Tuesday of 5, 6 July and 12, 13 July and then Monday to Wednesday on 19, 20 and 21 July. In addition to these seven days, there is a field day on Wednesday14 July.
The pre-recorded lectures present foundational information. They are on LMS and MUST be viewed before the associated face-to-face sessions. This is because the face-to-face sessions will build on the pre-recorded lectures and you will also be using the information to work on precision agriculture data for your assignments.

Software requirements
Three software programs are used (QGIS, VESPER and MZA). QGIS should be downloaded onto your own computer and Windows and Mac versions are available. Where possible VESPER and MZA should also be downloaded, although students will be able to access these programs via a university computer in Lab G017. 
You will need to install three software packages on your computer (QGIS (GIS/mapping software), VESPER  (Variogram Estimation and Spatial Prediction with ERror i.e. Kriging software) and MZA (Management Zone Analyst - for cluster analysis) before the face-to-face lectures/workshops. Details on how to do this will be posted on LMS announcements and emailed to you. There are a series on videos on LMS which explain how to use the basic functions in these programs for importing, analysing and visualising your data. 
It is important you install and test the programs well before the first face-to-face sessions and to let the unit coordinator know beforehand if you have problems with the installation -  as these programs will be used in the face-to-face workshops/lectures. The software will also be made available on the university computers in Agriculture Computer Lab G017, in case you have any issues installing them on your own computer. Note that most problems occur with Mac computers installing VESPER and MZA as there are no Mac versions. QGIS has both Windows and Mac versions, so should not present installation problems.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
