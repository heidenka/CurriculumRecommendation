Academic Information

Content This unit introduces students to place-based and participatory approaches and tools for development at the local and regional (sub-national) scales. The unit takes a 'hands-on' approach, and allows students to develop skills which are useful not only in development but also in many other types of work in the public policy, private and voluntary sectors. The central themes covered include techniques for place-based assessments, project design, stakeholder and power analysis, participatory engagement, and monitoring and evaluation. Specific techniques covered include participatory appraisals and the logical framework approach as well as a suite of other participatory methods.

Details of Assessment
I.	Reading & reflection log (25% of the marks for the unit, 250 pts):
Over the unit you will read 17 scholarly papers relevant to the learning objectives. In the class sessions, time will be dedicated to discussing and critiquing these papers in order to enhance your conceptual and analytical understanding of key issues in development practice. Prior to class, you need to have read the assigned papers and completed a ‘reading & reflection log’ for each of the 17 articles. You will not need a log for the first article (Pugalis & Bentley, 2014).
A log is simply a 1 page précis and critical digestion of each paper (11pt Times New Roman, single spaced). I am not interested (and you won’t benefit) from summaries or slightly modified abstracts. The logs are your reaction to the paper, based on careful reading and reflective and critical thinking. 
Each reading log should start with the full name of all authors and their affiliations (who are they?) – not counted toward the half page –  and then address the following four parts (all 4):
•	What is the core message/argument of the paper? What is/are the author(s) trying to say?
•	What are the strengths and limitations of the paper (i.e. is it well argued, or are there gaps or weaknesses)? What do you like, and what leaves you confused?
•	How does the article affect you? Does it trigger new insights, does it make you feel angry, depressed, does it inspire you, does it challenge you or your way of thinking to the core?
•	What are the key ideas or questions that you would like to discuss or clarify as part of a group discussion in class? This is particularly important for meaningful class debates! 
Reading & reflection logs need to be submitted to the Discussion Board (Forum) on LMS, no later than 6pm the day before class! A late submission penalty applies for logs submitted at the beginning of a class session. Late logs will not be accepted once discussed in class. 
Marking criteria:
I will review ALL reading logs before class and randomly select logs from THREE weeks to count toward your mark for this assessment. Hence, it is in your best interest to submit outstanding reading & reflection logs every single week! Remember, these logs help you stay on track with your readings and come prepared to class ready to have a meaningful discussion! You will need a close understanding of the readings for the critical essay!      
Points for each R&R log will be allocated as follows:
1.	Evidence of clear understanding of the arguments, theories, and methods of the paper, as presented by the author(s) (6 pts);
2.	Ability to raise critical questions for class discussions, understand strengths and limitations, spot ambiguities and/or novel ideas, and state emotions (6 pts);
3.	Quality of the summary: good grammar, eloquent writing style, perfect spelling (3 pts). 
II.	Participatory tool presentation (20% of the marks for the unit, 200 pts):
In this presentation, you (in pairs) will introduce one particular participatory tool or method to the rest of the class. You are asked to draw upon suggested readings (unit outline pp 5-6) and other material you consider helpful to explain the tool or technique. Provide a short overview of the tool, opportunities for usage, pitfalls, and potential criticism (~15min). Use another ~15 minutes to lead your colleagues through a hands-on, interactive learning activity to experience how this tool is used. Sign up for one tool/technique by 2 March. 
Come and see me before your presentation day for any potential questions and tips! 
Marking criteria:
1.	Evidence of sound understanding of the tool/technique presented (60 pts);
2.	Quality of presentation to the class (description, examples, issues to be aware of) (60 pts);
3.	Creative and easily understandable interactive exercise of how to use the tool (80 pts).

III.	 Critical essay (30% of the final marks for the unit, 300 pts):
Your task is to write a critical and reflexive paper, digesting the readings and discussions from class, potential confusions and uncertainties, criticisms and compliments, or any other reactions to the material. The objective is to deepen your understanding of place-based development, the tyranny critique of participatory development, and critical issues related to power, knowledge and partnerships. It is essential that you have done all assigned readings for weeks 8-12 of class and your reading logs. I expect you to incorporate at least 10 of these class readings. You also want to incorporate notes from class that you have taken during discussions. This essay is due 1 April at midnight. 
Length: between 1,800 and 2,000 words (no exceptions!)
Format: 12 point, Times New Roman font, 1.5 spaced, with 2cm margins on A4 paper
Structure and sections of the essay/critical review:
1.	Introduction to the role of place, space, and power in development projects;
2.	Pitfalls and abuse of uneven power positions;
3.	Opportunities and standards for fair participation and transformation;
4.	Recommendations for place-based development project design and implementation.
Marking criteria:
1.	Evidence of sound understanding of the readings and key points of debate (160pts)
2.	Quality of critical reflection of key arguments, not merely a summary (100pts);
3.	Quality of written expression ‌(style, grammar, spelling, format), incl. references (40pts). 
The essay rubric is available in the pdf version of the unit outline and will also be introduced in class.

Student Complaints Protocol
The Department of Geography and Planning operates a carefully designed protocol with respect to student complaints. These apply to the delivery of any unit with the prefix GEOG or PLNG. All matters are followed up, whether these are made in person, via email or in writing. Strict confidentiality and anonymity is applied at all stages. 
Student complaints relating to administration, teaching, supervision or the conduct of either staff or students should be directed to the relevant Head of Teaching and Learning within the Department. Student complaints relating to any other matter should be directed to the Responsible Officer as detailed at https://www.web.uwa.edu.au/university/complaints/students/complaint-policy-and-procedures
The Departmental Head of Teaching and Learning is currently Dr Linda Robson (linda.robson@uwa.edu.au) for postgraduate units. In the event that the complaint involves either Head of Teaching and Learning, the complaint should be directed to the other Head. 
Any complaint from a student will be acknowledged in writing within 2 working days of receipt. 
Complaints received by the Head of Teaching and Learning will be followed up by an invitation to the student to meet with the relevant Head of Teaching and Learning, together with the Head of Department (currently Dr Julian Clifton). This discussion will be an opportunity to explore the background to the complaint and discuss it in more detail, not to determine the validity of the complaint. The student is welcome to bring along a friend or other supporting individual if s/he wishes. Staff may take notes during this meeting but these records will be strictly confidential and not divulged to anyone outside the meeting.
The Head of Department and the Head of Teaching and Learning will then hold a separate meeting with the member of staff identified within the complaint. This will explore the staff perspective on the complaint. The identity of the complainant will not be revealed, nor will any material be used which could identify the complainant. 
Both of the above meetings will be held within 10 working days of acknowledging the complaint.
Within five working days of the above, the Head of Department and the Head of Teaching and Learning will reach a decision on the complaint and communicate this via email to the student and member of staff involved. The student retains the right to appeal this decision. 
Further information on the official complaint procedure is available at https://www.web.uwa.edu.au/university/complaints/students/submit

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.

Special Considerations

If your study has been adversely affected by illness or other significant circumstances outside of your control, you can apply for special consideration. Significant circumstances may include but are not limited to:

  • serious illness or death of a member of the student's immediate family or household or of a close friend
  • serious injury
  • being a victim of a crime
  • breakdown of relationship
  • sudden loss of income or employment
  • serious disruption to domestic arrangements

The full policy and information on applying for special consideration is available at http://www.student.uwa.edu.au/course/exams/consideration

Compulsory online modules

UWA has a wealth of resources available to support your learning. These online modules are compulsory for many students:

  • Academic Conduct Essentials (ACE) introduces you to the basic issues of ethical scholarship and the expectations of correct academic conduct at university. All students are required to complete ACE in the first semester of their course.
  • Communication and Research Skills (CARS) aims to assist you to develop communication and research skills in an academic context and is compulsory for all new coursework students.
  • Indigenous Study Studies Essentials (INDG1000) introduces you to the shared learning space that UWA embodies. This learning space includes both western and Indigenous knowledge systems. INDG1000 is compulsory for all students completing a first year undergraduate course.

More information about these units can be found on the Learning resources website
