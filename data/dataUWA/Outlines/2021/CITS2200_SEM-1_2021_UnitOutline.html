Academic Information

Content At the core of most computer applications is the storage and retrieval of information. The way that the stored data is structured has a strong impact on what can be retrieved, how quickly it can be retrieved and how much space it occupies. The use of generic structures, or abstract data types (ADTs), to encapsulate the data also facilitates software engineering principles of independent modification, extension and re-use. This unit studies the specification, implementation and time-and-space performance of a range of commonly used ADTs and corresponding algorithms in an object-oriented setting.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

This unit has face-to-face lectures, workshops and laboratory sessions. Students are strongly advised to attend two one-hour lectures, one workshop (starting second week) and one two-hour laboratory session every week. There may be restrictions on number of students attending lectures due to Covid-19, and students who can't attend should listen to recorded lectures and workshops. The recordings will be available within half an hour of each session. Students attending this unit completely online can listen to the lecture and workshop recordings immediately after the sessions. There will be a sufficient number of online labs for students attending the unit completely in online mode.There is also a one-hour consultancy session every week over zoom.

Unit Structure

Week1: Introduction to data structures, Introduction to algorithms (Lectures), no lab
Week2: Data abstraction, Queues (Lectures); sorting (Lab)
Week3: Lists, Complexity (Lectures); Stacks (Lab)
Week4: Asymptotic analysis, amortised analysis (Lectures); Queues (Lab)
Week5: Iterators, trees, tree representations (Lectures); Lists (Lab)
Week6: Trees and traversals, priority queues (Lectures); Binary tree (Lab)
Week7: Mid-semester test, Minimum spanning trees (Lectures); Tree search (Lab)
Week8: Shortest path algorithms (Lectures); Priority queues (Lab)
Week9: Maps, sets and tables (Lectures); Priority first search (Lab)
Week10: Search trees, hash maps (Lectures); Project (Lab)
Week11: Dynamic Programming (Lectures); Project (Lab)
Week12: Revision (Lectures); Project(Lab)

Late submission of Assignments
A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.
For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).
If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.
If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;
Penalty for exceeding word limit
Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
