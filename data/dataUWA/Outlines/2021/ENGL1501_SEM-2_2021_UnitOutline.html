Teaching contingency plan (In response to Covid19 restrictions)

Should we be required to go into full or partial lockdown this semester, it’s important that we have a clear plan for how to proceed with our unit. Because of the venue capacities of our classrooms – including the lecture theatre – any form of lockdown will require us to shift to online modes of delivery, specifically Blackboard Collaborate. Lectures will be delivered live via Collaborate, and will also be recorded should you need to re-watch them, or tune in at a later date due to a timetable clash. Tutorials will shift to Collaborate too, with Discussion Board – accessible via your LMS group – serving as a back up to assess your participation if you are unable to make it to class for reasons relating to the pandemic. It will be the decision of each tutorial group as to whether or not their tutorials are recorded.

It’s clear that these aren’t usual times, and that we may need to be flexible at various points during the semester. Please know that, as a teaching team, we are committed to this course, and to maintaining the necessary closeness and collegiality of our classrooms. Though this will, of course, require your own good will and commitment! If you have any worries or queries, or would just like clarification on any of the above, please don’t hesitate to get in touch with Daniel, the Unit Coordinator (daniel.juckes@uwa.edu.au). There are some instructions for how to access Collaborate in the ‘“How To” Resources for LMS’ section of the LMS page, but, should we need to make any changes to how course content is delivered, we will do our utmost to communicate the best ways for you to keep learning and working together.  

Academic Information

Content This unit engages with the joy and power of the written word. Investigating several aspects of writing practice, it aims to develop craft and narrative technique. Students work throughout the semester to plan and then write one or more pieces of creative work (fiction, poetry or drama/script). They have their writing subjected to workshop critique led by a tutor who is a published creative writer. The use of a range of techniques and strategies is explored through the discussion in lectures and tutorials of a selection of creative texts, as well as in reference to the students' own writing. Students are encouraged to read widely as a further important tool to develop their creative writing skills. Please note that this unit does not deal with journalism or non-fiction.

Unit Information

This unit engages with the joy and power of the written word. Investigating several aspects of writing practice, it aims to develop craft and narrative technique. Students work throughout the semester to plan and then write one or more pieces of creative work (fiction, creative non-fiction, poetry or script). The use of a range of creative techniques and strategies is explored through the discussion in lectures and tutorials of a selection of creative texts, as well as in reference to the students' own writing through a series of creative exercises across the course of the unit. Students are encouraged to read widely as a further important tool to develop their creative writing skills. Please note that this unit does not deal with journalism or standard non-fiction.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

ENGL1501 is about reading creatively and writing creatively: that is, fully engaging with literature both as a reader and as a practitioner. The course aims to establish a sense of the pleasure and significance of reading, for the writer, and utilise this knowledge in developing new writing. It is about the joy and power of the written word, and develops students' skills in writing as a form of expression. 
The unit combines both online and face-to-face teaching and learning strategies, to suit each student according to their needs at this time. The unit provides a set of readings to expand students' consciousness of creative approaches, which will be introduced through lectures. Lectures will also see us engage in interactive discussion of a series of writing exercises to develop on the theory approached in reading. These lectures will be delivered live, and also recorded for students who aren't able to participate (for whatever reason) via that means. 
Small-group learning in tutorials (with some run online and some in person) will involve more free-flowing discussion of the unit materials and more flexible exercises to hone craft. All students will actively produce writing both on the LMS and in class across the unit, allowing independent development. We expect you as a student to engage with both in-class and online learning environments, but will help ensure that students are allocated to the tutorials that suit best (online or in person) according to your specific learning needs and situation. 
Assessments are structured to build on course content, and to develop both learning and creative practice over the course of the semester. This requires students to develop new work in tandem with the ideas and practices discussed. Detailed information about each assessment, alongside rubrics, can be found on the LMS.

Participation

This unit includes participation as one of the assessment components (20%). You will be expected to participate actively in tutorials, regardless of whether you are enrolled in an online or face-to-face group. 
Your participation will be assessed according to the evidence of your engagement with the unit concepts and materials. You will be expected to meet set requirements for posting on the LMS, in completing exercises in your blog space. These will be explained on the LMS and in the first lecture. You will also be assessed by your tutor as to your active involvement in class conversations. 
Participation in the unit, whether it is listening to a lecture or getting involved in tutorial activities, is an important part of the learning process. It is therefore important that you attend classes. More formally, the University regulations state that ‘to complete a course or unit students shall attend prescribed classes, lectures, seminars and tutorials’. Where a student, due to exceptional circumstances, is unable to attend a scheduled class, they are required to obtain prior approval of the unit coordinator to be absent from that class, or present evidence for special consideration. Any student absent from class without having had such absence approved by the unit coordinator may be referred to the faculty for advice and may be required to withdraw from the unit. It is expected that students will miss no more than two tutorials over the course of the unit. Any more (without special consideration being approved) will result in an automatic fail being awarded for the participation component of assessment (20%).
Should any students enrolled online experience technical difficulties when participating online, they should contact the unit coordinator immediately, and an alternate solution will be found to support their enrolment. 
Participation also comes with the expectation that you will maintain appropriate behaviour in class, whether online or face-to-face. This unit is designed to be an inclusive and open space. Maintaining this is essential to the success of tutorial learning in particular, and violations of this will be treated very seriously. Any student acting in any way that excludes others or sits contrary to our expectations for polite, sensitive and inclusive behaviour will be asked to leave the unit.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
