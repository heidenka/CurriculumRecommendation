Academic Information

Content This unit examines contemporary issues in economic development, mainly in developing countries but with relevance to developed countries. The unit is problem- and policy-oriented. It covers thematic issues/areas of development from five capital perspectives: social, human, technical, natural and financial perspectives, and considers how the economic, social and institutional problems of under-development are interrelated and that require coordinated approaches to find their solution. The thematic areas covered include poverty and inequality, labour markets and migration, education, health, population, infrastructure, technology, markets, trade, microfinance, the environment, conflicts and corruption, and their relationship with under-development and economic development. In addition, a brief history of economic development is covered along with contemporary theories and analytical methods of economic development. Each theme is examined in an integrated and holistic approach to gain insights on success and failure of development policy/intervention. For example, why do poverty traps persist and what can be done to help people get out of poverty? Why does labour migrate and what are its consequences? Why do institutions matter for development? How do environmental protection policies affect local economic growth? What is the role of technology and infrastructure in development?

Unit Information

This unit examines contemporary issues in economic development, mainly in developing countries but with relevance to developed countries as well. The unit is problem and policy oriented. It covers thematic areas of (under) development from the perspectives of five capitals: social, human, technical, natural, and financial. It places emphasis on interrelated economic, social and institutional problems of underdevelopment, requiring coordinated approaches to explore solutions. The thematic areas covered in the unit include: poverty and inequality, labour markets and migration, education, health, technology, markets, trade, microfinance, the environment, conflicts and corruption. In addition, the relationships between these themes and economic development or underdevelopment are explored from contemporary theoretical and analytical perspectives, including techniques for impact evaluation. Each theme will be examined in an integrated way to gain insights on success and failure of development policies or interventions. For example, why do poverty traps persist and what can be done to help people for getting out of poverty? Why does labour migrate and what are its consequences? Why do institutions matter for development? How do environmental policies affect economic growth? What is the role of technology and infrastructure in economic development? 
The goal of this unit is to broaden students’ understanding of contemporary issues in economic development and equipped them with concepts and tools to critically examine such issues to find potential policy solutions. 
The objectives of this unit are to facilitate and encourage student to develop the ability and desire to:
1.	critically examine contemporary issues in economic development and their causes and consequences
2.	critically analyse economic development issues using models from economic, social, technical, human and natural capital perspectives
3.	critically evaluate development policy interventions (as case studies) to learn lessons for improving development policy-making
Learning outcomes
On completion of this unit students should be able to:
1.	discuss, explain and review current debates in economics of development such as role of different forms of capitals in economic development 
2.	develop critical thinking in suggesting solutions to development problems such as chronic poverty, poor health and education, environmental degradation, and institutional failure
3.	learn to apply basic economic tools and approaches to address underdevelopment issues such as poverty, vulnerability and inequality; and also to evaluate impacts of development interventions
4.	understand the causes, consequences, and patterns of development and disparities in development across regions and nations
5.	provide critical analysis of real world challenges in development and propose potential solutions to promote development through policy interventions and investment

Learning And Teaching Strategy

Lecture and workshop/lab sessions:
The unit is delivered on both online and face to face mode this semester (Semester 2, 2021). In general, the lecture materials will be recorded and posted in advance in LMS. 
For weekly workshop sessions, preparatory materials will be made available in advance through LMS. These sessions will be used for a range of activities, including discussion on lecture materials and thematic/assigned readings, problem solving, tutorial quizzes, and student presentation (see a session protocol posted in LMS for details)
It is expected that students come to the session with adequate preparation so that they can contribute to the discussion effectively. Students are expected to read the essential readings assigned for each session to develop conceptual understanding covered in the lecture and also to foster mutual learning through sharing of ideas during the discussion.
There will be some assigned readings or case studies each week. Check the detailed unit outline for specifics. In addition, each student will read a number of papers relevant to their assignment topic, which will be assigned once the assignment topic is decided during the first two weeks.
Students will play a dual role of presenter as well as discussant during the semester at least once. They will present an assigned paper to the class and also comment on someone's presentation as a discussant.
Assessment details
1. Quiz
There will be three online quizzes mostly composed of multiple choice and short answer questions. These quizzes are designed to test your conceptual understanding and the progress, and typically cover the contents of preceding weeks (for quiz 1 and 2). For example, quiz 1 covers the materials presented and discussed during the weeks 1 to 4, while the quiz 2 covers the contents of weeks 5 to 8. The last quiz is a summative quiz that covers the entire unit, but the emphasis (50%) will be given to the materials not covered in earlier quizzes. Some of the questions in this quiz may demand extended answers. 
2. In-class / problem solving activities (ICA)
In-class activity consists of three types of assessment items that students do during the workshop sessions or immediately after during such sessions. Session quiz and presentation will be done during the session itself, whereas problem solving part will be given as take-home assignments and student will submit their answers to problem solving questions through LMS by specified due date (which will be announced while releasing the problem solving questions). 
Your active participation and engagement in in-class activities are essential to secure the marks that are based on how well you performed the given task.
a)	Session quiz (SQ)
Each week, except the first and last one, there will be a short online quiz based on the assigned reading for that week. You will be asked to read ONE paper for this purpose. The quiz will have multiple choice questions and take only about 10 minutes.
b)	 Problem solving (PS)
Depending on the lecture content of the week, during the weekly sessions you will do at least one problem solving activity which would relate to conceptual idea covered in the lecture. Such problem can also be based on discussion section of the theme paper(s)/case study(ies) of the week or it can just be a set of clarifying questions about the topic discussed in the lecture. In some cases, you will be given an assignment consists of a set of questions and data to analyse and submit a brief report on your findings at a later date. There will be about three such problem sets. [Note: weekly theme paper(s) is the paper assigned by the instructor for you to read during a particular week].
c)	Class Presentation (CP)
Presentation in the class about specific paper assigned to you is another form of in-class activity. Once a topic for your final assignment is identified within the first 3 weeks, a presentation schedule will be developed. In the following weeks, one of you will give a presentation on the papers you have read as a part of your final assignment-related reading. Students will propose a set of two papers that are linked to their final assignment and the instructor(s) will choose one paper for presentation. [Note – these papers are specific to individual student and are different from weekly theme paper(s)/case study(ies), i.e. two of you may have different papers to read for the presentation purposes]. 
The presentation marks will be divided into two parts: presenter 70% (of your own presentation) and discussant 30% (critiquing someone’s presentation).
Presenter (7%): The marking of your presentation will be based on the following aspects. The preciseness and accuracy of responses to the raised questions also counts.
 How well you have covered the assigned task/topic (7 key points)? It includes:
1.	Identification of the issue or the problem
2.	The economic causes and consequences related to the issue/problem
3.	How the author(s) approached the issue (methodological aspects) and why they did so instead of using some other alternative approaches?
4.	What did the author(s) find?
5.	What are the key messages of the paper? 
6.	What are the policy relevance of the key findings? Discuss they could influence the policy.
7.	How well you are able to link the paper and its content to the 5-capital framework?
Discussant (3%): One of you will be assigned as a discussant for one of your colleagues’ presentation. As a discussant, you are required to critique and provide comments on the presentation. You will comment on the presentation as a curious intellect. Your comments should concisely summarize the major contributions of the paper, highlight strengths and weaknesses as well. As a discussant you will ask a few exploratory question (minimum 3 maximum 5) to the presenter based on the presentation to explain or justify methods/analysis used, and key results, recommendations, and policy implications mentioned in the paper. Such questions should generate further discussion in the class.
3. Final assignment (Term paper)
Assignment Details
This assignment is not an essay and must not be an essay. It is a different kind of exercise. It is meant to reflect the kind of analysis you would do in the real world for whomever would pay you for it. The problem you will analyse must reflect the content of this module and refer, in a given area or country, to the analysis of economic development and its challenges. 
The assignment topic will be selected in consultation between the student and the instructors within the first three weeks of the semester. During the first week, students will be asked to indicate and explore at least two (up to three) topics in which they would like to work on for the written assignment. Instructors will collect the topics of interest from each student and narrow it down to one for each student, considering student’s interest as well as weekly coverage of topics in the unit. 
From week three, sometime will be devoted to the final assignment during the workshop sessions. Students will be provided with a checklist comprised of various elements to cover in their assignment. This weekly work on final assignment is a standing-work for the entire semester but incrementally builds each week. There are four parts to the final assignment: idea note, outline, progress update, and final assignment (term paper).
a)	Idea Note
The idea note should be limited to one page only (A4 size, 12 point font, single space). It is a free writing, but should contain the issue or problem you are interested in, where you want to work on (geographic focus), and why this idea is worth exploring. No references are needed at this stage. Please upload your idea note in LMS via the Turnitin link provided by the due date and time.

b)	Term paper outline
Once the assignment topic is finalised, the first few weeks of work during the workshop session help you to develop assignment outline. c)	Progress update
This is where you show your progress made on the final assignment. It is an expanded outline with relevant texts, tables or figures. You need to write at least one page per main section of the term paper to show the progress. At least you will have these sections in your progress update: i. introduction, ii. literature review, iii. results, iv. discussion, and v. list of peer-reviewed references. The written prose should be logically linked and developed in structured paragraphs in each of the sections. No bullet points allowed. References will just be a list of peer reviewed literature you have cited in the text. Consistent formatting of the references following the suggested citation style is required. There is no limit on the number of references you can cite in the body of the text but this should show the progress you have made beyond what was indicated in the outline.

The final version of the term paper is where you present your detailed analysis drawing from earlier works and the use of template (Table 6). Assignment grading criteria
The grading/evaluation criteria for the outline and the final version of the term paper will follow what is asked for in the provided templates for contents (Tables 5 and 6, see the detailed unit outline posted in LMS). In addition, we will look at the quality of the work, specifically – originality of the work, relevance of the work to the content thought in the unit, synthesis of the arguments presented, information efficiency (presenting information in a clear, concise, and precise manner), and the structure and presentation of the written work. 
The idea note and progress update will also be evaluated based on whether you have fulfilled the requirements stated for these specific assignments (see specific part above). The quality of the work and information efficiency will be integral part in marking these assignments.

The unit material is divided into three modules: basic concepts and tools; the economy and three capitals, and financial and social capitals. First module aims to provide basic concepts and tools required to examine economic development critically and objectively. The modules 2 and 3 focuses on the economy and five forms of capitals that include technical, human, natural, financial and social. The weekly recorded lectures, sessions (labs/workshops) and assigned readings will help students to examine the issue of economic development or under-development from these five capital perspectives.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
