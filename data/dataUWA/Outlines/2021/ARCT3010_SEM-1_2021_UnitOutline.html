Academic Information

Content This unit accounts for historical developments and theory guiding design decision-making in architecture and the built environment. It highlights the evolution of conceptual frameworks that underscore many of the choices designers face today. It promotes ethical understanding whereby the study of decision-making and choices position the designer-architect as a creative agent.

Unit Information

The Unit comprises Lectures and discussion on the history and development of key philosophies underlying theories of architecture and the built environment with an emphasis on key theorists as well as the cultural and political contexts which were to influence their work. These will last two hours and are held each week.
Tutorials will be held regularly, each week, and enable a detailed examination of issues emerging from the lectures and readings. Students will be encouraged to form themselves into reading groups, each expected to meet regularly before the tutorials and to discuss not only required readings, but to bring additional material to bear on the subjects covered during the week. Each group and individual group member is responsible for commenting on the readings and for raising relevant questions to be discussed.

Lecture Capture System

In accordance with UWA’s policy on lecture capture all lectures* in enabled venues will be recorded and made available to stream and download by enrolled students via the Lecture Capture System (LCS) for the duration of the unit (including Supplementary and Deferred Examinations period).

* Please note that in some limited circumstances lectures may not be recorded due to:  ethical and/or security related issues; the use of commercially sensitive material; the use of third-party copyright teaching material; or culturally sensitive content. Students will be advised, in advance, when this applies.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

Teaching and learning strategies are strongly didactic, incorporating face-to-face encounters between expert staff and engaged students in lecture and tutorial arenas. Under normal circumstances lectures will be recorded and made available for viewing for a period following the lecture to encourage continued synthesis of material and its regular incorporation into tutorial programs and discussions. Recordings are not intended to replace ‘live’ lectures and accompanying group discussion. Other supports are available (key words and concepts, the exam format and lecture readings in many cases) should a student require additional guidance in the absence of recordings or if the lecture capture system and associated technology fails or its availability curtailed. The Unit coordinator, lecturing and tutoring staff accept no responsibility for either the continued availability or quality of recordings as these are matters beyond their control.

Participation

Attendance and active participation is required at all lectures and tutorials.  Compliance will be monitored and contribute to the assessment and feedback process. Lectures and tutorials are the primary means and occasions for consultation with your Unit Coordinator and tutors. Do not expect questions relating to content missed through unjustified absence to be answered. Additionally, it will be assumed that students have read all relevant course materials, particularly required readings, and have reviewed their notes and the recordings of lectures before attending the tutorial where they will be discussed.  Note: Alternative 'online' tutorial arrangements for international students obliged by the COVID-19 crisis to remain resident overseas will be made. 
Tutorial attendance and participation are especially vital to both effective teaching and learning.  Tutorials include presentations  based on work undertaken and presented by the reading groups as well as individual responses to presentations and discussions over the course of term.  The marking scheme for presentation is as follows:
Preparation for tutorials is facilitated by regular, weekly online quizzes and questionnaires that are to be completed individually within the specified period of time, before the associated tutorial.  These will be scored and a cumulative mark derived from the total number of quizzes and questionnaires completed by the end of term.  Each of the ten total number of quizzes is composed of ten randomly generated questions from a question bank.  Successfully answering them all results in 10 x 10 or 100points.  This maximum score equates to a High Distinction (HD) or 80% cumulative mark for this assessment component. The mark may be subject to variation based on a coefficient applied by the tutorial coordinator, given their view of the overall quality of attendance and participation of the student.
Coefficients that may be applied range from .80 (80%) to 1.20 (120%) meaning:
 X 1.00 (100%) Satisfactory material from quizzes and questionnaires, readings and lectures is often brought into tutorial presentations and discussions.  The student has attended, spoken and asked questions often and delivered satisfactory presentations.
X 1.10 (110%) Good material from quizzes and questionnaires, readings and lectures is routinely brought into tutorial presentation and discussions.  The student has attended, spoken and asked questions routinely and delivered good presentations.
X 1.20 (120%) Very Good material from quizzes and questionnaires, readings and lectures is always brought into tutorial presentation and discussions.  The student has attended, spoken and asked questions always and delivered very good presentations.
X .90 (90%) Marginal material from quizzes and questionnaires, readings and lectures is sometimes brought into tutorial presentations and discussions.  The student has attended, spoken and asked questions only sometimes and delivered marginal presentations.
X .80 (80%) Poor material from quizzes and questionnaires, readings and lectures is on rare occasion brought into tutorial presentations and discussions.  The student has seldom attended, spoken and asked questions and delivered poor presentations.
Example 1:  A student has earned a 'perfect' cumulative score of 80% for their online quizzes and questionnaires for the term.  Moreover, the student was also very good at bringing material from quizzes, readings and lectures into tutorials; they were excellent attendees, spoke and asked questions always and delivered very good presentations.  Their final tutorial presentation and participation mark would be 96% (80 x 1.20).
Example 2:  A student has earned a cumulative score of 80% for their online quizzes and questionnaires for the term.  However, the student performed poorly in bringing material from quizzes, readings and lectures into tutorials; they were infrequent attendees, spoke and asked questions only rarely if they did attend and generally, delivered poor presentations.  Their final tutorial presentation and participation mark would be 64% (80 x .80).    

Referencing Requirements

Academic Conduct Requirements All submitted work is to be presented in a suitable academic manner. Assignments will be submitted digitally via the LMS ARCT3010 site. Work is not to be submitted elsewhere, in hard copy or as data to the front office, to tutors or placed under the Unit Coordinator’s door. Students must keep a copy of all assignments submitted, in digital form, the file dated before the submission deadline and unaltered from that point in time.ReferencingEvery time you quote or use images, text or the ideas of another person in your assessment submission, you must provide a properly formatted reference to the source. For ARCT3010 the only acceptable format for referencing and notation is the ‘Chicago’ academic or citation style. Details of this style are widely available but are easily consulted via the library website and notes which have been prepared by library staff: http://guides.library.uwa.edu.au/Chicago_citation#ima.The most common type of academic misconduct arises from instances where material is incorporated into text verbatim, without quotation marks, and thus represented as a student’s own words. (This is commonly called plagiarism.) Even where foot- or end-notation referencing is provided identifying where the appropriated material comes from, this is nonetheless misconduct if text is reproduced verbatim. Take care and be sure to paraphrase (put into your own words) such material instead, as well as provide a properly formatted reference to the source. As well as plagiarism, contract cheating, collusion, falsifying data and representing work that has already been submitted to the University or elsewhere as an original response to an assignment, academic misconduct may include an additional activity or practice engaged in by a student that violates:"...explicit guidelines relating to the production of work for assessment, in a manner that compromises or defeats the purpose of that assessment. Any such activity undermines an ethos of ethical scholarship.” (From the University's Policy on Academic Conduct) Arguably, such activity or practice includes failure to cite sources at all (not only improperly citing them) even though this is clearly required, failure to cite page number(s) for a foot- or end-notation where required, and citing a range of pages (for example, pp 1-169) from a source when a more precise location is clearly required. (In some case it is appropriate to cite a range of pages, say, where you want to reference the content of an entire chapter or section of a source. Quotations, however, typically appear on one or, at most two pages and these should be indicated precisely.) Whether not such infractions are charged as academic misconduct they fail to satisfy unit guidelines and will be penalized by a lower mark or a failed mark depending on their severity.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
