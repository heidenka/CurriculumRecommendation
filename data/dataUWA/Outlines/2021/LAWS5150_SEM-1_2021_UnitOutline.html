Academic Information

Content This unit is designed to introduce students to the major intellectual property law regimes. Intellectual property is a dynamic area of law which governs most forms of creativity and plays an important role across an incredibly broad spectrum of industry, science, design, technology and culture. It impacts everyone in their personal and professional lives, irrespective of their interests or occupations. It is therefore an essential unit for those seeking to better understand how intellectual property is protected and exploited, and of course those who wish to gain specialist expertise in intellectual property law. The unit begins with an overview of all the major regimes and the major international frameworks that shape intellectual property laws nationally and internationally. The unit then briefly explores the essential theoretical foundations of intellectual property rights, and contrasts the laws relating to registered and unregistered trademarks (including the statutory prohibitions on misleading and deceptive conduct and the common law tort of passing off), patents, registered designs, copyright and moral rights. The unit takes an Australian focus but also engages with the international instruments which shape intellectual property law globally, and may undertake select comparisons of foreign intellectual property law. It focuses on the essential principles of intellectual property law, policy and practice, and also explores emerging issues in intellectual property law.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

This unit is taught by means of a weekly two hour lecture and weekly 45 minute tutorials, which commence in week 2 of the semester. Students are expected to participate actively in class and prepare for classes by undertaking the required reading in advance.
Outcomes
Students are able to 
1.	Explain the nature and purpose of the laws relating to registered and unregistered trademarks, patents, registered designs, copyright and moral rights. 
2.	Critically analyse complex legal, policy, ethical and reform issues in relation to intellectual property rights.  
3.	Apply legal reasoning to hypothetical fact scenarios and solve problems relating to intellectual property rights.
4.	Engage in critical analysis of case law and application of statutory interpretation in relation to intellectual property law. 
5.	Communicate orally and in writing  in ways that are effective, appropriate and persuasive for legal and non-legal audiences.

Unit Structure

Lectures
There will be 2 hours of lectures per week on Wednesdays 10am -12pm. Attendance at lectures is not compulsory but is highly recommended. All lectures will be recorded. Lectures that fail for technical reasons will not be re-recorded. Readings for each lecture are set out in the unit schedule below. In addition, lecturers may add additional readings, useful links or other items of interest to the relevant lecture folder in the “lecture topics” tab on LMS.
The first six weeks of lectures will be delivered by Jani McCutcheon and the remaining six weeks by Michael Blakeney. As a general rule please direct questions about the content covered in lectures to the lecturer responsible.
Tutorials
There are 11 tutorials for each tutorial group, commencing in week 2 of the semester. All tutorials are on Thursday. A number of questions relating to hypothetical fact scenarios will be discussed at each tutorial. You will find the tutorial questions at the “tutorials” tab on LMS. Note that there may also be additional or prescribed readings uploaded to each question folder.
Questions will be released on LMS  one week prior to each tutorial.  
All students are expected to participate in class discussion of the various tutorial topics. The quality of their contributions will be assessed by the tutors. Please see LMS for further details regarding tutorial participation.
Exam
All students must sit the Final Exam. The Final Exam will be held during the Exam Period at the end of the Semester at a date to be advised. It will be an open book exam of two hours duration. Students will be provided with further information on the format and broad content of the Final Exam during semester. 
Research paper
Students must write a research essay of 3,200 words (excluding footnotes) on a topic of the student’s choice relating to one or more issues raised in the unit. Students are required to undertake independent research to complete the essay. However, research papers must not relate to any material covered in any other university unit completed or being undertaken by students.
The final date for submission of the research essay is Friday 14 May at 11.59pm.

Participation

The Objectives of the Tutorial participation Mark are to Promote:
•	engagement, 
•	preparation, 
•	understanding,
•	reflection, 
•	responsible tutorial membership; and
•	quality of contribution to the tutorial.
Engagement will most obviously be in speaking but a student might also be engaged whilst listening. This will be important to note for students who are by nature quieter. They may be engaged, though speak less often.
Preparation might be evident in notes the student has come with or in the quality of answers and comments in discussion.
Understanding of the issues raised in tutorials will most commonly be demonstrated though a student’s overall contribution to the tutorial discussion, including small group discussions within a tutorial, whether by way of answering questions, asking questions, making observations, or listening and responding to other students. 
Reflection on the issues (and preparation) might be evident in the student drawing on material outside the material directly related to the tutorial problem, perhaps from other topics in the course and in questions asked during (or even after) the tutorial.
Responsible tutorial membership refers to the respect given to other student’s comments and refraining from attempting to dominate the discussion.
Quality refers to participation being  about the quality not necessarily the quantity of contributions (where ‘quality’ means considered and reflective contributions – you are allowed to make mistakes!). The aim of including this component is to encourage unselfconscious discussion amongst students, not an atmosphere of assessment.
In order to encourage and facilitate student engagement and participation, the use of electronic devices is generally not permitted in tutorials in this unit. Students requiring permission to use electronic devices in tutorials on equity grounds should see the unit coordinator.
The Assessment Criteria for Tutorial Participation:
HD (80% or above) - Student is well prepared for every tutorial; demonstrates good understanding of and insightful and/or original reflection on the issues raised in tutorials throughout the semester; critically engages with the subject matter of each tutorial; and consistently demonstrates responsible tutorial membership.
D (70% - 79%) - Student is well prepared for most tutorials; demonstrates good understanding and reflection on the issues raised in tutorials throughout most of the semester; is engaged with the subject matter of most tutorials; and consistently demonstrates responsible tutorial membership
C (60% - 69%) - Student is well prepared for most tutorials; demonstrates developing understanding of and sound reflection on the issues raised in tutorials throughout most of the semester; is engaged with the subject matter of most tutorials; and demonstrates responsible tutorial membership throughout most of the semester
P (50% - 59%) - Student is not well prepared for tutorials throughout most of the semester; demonstrates little understanding of reflection on the issues raised by the tutorials; is not engaged with the subject matter of most tutorials; and shows little responsible tutorial membership
F (below 50%) - Student is not well prepared for any tutorials; demonstrates little or no understanding of and reflection on the issues raised by the tutorials; is not engaged with the subject matter of any tutorials; and does not demonstrate responsible tutorial membership.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
