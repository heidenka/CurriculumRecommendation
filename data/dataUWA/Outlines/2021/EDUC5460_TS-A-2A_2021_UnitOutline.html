Unit Information

Welcome to the beginning of your career in the English teaching profession. This unit introduces the knowledge, skills and competencies required of a secondary school English teacher (years 7-10). It addresses the preparation of preservice teachers for the graduate level of the Australian Professional Standards for Teachers (APST). These standards will be a tool for your continuing career development. Throughout the unit, you will be expected to reflect on your attitudes and values, your previous experiences of studying English and to use these ideas to develop your personal philosophy for teaching English.
This unit is designed for preservice teachers who plan to teach secondary school English. It provides a theoretical framework for teaching, learning and assessment. Familiarity with the current curriculum documents is developed. Through active participation in workshops, preservice teachers acquire strategies for effective teaching and learning, gain skills for planning and programming, learn about instructional materials and resources, and develop an understanding of issues relevant to English teaching.
As part of this process, you will explore language development, the teaching of literacy and approaches to literature. We will examine teaching and learning strategies designed to promote language development, critical thinking and a range of literacy and literary competencies. You will be introduced to the Western Australian Curriculum strands of Language, Literature and Literacy.
Assessment in this unit is through assignments designed to develop the skills and competencies required for teaching English, but which are also intended to produce materials (programs, lesson plans and curriculum resources) for your practicum and first year of teaching. You will also have the opportunity to practice your English teaching.
This unit assumes that students have already developed certain basic skills, such as an adequate command of English and related communication skills. Students are expected to understand and follow the principles of accepted expression, style and good argument; understanding content, language and critique skills in at least some of the following areas: literature, film and television and linguistics; possess the skills and abilities to acquire new content knowledge in areas where this does not already exist; develop technology skills and research skills.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

This unit is based upon workshops, online participation and an active learning approach. In this unit, students will experience a range of learning strategies that are both designed to engage post graduate students and also to model strategies and activities for the English classroom. The types of strategies to be included are concepts based learning, collaborative learning, close and active reading, direct instruction, experiential learning, modelling, and visible thinking and cognitive approaches. 

Unit Structure

There are ten workshops of three hours duration each. The workshops are intended to be practical and to build your 'craft' as an English teacher. It is really important that you come to workshops prepared by doing the reading(s) and also any preparation activities. There will be a number of short recordings available and students will be able to participate in some components of the unit online. 
There are four modules:
Module One: Who is the English teacher and how do they teach? Identity, Curriculum and Designing Learning 
Module Two: Teaching and Learning Literature
Module Three: Teaching and Learning Language and Literacy 
Module Four: Assessment, Differentiation and the Equitable English Classroom

ASSESSMENT TASKS
ASSIGNMENT ONE – LESSON PLANNING 
You are required to develop 3 consecutive lesson plans for a year 7, 8, 9 or 10 English class, each of approximately one-hour in length. You will be provided with a preferred format. Your lesson plans must contain all of the following:
•	organisational details (Year level, day, time, room, topic etc.); •	title or lesson focus; •	links to the Western Australian curriculum; •	specific objectives that help to implement the curriculum; •	an introduction; •	a motivational activity/task; •	sequence of learning activities that engage students in active learning tasks; •	conclusion; •	timing; •	a list of resources prepared, selected and/or adapted resources; •	questions to stimulate reflection on the lesson.
The lesson plans must be accompanied by all of the resources that are necessary for the lesson, ready for photocopying or use in the classroom. You need to develop some of your own resources and to evaluate, select and evaluate a range of existing resources. The source of existing resources must be clearly identified. Please create at least two resources, which may each be one page in length and please submit a maximum of six pages of resources for the overall assessment. 
ASSIGNMENT TWO – MICROTEACHING
You will deliver 7-10 minutes of a lesson and may build upon the lessons plans prepared for the previous assessment. This means that you may create a lesson segment to take place before or after your segment of three.  Please create an original lesson segment plan. The lesson segment should focus on a concept from the middle school curriculum and may be a lesson aspect such as a general introduction, activity opening, introduction to group work or a significant concept. You will work in small groups and wherever possible your segment will be recorded for your use only. Please take advantage of the opportunity for formative feedback. 
Details: The purpose of this microteaching activity and assessment is to give you the opportunity for some hands on practical experience in English teaching before heading off on your first practicum.
Overview: You will deliver a 7-10 minute microteaching session in groups of 4-5.
Select an aspect of teaching middle school English. You may build upon an aspect of your lesson plans or you can create something entirely new. Work with a concept such as symbolism, persuasion or identity.
You need to provide a 30 second explanation of your microteaching session before beginning with details about: •	The year level; •	The topic; •	The ability level if relevant; •	The topics/activities that need to be covered
After you teach, your colleagues will provide constructive feedback on your micro-teaching by describing and evaluating what worked for them and ways in which the teaching could be improved. Micro-teaching provides a safe and valuable set of experiences for learning to teach. Watching others teach can provide a springboard for discussion and reflection. You must bring all materials you need, including copies of pictures or text. You can expect your learners to have paper and pencil, but you should be able to provide everything else you need for your lesson. Please submit your plan and resources on LMS.
                                          ASSIGNMENT THREE 
You are required to prepare a program of work based on the Western Australian Curriculum, for a year 7-10 class. The program should be for approximately 8 hours, or 2 weeks with 4 x 60 minute lessons per week. 
A program is a document that outlines the teaching and learning that is planned for a period of between 2 weeks to a whole term. It is based on some unifying concept – a collection of skills (e.g. Formal speaking skills), a topic (gender issues/youth culture/war), a concept (point of view) or text (short stories/genre study/a particular film or novel). It provides direction and a time frame to assist with the planning of daily lessons.
 Programs are written firstly for the teacher, but are important documents of accountability that may be presented to Heads of Learning Areas and other senior staff, the School Curriculum and Standards Authority, parents and students. Your program conveys your philosophy and approaches to teaching and learning, to students and assessment.
Your assignment should include: •	a description of the context for which the program has been constructed – the school context and the particular class – school type, SES, number of students, gender, ability level etc); •	a rationale to justify the choice of theme, topic, text etc (200-300 words – please use references), and how this choice relates to adolescent development (social, emotional, cognitive, physical); •	links to the Western Australian Curriculum; •	specific objectives for student achievement; •	learning activities in sufficient detail for the program to be enacted by another teacher. (Consider including key questions or issues where discussions are involved). Learning activities should incorporate literacy and numeracy strategies and evidence of the use of ICT to support and enhance learning experiences; •	a list of resources to be used by students and teacher – please submit a maximum of six pages; •	texts, including visual texts, (appropriate to age and syllabus requirements); •	assessment tasks, including the assessment criteria to be used; •	questions to focus future evaluative comments and reflections; and •	approximate timing – number of hours or number of periods.
PARTICIPATION ACTIVITY 
Group presentations and teaching in action
Curriculum sessions assist you to understand subject English, develop practical strategies for implementation in English classes and provide opportunities to develop teaching and learning centred resources. In a student centred environment, the best way to learn is to do.  Teaching in action will allow you to try implementing a learning strategy, suitable for use with secondary school students, while helping each other to process the readings and concepts in your own tertiary study. 
Working in groups of two or three, you will prepare a 10 minute activity linked to the weekly topic or readings. Please ensure that your activity is informed by research in some way – the motto of the course is ‘research led practice’. 
The purpose of this task is to: 	provide you with an experience in implementing a learning strategy; 	provide class members with the experience of observing and participating in a learning strategy; 	provide a summary sheet to help others in use of the strategy; 	assist with the processing of the concepts in the reading and to encourage a collaborative enterprise.
Ten minutes is not very long and you will inevitably over plan. A great learning experience can be short and so keep your ideas and actitvity(ies) very focussed. Participation is strongly encouraged. This provides you with a valuable learning experience. There is no formal assessment of your presentation and it is opportunity to develop your skills in a non-threatening environment.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.
