Academic Information

Content This undergraduate unit provides an overview of leadership principles and approaches relevant to the health sector, and with a particular focus on population health. Leadership in this unit is defined broadly, and includes: leading as individuals; leadership in groups and teams; leadership in health organisations; and concepts of shared and collective leadership around priority health issues facing society. Different ways of describing and fostering the characteristics of effective leadership are reviewed. A distinction is made between management and leadership, and factors contributing to effective leadership in different contexts and settings are explored. The important role of interpersonal and communication skills in health leadership is considered, and the unit provides opportunities for student self-reflection and development of skills relevant to leadership. The unit explores a range of contemporary issues that impact on health leadership in the twenty-first century, both nationally and internationally. An introduction is provided to several other substantive topics relevant to health leadership, such as ethical leadership and the importance of good governance, leading change, and the challenges for leadership in the face of a health crisis.

Unit Information

This unit provides an overview of leadership principles and approaches relevant to the health sector, and with a particular focus on population health.  There is a strong focus throughout the unit on critically thinking about how to apply leadership theory and skills taught to 'real world' health issues and contexts.  In line with this latter focus, students will have the opportunity to hear from and engage with our state's world-renowned health leaders across the semester.
Leadership is defined broadly in this unit, and includes:  leading as individuals (and the importance of self-insight); leadership in groups and teams; leadership in health organisations; and concepts of shared and collective leadership around priority health issues facing society.  Importantly for students, the unit emphasises that leadership is not contingent upon being at the top of an organisation, or one's job title, or being 'a boss:  we can demonstrated good leadership from the outset of our careers, and in a range of contexts, not only related to where we work.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

The learning approach in this unit is based on the premise of adult-education philosophy in which each student is an independent and self-motivated learner, with the role of the lecturer being one of facilitation and direction rather than the source of all knowledge.  Notwithstanding this, students are encouraged to consult the unit co-ordinator if they are having difficulty with any aspect of the unit.  Throughout the unit students are encouraged to develop their ability to work both independently and collaboratively.  This is a third year unit and it is expected that each student will take responsibility to engage in the unit as an independent and self-motivated learner.
Part of the learning and teaching philosophy of both lecturers in the unit (Prof Colleen Fisher and Prof David Preen) is to highlight the 'real world' relevance of the unit content to topical health and/or leadership issues.  This is incorporated into lecture and tutorial content, but is amplified through the weekly inclusion in the unit of some of the highest profile health leaders in WA.
The readings set for each week are an important complement to the lectures, guest presentations and weekly participatory activities, so please keep up to date with these.  There are a variety of materials included in the 'readings' for this unit and they are constantly reviewed and updated for currency and relevance.   
Should you experience any difficulty with the unit, please do not hesitate to discuss this with Colleen Fisher (unit co-ordinator) as soon as possible.  If you are having broader difficulties with your studies or a personal matter, remember that you can contact UWA Student Services, which provide a range of very useful support services for UWA students.  These include academic and personal support services, language, learning and study kills, and counselling.  Website:

Unit Structure

The unit is taught as a three hour 'seminar block' and readings.  All the content of the unit is examinable.
There are 12 seminars in total for the unit. Attendance at seminars is compulsory.  The seminars consist of three inter-related activities:  an interactive 'lecture', a guest presentation and relevant 'tutorial-style' activities.
The unit assessment comprises seminar participation and attendance (20%) including team-based research on a guest presenter and introducing the guest presenter at the start of their presentation, a major written assignment (40%) and a final examination (50%).

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
