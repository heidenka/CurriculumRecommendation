Academic Information

Content This unit examines the foreign policy directions of Middle Eastern and South Asian states in the contemporary international system. Students explore the context in which these states conduct their foreign relations: historical links between these states, the impact of colonisation and postcolonisation in shaping their identities, and the relationship between them and the changing dynamics of international politics are considered. The unit focuses on questions of state and human security, and explores how major and small states in these regions have responded to international pressures in the post-Cold War world. It explores the nature and limits of growing interdependence between South Asia and the Middle East in political, social, cultural and economic spheres with reference to select case studies. Students are given the opportunity to discuss the impact of the globalising world on the nature and limits of cooperation and competition between these states with reference to two sets of issues: (1) human security issues such as migrant workers, child trafficking, population control, women's rights and food security; and (2) developments in Palestine, Kashmir, Afghanistan and the Arab Spring. Students are encouraged to link contemporary developments in these regions with available literature, so that they appreciate the value of ongoing knowledge acquisition, and develop the ability to critically analyse available ideas and discourses.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

Teaching and learning in this unit is based on the premise that we learn best when we enjoy the subject, have the freedom to ask questions, conduct independent research, collaborate with others and ensure that we learn techniques of research which could be policy relevant. To this end, it employs face-to-face learning in the form of lectures and tutorials. For those unable to attend face-to-face classes on a regular basis are given the option of listening to recorded lectures on-line once they have been delivered, and on-line tutorials. 
The focus on student-centered learning means that the lecturer and the tutors assume the role of facilitators and guides for students who take on the responsibility of the following:
1. familiarize themselves with the requirements of the unit by thoroughly going through the unit outline
2. seek elaboration or clarifications at the start of the semester, or as the need arises
3. listen to the lectures each week PRIOR to the tutorial allocated for each student
4. do the required readings with notes for EACH Tutorial discussion PRIOR to the tutorial
5. participate in tutorial discussions ACTIVELY seeking more knowledge
6. write a weekly journal on what they have learned in the week, so that they can start building blocks for their knowledge
7. contact the lecturer/tutor during the allocated consultation times to seek elaboration of any points relevant to learning
8. Be open to challenge of learning about cultures and ways of thinking that may differ across state boundaries
The staff are responsible for ensuring that the learning materials, guidance and support is available in timely manner. They are, however, not expected to be available outside working hours and on weekends.
Employing this approach to learning, the unit consists of the following:
1. Face-to-face one hour lecture per week
 The lectures are designed to introduce students to the main themes of the unit, including linkages between developments in South Asia and the Middle East with developments at the global level. My approach is to use lectures as a space in which students can focus on what is being communicated, develop their listening skills, and be able to ask questions about a dimension they are unclear about. So I would allocate 5 minutes towards the end of each class to let students ask questions. Additional questions could be asked on Discussion Forum if you wish. 
I would also provide relevant readings at the end of each lecture for those interested in additional information.
2.Two hour long tutorials
The two hours long tutorials are designed to enable students to deepen their understanding of the concepts and issues covered in the unit within the broader context of the contemporary international system. 
To promote student-centered learning, each student is allocated a question from the tutorial topics identified in the unit program. The students are required to do the following with respect to their allocated topics:
a) prepare a 900-1,000 words written presentation (like a mini-essay, with full references and bibliography). The word count does not include references and bibliography. 
b) prepare a powerpoint presentation to use in the class to make a 8-10 minutes presentation to other students. 
c) Once all presentations for the day are given, students are expected to ask questions to the presenters, and share their own views on the issues being discussed. Each student who is NOT presenting on a given tutorial topic is expected to have read at least two recommended readings for the week to participate in the discussion.
Students are also guided to explore other related issues in smaller groups in each tutorial group.
So, if you think two hours is too long, rest assured you would wish there was more time!
3. Simulation Exercise
The unit includes a simulation exercise in which students are allocated roles as representatives of selected countries. They are expected to work in teams collaboratively to develop a position paper for their assigned country, and then use it as the basis for presenting their arguments and suggestions in an hypothetical summit meeting dealing with the selected issue.
This exercise is designed to give students the opportunity to learn about specific issues, conduct research in smaller groups, and learn the art of negotiation and deliberation in international meetings. Their ultimate aim is to find a resolution to the specified issue...or at least attempt to find one.
4. Self Reflections
Each student is expected to write a summary of what they have learned during the week and upload it each week as Journal Entry by 4pm Friday. Please note it is part of both formative and summative assessment. Your weekly entries will be used to determine your tutorial participation mark.
Students are required to reflect on knowledge and experience acquired in the unit at the end of the semester in the form of a 500 words self-reflection. 
The weekly entries  assist with this process and help your tutors to monitor your learning as the semester progresses.
5.Final examination
The final examination will consist of two sections: 
a) a set of Multiple Choice Questions, and
 b) an essay question that will assess students' learning and ability to integrate information acquired during the semester. 
Guidance about the examination questions will be provided in the last lecture of the unit.

Participation

Tutorial Participation:
Students are required to attend all tutorials and actively participate in the discussions following the Tutorial Presentations. Please note active participation is based on the understanding that you would be reading at least two of the recommended readings each week, demonstrate that knowledge willingly and also work actively with other members of the smaller in-class groups to answer questions identified during the course of the discussion. 
Participation in Simulation Exercise:
The Simulation exercise requires all students to work collaboratively in small groups and prepare Group Reports on the allocated country roles.Once the roles are allocated, it is the responsibility of the students to contact their partners, develop a plan to write the report, conduct research as agreed upon by a mutually agreed date, and prepare the final report with participation from all members of the group. 
Please note if there are issues in terms of getting your partners to work collaboratively, you must bring it to the notice of the tutor in the first three weeks after the roles are allocated. 
Each  'Country ' would be required to identify a Representative who would summarize their country’s position on the Palestinian issue and how it could be addressed globally and locally.
During the two Simulation Exercise Meetings, students are required to listen attentively, keep notes on the positions of other countries, confer with their own Country members in the tutorial on how to respond to the positions taken by other countries, conduct independent research when required, and develop strategies for collaboration with other countries.
The Exercise requires students to identify potential partner countries, work with them, and develop draft resolutions. They are also required to critically analyze other draft resolutions and strive to find some common grounds to reach a resolution.
Please note, it is ESSENTIAL that students do not miss the Simulation exercise (including the preparation of the Group Report and developing draft resolutions). Failure to do so without a valid reason (approved through Students Office Special Consideration) would mean losing ALL marks for the Simulation Exercise.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
