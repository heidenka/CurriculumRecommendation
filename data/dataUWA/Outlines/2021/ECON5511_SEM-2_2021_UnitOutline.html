Academic Information

Content Without energy or water, our way of life would collapse. Energy use and water availability are closely connected to climate change. How can we make effective decisions as a community about these three interconnected issues? This unit tackles this problem in three modules. Module 1 (energy and climate change) seeks to understand what drives society's demand for energy, how it is affected by energy markets and how energy policies affect greenhouse gas emissions. Module 2 (the economics of climate change) investigates the difficulties of evaluating the economic, environmental and social impacts of climate change and how this affects the effectiveness of climate change policies. Module 3 (water management and policy) seeks to understand what drives water demand and supply, how climate change affects this delicate balance and what policies can help societies adapt to the new climatic conditions. The unit focuses on the economic concepts and tools needed to explain and solve these problems, including supply issues and cost structures, pricing policies, property rights, externalities, cost-benefit analysis and risk management. The unit is a good preparation for those interested in working with electricity, renewable energy or water management agencies, relevant government departments, and industries that intensively use water or energy, such as mining, smelting, transport and power generation. Students will learn much of the content by studying in small groups a set of articles carefully selected according to their specific backgrounds, so that students in each group can complement their understanding using their respective strengths.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

In normal circumstances, this unit uses both face-to-face sessions and online delivery of recorded material, with active participation (both formal and informal) from students. Under Covid19 restrictions, this Unit will be held completely online, but run in much the same way as usual. 
The Unit will be run through BB Collaborate, which is integrated within LMS, and operates very similarly to Zoom.
An important aspect of this Unit is to allow and encourage students to learn from each other. One way this will be done is to get them to share research work then present their findings to the rest of the class, either individually or in small groups. This is especially useful as students come from various backgrounds with different levels of exposure to the topic or its context. 
Another aim will be to focus on getting students to relate what they learn, in class or through their readings and research, to specific case studies, if possible relevant to their country of origin. Students often find this most difficult to achieve, and tend to prefer just learning material and repeating it in exams. So this year, rather than assuming students already know how to apply concepts, theories or techniques to specific case studies, special emphasis and preparation will be devoted to this aspect. Students can take away with them this skill in their future profession.   

Unit Structure

This course Unit is structured in two modules, as described in "Unit Information", and involves one 3-hour session every week, run like a workshop. Each session will include some lecture material, student presentations on work previously prepared and Q&A exercises. 
Student attendance is required and not optional as students are expected to contribute to the learning process through interaction during lecture times and by participating in student presentation as presenters and feedback providers, formally and informally. Assessment tasks are structured to reflect this. Further, not all lectures or class presentations will be recorded and you need to attend each lecture to keep on top of things. 
The unit is team-taught by Dr Steve Schilizzi and Dr Atakelty Hailu. Please direct any queries about the unit to Steve. The modules will be highly interactive and designed to teach you to be critically reflective about your understandings of agriculture and economic development, as well as equip you with practical skills to be able to engage in economic development work and research related to agriculture. The unit is also designed to develop your oral and written communication skills. These are critical skills for anyone embarking on a career in the development world and will thus be integrated into the teaching and learning process of the unit.

Referencing Requirements

Students are not required to use a particular referencing style. However, the style used in the Australian Journal of Agricultural Economics (AJARE) is recommended. See here for AJARE referencing style: https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/page/journal/14678489/homepage/forauthors.html ; 
Alternatively, if space is at a premium, the Science journal style can be used, with numbers between square brackets in order of appearance in the text.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
