Academic Information

Content This unit equips students with knowledge and skills necessary to understand economic policies used in international agricultural trade and management of price risk using commodity derivatives. The unit is taught in two modules. The first module introduces students to international agricultural trade by focusing on why trade occurs, trade theories with applications to agriculture, and public policies and government programs affecting agricultural trade. The second introduce students to the purpose, operation, and use of commodity futures contracts and options. At the end of the unit, the students will have developed an appreciation and understanding of the economics principles that underpinning international trade policies, including (a) determinants of trade patterns between nations; (b) gains from international trade; (c) effects of international trade on an economy; (d) winners and losers from international trade policies; (e) main arguments for and against protectionism; and (f) process and implications of international economic integration. They will also understand (g) how to hedge price risk using futures and option contracts.

Unit Information

This unit provides an economic analysis of agricultural commodity markets with a focus on agricultural international trade and price risk management using futures and options contracts. The emphasis is on the application of economic principles and analytical tools to understand the welfare effects of different agricultural policy instruments and price risk management using commodity exchanges. This unit has two modules. Module 1 focuses on Agricultural International Trade and Policy and Module 2 on Commodity Market Derivatives.  Each module is taught in six weeks. 
Module 1 examines the links between producers and consumers and price determination in a competitive market and the role of governments in regulating markets. The focus is on both domestic and international policies to boost agricultural production and consumption. Topics covered include basic international trade theories; policy instruments in international trade; and the impact of both domestic policies and agricultural protectionism policies on agricultural markets and overall social welfare. Case studies are used to highlight agricultural policies in selected developed and developing countries. The module concludes with a review of the global food crisis and different policy instruments that were used by different countries to manage the situation.  
Module 2 provides students with a solid understanding of the purpose, mechanics, and pitfalls of using
g futures and options contracts to manage price risk of agricultural commodities. Emphasis is on price risk management for major commercial field crops (mainly wheat in Western Australia). Topics covered include hedging with futures and options, fundamental and technical analysis of agricultural commodities, and determination of optimal hedge. All students will participate in a simulated trading game. 
This unit is analytical in nature and extensively uses quantitative reasoning and analytical methods. T




herefore, some algebra and graphing skills are necessary. For example, students will answer questions that involve simple algebra and graphing and play simulation games to analyze hedging strategies in a futures exchange. No basic knowledge in economics is required although this would be an advantage.  
Learning Outcomes
On completion of this unit, a student should be able to:
1.	Demonstrate understanding of how agricultural markets function, including price determination and discovery and role of government interventions in markets. 
2.	Provide a critical analysis of the welfare effects of various domestic and multilateral trade policies.
3.	Demonstrate knowledge of the mechanics of using futures contracts and options by agricultural producers and agribusiness firms to manage commodity price risks. 
4.	Appreciate the use of economic concepts and principles in solving real problems in the agri-food marketing systems.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

The unit utilizes the Flipped Classroom approach. Pre-recorded lectures are available on the unit’s website in the learning management system. The lectures for each module will follow the scheduled timetable for the unit. Students will listen to the online lectures for each week and come for a face-to-face tutorial session where a problem-based approach is used to review key concepts from the lectures. The tutorial sessions will comprise various activities including group discussions, oral presentations, and computer simulation games as appropriate. Face-to-face lectures will also be arranged on a need basis. 
There is an LMS site available for this module at www.lms.uwa.edu.au. It is the student’s responsibility to check this site regularly for pre-recorded video lectures, required readings and assignment handouts. Any changes to the unit schedule or other announcements will be communicated via the LMS.

Plagiarism
All forms of cheating, plagiarism, and copying are condemned by the University as unacceptable behavior. The Faculty’s policy is to ensure that no student profits from such behavior. Generally, a failure will be recorded for the unit in which the cheating has occurred. Serious cases will be referred to the University’s Board of Discipline. Cases of copying are automatically reported to the Dean and documentary evidence along with associated correspondence is placed on the student’s permanent record.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
