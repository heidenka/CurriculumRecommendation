Teaching contingency plan (In response to Covid19 restrictions)

As described below, this unit is designed around three engagement activities: lectures, workshops, and the unit operations laboratory. These engagements are designed for flexible delivery depending on government restrictions, where Microsoft Teams will be the primary platform used to manage engagement at all times. 

Lectures are only delivered digitally, and as such are not affected by any government restriction. 

Workshops require in-person participation where possible and will carry a hybrid participation option for offshore students. In the event that government restrictions prevent us from gathering together for the weekly workshop session, it will be executed solely as an online activity through the same platform (Microsoft Teams). 

Laboratory sessions also require in-person participation where possible with a hybrid participation option for offshore students. Identical to workshops, Microsoft Teams will be used to facilitate laboratory sessions virtually, in the event that restrictions prevent us from gathering. 

Academic Information

Content This unit covers the introduction of the mass and heat transfer principles as it applies to typical unit operations. Topics include (1) heat exchangers; (2) distillation; (3) multi-effect evaporators; (4) liquid–liquid and gas–liquid extraction; (5) solid–liquid separation; (6) refrigeration; and (7) dehydration. The unit also includes applications of the unit operations in the process of liquefied natural gas (LNG) production, transport and re-gasification.

Unit Information

ENSC3019 (Unit Operations and Unit Processes) is a subdiscipline of how fundamental topics of Chemical Engineering -- including fluid mechanics, thermodynamics, and heat and mass transfer -- are applied to practical engineering applications. The unit is designed around six fortnightly learning modules, which are balanced across both self-guided learning and student-led activities: (i) applications of fluid mechanics; (ii) heat exchangers; (iii) absorption; (iv) distillation; (v) adsorption; and (vi) engineering design. 
These six modules will provide students with competence across eight learning outcomes: (1) write concise technical reports; (2) prepare documentation associated with unit operation design; (3) work in teams; (4) demonstrate information literacy through the location and evaluation of technical and related information as required for assignments; (5) determine the number (trays or units), size and design of unit operations (e.g. gas absorption, liquid–liquid, distillation, cooling tower, etc.); (6) analyse, evaluate and design unit operations with HYSYS and the use of thermodynamic fluid packages; (7) develop strategies for dealing with open-ended problems; and (8) develop abilities to deal with uncertainty in input data and empirical formulae.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

The six modules referenced above are designed around two types of learning modes: textbook chapters and lecture content will be available before the start of each module, allowing students to proceed through basic content at their own pace; both our weekly workshops and fortnightly laboratories require participation, either in-person for onshore students or in a hybrid mode through Microsoft Teams for offshore students. The purpose of these engagement activities is to provide a chance for student-led discussion and exploration of the topics, and to socialize concepts and questions with both peers and instructors. 
Self-paced content, including lecture content and introductory laboratory videos (prior to in-person/hybrid laboratory activities) will only be available through the Microsoft Stream: that is, Echo360 will not be deployed in Semester 2 of this unit. The purpose of this is to standardize the mode of self-paced learning, where complementary tools are available through the Microsoft Stream to assist students. Microsoft Teams is designed to provide a central organizing structure for all unit handouts, video content, and materials (except for the textbook, which is only available as a physical text at present): the platform will also be used as a primary means by which to facilitate discussion between students -- whether on- or offshore -- and instructors. 
That is, the Blackboard LMS will only be used for quizzes and the final examination (two of three assessment items, as referenced below), while laboratory and design reports will be submitted through Microsoft Teams' Assignments function. Similarly, Microsoft Teams will be used for all formal announcements: the Blackboard LMS "announcements" feature will not be used in this unit. The remaining assessment item (Laboratory Assignments) will be manually translated from Microsoft Teams Assignments to the "My Grades" portion of LMS. 

Unit Structure

Each of the six technical modules in this unit will be supported through:
(i) Textbook reading. Students are highly encouraged to purchase a copy of the textbook, which is only available (at present) in physical form. The textbook chapters corresponding to each module are identified below, with reminders put forward in both the Teams channel(s) and corresponding lectures. In general, the textbook should be used as a preliminary guide through the content, which is complementary to lecture content, in framing and explaining the foundational knowledge required to complete assessments items. 
(ii) Lectures. The 33 lectures associated with this unit will be available through the Microsoft Stream, in advance of the technical module to which they correspond, which will allow students to pursue foundational knowledge (i.e. textbook and lectures) at their own pace and in a time frame that is convenient for them. In general, lectures are designed to take between 30 and 45 minutes to watch, and will include brief, formative questions so viewers can check foundational knowledge as they progress through the content. 
(iii) Workshop Meetings. The primary mode of engagement in this unit will be through the use of weekly workshops, where in-person participation is required for onshore students where possible and remote/hybrid participation is available to offshore students through Microsoft Teams. In each workshop, instructors will provide a very brief summary of the week's primary take-home messages, and will propose an open-ended problem associated with the week's content: students will be asked to self-organize in teams (nominally of 4 students each) and work for 45-60 minutes to propose a solution to the problem, with support and consultation from instructors. Student teams will then present their solutions to instructors and peers for feedback. The purpose of workshops is to both solidify foundational content associated with the textbook reading and lectures, and to provide students with a chance to apply higher-order thinking around each technical topic. 
(iv) Laboratory Activities. With the exception of the fluid mechanics and engineering design modules (#1 and #6, respectively), each of the four technical modules will carry an associated laboratory or design activity. In the first week of the module, a laboratory prompt will be released to students through Microsoft Teams along with associated support content (e.g. demonstration and explanatory videos of the apparatus in operation). In the second week of each module, students will have the opportunity to visit the Unit Operations Laboratory in a one-hour period of their choice, during which they will be able to test and manipulate the apparatus associated with that week's content, prior to submitting their individual laboratory reports at the end of the second week. For offshore students, hybrid participation in one-hour laboratory sessions will be facilitated through Microsoft Teams.

Participation

Participation in laboratory activities -- either in-person or virtually through Microsoft Teams -- is required where possible for the four primary unit operation modules: heat exchangers, absorption, distillation, and adsorption. If you need to be absent from any laboratory activity, please contact the unit coordinator and School office immediately to organize a make-up laboratory visitation. 
Participation in weekly workshops is required where possible, either through in-person (for onshore) or through Microsoft Teams (for offshore) students. These workshops provide a critical opportunity to apply complex concepts in a low-risk, student-driven environment, prior to submitting individual laboratory assignments that involve similar skill applications.

Referencing Requirements

Any references used in assessment items -- including laboratory activities (i.e. reports), quizzes, and the final examination -- should be formatted consistently using a standard engineering format (IEEE is preferred, but not exclusive).

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
