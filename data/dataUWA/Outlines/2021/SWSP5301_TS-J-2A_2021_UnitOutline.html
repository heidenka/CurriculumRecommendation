Academic Information

Content Social Work Knowledge, theory and Values primary focus is to start introducing social work students to the various dimensions, processes and debates involved in social work practice. Issues such as how to integrate and synthesise; content knowledge, legislative requirements, professional ethics, personal values and the working relationship will be examined. Key objectives of the unit include: - That students have a beginning understanding of the role of professional associations and the Code of Ethics that will guide their practice. - Be familiar with, and able to engage in, informed debate about the value base and common assumptions that underpin the profession. - Have a beginning understanding of some of the complex debate about methods, means and models that surround the profession. - Be able to identify the complex, and sometimes apparently contradictory, value base of social work and other human services and to start to articulate this within the context of their own value base and ideology. - Be able to identify and articulate how the role of a social worker may fit within the broader framework of human service delivery.

Unit Information

This unit introduces a number of frameworks for conceptualising human services in general and social work in particular. It also seeks to help students to begin to think of themselves as future practitioners in the human services. Whilst its focus is on introducing social work students to professional practice, the theoretical material provided is relevant to most human service professionals.
The course starts with the premise that social work – in all of its guises – is a profession that is informed by complex ideas about the nature of people and society as they intersect with the personal constructs and values of those engaged in the activity of social working. In presenting this material, we acknowledge and track some of the vigorously debated theories for, and theories about, human services and social work. 
In order to maintain a focus on the practice of social work and to illustrate the ideas that are presented, practice examples and case study material will be used extensively in the lecture/seminars.
There are three distinct themes that are covered:
•	Frameworks of knowing (knowledge)
•	Frameworks for understanding 
•	Frameworks for doing (theory)
These themes are clearly about knowing, thinking and doing. They are interwoven rather than being addressed in module form.  The unit has been planned this way because to do otherwise would indicate a misleading simplicity. The integrated structure of the lecture and seminar series captures some of the complexity of human service work and its context. Theoretical debates on issues such as values and ethics, authority and power, advocacy and statutory practice will be woven throughout the lecture/seminar series.
Students will be expected to use their developing critical thinking skills to develop their own framework for conceptualising practice in the human services in general and social work in particular. 
Central to the construction of this unit are at least five premises about human services and social work:
•	They are complex and contested activities which employs a range of methods and practices.
•	Social workers and other human service workers work with a range of individuals, social groupings and communities – voluntary, non-voluntary and mandated.
•	No decision-making in human services is context, or value free.
•	Decision-making in social work necessitates people interacting around intensely emotional issues in difficult and highly politicised environments – to work in this environment requires that social workers have an appreciation of their own internal frameworks for understanding, knowing and acting.
•	Understanding the philosophical and historical elements in the environment helps workers to conceptualise practice in ways that give them more authority.
Learning Outcomes
On successful completion of this unit, students will be able to: 
1.	Critique the attitudes, ethics and values important to social work as determined by the Australian Association of Social Workers;	
2.	Demonstrate the capacity to critically reflect on social work theory and practice issues; 
3.	Analyse different issues, problems and dilemmas that confront social workers in the context of their daily practice;	
4.	Develop confident communication skills in both written and verbal formats in group and class settings.

The flipped model includes a change in use of out of class time, completing traditional in class activities out of class, pre and post class activities, use of technology, a change in use of classroom time, and activities which emphasise active learning, peer learning, and problem based learning.

Participation

Lectures and workshops are an essential part of the professional learning in the unit and attendance is compulsory. Masters level students are expected to be active learners, able to contribute to class discussions, share knowledge and experiences and demonstrate the application of knowledge to practice. Attendance and participation in the learning activities are therefore essential. If a student fails to maintain an attendance rate of 80% they shall be taken to have not satisfied the requirements of the unit unless absences are formally approved in writing by the Discipline Chair and/or the Head of School. Students unable to attend for unavoidable reasons (e.g. illness) must notify the Unit Coordinator prior to the class. Absences due to illness must be validated by a medical certificate.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
