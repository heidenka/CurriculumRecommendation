Academic Information

Content This unit aims to introduce students to the regime of foreign investment promotion and protection. There are over 3300 international investment treaties. These provide substantive standards of investment protection, as well investment dispute settlement mechanisms to resolve disputes between foreign investors and their host states. This unit addresses a number of basic questions: What is an investment? What is foreign direct investment? Who is a foreign investor? What is the justification for international investment agreements? Why do countries enter into or conclude international investment agreements? To address these questions, this unit will critically engage with the history and political economy of the international law on foreign direct investment. Students will explore investment protection under international investment agreements and international investment dispute settlement mechanisms, the impact of foreign investment law on such topics as human rights, corporate social responsibility, labour rights and environmental protection, and analyse how international investment agreements and arbitration stand to limit the authority of states to regulate for the public interests in these and other areas.

Unit Academic Information
1.	Content
This unit aims to introduce students to the international legal regime on foreign direct investment promotion and protection. The legal protection of private investments abroad has a long history. Customary International Law has provided legal protection for property and certain investments of foreign nationals for a long time, and continues to be a source of International Investment Law. Since 1959, when Pakistan and Germany concluded a treaty for the promotion and protection of investments, bilateral investment treaties (BITs) and, more broadly, international investment agreements (IIAs) have become the dominant international legal instruments on foreign direct investment promotion and protection. Globally, there are over 3000 IIAs today. They consist of BITs and treaties with investment provisions, including free trade agreements and the World Trade Organisation Agreement on Trade-Related Investment Measures. These IIAs provide for substantive standards of investment protection as well as mechanisms to resolve investment disputes between foreign investors and their host states, the bedrock of International Investment Law that will be covered in this unit.
Conceptually and theoretically, this unit will define concepts such as an investment, foreign direct investment and a foreign investor. It will also discuss the justifications or reasons for the signing of IIAs and study the history and political economy of International Investment Law. The unit will also discuss the substantive standards of investment protection under IIAs and explore international investment dispute settlement by investor-state arbitration. 
The subject of the effects of investment treaty law and arbitration on the right of states to regulate in the public interest will be considered when analyzing specific standards of investment protection and the arbitral decisions interpreting them. Through this coverage and engagement, the unit aims to equip students with a sound knowledge of the field of International Investment Law (both historical and substantive law knowledge) to critically analyse its role in foreign direct investment promotion and protection and evaluate the relationship between International Investment Law and the authority of states to regulate in the public interest.
 
2.	Unit Assessment Methods
Assessment in this unit will be both formative and summative and consisting of various elements which are aligned with the learning outcomes. They aim to assess all levels of thinking skills, including higher order thinking skills (problem solving and creativity) and capacity for independence and participation in teamwork. Accordingly, the unit will be assessed as follows:
a)	Submission of answers to tutorial questions online prior to tutorials and actual participation in tutorials, (20%). The goal of the prior submission of answers is to get students to actively engage with the materials in preparing ahead for tutorials so they can participate during tutorials. Feedback will be provided on selected submissions. Students will be required to revise their answers following tutorials and resubmit. Submission answers prior to tutorials and resubmission of revised answers after tutorials will amount to 10% (5% for each segment) and actual participation in discussions during tutorials will amount to 10%. An attendance sheet will be used to mark tutorial attendance. Tutorial participation guidelines are provided in the LMS under the Assessment and Submission tap.  
b)	Arbitral Case Analysis (ACA) or a written essay: Students will be put in a group of not more than 5 and assigned a decided arbitral case to review and critique the decision and award reached by a tribunal. They will argue either for or against the decision and award, (30%). 20% of the 30% will be awarded by the examiner. The remaining 10% will be awarded by the students based on peer evaluation of each student’s contribution to the discussion and the final written work. Each student will self-assess themselves and fellow students, and award marks out of two marks to themselves and to each student in the group. Thus in a group of 5 students each student will be entitled to 10 marks, 2 marks being awarded by each student. If students are less than 5 in a group, the mark each students will award themselves or fellow students out of the 10% will be determined by the examiner. Students will be entitled to know only the total mark fellow students awarded them through peer evaluation. This is to encourage student participation in group work and promote effective problem solving. Each student will obtain the same mark awarded for the final written work. Forms will be developed for the peer evaluation. In the alternative, students will be assessed on an individual written essay graded on the 30%. 
(c) Multiple-choice/Constructed questions: This will consist of multiple-choice questions (accompanied by answers for students to select the correct answer); short questions that require very brief answers and definition-type questions that require brief definitions or explanations of key concepts. 
d)	Two hours written examination at the end of lectures, (50%). The examination will consist of one written essay question (20 marks) and one question consisting of constructed response and multiple-choice-questions (30 marks).

Learning And Teaching Strategy

This unit will be taught through a combination of face-to-face lectures and tutorials. Lectures will be held on Monday Days: 13:00 - 15:00, in General Purpose Building 03 [GPB03], Ground Floor Room G01: Simmonds Lecture Theatre, beginning on Monday 26 July 2021. Students are encouraged to listen to the recorded lectures and to ask relevant questions for clarification during tutorials or consultations. Effective teaching and learning through lectures should lay a solid foundation for everything else in the unit including achieving the learning outcomes and students’ overall experience and enjoyment of the unit. Active participation in the lectures and tutorials is encouraged. Students should read materials prior to listening to the lectures and before tutorials so that they will be in a position to raise appropriate questions and effectively contribute during consultations or tutorials.
While the formal lectures will cover the introductory matters and core principles of International Investment Law, the tutorials will be problem-solving driven involving hypothetical legal questions and actual legal problem questions, such as investment disputes, that have been resolved in investor-state arbitration. Students will not be able to make meaningful participation in tutorial discussions unless they have listened to the lectures and reviewed the required materials covered for the tutorials prior to going for the tutorials. The tutorial requirements of submitting answers to tutorial questions prior to tutorials is meant to ensure that students engage with the materials before they go for tutorials. Submission of tutorial answers post-tutorials is to allow students to revise their answers to reflect new knowledge acquired during the tutorials. Therefore, students should be prepared ahead of lectures and tutorials as questions will be put to them to answer in the course of the lectures and tutorials. Through this back and forth process, students should actively engage with the learning materials in order to acquire relevant knowledge and skills. 
Lecture PowerPoints and all prescribed chapters of the textbook are accessible through Unit Readings on the Learning Management System (LMS). 
Students should utilise consultation hours to seek any clarification they need. 

Unit Structure

The unit consists of two hours of face-to-face lectures per week for twelve (12) weeks, and one tutorial per week for 11 weeks. Lectures will be recorded and can be accessed through the LMS using Lecture Recordings tap. Students must read the relevant portions of the required texts and any other specified materials before listening to the lectures. Tutorials will commence in Week Two (2) being the week beginning 02 August 2021. Students should attend all tutorials. 10% of the final mark for this unit will be assessed on actual tutorial participation in attendance and 10% for preparing and submitting tutorial answers before and after the tutorials. All tutorial answer submissions must be done at 11:59pm the night before tutorials and all revised tutorial answer submissions must be done not later than the third day after the tutorials, latest by 11:59pm. 
Lectures will cover the requisite areas of study and analyze the main legal principles of International Investment Law deriving from treaties. Reading materials PRIOR TO THE LECTURES will be extremely beneficial to getting the most out of lectures. Further, reviewing the lecture notes and rereading the materials covered in a lecture after the lecture and before the next lecture will assure effective engagement and learning of the materials. This will avoid situations where the materials become overwhelming. Listening to recorded lectures and active participation in tutorials are essential for an adequate understanding of the subject.

Late Submission of Assessments
A penalty of 5 per cent of the possible mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays), after which the assigned work is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assessment is due. For example, a student, who has submitted a piece of work for an assessment item late by three days and was scored a mark of 45 out of a possible mark of 50, receives after penalty a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day as a penalty for each student from the mark obtained). Where there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied holistically to the assessment item after all tasks have been completed.
Where assessments are graded ungraded Pass/Ungraded Fail, failure to submit the assessment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the unit's subsequent summative assessment item(s).
Assessments submitted later than 7 days after the deadline receive a mark of zero, unless an application for mitigation is approved in accordance with the University Policy on Assessment: Special Consideration
