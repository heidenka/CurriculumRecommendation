Academic Information

Content This course gives hands-on experience in understanding and obtaining the molecular structure via X-ray crystallography, the gold standard for determining structure in chemistry. Advanced concepts and techniques in chemical kinetics, molecular collision theory, and molecular dynamics simulations of bulk chemical properties are also presented. Concepts and theory will be supported by practical workshops, use of world-class modern equipment, software and visualisation tools. The unit emphasises core STEM skills sought after by employers and invaluable for second-year physical chemistry.

Unit Information

This unit covers (1) Crystal Engineering, (2) Reaction dynamics, and (3) Molecular Mechanics and Dynamics.

What are these topics all about?
(1) Crystal engineering (by Dr. Stephen Moggach) involves a basic introduction to the crystalline state of solid matter. We will cover unit cells and Miller indices, diffraction and Bragg's law, X-ray, neutron and electron diffraction. The production of X-rays using synchrotrons is important these days and such facilities will explained (Australia has an excellent one in Melbourne). Of course, crystals are very beautiful objects, so symmetry and spacegroups will be discussed: these concepts are necessary for quantifying the 3D repeating patterns that atoms and molecules make in a crystal. The most exciting part of this topic is that you will measure an X-ray diffraction pattern and "solve" your own crystal structure - crystallography offers the gold standard for determining average molecular structure to very high precision and accuracy. Crystals under pressure may also be discussed: this is important for polymorphism and drugs which are delivered in crystalline form - the exact form determines the rate of dissolution of the drug in the stomach and this is important if we want to avoid an overdose!

(2) Reaction Kinetics (by Dr. Duncan Wild) concerns the rates of chemical reactions. We will review the basics of kinetics learnt in CHEM1001, and then move on to dive deeper into the mechanisms of reactions. The link between proposed reaction mechanism and the experimentally determined rate law will be established through several examples. Other topics to be covered are the Steady State Approximation (SSA), reaction mechanisms involving pre-equilibria, enzyme kinetics, and the kinetics of unimolecular reactions, polymerisation, chain reactions, and explosions.
(3) Molecular mechanics and dynamics (by Dr. Dylan Jayatilaka). In case you have not realised, molecules move around. Normally we need to use quantum mechanics, but that is hard work. Molecular mechanics describes the forces acting on atoms. We will cover in detail the notion of energy and force, and how "force fields" are made and used to simulate not only molecular structure (this is molecular mechanics; there is an interesting connection to X-ray crystallography here!) but also how they move (via Newton's Law's). This is important because the Gibbs free energy (which is crucial for predicting all sorts of things like equilibrium constants, and voltages of batteries etc). 

The material in this course involves a bit of mathematics. Dr. Jayatilaka will take you through what is required early in the course in a non-threatening and hopefully enjoyable way, so you can brush up and gain confidence in these very important skills which are becoming increasingly important for the workplace.
--Dylan Jayatilaka

Learning And Teaching Strategy

This course will used a "blended" teaching model.

Lectures: Lectures will be delivered face-to-face or online (and they will be mostly on-line
if there is a COVID lockdown). Online lecture material will usually be required to be watched
before the workshop.

Workshops: Workshops (on Wednesday's, see schedule) will go over material at a slower
pace, with examples and exercises, and may be accompanied by formative quizzes. 
Quizzes: Even though quizzes are worth marks, they are not designed with "grading"
in mind. Rather they are designed for you to make sure you fully understand the basics of
the material so that you aren't "left behind". For this reason, attendance at the
workshops is mandatory (unless there are COVID lockdown requirements) and they will
not be recorded. Please check the schedule and warn the lecturer if there are problems
with attendance.

Laboratories: Laboratories are also compulsory to attend. They will be conducted either
in the X-ray room or in the computer laboratory G32, and there will be two standard "wet"
laboratories in Room 244. The lecturers will inform you in person or on LMS about the details.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
