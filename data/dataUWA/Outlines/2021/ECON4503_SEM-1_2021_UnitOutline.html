Unit Information

This unit serves two purposes. First is its content: We consider terrorism from a theoretical and especially from an empirical perspective. After delineating types of terrorism, we investigate when and why terrorism has been (un)successful in reaching its goals. We then turn to the extraordinary role of the media before exploring terrorism in connection with religion, moving beyond superficial stereotypes. The course concludes with a theoretical and empirical assessment of counterterrorism strategies.
The course’s second purpose concerns a strong empirical focus by introducing several empirical techniques designed to isolate causal effects from correlational data (e.g., natural experiments, instrumental variables, synthetic controls and regression discontinuity designs). These segments are presented to particularly support the empirical understanding of research-focused students writing dissertations and go beyond the terrorism-related content. 
Active participation, intellectual openness, critical thinking, and dialogue constitute key elements in this unit. Although the phenomenon of terrorism has been studied extensively from a descriptive perspective, many of its underlying motivations and dynamics remain difficult to pin down empirically. This is even though the US alone has spent trillions of dollars on the ‘War on Terror’, only to harvest widespread hostility across most Middle Eastern countries, for example. 
This unit aims to highlight and discuss (i) what sets terrorism apart from other, more traditional types of warfare; (ii) how the public, commentators, and researchers assess and interpret terrorism; (iii) what constitutes ‘success’ for a particular group, understanding the consequences of terrorism; (iv) the extraordinary role of media attention for terror groups; (v) the role of religious and cultural perspectives in terrorism; and (vi) the theoretical background and empirical success of counterterrorism strategies. The hope is that you will come away with a better understanding of what we do and don’t know about al-Qaeda and ISIS, but also about the IRA, ETA, the KKK, and white supremacists. 
In the process, we will see a variety of examples for empirical strategies to isolate causal relationships from correlations – what constitutes the main advantage of econometric analyses and its fundamental contribution to social sciences. Thus, another way of viewing this unit (aside of its focus on terrorism) is as an introduction to empirical methods, which should provide skills that are applicable to virtually any other empirical research question.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.
