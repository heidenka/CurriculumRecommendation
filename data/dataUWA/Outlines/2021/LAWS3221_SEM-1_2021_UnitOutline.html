Academic Information

Content This unit critically explores how Australian law regulates and fails to regulate creative expression, which is interpreted broadly to include content across the artistic, commercial, cultural and scientific domains. Examples of creative content considered in the unit include artworks, films, multimedia creations, advertisements, musical creations, designs (architectural, industrial, engineering, product design, web design, trademark logos), computer software, literary and dramatic works (articles, books, poems and plays), performances, inventions and industrial secrets (agricultural, scientific and engineering). The unit briefly surveys a number of laws, which may include intellectual property laws, defamation, privacy, trade practices, contract and censorship, and investigates how those laws can both protect and impede creative expression. The focus of the unit is on the socio-legal issues generated by those laws in the context of creative expression. Students gain an understanding of, and critically reflect on, how a mix of Australian laws regulate what we can express, how we can protect what we express and how those expressions can and cannot be used by the creator and others.

Assessment details
Tutorial participation (10%)
Students are marked on their participation in tutorials throughout semester. See the “tutorial participation” tab on LMS for full guidelines, including how the final grade out of 10 is formulated, and the marking rubric.             
 Tutorial Presentation (10%)
From week 3, 1 or 2 students give a 5 minute class presentation in an allocated tutorial on a topic which extends student learning beyond lecture materials covered more generally in that tutorial. The objective of the tutorial presentation is to demonstrate and share your knowledge and understanding of the general topic but more importantly to help your classmates to understand and learn NEW information.
See the “Tutorial Presentation” tab on LMS for full guidelines, marking rubric and presentation allocations.
 Students must advise the unit coordinator of their presentation topic by email at least one week prior to their designated presentation. Presentation topics may not be duplicated and a proposed presentation topic may be refused if another student from any tutorial group, has already chosen something too similar.
Students submit all notes and sources relied on to the "Tutorial Presentation Upload" Blog in the Assessment and Submissions Area on LMS.   
 Research Essay (30%)
 Students research and write a 2000 word research paper marked out of 30, excluding footnotes and bibliography. 
 A 150 word title and abstract is to be submitted at least two weeks prior to submission of the research paper.
 Students may choose a listed essay topic, modify an existing essay topic, or devise their own research question broadly related to one or more of the lecture topics.  Students must develop a persuasive argument, and justify that argument with evidence.
 See the “research paper” tab on LMS for full instructions, suggested essay topics, guidelines and the marking criteria.    
 Reflection Paper 1 (25%) 
 Students write a 1500 word reflection paper on an item of popular culture which depicts or engages with intellectual property law and write a reflection piece on that example, drawing on the theories, laws, concepts and issues explored in the unit.
 See the "reflection papers" tab for full instructions, guidelines, marking rubric, a list of items of popular culture, and sample reflection papers. 
 Note – some of the items of popular culture are novels, and students are encouraged to begin reading them at the start of semester if they think they might be interested in reflecting on that item.  Not all of these items are available through the University library, and again, students are encouraged to begin sourcing the item of popular culture that they are interested in as soon as possible (eg through interlibrary loans).
Reflection Paper 2 (25%) 
Students write a 1500 word reflection paper on subject matter relating to one or more lecture topics, excluding topics covered in the student's oral presentation, reflection paper 1, or research essay.
See the "reflection papers" tab on LMS for full instructions, guidelines, marking rubric, and sample reflection papers. 
This reflection paper is designed to give you an opportunity to reflect upon some of the material covered in the unit, and the issues, themes and questions generated by that content. The student is expected to, at minimum, have attended the relevant lecture or lectures and undertaken the set readings. However, students must do more than merely summarise lecture content or the readings. The paper must demonstrate that the student has reflected on the issues, concepts and laws discussed in the lecture and the readings. The reflection may be personal or academic. For example, a student may reflect on how their understanding of a particular law or legal issue has changed, may draw on personal experiences that engage with the issues, or deliberate on how the readings or lecture content link to other topics or issues the student has encountered in their academic study. The very best papers will go beyond the set readings and lecture material and demonstrate profound reflection and critical thought.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
