Academic Information

Content This unit is designed to broaden and extend understanding of the methodological concepts underpinning the science of epidemiology. The purpose is to impart knowledge of more advanced epidemiology, especially regarding different study designs and sources of errors in epidemiological studies including a discussion of causality. Students engage in (1) reviewing methods for assessing and addressing the key methodological challenges in epidemiology through in-depth discussion of key research papers; and (2) critically evaluating the evolution of epidemiology as a discipline and gaining a greater depth of knowledge regarding epidemiologic methods.

Unit Information

Epidemiology II  is designed to challenge and extend your understanding of the methodological concepts underpinning the science of epidemiology, especially with regard to different study designs, sources of errors and causality. 
This unit is about the theory rather than the practice of epidemiology. You will be engaged in reviewing methods for assessing and addressing the key methodological challenges in epidemiology through in-depth discussion of key methods and research papers.We will commence with a discussion of the evolution of epidemiology and some current controversies. Then we will focus on the application of modern epidemiological methods to current issues in epidemiology. These issues include information bias related to outcome ascertainment and exposure assessment, selection bias, causal diagrams and integration of data across studies. 
The unit assumes familiarity with the basic epidemiologic lexicon and a level of understanding of principles and methods commensurate with successful completion of introductory to intermediate epidemiology units taught at the postgraduate level. Critical reflection and expression of different points of view by participants, including views different from those of the presenters, are welcomed and encouraged, as are contributions that add value to material by sharing of knowledge and expertise held by participants.
I hope you enjoy the unit and that it stimulates your interest in epidemiology as a public health science. Throughout the course I welcome questions, comments and lively discussion. The material for this course is challenging, but if you spend time on the readings, participate in the class discussions and ask questions if you are unsure, you will be fine. We will be a small group and this will be a great opportunity to learn together.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

Epidemiology II is based on the premise of adult-education philosophy in which each student is an independent and self-motivated learner, with the role of the lecturer being one of facilitation and direction.  Notwithstanding this, please do speak with me if you are having difficulty with any aspect of the course.
Background reading materials will be available for each topic and students are encouraged to read and annotate these prior to the class. Class attendance as well as preparation and participation in these classes are expected as they essential components to achieve the learning outcomes.
The classes will take the form of short presentations on content, with time for clarification/questions followed by an interactive group discussions/workshops related to the case studies and/or pre-readings.  There will be one to three pre-readings for each class, which should be read in-depth prior to the class.  In order to meet the learning outcomes it is necessary for students to pursue each topic in more depth than is presented in the lectures.  Student should do this through additional reading outside of formal classes.  
The classes will be recorded and a link to these recordings will be on the Epidemiology II Blackboard site.  However, please note the interactive tutorials will not record well as students will be discussing issues in small groups as well as the larger group. PowerPoint slides, more detailed lecture notes, pre-readings and additional resources will be available via Blackboard.
Opportunity will be provided each week for students to discuss issues/ problems that they may have encountered with material to date.

Unit Structure

We are a small group and we will meet face-to-face each week for a three hours class with regular breaks  I or the guest speakers will give lectures but questions and interaction is encouraged throughout. Following the presentation of the theory, we will have discussion of the pre-readings/ exercises. The lectures will be recorded for further review.

Participation

Student participation in interactive sessions is essential to the success of these discussion and hence enhancement of your learning. Participation contributes a substantial proportion of the grade in this unit and it will be assessed according to the extent to which a student actively prepares for and participates in discussion in the class sessions.  
To obtain a high grade the contribution should be significant but balanced and show good insight.  This involves active participation without dominating, as well as actively helping to ensure all students have appropriate opportunities to express their views. However, remember we are all learning and all questions/comments are considered valid.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
