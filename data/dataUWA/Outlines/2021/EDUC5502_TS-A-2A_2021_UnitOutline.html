Academic Information

Content This unit is delivered as a series of interactive workshops with an emphasis on developing students' mathematics content knowledge and pedagogy, and improving attitudes towards mathematics. Taking into account the development and characteristics of children, emphasis is placed on a range of teaching strategies that use concrete materials to engage children in mathematical learning, along with the use of storybooks (hard copy or electronic) to assist children to engage with, and make sense of, mathematics. Students become familiar with the three content strands (number and algebra, measurement and geometry, and statistics and probability) and the proficiency strands of the Australian Curriculum: Mathematics. They also become familiar with the place of mathematics within the Early Years Learning Framework. Students begin to develop a resource file and professional library as a means of developing their own mathematics content knowledge.

Unit Information

As with all Masters level units, there is a considerable amount of reading. Parts of this unit involve a flipped mode - where work must be completed before coming to class. You are asked to be fully prepared for each workshop by ensuring that you have completed all required work or readings. You are also expected to be an active participant in each workshop, whether this is performing hands-on activities, group or class discussion. 
Mathematics is one of seven general capabilities within the Australian Curriculum (along with Literacy, ICT, competence, critical and creative thinking, ethical behaviour, personal and social competence, and intercultural understanding). In a world where knowledge itself is constantly growing and evolving, students need to develop a set of general capabilities (skills, behaviours or dispositions) that apply across disciplines. Such capabilities equip students to be lifelong learners capable of operating with confidence in a complex, information-rich, globalised world. 
The aim of this unit is to enable pre-service teachers to develop their knowledge and skills for the effective teaching of Mathematics in the early years (Foundation to Year 2). Within this unit you will analyse key features of the Mathematics Learning Area of the Australian Curriculum and related documents; appraise a range of teaching, learning and assessment strategies suitable for Mathematics at the early childhood school level; plan and sequence teaching and learning experiences appropriate for early childhood students that achieve the outcomes from the Mathematics Learning Area; and begin to build a mathematics resource file and professional library.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

Various learning and teaching strategies are incorporated into this unit. These include:
- Concrete materials such as pop sticks, straws, counters, dice, unifix cubes, MABs, ten frames, dominoes, tape measures, jigsaws, pattern blocks, building blocks and number lines. Natural items such as gum nuts, leaves, bark, twigs and stones are also used.
- Storybooks are used to engage children and help them make sense of mathematics. These may be hard copy books, big books, electronic books or YouTube stories.
- Technology is utilised through a range of iPad apps, calculators, Beebots and YouTube clips.
- Games are presented as a means of practicing numeracy skills and to engage children. These can be bought games or handmade games.
- Songs and rhymes with actions are used to practice numeracy skills, engage children and to use the whole body to experience mathematics.
- Puppets and felt characters are used to present stories, songs or rhymes; practice numeracy; and engage children.
- Problem solving is introduced through group activities to extend children's thinking and to illustrate the different ways to solve problems.
- The natural environment is used as a context and a resource for teaching and learning mathematics.
- Individual, small group and whole class activities are used as appropriate.

ACTIVE INVOLVEMENT AND PARTICIPATION IN THE UNIT
In this Masters level unit we recognise that you bring a wealth of knowledge, experience and expertise and it is important to build on this rich resource in order to collaboratively develop and refine knowledge, understandings and skills around science education. Group activities and discussion are important components of the mathematics workshops, and we are confident that all members will feel able to contribute. 
Engaging in reading the literature will also be important in developing and refining knowledge and skills. Specific readings have been set for most weeks. It is important that you are familiar with the readings so that you can take part in group discussions and workshop activities. We encourage you to find alternative readings, particularly from recent publication, so that you can access the most current literature on research and practice of the teaching, learning and assessment in early childhood and primary schooling. 
Attendance at all classes is strongly encouraged. If you are unable to make class, you should let the unit coordinator know.

ADDITIONAL DETAILED ASSESSMENT INFORMATION
Assignment 1 - Case study (40 marks)
In order to determine where to start teaching, it is important to find out what children already know and understand about an aspect of mathematics. The purpose of this assignment is to provide you with the opportunity to find out children’s prior knowledge of numbers through the use of think boards, and then develop appropriate activities to address an area of misunderstanding. For this assignment you will select one Year l child case study from LMS which contains three examples of completed think boards . Please see LMS for a full description of this assignment, which is summarised below.
For the written assignment you will describe think boards, use the provided think boards to report what the child knows about number and where there are areas of misconception or gaps in knowledge, locate the child on various curriculum documents (First Steps in Maths - Number, WA Curriculum - Number and Place Value Foundation or Year 1, Counting principles), make recommendations for the next area of development based on the child's main area of misunderstanding or gap in knowledge and describe two sequential activities that could address these, provide a referenced rationale to justify why you have chosen these two activities, and write a personal reflection.
Assignment 2 - Planning documents (60 marks)
Stories can be used to assist children to develop mathematical skills and knowledge in a meaningful way. This assignment will give you the opportunity to select and analyse a story book that has the potential to be useful to reinforce understandings of number concepts by young children. Please see LMS for a full description of this assignment, which is summarised below. This assignment can be done in pairs.
There are five parts to this assignment: choosing and describing a book that has the potential for children to develop a number concept, making a manipulative that assists in the development of the focus number concept, critiquing the use of manipulatives in the classroom, writing two sequential lesson plans that would teach the chosen number concept using the book and the manipulative, a class presentation to receive formative feedback on your ideas for this assignment, and a description of how you have addressed a given Graduate Teacher Standards.

RESUBMISSION RULE
Failure to pass an assessment item within a unit (MTeach)
15. Students must pass all components of a Master of Teaching unit to pass the unit overall  Students who fail an assessment item in a unit may resubmit it if -
(a) this is their first request for a resubmission in the unit.
(b) they contact the unit coordinator by email within 5 University working days of the release of the results and formally request a resubmission.
(c) A resubmitted assignment that is deemed to be a 'fail' by the unit coordinator, will receive the original failing mark for the component.
(d) Where resubmission is approved, the reassessed mark is capped at the assessment pass mark, unless an application for mitigation is approved in accordance with the University Policy on Assessment: Special Consideration (UP11/23).
(e) A resubmission is normally due one week after being approved by the unit coordinator.
(f) Assignments that are failed on the grounds of lateness or academic misconduct will not normally be considered for resubmission.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
