Academic Information

Content The world currently faces numerous natural resource management problems including air pollution (e.g. climate change), water quality, over exploitation of fish, other fauna and flora, as well as controversies surrounding resource intensive economic activities such as mining and logging. This has resulted in a shift in attitudes to environmental and natural resource management policies. The ability to articulate the causes and identify regulatory and incentive-based solutions to problems is a highly sought-after skill in public and private employment. After taking the unit, students are able to think about environment problems and their solutions in logical and systematic ways.

Unit Information

This is an intermediate level environmental economics unit organised broadly into four modules: basic economic concepts, natural resource economics, environmental economics, and economic valuation. 
•	In the first module, we review some basic economic concepts: link between economy and the environment, welfare economics, market failure, discounting and benefit-cost analysis. 
•	In the natural resource economics module, the focus will be on optimal management of non-renewable and renewable resources and related policies. Mining, fisheries, forestry, ecosystem services and biodiversity will be used as core examples of natural resource management. 
•	The environmental economics module focuses on economics of addressing pollution and externality problems, and relevant policies. Water pollution, air pollution and climate change will be used as core examples of environmental management. 
•	The economic valuation module focuses on the underlying concepts of valuing goods and services that do not have market prices. The focus will be on introducing the concept and application of both revealed (hedonic pricing and travel cost) and stated (contingent valuation and choice experiments) preference-based non-market valuation techniques. 
•	In modules two, three and four, we will discuss government efforts aimed at regulating or influencing private resource managers through legislation or use of negative and positive incentives.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

The role of the instructors in this unit is to broaden students’ understanding of economic concepts and approaches applied to manage natural and environmental resources. The lectures, tutorials, and computer labs are a joint responsibility of the teaching team and students. So for learning to occur, efforts should be put in place from both teaching team and students.
On completion of this unit students should be able to:
1.	Understand the fundamental link between economy and the environment, and sources of market failure and environmental problems
2.	Formulate and solve simple resource/environmental management problems as optimisation problems
3.	Identify and compare the range of policy instruments that are available to influence how private resource managers use the environment or natural resources 
4.	Develop insights on, and understanding of, contemporary environmental issues, impacts, and potential solutions
5.	Describe different environmental values humans derive from the environment, and explain which non-market valuation technique is appropriate to estimate values in a given context.

Unit Structure

•	The unit consists  two recorded lectures per week delivered online. These lectures will be delivered in multiple short videos and uploaded in LMS to provide key economic concepts to guide your reading and weekly tasks. You are required to consult text books and suggested readings to develop deeper understanding of these concepts and their applications. If you need to consult instructor to get clarity on unit contents beyond workshop/tutorial or lab sessions, please contact the instructors to meet/discuss during consultation hours (see consultation time of each instructor).
•	You are required to go through assigned textbook chapters for an in-depth understanding of weekly topics. 
•	A workshop/tutorial session and a computer lab will take place every alternate week. For example, you will have tutorial sessions in teaching week 1 and 3; and computer labs in weeks 2 and 4 (see unit schedule for detail). You will attend only one workshop/tutorial and one lab session per week depending on your enrolment for these sessions. 
•	In the workshop/tutorial session, you will read an assigned paper or examine a set of problems to understand underlying economic concepts and policy approaches. In some cases, you may need to apply these concepts to solve assigned problems. Instructor or tutor will facilitate workshop/tutorial session and clarify any questions you may have on tutorial materials. But it is your opportunity to examine the materials, discuss with your peers, and clarify any questions with tutor/instructor during such sessions.
There will be a pre-workshop/tutorial task for you to complete on a bi-weekly basis, starting from workshop/tutorial session 2. You will complete the task online in-advance [i.e., prior to the session), which will be marked.
•	In the computer lab, you will be practicing problem-solving questions relevant to the topics covered in earlier weeks/ lectures. Instructor or tutor will provide you guidance on how to solve the problems, but it is your opportunity to learn and apply what you have learned in the class. You will work in a group of two in solving the problems, so there will be peer-to-peer sharing as well. You may choose a partner to work with you in the lab sessions during the first lab. 
•	The works you do in workshop/tutorial sessions and computer labs are linked to the assignments you do in this unit. These assignments are submitted individually. This means you work in a group or with another student in computer lab, but when it comes to working in assignments, you work and submit individually (for details on assignments see unit schedule and assessment mechanism).

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
