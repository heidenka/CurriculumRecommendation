Academic Information

Content Real-world problems can benefit greatly from applying psychological research to their solutions. Yet policy makers have not always made use of this research and psychologists have not always communicated it in a usable form. This results in less effective solutions and under-utilised research. This unit consists of an exploration of how psychological theories, methods, and evidence can be used to assist in understanding and solving contemporary social problems. It is also designed to enable students to develop the skills necessary to present this information to policy makers. The unit introduces students to some of the major social problems in contemporary society. The topics vary from year to year as key issues and debates in society and in the field of psychology change, but may may include climate change, gender inequality, social conflict, and educational disadvantage. In addition to lectures on these modern social problems, students are also be encouraged to identify and explore some real-world problems that interest them through the tutorials and assignments. The unit prepares students for further research in psychology with an applied focus, and for employment in organisations - including business, government and the non-government sector - whose work requires the application of psychology to policy development. It will also ensure that students understand the limits of such research. Although a lot is now known about human behaviour from fundamental, often laboratory based research, there is still relatively little evidence about how these research findings can actually be applied to influence the behaviour of communities and populations..

Learning And Teaching Strategy

This unit takes a blended approach, with lectures, online content, and tutorials. 
Lectures and online content will deal with a range of social problems to which psychological theories, methods and research findings are relevant. The content will focus both on a description and broad analysis of the selected social problems, as well as the application of psychological science to these problems. The aim of the lectures and online content is to advance understanding about complex social problems, help you appraise the key psychological aspects which contribute to this problem, and help you judge which of these are relevant to solving or relieving the problem. Overall, this content will show how scientific psychological investigation can assist in resolving societal debates.
In the tutorials, you will discuss a social problem in-depth, and identify the psychological constructs   that are relevant to understanding the problem and/or to intervening to change the problem. Through individual presentations delivered in tutorials, you will learn to communicate in a way that translates psychological theory into practical application for both lay and professional audiences. In and outside of the tutorials, you will work together in small groups to design an intervention based on psychological research and theory to solve a social problem chosen by the group. The group interventions will then be translated to presentations that will be given in the last two labs. This group assignment will teach you how to collaborate in problem-focused think tanks to devise solutions to real-life problems.

Unit Structure

LECTURES
There will be a lecture each week (except the non-teaching week) on Thursdays from 4:00 to 5:45pm in the Social Science Lecture Theatre (G.130). Most of the lectures will be given by Dr Notebaert, but guest lecturers will also contribute. These guest lecturers will highlight some of the research and applied work that is conducted at the School of Psychological Science in specific problem areas. 
Lectures will deal with a range of social problems to which psychological theories, methods and research findings are relevant. The first half of each lecture will focus on a description and broad analysis of selected social problems, while the second half will explore the application (and lack thereof) of psychological science to these problems. 
An overview of the problem areas engaged with in this unit in 2021 can be found in the unit schedule below.
TUTORIALS/LABS
Tutorials will be held most weeks (see unit schedule below) and will last for 1 hour. In the first seven tutorials, you will discuss a social problem in-depth, and identify the psychological constructs   that are relevant to understanding the problem and/or to intervening to change the problem. The tutorials will consist of individual presentations and group discussions. In weeks 10 and 11 there will be labs in which students deliver their group presentations to their fellow students.
ASSESSMENTS
Individual Policy report (30%): 
Report writing is an essential skill for all psychology graduates. This assignment is designed, in part, to give you the experience of writing a report based on a selected problem area and making it suitable for a non-specialist audience seeking to understand the psychological processes important to understanding and altering the identified behaviour. 
The policy report is different than the lab reports you have written for other units, as it is designed for a lay audience, rather than an academic audience. Requirements for the content of this report, suggestions for its structure and format, as well as the marking guide, are provided in the course outline. The word count is 2500 words. This includes the abstract, but not the glossary, table of contents, figure captions, table headings, references, or footnotes.
Individual presentations (10%): 
There are two individual presentation assessments, each designed to enable you to practice some of the key learning outcomes of this unit: describing a problem area and explaining the relevance of a psychological construct to addressing this issue in a way that can be understood by a lay audience. Through provision of feedback on your presentations, observing you peers’ presentations, and applying your learnings to your second presentation, these presentations present a critical opportunity to learn-by-doing. 
Each of your two presentations is worth 5%. Individual presentations are assessed in the first 7 tutorials. You must deliver your first presentation in one of the first 4 tutorials. If you miss this, there is no guarantee that you will be able to do both your presentations in the subsequent tutorials. The marking criteria for the individual presentations are provided in the course outline. 
Group presentation (20%):
The group presentation assessment is designed to enable you to learn and practice the skills necessary to work in teams to produce policy proposals suitable for presentation to government, business or other interested parties. The emphasis is on translating relevant psychological theory and research into a format and language that can be easily understood by such groups without doing violence to scientific accuracy and integrity.  
Requirements for the content of this presentation, suggestions for its structure and delivery, as well as the marking guide, are provided in in the course outline.
Final test -Take Home Assessment (40%):
The final test will consist of a case study which will be released to you through LMS. You will have several days to provide a written analysis of this case study, which will need to be submitted trough Turnitin.  The case will be a description of a modern world social problem, or a policy proposal/intervention. You will be asked to comment on this case by answering a set of questions in a way that conveys your grasp of the learning outcomes of this unit. This can include, but is not limited to, conducting a problem analysis, researching the contribution of psychological constructs, proposing a policy recommendation, and/or critiquing an existing policy. 
Information on how to prepare for the final test can be found in the course guide. The word count is 1500 words. This count includes your text and in-text references, but not the case, questions, or your reference list.

Participation

LECTURES
Although the lectures will be recorded and made available on LMS, it is strongly recommended that students attend. Previous years’ results indicate that students who attend lectures in-person consistently achieve better results than those who do not.
TUTORIALS
Participation in the tutorials will encourage greater in-depth processing of the course material, and application of learned material in a variety of ways. In addition, participation will teach you the communication skills for translating psychological theory and research into practical application.
Tutorial attendance is not mandatory, however attendance will add substantially to your learning experience. This unit is different to all other Psychology units, as it requires a different way of thinking and communicating. The tutorials are designed to be interactive and give you the opportunity to practice this way of thinking and communicating. In addition, tutorial attendance will help you learn through observing others and reflecting on their performance. Moreover, by attending tutorials you will be able to get to know your tutor better, as well as your fellow students who you will be working with in your group project. For all these reasons, tutorial attendance is strongly encouraged. Also we notice year after year that those who participate actively in totals obtain better grades. 
Note also that the ‘Presentations’ assessment component concerns presentations given in class. Therefore, you will need to attend these classes to obtain grades for this assessment component.

Referencing Requirements

Policy report written assignment
For this report we do not restrict the way in which references need to be done to one particular pre-defined way, because policy reports vary widely (and wildly) in how their referencing is done, and we want you to have this flexibility as well. It means you can use any referencing style that you think suits the lay-out of your report. As such, there will only be two things we care about when we look at your references: 
1.	References are done in a consistent manner throughout the text, and
2.	The source of the information can be clearly identified somewhere in the report (e.g. when referencing a journal article, somewhere -whether in a footnote or in a list at the end- the reference needs to include more than just the author(s) and year). 
This means you can use a referencing style such as APA, Vancouver (a numbered style), a system where you put references in footnotes. In general, we encourage a reference style such as Vancouver (https://guides.library.uwa.edu.au/vancouver), because your text will not be cluttered with names and dates which could be distracting for your readers.
Final test
References in the final take-home assessment need to be in APA7 (https://guides.library.uwa.edu.au/apa).

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.
