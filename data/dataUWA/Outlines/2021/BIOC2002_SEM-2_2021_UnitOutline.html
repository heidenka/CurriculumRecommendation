Academic Information

Content This unit focuses on the central role of proteins in controlling or affecting cell function and expands on information covered in BIOC2001 Biochemistry and Molecular Biology of the Cell. Cells are complex systems containing many thousands of small molecules and larger macromolecules. This complexity enables cells to divide, grow and respond to external changes, and allows specialised cells of multicellular organisms to perform their particular functions. In this unit, the focus is on how these cellular functions are controlled and regulated by intracellular signals and extracellular signals. Understanding how cells regulate their activities allows insight into how an organism responds to and copes with its environment, nutrients and/or diet, infection and disease. The content of the unit is divided into five major themes: (1) metabolic pathways; (2) organelles and energy production; (3) post-translational signal transduction pathways; (4) transcriptional signal transduction pathways; and (5) the cell cycle—regulation and cancer. Workshops and laboratories are interactive and designed to reinforce theory concepts from supplied learning materials. Techniques and technologies used in modern biological laboratories are introduced and train students in biological laboratory skills.

Unit Information

This unit focuses on the central role of proteins in controlling or affecting cell function and expands on information covered in BIOC2001 Biochemistry and Molecular Biology of the Cell. Cells are complex systems containing many thousands of small molecules and larger macromolecules. This complexity enables cells to divide, grow and respond to external changes, and allows specialised cells of multicellular organisms to perform their particular functions. In this unit, the focus is on how these cellular functions are controlled and regulated by intracellular signals and extracellular signals. Understanding how cells regulate their activities allows insight into how an organism responds to and copes with its environment, nutrients and/or diet, infection and disease. 
The content of the unit is divided into five major themes: (1) metabolic pathways; (2) organelles and energy production; (3) post-translational signal transduction pathways; (4) transcriptional signal transduction pathways; and (5) the cell cycle—regulation and cancer. 
At the completion of this course, students will:
1. Develop a broad knowledge of the fundamentals of Biochemistry and Molecular Biology.
2. Be able to understand the key principles in Biochemistry and Molecular Biology.
3. Be familiar with the chemistry, structures and functions of biological molecules in a cellular context.
4. Be familiar with the “language” of Biochemistry and Molecular Biology.
The laboratory course focuses upon Biochemical and Molecular Biological techniques that are currently used extensively in both research and commercial laboratories. At the completion of this course, students will:
1. Demonstrate in-depth knowledge about selected key techniques in biochemistry and molecular biology.
2. Be able to integrate and apply existing knowledge to solve complex scientific problems.
3. Apply appropriate laboratory skills to answer questions of relevance to the discipline of biochemistry and molecular biology.
4. Demonstrate effective critical thinking skills.
5. Demonstrate effective quantitative reasoning skills.
6. Effectively communicate scientific information in written and oral forms.
7. Engage collaboratively and independently in scientific processes.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

In this unit students will experience a teaching and learning model which is considered blended, interactive, and known to be a more engaging way to learn with the potential to dramatically improve the learning experience. The content for all Theory and Laboratory content follow the same Prepare-Do-Review structure. Students will be required to "prepare" for class using supplied pre-class materials (pre-recorded videos/"lectures", explanatory or animated videos, readings, practise questions) to introduce a Theme's main concepts. "Do" - For the Theory content, each week a workshop session is timetabled where as a class we discuss and apply concepts from the pre-class materials (interactive, problem solving, case studies, small group work, online quizzes and polls, practise questions). For the Laboratory content students conduct an experiment and complete questions based on their findings. Finally, "Review", for the Theory content (post-workshop) students should engage in revision and self-reflection of their learning, the content, and assess their knowledge using supplied revision questions. For the Laboratory content (post-lab session), as a class we will discussion the experiment findings and related questions, followed by completion of an assessment. 
> What does this mean for how students study?
This structure of teaching and learning provides guidance to students on what we expect you to undertake before timetabled classes, then we may utilise our time in face-to-face workshops and laboratories effectively. These classes are designed to be interactive and to reinforce theory concepts from supplied learning materials. Academics will facilitate these classes and will be able to clarify any misunderstandings and answer student questions. Students are highly encouraged to participate in the in-class activities ("Do"), chat to peers, and share their understanding in a collaborative approach to learning.

This will be implemented as more and more employer institutions and organisations move away from paper-based notebooks to more secure digital formats.

Unit Structure

Theory 
The theory component is divided into five(5) Themes (see the document "Theory Schedule" on LMS) (20% of final mark). Each Theme will have sub-topics. As described above, students will be expected to engage in preparation for face-to-face workshop sessions by watching recorded lectures or videos, completing readings and/or other supplied learning materials. Workshops will be interactive, attendance compulsory, and participation expected.
At the completion of each Theme, your knowledge of the theme will be assessed via online quizzes, approximately one(1) question per Theme sub-topic (lecture). At the end of the semester, there will be an examination (40% of final mark) which will consist of Multiple Choice Questions (MCQ) and Short Answer Questions (SAQ). Check the LMS nearer the end of semester for revision material and specific exam information. 
Laboratory Practicals
Laboratory Practicals and Post-Laboratory sessions occur in alternate weeks. 
Note: An Online Safety Quiz must be completed in order to gain access to the first Pre-laboratory quiz.
Week A – Pre-laboratory (Students complete online in own study time ahead of scheduled laboratory). Watch the pre-recorded video/lecture covering the theory and information about the laboratory (on LMS site and LabArchives eNotebook). Revise using the practise pre-laboratory quiz online (formative assessment) before completing the assessed pre-laboratory quiz online. Note: Quizzes close before the laboratory practical session.
Week B – Laboratory practical (Laboratory attendance is compulsory – 3 hours). Students will need to access the unit's LabArchives eNotebook and review the laboratory details. Then complete the experiment component and the relevant Results Sheet page and questions within the 3 hour class. 
Week C – Post-laboratory discussion and quiz session (Attendance is compulsory).
The laboratory component is worth 40% of the overall mark, comprised of the laboratory marks (35%) and a data presentation talk (5%); (note that the 10 marks for each laboratory are scaled to a mark out of 7).
Please read the document titled: “Laboratory Guide” on the LMS site for further details.

Participation

Satisfactory performance in both the practical and theory components of the unit is required to pass the unit. Completion of the practical requirements (attendance and all assessment tasks) is compulsory.
Laboratories
The practical laboratories are a compulsory component of this unit. If you have a continuing condition or situation that may impact your ability to carry out one or more of the labs in this unit, we advise you to contact the unit coordinator at the beginning of the semester to discuss what accommodations are possible. 
Detailed information about the laboratories can be found online in a document titled "Laboratory guide".
Pre-laboratory quiz (2 marks)
It is critical that you understand the underlying theory and the aims of the laboratory before you undertake the actual practical work. You must attempt the online pre-laboratory quiz questions. Note that quizzes close before the start of the first laboratory for a particular experiment (see “Laboratory Schedule” for dates and times). Important note: All online activity is recorded during the quiz assessment procedure and can be retrieved at a later time if required.
Practical session (1 mark)
It is imperative that the laboratories start on time as important health and safety information is delivered in the first few minutes of the practical session.  If you are more than five minutes late, you will not receive any marks for that particular laboratory. 
Attending the Practical Session is a prerequisite for the corresponding Post-laboratory discussion and quiz session. If you fail to attend a practical session you will not receive any marks for the post-laboratory quiz for that particular laboratory.
Post-laboratory discussion and quiz session (7 marks)
It is important that the discussion sessions start on time.  If you are more than five minutes late, your quiz will be marked out of 5 instead of 7 (i.e. the maximum will be 5 marks). If you do not attend the post-laboratory discussion and quiz session you will not receive any marks. 
Missed a laboratory
If you miss a laboratory but you do not have special consideration granted, you can take the laboratory (and the associated quiz) at another scheduled session, or at the end of semester. However, you will not receive a mark for the laboratory, and the post-laboratory quiz will be marked out of 5 (instead of 7). If you have undertaken the laboratory, but have missed the quiz session, then you can retake the quiz at a later time, but the quiz will be marked out of 5.
If you have a clash for any assessment task, including a practical session, please inform the Unit Coordinator before the due date, and alternative arrangements will be considered. 
Laboratories are compulsory and there are penalties for failure to attend the practicals and these have been enforced. There are also penalties for failing to complete laboratory components.

Other


Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
