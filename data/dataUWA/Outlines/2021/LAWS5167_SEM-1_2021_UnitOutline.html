Academic Information

Content Comparative law has long played an important role in statutory law reform but is also gaining increasing acceptance in the decision making process of appellate courts. The comparative study of foreign law can contribute to a deeper understanding of our own legal system and to the development of new solutions for legal issues. This unit introduces students to the comparative law method and to the basic structures and principles of selected major legal systems, with reference to various areas of law.

Unit Information

The course is designed to help students, especially those engaged in legal research, to understand how to make the best use of comparative law as a tool. It explores some of the main themes of comparative method and the uses (and misuses) of comparative law. It gives examples from several disciplines in law where comparative method has been employed, and enables students to be able to structure their own comparative research and method. This unit is highly skills-based and enabling; it will assist students in learning how to carry out research in the laws of other jurisdictions.
It will first devote some time to the basic methodology of comparative law, its uses and misuses, as well as legal families. It will then examine various legal disciplines and encourage the class to engage in the comparative methods of specialists in different fields of study. Guest speakers are invited to present on their comparative legal research and the class will discuss their methodology with them. Ultimately, this will lead to the ability for each student to structure their own intermediary jurisprudence of a comparative study, i.e their research paper. 
At the end of this course, students will be able to:
- analyse the advantages of comparative legal method
- identify the pitfalls and dangers of comparative research
- demonstrate a basic understanding of legal transplantation and globalisation
- identify various comparative methodologies and their various utilities
- understand the application of various comparative methodologies to different projects in different disciplines and areas of law
- manage a comparative research project applying an “intermediary jurisprudence”
PLEASE NOTE that as this unit is taught on the basis of interactive seminars, there will NOT be any recorded lectures.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

The unit is structured around weekly 3 hour workshops/seminars where students will debate the current topics of comparative methodology, including weekly presentations on Open Space subjects of current interest in a comparative context, occasional guest speakers presenting their research for the students to "unpick" methodologically during half the workshop, and highly interactive discussions of the students’ own method and developing intermediary jurisprudence of their chosen comparative research projects. 
There will be reading and reading lists available on LMS for students to come prepared and ready to engage. Students are encouraged to make use of the interactive discussion boards, which are central to many aspects of class. 

Unit Structure

There will be a total of 12 workshops/seminars of 3 hour duration held face to face every Friday except Good Friday and Reading week (the session on Good Friday is rescheduled to the preceding Thursday). The main point of the unit is to teach you to conduct your own comparative research and apply comparative method, so an essay is due end of the unit. The essay is unsupervised, BUT there will be a formative assessment due before reading week, which will be subject to a face to face outline workshop held on the THURSDAY (note change of day due to Good Friday) before reading week.
Students will also be allocated into groups for an oral group presentation on a comparative method topic - IN THE EVENT of a lock down this will be replaced with a reflective log.
IN THE EVENT of a lock down due to the pandemic, workshops and seminars will be conducted online at the same timetabled schedule.

Participation

Attendance and engagement will be monitored throughout the unit, and you will be expected to be highly involved in debating all aspects of the topic in class and on discussion boards. As these are interactive seminars, they are NOT recorded.

Referencing Requirements

The referencing of the comparative essay is mainly expected to follow the Australian Legal Citation (AGLC4), but as there may be variable elements of different disciplines outside law, and different legal regimes which are not covered by AGLC, students will be permitted variable referencing methods where needed

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
