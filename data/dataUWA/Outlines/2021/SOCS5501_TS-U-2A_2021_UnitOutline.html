Academic Information

Content How do you know whether a policy, project or program has worked? Evaluation involves a structured process whereby the success of a social policy, program, or enterprise, can be assessed. Best practice requires that all projects, whether government, NGO or industry funded, include an evaluation component. This unit provides students with the skills and knowledge needed to assess the effectiveness and efficiency of social policies, programs and enterprises. It covers a range of tools and techniques, including program evaluation, rapid rural appraisal, participatory action research, photovoice and more. Students are taken through the process of planning evaluations, from determining what measures are used (e.g. quantitative and qualitative), and what is taken to constitute success, to reflexive learning based on outcomes. It covers formative and summative evaluation. The unit is useful for anyone working in community development, international development, policy development, governance, service delivery, criminology, public health and social research more generally.

Unit Information

A warm welcome to SOCS5501 Evaluation.
This unit is taught by Dorothy Lucks. My background is in Sustainable Development, Evaluation, and Multi-stakeholder Engagement. I have over 30 years of experience in designing and conducting evaluation studies of development project and programs both in Australia and overseas as well as multi-lateral organisations. I run a social enterprise that undertakes a range of activities both domestically and internationally with a focus on sustainable development, strategic planning and evaluation. I am a past Co-Chair of EVALSDGs, a global network of evaluators for sustainable development, Past member of the Stakeholder Engagement Committee of the Australian Evaluation Society and member of the group that developed the AES Competency framework, Secretary for the International Organization for Cooperation in Evaluation and Executive Committee member of EvalPartners.  
I am teaching this unit on behalf of the regular coodinators - Farida Fozdar and Jeannette Taylor.  This Unit is informed by their writings and ideas as well as mine and the wide range of colleagues in diverse disciplines and countries, and I will be drawing on different examples to illustrate the variety of ways in which evaluation can be conducted and used.
Evaluation involves a structured process whereby the implementation and success of a project, policy, organisation or program can be assessed and changes made for improvements into the future. Good practices require that all government, NGO or industry funded interventions include an evaluation component. This unit provides students with the skills and knowledge needed to assess the relevance, effectiveness and efficiency of programs. It covers a range of approaches to evaluation, including formative and summative evaluation as well as other approaches. Students are taken through the process of gaining an understanding of the logic underlying a particular program or policy, and planning, conducting and reporting evaluations, including identifying stakeholders, determining key evaluation issues, and deciding what data (e.g. quantitative and qualitative) will be collected by and from whom, and how it will be analysed and presented to stakeholders.  The unit will focus on the key issues of program improvement and accountability, what is taken to constitute success, organisational learning and evaluative thinking.  The unit will be useful for anyone working in community development, international development, policy development, service delivery, criminology, public health, community-based sciences and social research more generally.
I want this Unit to be meaningful and useful to you. Students will come to this Unit from different disciplines and with interests in different issues and methodologies. We have tried to offer you opportunities to pursue these within the context of a broader understanding of evaluation methods.  It is your responsibility to take advantage of these opportunities to gain as much as possible from the unit.
I am looking forward to teaching the unit and getting to know you. Best of luck with the Unit and I hope you enjoy it.
Dr Dorothy Lucks

Learning And Teaching Strategy

This unit has a focus on evaluation in the real world. The classes for this unit will be a combination of lecture style presentations and more participatory tutorial style sessions. I look forward to working collaboratively with you over this relatively short period to develop a sophisticated understanding of the basics of evaluation. We also hope you will get to know each other, enjoy working together and have some fun. The flip-side of an intensive unit is that there is very little time to catch up if you fall behind and you need to start the unit having done some pre-reading. So, please check the unit texts list and the LMS welcome for advance materials and ensure you have read the 'weekly teaching materials' on LMS prior to class. 

Unit Structure

This unit is comprised of six classes which are run from 4 to 7pm on a Monday evening for 6 weeks. 
The topics covered in each weekly session are:
1	Introduction to Evaluation and Program Logic 
2	Evaluation in Practice 
3	Evaluation Approaches 1: Developmental 
4	Evaluation Approaches 2: Outcome and Impact 
5	Performance Management 
6	Contemporary Issues in Evaluation and Evaluation Use 
it is expected that you complete the readings and prepare two discussion questions prior to the session.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.
