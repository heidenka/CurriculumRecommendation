Academic Information

Content Engineering mechanics is the study of how physical systems behave under applied forces and moments. In this unit students are introduced to the fundamental tools and techniques used to analyse a range of 2D engineering mechanics problems. Topics include the relationship between displacement, velocity and acceleration, coordinate systems, forces and moments, free body diagrams, static equilibrium, and momentum and energy methods. Throughout the unit, students will also be exposed to causes of structural failure and how failure can affect people and the environment.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

The unit is structured to encourage you to learn independently (using the reading material, lectures, examples, and the text book) and also in groups (collaboration and team work during practical classes and laboratories). 
Online practice tutorial problems are provided for you to gauge your understanding of the content as you progress through the unit. Weekly practical classes will allow you to consolidate your learning by seeking clarification on concepts and topics, participate in a peer-to-peer learning environment, and develop your engineering problem-solving skills. The two laboratory projects allow you to connect the concepts and methods you learn with a physical system.
The Statics and Dynamics laboratories will allow you to test the physical behaviour of selected systems and improve your ability to model those systems.
On successful completion of this unit you will be able to:
(1) apply a range of techniques to determine the kinematics of particles moving in the plane and simple rigid bodies undergoing fixed axis rotation; 
(2) apply the principle of static equilibrium to rigid bodies and 2D structures to determine external and internal forces and moments; 
(3) apply momentum and energy methods to determine the dynamics of particles moving in the plane and simple rigid bodies undergoing fixed axis rotation; 
(4) discuss the causes and impact of failure of engineering structures on people and the environment; and 
(5) communicate working clearly and concisely using standard engineering processes and conventions.

Unit Structure

Lectures
Two lectures will be provided each week to introduce content and present worked examples. 
Practice Classes 
The practice classes are an important component of the unit and you must you must attend your assigned session. There will be a short quiz at the beginning of each prac class to test your understanding of the basic concepts covered in lectures from the previous week. Following the quiz, you will work in small groups and individually, to apply the theory provided in lectures to solve selected tutorial problems. You should prepare for each class by reading the relevant material before your class and attempting the online tutorial problems. 
The short in-class quizzes will be conducted using an online system. You are therefore asked to bring a laptop, tablet or smart phone to complete the quiz. Your best 8 quiz marks will count toward 5% of your overall unit mark. 
Online Tutorial problems
Each week a set of online tutorial problems will be available. The tutorial problems will be accessible via LMS. These problems are designed to help you practice applying the theory presented in the lectures and do not count toward your mark. 
Online assignment problems
Each week a set of online assignment problems will be available. The assignment problems will be accessible via LMS. These problems should take around 30-45 minutes to complete and are designed test your ability to apply the theory presented in the lectures. They count for a total of 15% of your overall mark. 
 
Laboratories
You must attend your allocated lab session. In the laboratories you will complete two projects in small groups, one on the topic of Statics and another on the topic of Dynamics. Working in assigned teams, you will build, measure and analyse the behaviour of different systems through measurement and modelling. The workshop activities have been designed to help you understand and identify the similarities and differences between a system model and the physical system. Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
