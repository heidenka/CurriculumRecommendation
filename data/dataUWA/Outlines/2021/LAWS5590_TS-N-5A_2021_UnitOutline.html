Unit Information

This unit introduces the law of water resources management, focusing on Australia.  It explains the main principles of water resources law; ascertains the legal effect of the National Water Policy reforms; and familiarises students with the principal legal and policy materials applied in the management of water resources.  The topics of the unit are: (1) the governance framework; (2) water access rights; (3) water resources planning; (4) administration of statutory entitlements; (5) water quality management; and (6) legal perspectives on mining and water. 
How does the law provide for the management and sharing of water resources between competing human consumptive uses while also sustaining our natural ecosystems?  This question has garnered much attention as water becomes an increasingly contested resource across Australia and globally, and as periods of serious scarcity and extreme weather events increase with the emergent impacts of climate change. 
Australian attention to these issues has seen the State (except Western Australia) and Commonwealth Parliaments pass an array of new laws in pursuit of a national program of fundamental law reform - the most significant reform since water resources statutes were first enacted over a century ago. The key features of these new laws are statutory water resources planning to secure environmental allocations and share entitlements for consumptive uses that are tradable in a regulated market. The Australian model of water resources law is also gaining international attention that invites interesting comparisons. 
Western Australian Governments from both sides of politics have promised water resources legislation reform for 15 years. The current State Government announced in September 2018 ( https://www.water.wa.gov.au/legislation/water/water-resource-management-legislation ) that it had approved the drafting of a Water Resources Management Bill, though still no such bill has been seen. Can it be much longer?

Learning And Teaching Strategy

The unit will be taught by a combination of recorded lectures available through LMS in early September and six interactive in-class sessions across the three days 28-30 September.  The recorded lectures will provide an overview of the core principles in each of the six topics and the interactive class sessions will explore current issues in respect of the six topics. 
The interactive sessions will be led by Alex Gardner and each will feature one or more guest contributions from industry experts. The morning sessions will run from 9.30am - 12.00pm and the afternoon sessions will run from 2.00 - 4.30pm, leaving time each day to prepare for and reflect on the sessions. Details of the program of interactive sessions will be available on LMS. 
Before the interactive sessions, students will be expected to have listened to the recorded lectures, read a good selection of the key references in the reading guide and to have completed the minor essay. All of this will facilitate their participation in the interactive sessions. Non-degree registrants from the public and private sectors, both lawyers and non-lawyers, will also participate in the interactive sessions. Their participation enriches the learning experience because they frequently bring valuable practical experience to the seminar discussions. Degree candidates are automatically registered for the short course by enrolment in this unit. 
If COVID19 restrictions apply, the interactive sessions will be conducted by video conference use Microsoft Teams.

Unit Structure

The unit is structured around the six topics mentioned above: 
(1) the governance framework - institutions and objectives of water resources law; 
(2) water access rights, including Native Title to water resources; 
(3) water resources planning, including allocation of environmental water; 
(4) administration of statutory entitlements, including transfer of entitlements; 
(5) water quality management, including tracing the sources of diffuse source pollution; and 
(6) regulatory perspectives on mining and water.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
