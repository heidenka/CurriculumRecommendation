Academic Information

Content Taking a broad view of what might constitute furniture, this unit asks students to engage critically with furniture and product design through research, representation, and making. Students are asked to critically examine the joint concepts of ‘assembly' and ‘disassembly' and to engage in a hands-on way with the materials and processes used in contemporary design production. During the unit students ‘learn through making' as they design, construct, and document a number of full-scale, working prototypes. They also acquire the skills required to produce professional working drawings, enabling them to communicate their designs with industry.

Unit Information

Assembly/Disassembly
Taking a necessarily broad view of what might constitute ‘furniture,’ this unit asks students to engage critically with furniture design through: research, representation, and making. It requires participants to consider the joint concepts of ‘assembly’ and ‘disassembly,’ critically and pragmatically. In this unit, these concepts provide the primary basis according to which design proposals are developed.
Design-for-(dis)assembly
As we move further into the twenty-first century, architects and furniture designers face similar trials. The environmental and social implications of design production constitutes an enormous challenge—excessive waste, designed obsolescence, social inequity in production chains, habitat degradation, carbon emissions, resource depletion, and material toxicity are just some of the difficulties with which both disciplines must rapidly come to terms. By engaging with the notion of ‘design-for-disassembly,’ furniture designers have a practical way of ameliorating some of the negative impacts of their design production. The core precept of design-for-disassembly is that each of the components making up a manufactured product should be put together in such a way that they can readily be pulled apart again—the assembled should be easily disassembled. Design-for-disassembly allows different components, made of different materials, to be separately recycled or reused. It is a pragmatic way to prevent things from entering the waste stream unnecessarily Design-for-disassembly also has social and political implications—there is something inherently democratic and liberating in allowing users to repair or recycle a design-object. It expands the possible ways that the items we make and buy can be repurposed.

Workshop Protocols
This unit is founded on an active and productive relationship with the School of Design workshop. Therefore, there are a number of unit specific workshop protocols (below) with which it is important that you familiarise yourself.
Workshop Skill-Building Sessions:
This unit is structured in such a way as to acknowledge that participants will likely be proficient architectural designers but may not yet be proficient when it comes to furniture design. The first stage therefore, is designed to bring participants up to speed with the skills and knowledge required to ensure that participants will have the capacity to successfully complete assessments. Attendance and participation in Workshop Skill-Building Sessions (Weeks 2-5) will be essential.
Workshop Safety Induction:
Successful completion of this unit requires a valid completion of the workshop safety induction (Part A and B). You will not be able to complete your submissions without workshop access. If your induction requires updating, or if you have not completed an induction it is imperative that this is done before the end of Week 1.
Workshop Priority Access:
The School of Design workshop has been reserved for priority use by this unit on Friday's between 11am and 5pm. Depending on demand, participants may be split into seminar groups with each given a 2 hour priority use time slot during this time.
Material Supplies:
Students are generally required to supply their own materials (including fixings and adhesives). Materials for Workshop Skill-Building Exercises will be supplied by the unit coordinator.
Some large, uncut sheet material or specialty materials not readily available from retail stores may be ordered through the School of Design Workshop. All material must be paid for at time of ordering. Please allow sufficient time for ordered materials to be received and note that delays in receipt of materials are outside the control of School of Design staff and will not constitute grounds for an extension or special consideration. If in doubt, make other arrangements.
Cutting Lists:
You will receive hands-on training in the operation of many or the workshop machines and processes. Students must attend the required workshop inductions and if in doubt about the procedures for the use of any of the machines it is your responsibility to seek assistance and clarification. Workshop staff are there to advise and assist you.
A limited number of the machines (including the panel-saw) are considered too specialised for student use and workshop staff are therefore the only individuals qualified to operate them. If you require materials to be cut on these machines requests must be submitted via a printed cutting list.
Cutting by workshop staff will generally only be done once per week. Students who submit their requests, with all required information, prior to 12 noon-Monday will generally be able to collect their cut items by 4pm-Wednesday. Please note that this will only occur if material has been supplied to the workshop by the time cutting is scheduled to take place. Workshop staff will make every effort to ensure that this process runs smoothly and efficiently; however, at times of high demand there may be delays. Wherever possible these delays will be communicated to any students impacted. Please note, however, that delays in processing cutting lists will not constitute grounds for extension or special consideration. If in doubt, make other arrangements.
Workshop Clean Down:
Workshop areas are expected to be left clean and tidy. There are provisions for students to store work in cupboards and underneath benches throughout the duration of the unit, as long as it is kept neatly arranged and clearly labelled. All work that remains after the completion of the unit will be disposed of.

Required Assessments.
There are two projects in this unit. Although these projects have separate outputs and assessments they are intimately related.
ASSESSMENT 1 – TASK LAMP DESIGN AND PROTOTYPE (DUE WEEK 7 THURSDAY SEPT 16 between 3-4pm venue TBC.  40%)
The first project requires students to design and produce a full size working prototype for a low-voltage LED task-lamp. The first part of the project is organised around the disassembly, study, and documentation of a pre-existing, inexpensive design object—the HÅRTE task-lamp, produced by Ikea. Students will be issued with this during the first week of semester. Through the disassembly and documentation of this inexpensive piece of design, students are exposed to a number of commercial production processes, techniques, and materials. Pulling apart and documenting this small object also requires students to engage with concepts of sustainability and consider to what degree an item like this is able to be disassembled and re-purposed.
Students will be required to digitally document their ‘HÅRTE’ lamp using the industry-standard Autodesk Inventor software. This software is installed in the computer labs. Personal academic licenses are available for free at: https://www.autodesk.com/education/free-software/all

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
