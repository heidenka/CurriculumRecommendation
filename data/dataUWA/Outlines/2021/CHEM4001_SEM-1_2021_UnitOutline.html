Academic Information

Content The topics covered in this unit build on those presented in undergraduate units, and reflect cutting-edge developments in chemistry, providing students with an understanding and knowledge of the discipline at an advanced level. Four topics, each with three lectures, are offered per year. The choice of topics delivered is dependent on staff availability—examples include nuclear magnetic resonance; electrochemistry; chemical biology; catalysis; molecular reaction dynamics; spectroscopy; computational chemistry; mass spectrometry; X-ray crystallography. Students develop and present a short lecture on an aspect of one of the topics through independent readings, in consultation with the academic staff member in charge of the topic, and submit an assignment related to this lecture. The lecture highlights the research that has led to our current understanding of the topic. The main academic objective is for students to gain an understanding and an appreciation of how the knowledge in a field of chemistry progresses. Students' abilities in critical thinking, data analysis, and interpretation of results are extended. They also broaden their knowledge base and gain experience in presenting material unrelated to their research project.

Unit Structure

The topics covered in this unit build on those presented in undergraduate units, and reflect cutting edge developments in chemistry, providing students with an understanding and knowledge of the discipline at an advanced level.
Four topics are offered per year with topics chosen to survey important ares of the subject, and this year comprises Covalent Bonding in Chemistry, Natural Products (Secondary Metabolites), Chirality in Synthesis, and Molecular Reaction Dynamics. Each topic is presented as a three-hour workshop, allowing time to present and discuss ideas and concepts with the class.
Following the workshops, students will be develop and present a 10-minute lecture on an aspect of one of the topics through independent readings, in consultation with the academic staff member in charge of the topic. The lecture highlights the research that has led to our current understanding of the topic. Each lecture will be accompanied by 5 minutes of questions. Where necessary, allocation of students to topics will be made in order to balance coverage.
Prior to the presentations, students will submit a written summary of the lecture which has to include a dot-point list of the content, a statement of the expected learning outcome for the audience and a list of peer-reviewed references used for the presentation.
The main academic objective is for students to gain an understanding and an appreciation of how the knowledge in a field of chemistry progresses. Students' abilities in database searching, data management, critical thinking, data analysis, and interpretation of results are extended. They also broaden their knowledge base and gain experience in presenting material in a topic unrelated to their research project.
1. Covalent Bonding in Chemistry - Convenor: Paul Low
Description: The concept of 'bonding' is central to chemistry, yet across the field there are different, and sometimes contradictory, models used to describe the nature of the chemical bond. This short course will present a general overview of the molecular orbital approach to bonding based on symmetry and group theory considerations. By the end of the course, many of the other common bonding schemes such as the hybrid orbitals used in organic chemistry, ligand field theory from coordination chemistry and concepts such as the 18- electron rule in organometallic chemistry will be seen as extractions from the overall MO description.
Syllabus: 1) An introduction to symmetry elements and operations; 2) Group Theory and Character Tables; 3) Linear Combinations of Atomic Orbitals; 4) Molecular Orbital Diagrams Reading and supporting texts: Molecular Symmetry and Group Theory, A. Vincent, Wiley (any edition); Inorganic Chemistry, 6th Edition, Chapter 6, Weller Overton, Rourke, Armstrong, University Press
2. Natural Product Chemistry (Secondary Metabolites) - Convenor: Scott Stewart
Description: This topic covers the basic concepts of natural products chemistry and the biosynthetic pathways leading to secondary metabolites
Syllabus: 1) Practical issues of isolation, characterisation and secondary metabolites as pharmaceuticals, 2) Discussion of specific classes of compounds including a selection from fatty acids, prostaglandins; grandisol and various monoterpenes, Artemisinin, Taxol, steroids and various alkaloids.
Reading and supporting text: a) Medicinal Natural Products (A Biosynthetic Approach), Authors: P. M. Dewick, Publisher: John Wiley & Sons Ltd. b) Organic Chemistry; Authors: J. Clayden, N. Greeves, S. Warren, P. Wothers, Publisher: Oxford University Press

3. Chirality in Synthesis - Convenor Reto Dorta
Description: The topic covers the concepts of chirality and will give a short overview on how to apply them to the expedient synthesis of both small molecules and polymers. 
Syllabus: 1) General overview on chirality in chemistry, 2) Different approaches to transferring chirality to small target molecules, 3) selected examples of catalytic chemistry that involves chirality, with a special focus on Ziegler-Natta type polymerisations.
Reading and supporting texts: a) Ernest L. Eliel, Samuel H. Wilen, Michael P. Doyle 'Basic Organic Stereochemistry'; Wiley Interscience, 2001, New York (NY), USA. b) John F. Hartwig 'Organotransition Metal Chemistry, From Bonding to Catalysis'; University Science Books, 2010, Sausolito (CA), USA.

4. Molecular Reaction Dynamics - Convenor: Duncan Wild
 
Description: Understanding chemical reactivity at the molecular level through theory and experiment. As opposed to the top down approach of studying reaction kinetics from experiments as you have encountered in your undergraduate courses, this course highlights the bottom up approach to understanding reactivity by considering the essence of a reactive collision between chemical species. A deeper understanding of the Arrhenius expression will be established.
 Syllabus: The theory of reactive molecular collisions, potential energy surfaces, scattering as a probe for reaction dynamics, access to the transition state regions of the potential energy surface via spectroscopy and scattering, calculating reaction rates from structural considerations. Links will be made throughout to Reaction Kinetics that has been studied at the undergraduate level.
 Reading and supporting text: Notes derived from Atkins’ Physical Chemistry and other sources will be provided.

Other

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
