Unit Information

This unit introduces students to the role of Asian economies in the world economy. It investigates the recent growth of these economies and current relationship between these economies and the rest of the world.
In 1990, 56 percent of the world’s population lived in Asia where it produced 25 percent of the world’s output. Today, Asia’s share of the world population has changed little at 55 percent, but its share of the world’s output has sharply increased to 40 percent. Underlying this dramatic increase in production is the improvement of the standard of living for millions of people living in this region. East Asia and Pacific have seen the most remarkable transformation in more than doubling its income per capita between 1990 and 2015. How did the Asian economies grow so fast in the last two generations, while many economies in the Latin America and Africa were stagnant and even collapsed? Is there an Asian model that can be learned and emulated by other developing countries? How much longer are these Asian economies able to sustain their high growth rates? Moreover, should we count the high costs of rapid modernization that include severe environmental degradation, deteriorating quality of life, restricted political and human rights, rampant corruption and extreme income inequality? This unit aims to equip students with the knowledge for discussing the development strategies of the major Asian economies in the post-war period and for evaluating the growth prospects of these economies for the future. The emphasis is placed on China, India, Japan, South Korea, Taiwan, and Southeast Asia.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

Tutorial Class Assessments (Participation = 10% + Presentation =5%)
Tutorials will start from the week of Monday, 1 March 2021 (Week 2). Tutorial attendance and participation in class discussions and presentation are required for Week 2-6 and Week 8-12. There is no tutorial class for Week 7 due to the mid-semester exam being held in the week. Participation is worth 10% of the total assessment. To obtain the participation mark, students have to participate in discussions in every tutorial. Questions from lectures will be discussed in tutorials in the subsequent weeks. You need to actually say something in class to show that you have read the lecture material and attended (or watched the recording of) the lectures. The second assessment component of tutorials is a class presentation, which is worth 5%. Students in a group of two or three will do a 5-minute presentation on the topic of a research essay of their own choosing subject to the prior approval of their tutor (See below for more information about the research essay). The presentation should be about 5 minutes and should include no more than three PPT slides. In the presentation, students are expected to tell the class why they choose the topic for their research and what is the background of the topic. Students who fail to attend his/her enrolled class will receive a mark of zero for that class. For online students, students will not receive any participation mark if they cannot be seen and heard properly by the tutor during the class. Online students must turn on their Zoom video and audio functions during the class. 
Research Essay (20%)
Students are required to submit a research essay of no more than 2,000 words. This research essay can be written by a group of two or three students. Members of a group cannot be changed after approval by the tutor. The topic of the research essay must be related to a substantial aspect of an Asian economy or a group of Asian economies. You should pick a topic that is of your interest and should discuss your idea with your tutor early in the semester. For example, you can choose a topic such as: the effects of the trade war between China and US on ASEAN economies, the benefits and costs of Asian migrant workers for their home countries, what are the causes of inequalities in China? does sanitation impede growth in India? population ageing and technological progress in Japan, and many more. These are just examples for reference. An electronic copy of the research essay should be submitted to the tutor via LMS by 5 PM, Friday, 14 May 2021. More information about the research essay will be provided early in the semester. 
The Mid-Semester Exam (25%)
The mid-semester exam will be held in class (online) on Thursday, 15 April 2010 at 3PM. The exam will be an hour in duration and comprised of essay questions. The coverage of unit material by the mid-semester exam will be announced in class.
 

The Final Exam (40%)
A two hour examination held in the normal examination period. Material covered in the mid-semester exam may be re-examined. More information will be provided in Week 12.

Participation

Participation in class, whether it is listening to a lecture or getting involved in other activities, is an important part of the learning process, therefore it is important that you attend classes. More formally, the University regulations state that ‘to complete a course or unit students shall attend prescribed classes, lectures, seminars and tutorials’. Where a student, due to exceptional circumstances, is unable to attend a scheduled class, they are required to obtain prior approval of the unit coordinator to be absent from that class. Any student absent from class without having had such absence approved by the unit coordinator may be referred to the faculty for advice and may be required to withdraw from the unit.

Referencing Requirements

It is important that the referencing of any sources used in your written work is done properly, if only to substantiate the points you are making in your assignment or project. The Harvard style is the preferred used unless otherwise stated by the unit coordinator. There are some notes for guidance which have been prepared by the library staff: ‘Citing your Sources Harvard Style’ . The EndNote software package is a really good system for building up a database of references. Not everyone will want to invest the time in using this system but you should consider it if you intend to build up resource materials or plan to undertake extensive research in a particular area. The library staff have also developed a tutoring package: ‘A Quick Guide to Using EndNote’ which provides the basics for using EndNote with an essay http://guides.library.uwa.edu.au/endnote

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
