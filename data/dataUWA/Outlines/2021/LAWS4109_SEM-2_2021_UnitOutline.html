Academic Information

Content This unit examines the role of the lawyer in the legal profession and wider society through the study of legal theory and legal professional ethics. It addresses questions about the concept of the legal system, the nature of legal argumentation and reasoning and different approaches to questions of justice. It explores the framework of laws, professional rules and institutions governing legal professional ethics in Western Australia, in the context of both litigation advocacy and commercial practice, as well as the background and rationale for professional ethics. A critical and reflective understanding is the central focus through close reading of theoretical texts and analysis of professional ethics challenges in a variety of scenarios. Students develop research skills in using theoretical scholarship and the regulatory, administrative and judicial decisions on legal professional ethics.

Unit Information

This unit examines the role of law and the role of lawyers through the study of legal theory and legal professional ethics. 
The legal theory component, which is covered in the first five weeks of lectures, explores questions about the nature of law, the nature of legal argumentation and reasoning, and different approaches to questions of justice, from both Western and Indigenous perspectives. This part of the unit focuses on a close reading of legal theory texts and analysis of those texts.
The professional ethics component, which is covered in the the remaining seven weeks of lectures, explores the framework of laws, professional rules and institutions governing legal professional ethics in Western Australia as well as the background and rationale for professional ethics. Understanding of and compliance with legal ethical rules is required for every lawyer in every area of legal practice.  As it is critical to legal practice, the ethics portion focuses on understanding and application of ethical principles, including critical reasoning, in a variety of scenarios.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

Teaching and Learning
The tuition in this unit consists of weekly two hour lectures, and weekly two hour tutorials. The subject matter of tutorials generally explores the material covered in the lecture of the previous week. Lectures can be attended in person (which is encouraged) or accessed online through LMS (see above). All tutorials will be face to face.
The lectures commence in week 1 of the semester and the tutorials commence in week 2 of the semester. 
Lectures will focus on introducing students to key concepts and principles in legal theory and legal ethics. The tutorial readings and exercises will enable you to engage with the law and ideas we address in the lectures more meaningfully and in greater depth. Tutorials provide students with the most significant opportunity to assist their understanding, engage in in-depth thinking and discussion, and develop the concepts and ideas presented in lectures. Students must complete the assigned tasks and readings provided in the weekly tutorial question papers and work through the tutorial questions prior to attending the tutorial. Preparation for tutorials will ensure that students get the most out of lectures and tutorials.
NOTE: Students are not permitted to pass any lecture recordings to any other person by any means. Students are also not permitted to pass copies of any unit outlines, lecture outlines or slides, tutorial question papers or any other course materials by any means to any person not currently enrolled in Legal Theory and Ethics. Doing so constitutes a breach of the University's copyrights and constitutes misconduct.

Tutorial Eleven

Participation

There are eleven tutorials over the semester. Students will be given a mark out of 10 for each tutorial, which will be converted to an average mark out of 10 at the end of semester. Students will be entitled to one 'grace' absence from tutorials. That is, you are entitled to be absent from one tutorial (only) over the semester without that affecting your participation mark. In that case, your average will be calculated by reference to 10 tutorials.
Students will be awarded a mark on the basis of their preparation for, and participation in, tutorials in accordance with the following criteria.
HD (8 to 10 marks) : Student shows excellent preparation for all tutorials; exceptional standard of participation with contributions being clear, substantive and insightful; student is punctual, involved in discussion, and listens respectfully to others; student shows excellent command of subject matter with articulate, well-constructed arguments.
D: (7 to 7.5 marks): Student is well prepared for tutorials; contributions to class discussion are clear, relevant and well considered; student is punctual, involved in discussion, and listens respectfully to others; student shows good understanding of subject matter and arguments are informed and thoughtful.
CR (6 to 6.5 marks) : Student is adequately prepared for all tutorials; attends and is punctual, shows interest, contributes to discussion and contributions are generally correct and mostly clear.
P (5 to 5.5 marks): Student is not adequately prepared for most tutorials; attends; contributions to discussion are mostly adequate but reflect little understanding of the issues raised by the tutorials.
F (0 to 4.5) : Student attends but may be late, shows little or no interest, contributions or preparation are inadequate; or student does not attend.
Students who do not attend a tutorial will be given a mark of 0 out of 10 for that tutorial unless there is special consideration granted. All inquiries regarding tutorial absence or tutorial group changes must be directed to the Unit Coordinators, not individual tutors.
NOTE: Students may perform at a level above or below some of the elements of each level descriptor. In that case, the mark will be allocated on an overall weighing of how the student performs

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
