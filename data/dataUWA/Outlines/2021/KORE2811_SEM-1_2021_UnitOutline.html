Academic Information

Content This unit is designed to enhance students understanding of Korean culture and society through introducing students to a broad range of topics and issues that relate to Korean culture past and present. It aims to enhance students' critical understanding of South and North Korean cultures as sites for identity formation and political practice.Through examining the relations of culture and power, this unit encourages students to develop solid understanding of the broader context of the development of national identity discourses in South Korea, and to a lesser degree, North Korea. Topics covered in the unit include Korean culture under colonialism, protest cultures, trauma cultures, traditional culture, cultural diplomacy, and North Korean arts and public culture. The unit introduces students to researching and reading a range of materials both in English and Korean, albeit only a very basic level of Korean language ability is expected at this level as all assignments are submitted in English. Students have the opportunity to engage with a range of visual media (such as films and television dramas), art, literature and music, and to examine why certain meanings are attached to cultural texts at the moment of their production. This unit enhances students' ability to analyse cultural texts in a non-Western context, and to identify, analyse and evaluate the significance of the political and philosophical discourses that underpin and inform cultural production in the two Koreas.

Unit Information

This unit is so-called ‘blended learning unit’. This means that based on previous years’ feedback I’ve tried to develop this unit in such a way that it is flexible and allows you as a learner to pursue areas of your interest in the area of Korean culture. However, this unit will not be an easy ride either. Many of you will learn new skills, new ways thinking about culture and cultural production, as well as many transferable skills which I hope you can put to use in the workplace when you eventually graduate!
So while this unit is about Korean culture and we will be covering many topics ranging from colonial period literature to North Korean propaganda art, the key skills and aptitudes that I hope you will gain during this semester are:
•	How to analyse cultural texts and present your findings in academic writing and also present/defend your arguments orally
•	Critical thinking and reasoning skills
•	Understanding of the significance and power of cultural discourses in everyday contexts, as well as in broader contexts such as politics
Finally, I hope that this unit will inspire you to study Korean culture further on your own (or maybe at postgraduate level even!). We can only cover a limited number of topics, and the main aim of this unit is therefore not to teach you all you need to know about Korean culture, but give you the tools to keep on learning beyond this semester.
All your learning materials (or links to thereof) will be available via LMS.

Each week, there will be a recorded online lecture and a 2 hour tutorial. Tutorial duscssions will be assessed so if you cannot attend, please apply for special consideration via the Student Office or request for alternative assessment.

Participation

There will be no presentations by individual students or groups of students in this unit. Students will therefore be assessed on their participation in the tutorials over the course of the semester - see LMS how this works in terms of marks awarded for evidence of critical engagement with set tutorial questions - there are clear advice on how to demonstrate this on the LMS). Particular attention will be placed on evidence of reading and critical reflection on the set questions and and evidence of engagement with the lecture materials (recorded and available online). You will be expected to have read the ‘Essential Readings’ listed in the tutorial readings for each week and show evidence of participation and preparedness. 
Remember that a workshop is NOT a second lecture, but an opportunity for students to discuss, clarify, and further explore issues raised in the recorded lectures. A successful workshop is one in which all students contribute to the discussion and each session will count toward your final mark for this component. Passive attendance attracts a low mark for this component of the final mark. 
Our workshops are designed to be interactive in nature, and will provide ample opportunities for all to participate and make a contribution. Each session is graded as follows:
•	Fail (0-49%) - No show, or completely distracted on social media etc/asleep.
•	Pass mark (50%) - Attends passively, no meaningful contribution or evidence of preparation.
•	Credit (60-69%) - Demonstrates some evidence of prior preparation, participates actively in class discussions to expected level.
•	Distinction (70-79%) - Demonstrates clear evidence of thoughtful engagement with the learning objectives and tasks set for the tutorial session, active participation and thoughtful contribution to class discussions.
•	High distinction (80%+) - Demonstrates high degree of critical engagement with learning materials, makes original and highly relevant contributions to class discussions and tasks set for the tutorial session.

Other

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
