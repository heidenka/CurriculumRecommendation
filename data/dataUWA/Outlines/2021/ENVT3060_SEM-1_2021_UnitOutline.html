Academic Information

Content This unit seeks to examine the soil as an environment for the growth of plants, and to develop a basic understanding of plant nutrition. The unit integrates knowledge of biological components of soil fertility with the physical and chemical components of soil. The role of soil biota in cycling nutrients for plants and in the development of a suitable environment for plant growth is emphasised. These aspects are considered in the context of soil management to maintain sustainable agricultural, horticultural and forest production, to restore disturbed natural ecosystems and to understand processes in managed and natural ecosystems.

Unit Information

This unit is an introduction to soil-plant interactions in managed and natural ecosystems, with an emphasis on plant nutrition and soil biology. It builds on ENVT2236 Soil Science (old names: Geomorphology and Soils, Earth Surface Processes and Soils, Soil Science Essentials, Physical and Chemical Aspects of Soil Science; Soil Science 201/230) that deals with soil physics, soil chemistry and the formation of soils.
Soil-Plant Interactions ENVT3060 seeks to (i) examine the soil as an environment for the growth of plants, and (ii) develop a basic understanding of plant nutrition. The role of soil biota in cycling nutrients for plants and in the development of a suitable environment for plant growth is emphasised. These aspects are considered in the context of soil management to maintain sustainable agricultural, horticultural and forest production, to restore disturbed natural ecosystems and to understand processes in managed and natural ecosystems.
The abilities that will be developed and assessed in this unit are:
         - knowledge of soil biological processes
         - knowledge of processes by which plants acquire and use nutrients
         - knowledge of soil-plant interactions that govern nutrient cycling in the soil-microbe-plant continuum
         - skills in designing experiments to test hypotheses
         - skills in writing research reports
         - skills in using scientific research papers
         - capacity to critically appraise and place research findings in a broader context
         - to record, analyse, present and discuss experimental data.
The unit also encourages collaboration with other students in designing and conducting a small research project. Development of collaborative skills is an important aspect of this unit.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

The practical sessions and lectures are organised in a complementary manner. Sometimes information will be first introduced in a lecture, and followed up in a practical session. On other occasions, new information will be first introduced in the practical class.

Unit Structure

The emphasis in the practical classes is on experimental design, detailed observations and reports that interpret your findings in relation to published data. A number of experiments will be carried out; you will work mostly in groups. Collated class data may provide a basis for questions at the final exam.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
