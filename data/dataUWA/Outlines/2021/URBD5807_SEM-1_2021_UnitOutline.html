Academic Information

Content This unit introduces the concepts of urbanism and urbanity, and the changing form and meaning of the city. Students examine key factors that have shaped urban form throughout history including the environment and our attitudes to it, transportation, technology, building practices, economics, politics, culture and the arts. By tracing the influence of these different factors, students are encouraged to speculate on the challenges of our swiftly changing world and the types of design responses required to meet them.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

This unit is an interactive ongoing development experience that encourages students to question assumptions and critically analyse complex urban projects. Students who enrol in this unit should, therefore, bring an inquisitive mind and be prepared to engage with peers and guest practitioners.
The learning activities in this unit include: 
Practical in class face-to-face activities; 
Self directed research;
Weekly seminars with guest practitioners and participating in class discussion; 
Critical and constructive feedback to peers;
Student self study including reading of scholarly articles and books; 
Online  communication;
Class and group discussions on specific topics.

Unit Structure

The class meets weekly on Wednesdays from 5pm-8pm for a 3-hour session which regularly will include student/presentation, thematic discussion, lecture/presentation and seminar/group discussions . 
Students will be able to attend either online via MS Teams or in-person at the Australian Urban Design Research Centre (AUDRC), Level 2, 1002 Hay Street, Perth. 
Students are assigned a case study city and presentation focus theme and presentation date at the commencement of semester.
Lectures and presentations will explore how key forces shape our cities. Each week, following a relevant lecture or presentation, students discuss in groups how that week's theme (or force) has impacted the form and organisation of their case study city. Students prepare notes from the lecture and discussion which then forms the content for the notebook submission at the end of semester. If the theme being discussed is the focus theme allocated for that student, they then prepare a short presentation which communicates the impact of that theme with respect to their case study city (either the entire urban region or a specific part) and also provide comment with respect to Perth (again either the entire urban region or a specific part).
This format repeats until all of the 'forces' have been covered and all of the students have made one presentation each.  
At the end of semester students will compile each their lecture notes and weekly analyses of their assigned case study city into a clear and legible notebook and submit an essay which captures their reflections on the theory, case studies and discussions throughout semester .
Throughout each week following the student presentations and prior to the lecture, the lecturer will facilitate a discussion which will focus on how this particular theme has influenced Perth's urban form and spatial organisation. Students are encouraged to bring their own observations and local reflections to participate in this discussion.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
