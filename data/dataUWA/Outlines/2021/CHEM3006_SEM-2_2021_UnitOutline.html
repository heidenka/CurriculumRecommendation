Unit Information

The unit is designed to introduce the concepts and techniques of chemistry related to real-world applications of physical and analytical chemistry to industrial processes, materials and their effects on the environment.
Lecture Topics covered include:
Prof Rob Atkin- Colloids, Surfaces and Polymers
Colloids, Surfaces and Polymers underpin key Australian industries in Health and Energy and Environmental, and for applications including: recovery of metals from ores, explosives, paints, coatings, fertilizers, water purification, lubricants and cutting fluids, food, cleaning products, extractions, consumer care products, pharmaceutical delivery devices, and clean energy etc. Companies associated with these applications include Australia’s largest companies and employers of chemists, including but not limited to Westfarmers, BHP, Woodside, Chevron, Alcoa, BASF, Fortescue, Incitec Pivot, iCeutica, Dyno Nobel, Orica, Rio Tinto, etc. This course introduces students to the science of polymeric and colloidal materials and their applications. The relationship between the structure and properties of the materials and their performance will be highlighted. 
Prof Killugudi Swaminatha Iyer-Industrial Nanotechnology
The principle of nanotechnology is the ability to work at the molecular level, atom by atom, to build large structures with fundamentally new properties that are not achievable in the bulk state of materials. Nanoscale materials offer a new paradigm for materials manufacture by submicron- scale self-organization and self-assembly to create entities from the “bottom up” to develop industrially real world applications in information technology, healthcare, therapeutics, diagnostics, environment and energy. 
The lectures in industrial nanotechnology covers the current cutting edge industrially relevant applications developed using major advances in nanotechnology. The lectures will cover the phenomenon of nanoscale materials and how scale contributes in developing advanced materials for real world applications.  We will focus on the key concepts of surface, size, shape, self-assembly and their significance in nanotechnology using the current industrially relevant case studies. To illustrate this, the lectures will be dedicated to materials that have been a mainstay in enabling key advances in the industry today. We will cover applications of silica, titanium, gold, iron oxides, polymers, quantum dots and carbon.
Prof. Dylan Jayatilaka-Environmental and Planetary Chemistry
Life is a process which depends critically on the chemistry of the environment. This part of the course is concerned with describing the chemistry associated with the formation of the Earth, focussing on the aquasphere and atmosphere. For comparison, we will describe the chemistry of Titan, a moon of Saturn, and one of the earth-like planets in our solar system. 
Topics will include: origin of the universe and elements, the chemistry of the origin of pedosphere, aquasphere, and atmosphere, and the role of life chemistry in these processes; the composition of natural waters, focussing on the closed and open carbonate system, aqueous redox reactions, Pourbaix dagrams and corrosion; atmospheric chemistry, focussing on the ozone layer, and the role carbon dioxide in radiative processes and global warming, and the role of human economics in these chemical processes; and finally a description of the chemistry of Titan, focussing on its atmosphere and possibilities for supporting life. 
The lecture topics are supported by three relevant laboratory experiments: an alaysis of lead in soil samples taken from near Beatty Park aquatic center; the analysis of particulates in the atmosphere in locations around UWA; and the analysis of corrosion via the Tafel plot method.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

This unit will be using a blended approach to learning. This means that lecture material will be presented as online videos. The videos are accessible from the units LMS site every week with designed content and learning objectives. It is strongly recommended to watch these videos in good time, as there will be assignments based on its content as well as the final exam. The in workshop assignments are submitted during the lecture-workshop session on Thursday 9-11am. In your lecture-workshop session be prepared to work through problems in groups and discuss the solutions as a class. We have designed activities for you to do in class to help you gain a deeper understanding of the material.

Unit Structure

In-Class Workshop Assignments 
It is expected that you watch the weeks content of videos before attending the tutorial class. In the tutorial class the lecturer will post discussion questions and tutorial-based questions beforehand for you to complete during the class. Towards the end of the tutorial class there will be an in-class assessment (quiz, short answer question) for you to complete and hand in for grading.
 Video Assignment 
This assignment will be based on the lecture material presented by all lecturers (videos and tutorials). This assignment will involve developing a 8-12 min video presentation on addressing a key concept introduced in this unit. These presentations will be done in pairs who will be randomly grouped by the unit coordinator. The assignment is due at the end of week 11. Part of the assignment is to view and comment on at least two other groups videos. This will be done on the LMS discussion forum. You can ask a member of staff in this unit for feedback on your video at any point in semester. Please see details on the LMS for more information on the video assignment.
 Laboratory
There are five laboratories in CHEM3006, each are equally weighted and worth a total of 20% of the total unit mark. 
The laboratory reports/worksheets are due one week after you completed the laboratory. Further details about the laboratories will be made available via the LMS site. 
There are five laboratories in CHEM3006, each are equally weighted and worth a total of 20% of the total unit mark. There are three compulsory laboratories that all students will complete and submit a laboratory report/worksheet for marking on the LMS.
1.	Assessment of the lead contamination in soil samples
2.	Pendant drop tensiometry
3.	Titanium dioxide raspberry solar cell
There are two optional laboratories and you will choose which two laboratories you want to complete out of the following laboratories:
1.	Adsorption isotherm on silica
2.	Coagulation of a Sol-Shulze Hardy Rule
3.	Measurement of the iron corrosion exchange current using the Tafel plot method 
4.	Alcoa Bayer liquor TOC/TA determination laboratory
5.	Synthesis of ruby red colloidal nanoparticles
The laboratory reports/worksheets are due one week after you completed the laboratory. Further details about the laboratories will be made available via the LMS site.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
