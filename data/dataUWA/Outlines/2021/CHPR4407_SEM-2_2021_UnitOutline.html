Academic Information

Content Students completing this unit demonstrate competence in computational fluid dynamics (CFD) analysis, the ability to understand practical issues of multiphase fluid transport, the ability to estimate the dispersion of species in gas and liquids, and knowledge of emulsion and suspension viscosities. This experience provides students with experience for working in the oil, gas and renewable energy sectors. Topics covered include turbulent flow and its analysis; introduction to CFD; non-Newtonian flow and fluids; suspensions and emulsions; multiphase flow in pipes; mixing and dispersion; and contaminant transport in turbulent flows.

Unit Information

This unit introduces students to the use of numerical methods to develop analyses of complex problems in transport phenomena (momentum, energy, and mass transport). 
The course will initially focus the general Transport equation, to introduce the modeling of diffusive transport phenomena. We will then expand to modelling advective transport. The finite difference and finite volume methods will be introduced.
This will lead into Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD). The mass, momentum and energy balance equations in a fluid continuum will be reviewed, before moving on to a more detailed review of turbulent flow and the models used for its analysis. We will investigate the methods by which models are formulated as transport equations and solved for steady-state and transient fluid flows. Boundary and initial conditions (and their effects on analysis) will also be a focus.
The course will then move on to consider multiphase flow phenomena. This will encompass Gas/Liquid and particularly Liquid/Solid (slurry) flows. 
Participation in the full workshop session each Thursday will be essential, as there will be assessments to be submitted after the workshop session. A “boot camp” on the Fluent CFD software package will be delivered in the first week – it is imperative that students not miss the first week of class (it will be difficult if not impossible to catch up if the session is missed – it will not be repeated). 
At the workshops, students will undertake individual and group exercises, and then will complete an ANSYS Fluent exercise each week - selected results from which must be submitted for assessment at the end of the session. The Fluent exercise will be made available during the workshop session; video guides will be made available to assist students in developing the skills needed to complete the Fluent exercise, and you will be able to ask the lecturer and facilitator questions as you work through the exercise.
Students will develop their skills and demonstrate their understanding through the completion of two assignments – one involving the development and analysis of a CFD model of a practical industrial flow field, the second dealing with multiphase flow
A practical exam will take place during the workshop sessions in Week 10 of the semester), and will encompass a practical test of CFD model formulation and execution.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

Lectures
In this course, the lectures will focus for the first 8 weeks on the fundamental theory and techniques for modelling transport phenomena and in particular fluid flows. For weeks 9-12, the focus will be on understanding the nature of multiphase flows and undertaking some fundamental calculations.
Each week, reading may be assigned from the text, or additional literature. Any assigned reading will notified by 5:00 pm on Friday of the previous week. Students will be expected to have completed the assigned reading in preparation for reviewing each week’s lecture.
For semester 2, 2021, all lectures will be delivered face-to-face and recorded by the Lecture Capture System.
It is essential that you view the lecture material before participating in each week’s workshop.
Workshops (including in-class Fluent exercises)
The workshop format is flexible, but the sessions will be broadly structured as follows (with both the face-to-face and online sessions following the same format):
• A variety of group activities, which may include
o An unstructured “tutorial” period, where students will work on group and desktop exercises to develop understanding of the theory of numerical modelling, and multiphase flow.
o Discussion of model development and meshing strategies
o Student group work on model development and meshing strategies (with groups reporting back to the class on successful approaches).
o Unstructured time to address issues identified during the week’s sessions.
o Literature Reviews dealing with topics relevant to the week’s material or the assignments
• A weekly ANSYS/Fluent modelling exercise, which will be assessed, and must be completed and submitted on the day of the class (in fact shortly after the end of the class).
The exercise will be made available during the workshop session, along with videos demonstrating any new skills required to complete that week’s exercise. Students will then work on the exercises individually, though they may ask for assistance from the lecturer and facilitator, and/or work in groups – collaboration during the workshops is encouraged.
Students will work with ANSYS/Fluent in a virtual desktop interface (VDI) which will be introduced in the first workshop – students will need to bring their own laptop to each workshop (both PC and Mac laptops can access the VDI). Given the use of training videos, bringing headphones/earphones to each session is also strongly encouraged for the face-to-face sessions.
The first week’s workshop is particularly important, as a “boot camp” introduction to the Fluent CFD software package will be delivered in the first week – it is imperative that students not miss the first week of class (it will be difficult if not impossible to catch up if the session is missed – it will not be repeated). The video presented in the first week will naturally be the most comprehensive, as the software will be new to all students – in subsequent weeks only the new skills required for that week’s exercise will need to be introduced.
Students are strongly advised to maintain their own handwritten notebook summarizing the Fluent models developed in the workshop – there is considerable benefit in the act of handwriting notes (it is known to be superior to typing notes when it comes to recall), and the notebook will provide a quicker means of reviewing skills than re-watching videos. You will be able to use your notebook in the practical exam.
Given the mix of assessment and interactive activity, it is essential that students participate in the entire workshop every week – students who do not attend the entire workshop will miss assessments and are unlikely to be successful in the unit.

Assignments
During the course, the students will complete two major assignments. The first project will be a CFD project using ANSYS/Fluent, which students will be able to access on campus and remotely via a VDI. Students will be required to develop and execute a CFD model, determine whether the mesh is optional, undertake verification and validation of the model by comparing their results with experimental data, and address questions specific to the process or phenomenon being modelled.
The second project will deal with issues in multiphase flow, and will involve both qualitative questions relating to multiphase flow phenomena, and quantitative calculations relevant to the design of systems to transport multiphase flows.
Students will submit reports for each assignment (the form will vary according to the assignment) for assessment via the LMS. Assignments will be marked and returned via the LMS as marked up pdfs. The assignments will predominantly typed (incorporating various hardcopy results outputs), and the assignments must be submitted in a single pdf to LMS (scanning the written assignment into a pdf or exporting a word document into a pdf is the required approach). If multiple files are submitted, only the first file will be considered for assessment,
Note that professional presentation of assignment submissions is essential, and marks will be allocated for presentation quality in the marking rubric. In addition, deductions up to 5% will be applied if submissions have pages upside down, rotated, or out of order. Submissions that are partly or entirely illegible will only have the legible portions marked. Submissions must be in pdf form – a penalty of 5% will be applied to a non-pdf submission IF it can be converted to pdf form for marking (if it cannot be converted, it may not be possible to mark the assignment and a mark of zero will apply).
Practical Exam
In the workshop sessions in week 10, students will complete a practical exam, where students will develop and execute a CFD model in ANYSYS/Fluent (once again, the VDI will be used, so students need to bring their laptop to the exam). 3 hours and 45 minutes will be allotted for students to create the model, execute the model (or models), and perform the requested interpretation and presentation of the results. The model to be created will be revealed on the day of the exam. The exam may be completed on site during the face-to-face session, or online during the online class session – both sessions will employ the LMS for distributing the exam paper and receiving submissions.
The practical exam will be open book, and students will be permitted to access the internet – BUT the exam will be an individual exercise, and while students will be permitted to access the internet, they will not be able to communicate with other parties in any way during the exam. Any student caught emailing, texting or in any way communicating with another party during the exam (including forums or “tutoring”/cheating websites such as chegg.com) will be assigned a mark of zero for the practical exam.Lectures

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
