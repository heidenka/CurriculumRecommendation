Academic Information

Content This unit has been designed to allow students to critically reflect on challenging and pressing issues confronting Asian societies in an increasingly globalised world where different worldviews and value systems intersect and sometimes collide. The unit examines different approaches to the study of social transformations in contemporary Asia , and its interactions with the rest of the world. These may include topics such as environment, inequality, corruption, development, education, religion and human rights. The specialised knowledge acquired through the unit gives students a skill set and understanding to engage meaningfully with these key national and transnational issues in the Asian region. Students develop research skills and a critical capacity which they can utilise in a variety of professional contexts.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

This unit uses a flipped classroom strategy where students are prescribed preparatory work to do before class. The two hours a week we spend in seminars is used entirely for student focused activities.

The unit has 10 two hour seminars with a two week break between Weeks 5 and 7. The unit will be tught face to face, COVID restrictions allowing, but will also be available via remote learing for students unable to attend.

Referencing Requirements

The essay must be fully documented, using an appropriate academic referencing system. (Please check the university policy around ethical academic conduct.)
We recommend the use of APA-style references however I will accept any recognised system. Please note that you must cite your sources even when paraphrasing material.
When it comes to citing your sources it is better to err on the side of caution and use more citations - this way you cannot be accused of not referencing your sources adequately.
Please note whichever method of citation you select I expect you to use page numbers to indicate where material was taken from even when you are paraphrasing. So, (Author 2013,
p. 10) and not (Author 2013) at all times. The only time it is appropriate to use the name and date without the page number/s is if you are making a general statement about the content or argument of a source. At all other times USE PAGE NUMBERS.
Your essay must include a Reference List or Bibliography containing all the works you have used in writing the essay (A bibliography contains all the works you have examined whether you have used them or not - it is better to use a reference list for these essays).
Important: Research and writing up the results of your research is one of the primary skills that you will take from a University Education. There are no short cuts if you want good results. The expectation is that you will read widely around the topics you research and that you will not simply fall into the trap of believing that you can get everything you need from the Web. We expect your essays will be informed by reading academic texts and peer reviewed academic articles from journals.
The following practices will result in lower marks:
•	Do not cite Web sites with no academic credentials. Our advice, don't use them! Anybody can create a web site (I have a fantastic one devoted to quantum physics - not really but you see my point) if you cite "anybody's" web site you will weaken significantly your essay. There is no substitute for monographs and academic journals and these should be the underlying basis of your essay.
Do not cite from Encyclopedias. These types of works are useful starting points for getting some information however they are summaries of whatever issue you look up. Whilst they are usually written by experts in the particular field (I have contributed to a couple of these myself) they are to be seen as a starting point only. I do not consider the use of an encyclopedia and particularly large sections of essays dependent on this type of source to be appropriate in your essays.
•	Do not cite Wikipedia. Wikipedia is more problematic than most encyclopedias in that anyone can simply add to it and whilst I know there are some good materials there these always have to be double checked for accuracy. For the most part when I go to Wikipedia I find large amounts of other people's work simply cut and pasted in. I know this because I know the materials in those areas of interest to me.
•	Use newspapers and news journals appropriately. Newspapers and
news journals can provide interesting and useful information to include in your research. However, they should never be seen as the basis of an essay. They can provide some relevant examples around a particular issue or some "fact" or other related to a topic and this is how they should be used. Journalist are not required to provide citations and there is no peer review of these articles.
•	Do not misrepresent the works you are citing. I have become aware over the past several years of an increasing number of essays where sources/authors are being misrepresented. This means that you need to read more carefully and gain at least a basic understanding of the works you are citing.
•	If you are in any doubt as to the appropriateness of materials you are looking at then please ask one of the teaching staff about them. We are more than happy to help you with determining the quality and appropriateness of sources.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
