Academic Information

Content This unit equips students with the combination of theory, strategy and practical skills to lead and manage within healthcare organisations. The unit will review a variety of leadership frameworks. Students will learn effective leadership approaches to develop high performance teams, and creatively respond to the power and politics of health organisations through increased system awareness. The contemporary complexities and dilemmas of healthcare provision in a challenging and increasingly disrupted environment will be addressed. Students will demonstrate their leadership in real-world situations through a leadership project. Students will emerge with a deeper understanding of the theory and practice of authentic and transformational leadership with a stronger ability to articulate this in a new and evolving health environment.

Unit Information

"To lead is to live with danger. Although it may be exciting to think of leadership as inspiration, decisive action, and powerful rewards, leading requires taking risks that can jeopardize your career and your personal life. It requires putting yourself on the line, disturbing the status quo, and working with organisational and political conflicts. Those who choose to lead take the risks and sometimes are neutralised for doing so." Ronald A Heifitz. An essential skill of leadership in our time is the ability to work with people to tackle challenges and changes which are not only technical in nature but which require people (including ourselves) to change. Bob Anderson and William Adams have written that, "Leadership is the act of creating outcomes that matter most". 
We all balance the tension between progressing in our careers through keeping our heads down, versus advocating for what we want and what we believe is in the best interest of the organisation. Leadership is inherently risky as there is, "no safe way to be great, and, there is no great way to be safe". If we orient primarily to safety then we live and lead reactively. To lead is to live at the edge of our competence while orienting to our leadership purpose and vision. By exploring self-awareness, through deliberate practice of what you learn and through developing a personal leadership philosophy you will increase your ability to be less reactive in your leadership and better equipped to lead others in volatile, complex and disruptive environments. 
This unit has been designed to support you to take the next steps in your leadership journey including sharing your unique leadership stories and voice. You will have the opportunity to consider contemporary leadership practices and insights, while critically engaging with the challenges of effective leadership in healthcare organisations. You will be encouraged to challenge the ‘conventional wisdom’ of current leadership thought, and reflect on your personal perspective and experience in organisations. This includes the opportunity to explore real-world situations through the analysis of real life leadership challenges, case studies, including sharing knowledge of leadership practices within your own health vocation or organisation. 
The unit will review a variety of leadership frameworks with a particular emphasis on the work of James Kouzes and  Barry Posner - The Leadership Challenge and you will emerge with a deeper understanding of the theory and practice of authentic and transformational leadership and other approaches with a stronger ability to articulate this in an increasingly disrupted healthcare environment. The Unit has been structured to take you on a leadership journey to strengthen your self awareness and self mastery, your ability to lead others to obtain extraordinary results, team leadership and the impact of leadership on change and culture in organisations allowing you to respond more creatively to the power and politics of healthcare organisations.
Learning Outcomes:
 Students are able to:
 (1) Demonstrate thorough understanding of contemporary leadership frameworks and theories and the application of these to a healthcare organisation;
 (2) Develop personal understanding and self‐awareness of your leadership style, and how to develop new skills and techniques to support effective leadership skills;
(3) Develop the knowledge and skills for team leadership to create psychologically safe teams and deliver cross-organisational collaboration and teamwork; and
(4) Examine leadership practices in healthcare  organisations including understanding the impact of collective leadership approaches to developing leadership capacity and capability within organisations.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

The Unit will utilise a variety of learning opportunities during the times that we meet, including lecture, discussion, individual and group exercises, videos, student research and presentations. Productive participation and discussion requires that students come prepared for each class meeting to explore the assigned readings and case studies with their thoughtful critique and evaluation of the content, to be augmented by reflections on their own work experiences and additional reading as required. You are encouraged to share your own professional experience as it will enliven class discussions and lead to a better learning environment. For learning to occur when the class material is controversial, both tension and safety need to exist. There needs to be a safe classroom environment, so that students understand that they can speak their minds, and so that everyone feels comfortable and free to articulate ideas or viewpoints. Such a learning environment helps students to:
•	Develop their abilities to understand the perspectives of others;
•	Develop mutual respect;
•	Make a commitment to understand positions that they do not understand from the context or point of view of their peers;
•	Take risks in speaking honestly in the class in the service of their learning; and
•	Practice active listening skills, the ability to listen to understand rather than to speak and how to build on the ideas of others to extend the thinking.
Expected class norms to enhance a safe learning environment include:
•	Students should listen respectfully to different perspectives ‐ let people finish sentences before responding;
•	Students should respond to what has been said, not the person saying it ‐ responses should not be personalised;
•	Students should speak for themselves and not for others (including groups to which they belong);
•	Students have the right to be silent in large group discussions.
•	All classroom discourse will exhibit respect for all other persons, not only within the confines of the classroom but also in general. 
•	Students are expected to attend all classes and to participate and actively engage through making contributions to the discussions and activities.
•	 Respectful engagement with others includes resisting the checking of mobile phones or other electronic devices for messages, texting, and ensuring that your environment is conducive to your full attention to the online learning.

Unit Structure

Seminars are on Thursdays 6:00pm to 9:00pm throughout Trimester 3. Please check timetable on the UWA Business School website: http://www.business.uwa.edu.au/students/timetables
Leadership in Healthcare consists of four parts: (1) Leading Self (2) Leading Others (3) Leading Teams and (4) Leading Organisations.
In Leading Self, the unit aims to support your development so you can deal with challenges associated with 'leading oneself'. We draw upon a 'leadership from within' approach including the importance of self awareness and using mindful leadership to create a space to lead with clarity, focus, compassion and creativity. Students will focus on identifying leadership mindsets required for more effective leadership and will uncover self limiting beliefs while learning skills to transform these (Sessions One, Two and Four).
In Leading Others, we focus on Adaptive Leadership and the 'Five Practices and Ten Commitments' developed by Jim Kouzes and Barry Posner whose 35 years of research is included in the recommended textbook, The Leadership Challenge 6th Edition (Sessions Three, Five to Nine).
In Leading Teams we will explore the research on how to create psychological safety in teams and workplaces to deliver cross-organisational collaboration and teamwork (Session Ten).
In Leading Organisations, there is an emphasis on how to scale leadership culture across the organisation so that leadership development occurs at both the individual and collective level. There will be a particular focus on the impact of contemporary models of leadership such as boundary spanning and distributed leadership and how these can be used to break down silos and hierarchy in preparation for more distributed workplaces (Session Eleven).

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
