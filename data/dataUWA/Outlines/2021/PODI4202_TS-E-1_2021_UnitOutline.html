Academic Information

Content Students will be learning podiatric clinical skills via hands on training, practical classroom demonstrations, role plays and simulated patients. Students will learn how to conduct patient-centred interviews, history taking and presenting cases; perform biomechanical assessments of the lower extremity; learn how to diagnose musculoskeletal conditions affecting the lower leg and foot and develop the practical skill of modifying functional and over-the-counter foot orthoses; and learn how to perform general routine care. Students will begin to have patient contact half-way through the semester.

Unit Information

This unit is the first of the clinical units in the Doctor of Podiatric Medicine program. PODI4202 begins by introducing you to general aspects of podiatric medicine such as hand hygiene, sterile technique, non-touch technique, wound cleaning and dressing. Other essential topics which are covered under general podiatry is cultural orientation, professionalism, privacy and clinical records, and case presentations. All of which are to prepare you for your external observational placements. As you will be representing the Discipline and the University on these placements, it is essential that you understand the importance of professional conduct. The supervisors on these placements may be your potential employer when you graduate, this is especially important as you’ll soon learn the podiatric medical community in Western Australia is small.
Week 6 students will begin learning how to make an orthotic. This will occur in the orthotic laboratory at Park Avenue. All students must undergo a competency assessment for the equipment in the laboratory, prior to the first laboratory. Students are expected to familiarise themselves with the relevant OHS for the orthotic laboratory, in addition to reviewing the standard operating procedures for the orthotic laboratory equipment under the OHS folder on the Unit contents section.
You will also learn musculoskeletal podiatry which covers orthopaedic examinations of the Hip, Knee, Ankle and Foot. In additional specialised tests for the foot and ankle, and examination of reflexes, gait and coordination.

Participation

Attendance at lectures is expected and is compulsory for laboratory, tutorials and clinical placements. Attendance will be taken at these compulsory sessions. Students must ensure they have signed in for the teaching sessions where roll is kept. It is not the responsibility of the tutor or demonstrator. You will be considered to be absent if you have not signed in for the session. Students can only miss 1 compulsory session during the semester without a medical certificate. In the case of illness, a medical certificate (not provided by a family member) must be supplied to the unit coordinator within one week of return to classes. Any missed tasks will need to be completed by this time. Note that the onus is on the student to make contact regarding absences. If a student misses more than this, he/she will be expected to contact the unit coordinator with an explanation. A failure to provide a valid explanation, or further missed classes will result in action according to the FHMS Professional Behaviour Assessment.
Lateness: Students who are consistently late to teaching sessions and clinics (more than 10 minutes) will be marked as late and an explanation will be sought from the student. Your fellow students and teachers appreciate punctuality which is part of professional behaviour and courtesy. Multiple late arrivals will be managed according to the FHMS Professional Behaviour Assessment. Late arrival at lectures and clinical laboratories is also discourteous and unprofessional. If you are unavoidably late, it is important that you do not enter a lecture theatre by the front entrance.
It is critical that where a student is assigned to attend a particular session at a particular time, only that session may be attended. Students are not to attend non-assigned sessions for their convenience. Students doing so will be subject to the FHMS Professional Behaviour Assessment process. Session times are allocated based on a random group allocation for laboratory/tutorial sessions.
If you’re unable to attend the clinical or orthotic laboratories or if sick, please email Amelia Na in a timely manner.
If you’re unable to attend your clinical placement, please email Hannah Chun, Amelia Na and your placement lead in a timely manner.
Please do not leave that responsibility to a class mate. We expect professional behaviour from students at all times.

Dress Code for Clinical Placements, Student Clinics and Simulated Patient Sessions
Podiatry students must present professionally at all times. Whilst on clinical placements and clinical settings, smart, professional attire is required.
Students must wear trousers (shorts/skirts are not permitted). Arms should be bare above the wrist. Students who need to wear long sleeves for cultural or other reasons can wear them however long sleeves will be required to be pushed up for hand washing and in situations of possible direct patient contact and contamination.
Infection control standards require the clinical clothes be washed after each wear.
Hair must be tied back where possible.
Students must wear a name badge in all clinical settings including student clinic and external placements.
Jeans, shiny leg, skinny leg, leggings, track pants, no causal T-shirts and baseball caps are NOT to be worn.
NO part of the trunk is to be exposed.
Revealing clothing should not be worn. No shear clothing. No clothing with cut-out parts.
Sports clothing should also not be worn.
No visibility of bra straps.
Tattoos must be covered.
No chewing gum.
Footwear: For safety, only fully enclosed non slip shoes are to be worn in the Orthotic Laboratory and all Clinics. High-heeled shoes, stilettos, sandals, thongs, joggers and other types of casual footwear are unacceptable. 
Late submission of Assignments
A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.
For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).
If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.
If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;
Penalty for exceeding word limit
Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
