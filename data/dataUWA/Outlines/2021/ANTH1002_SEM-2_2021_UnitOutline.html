Unit Information

What does globalisation mean at local levels? In the face of so much 'progress', why are many people still so poor and others so rich? In these extraordinary times, this unit allows us to contemplate some of the key issues of our time - racism, gender and class inequality, environmental damage, to name but four. This unit explores how people's lives have been transformed by colonialism, economic development and change, as well of course by globalisation itself. With case studies drawn from around the world, including Australia and our region, this unit examines various social science perspectives on inequality, migration, urbanisation, work, modernisation, culture, communication and the environment.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

We are living through extraordinary times, a period when face to face lectures have been "onlined" and learning and teaching in many universities across the globe is requiring unprecedented levels of online engagement. Fortunately, we have been able to conduct F2F tutorials over recent months, allowing many students more immediate contact with peers and teachers - at least for the time being.
The extensive use of online facilities is not new to this particular unit. Focused very much on active learning, inviting you to participate as fully as possible in the unit, Anth1002 is taught in "flipped mode". Essentially this means placing the unit material into your Learning Managment System (LMS) opening up spaces for informed exchange between students and tutors, primarily through tutorials, but also in a workshop that will be broadcast live online and facilitated by the unit coordinator - Assoc Prof Martin Forsey. This year we are also piloting the use of Cadmos an online platform aimed at supporting learning through engaged forms of tutorial preparation and reflection.
The tutorials are designed to test out ideas, reinforce knowledge, and to raise important questions - opening up spaces for you to engage as actively as you can with the unit material with and through your peers, under the experienced, expert guidance of your tutors.
Research shows flipped approaches to teaching to be highly effective form of teaching - mainly because it is so strongly focused on how students learn. This same research shows that, providing expectations for the unit are clear and the learning processes are well designed, most students respond favourably to flipped learning. Vital to this learning in the social sciences, is the provision of opportunities for students to engage with the unit material in a careful, considered manner, with regular opportunities to test out the learning and to reinforce the knowledge gained in consultation with fellow students and the teachers.

Unit Structure

Unit material will be delivered via your LMS (Blackboard) on a weekly basis
A weekly online Workshop delivered via Zoom
A weekly tutorial - supported by Cadmus (an online tutorial engagement system through which you are encouraged to respond to key questions and points of reflection on unit material)
Weekly modules will typically contain some short video lectures, some reading, questions aimed at thinking through key matters and issues associated with the unit material.
The weekly workshop is open to the whole class in the time usually allocated for lectures - you are strongly encouraged to attend. Typically they will involve some input from the unit coordinator but will mainly focus on reinforcing the learning from the week through various forms of interactive engagement involving, among other things, the use of quiz questions, small group discussion, short writing exercises, moments of reflection and other forms of engagement with key concepts and ideas for the unit.
The tutorials are designed as active learning spaces consolidating the ideas of the unit and providing opportunities for you to sharpen varying skills associated with analysis, social research, writing, oral presentation and collaborative learning. You are expected to attend the eleven weekly one-hour tutorials, and to prepare for these by accessing the online content, reading the assigned articles and textbook chapters, and completing the weekly tutorial activities that will be presented through Cadmus. Consistent with normal expectations of a unit at UWA involving 120 hours of work from you, this preparation may take up to eight hours per week. Tutorial participation and completion of the tutorial worksheets form an important part of learning and assessment.
The use of regular quizzes and reflective writing exercises invites you to identify and gauge your level of understanding of the concepts and ideas on a regular basis. These will be further consolidated in the tutorial/workshops, for which students will usually be required to conduct some research into key ideas, concepts, and case studies.

Assessment - further notes
PARTICIPATION in addition to assessing your participation in the unit, your grasp of key concepts and ideas will be tested through the use of online quizzes administered regularly through the LMS. you are expected to complete three of these online quizzes, the scores for which will account for 6 of the 25 marks allocated to your participation grade. The remaining marks will reflect the level of participation in the tutorial class which will in part be measured by your ongoing commitment to reflecting upon the key ideas of the unit through the alloted worksheets. 
You will be notified of the availability of the quizzes on the week they are to be administered. 
MAJOR ESSAY - a list of essay topics will be made available early in the semester. They are designed to allow you to reflect more deeply on one or more of the weekly topics offered in the first nine weeks of the unit. 
UNIT EXAMINATION - an examination will be administered in the exam week following completion of the unit. The exam will be in two parts: PART A - comprising 30 mulitiple choice questions; PART B - a piece of writingusing sociological and anthropological lenses as a means of communicating your understanding of one of the key social issues of our time - Black Lives Matter, Climate Change, Contemporary Femimism and/or the social effects of the Covid19 pandemic. It will administered through Cadmus which will also be utilised to help you prepare for the examination essay through a series of exercises aimed at scalfoding your approach to preparing to writing the essay. 

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
