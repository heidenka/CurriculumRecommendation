Academic Information

Content This unit focuses upon the practices and concepts surrounding digital media. It equips students with core professional skills in media production and develops their understanding of scholarly perspectives on the uses of digital media technologies in contemporary culture. Through the combination of a hands-on approach to media practice and a theoretical exploration of the key concepts surrounding it, this unit offers students creative and critical engagement with digital media forms.

Unit Information

Established and emerging technologies along with the convergence and decentralisation of media production and distribution has seen an explosion of pervasive forms of multimodal communication across many fields and sectors. This unit introduces students to core elements in multimodal literacy and practices, particularly in relation to digital story production, and examines how cultural knowledge and meaning is produced and negotiated in these increasingly collaborative texts. Through a combination of theory and practical activities, primarily using digital video equipment and software, this unit offers students an understanding of the communicative techniques involved in creating compelling digital narratives and the technical, creative and collaborative skills required to communicate effectively in rapidly evolving Media and Communications industries.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

This unit is delivered in a blended f2f and online multimodal learning style in which students engage with a variety of communication forms - oral, digital, written, online - to encourage them to articulate understandings of the media, language, concepts and practices of their chosen field, and of themselves as students.
Most digital media production is collaborative. In order to model industry processes and due to the practical requirements of shooting and editing digital media, a large weighting of the assessment work in this unit involves working in a group. It is therefore essential that students work effectively as team members via face to face and online meetings.
Groups are strongly encouraged to exchange reliable contact information with each other as early as possible, and to maintain regular contact throughout the semester. It is each group’s responsibility to effectively organise itself. If serious problems arise students should contact their tutor immediately, but should make every effort to plan carefully to avoid difficulties wherever possible. 

Unit Structure

There are three different kinds of classes that students are required to participate in to support their successful completion of the assessment tasks. A detailed Unit Schedule is available on LMS:
Lectures (2 hours - online only) will provide students with detailed information and examples to understand the theories and processes involved in digital storytelling, including its conceptual, creative, organisational, technical, and communicative elements. Lectures will also address all relevant information regarding the assessment requirements and the assessment criteria
Workshops (1 hour - f2f only): workshops will act as a practical forum where student groups will meet for consultations and liaise closely with their tutor who will inform, support and guide the progress of their work. Workshops are also an opportunity for students to discuss wider issues they have found interesting or difficult to understand. Due to COVID-19 restrictions, the delivery mode of workshops may change so students are required to regularly check unit announcements and Unit Schedule updates.
Demonstrations (2 hours - f2f): demos will involve technical instruction relating to digital video production and editing software. Due to COVID-19, the delivery mode of demos may change so students are required to regularly check unit announcements and Unit Schedule updates.
Meetings(f2f & online): students are expected to do further preparation, and regularly meet outside of scheduled classes with group members to work on their assessments.

Important Information
Teamwork and group assignments
To model the collaborative way that digital media is produced in various creative media industries, a large proportion of the work and assessment in this unit involves working in teams. Teams are strongly advised to exchange reliable contact information with each other as early as possible, maintain continuous contact throughout the semester. Organising yourselves professionally and effectively is the team’s responsibility. If serious teamwork related problems arise, you should contact your tutor immediately, but students should make every effort to communicate with their teams and plan and execute their productions efficiently to avoid difficulties wherever possible.
Copyright
The production, distribution and use of media content are subject to Intellectual Property and Copyright laws. In your Digital Story project, you are allowed to use audio-visual material created by people other than yourself/your team (this material must be appropriately credited) under the following conditions:
 - audio: you can use an unlimited amount of sound, music and sound effects which are in the public domain, or under creative commons licence; or, if you want to use material under copyright you must obtain written permission from the rights holder. All such material must be appropriately credited.
 - video/image: you can use a very limited amount of any image or video (a matter of a few seconds on screen; you must consult with your tutor), but you can use this material only if it is in the public domain, or under creative commons licence, or, it is under copyright, but you obtain written permission from the rights holder. All such material must be appropriately credited.
If groups fail to meet these copyright rules, the Digital Story can only be used/screened in an educational setting.
Contacting academic & ADL staff
You must use your UWA student email for all university correspondence. Please also include your class day/time in the email header.
Questions related to thematic elements or the quality of your project must be addressed to the Tutor or Unit Coordinator. This includes things such as:
 - narrative, script/dialogue, direction, plot development, character development, timing/flow/pace of film, elements of story that could be deleted, elements of story that need to be added, location and setting, casting, mise-en-scene (props, wardrobe, makeup, composition, performance, lighting), genre-specific tropes.
The ADL staff are available to answer any questions you have regarding the technical aspects of filming and editing only. This includes things such as:
- filming techniques, the use of filming equipment, cutting, colour grading, syncing audio and video, adding and fading music, adding sound effects, adding special effects, adding titles.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
