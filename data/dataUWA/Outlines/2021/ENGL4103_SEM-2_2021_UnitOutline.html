Teaching contingency plan (In response to Covid19 restrictions)

Should we be required to go into full or partial lockdown this semester, it’s important that we have a clear plan for how to proceed with our unit. The particulars of our seminar mean that continuing some form of face-to-face delivery is important, whether this occurs on campus or via Blackboard Collaborate. Some partial lockdown models will allow us to continue meeting on campus, given the size of our assigned classroom. Full lockdown will require us to shift to online modes of delivery, specifically Blackboard Collaborate. I will communicate as best I can with you all, and, should any form of lockdown be enforced, you will have the option to work from isolation should you wish.

It’s clear that these aren’t usual times, and that we may need to be flexible at various points during the semester. Please know that I am committed to this course, and to maintaining the necessary closeness and collegiality of our seminar. Though this will, of course, require your own good will and commitment too! If you have any worries or queries, or would just like clarification on any of the above, please don’t hesitate to get in touch with me (daniel.juckes@uwa.edu.au). There are some instructions for how to access Collaborate in the ‘“How To” Resources for LMS’ section of the LMS page, but, should I need to make any changes to how course content is delivered, I will do my utmost to communicate the best ways for us to keep learning and working together.

Unit Information

This unit introduces students to approaches to creative writing as a research endeavour and discipline. It examines various facets of the discipline (including practice-led research and exegetical approaches), and considers the intersections between theory in creative writing and literary studies.
The unit is designed for students to consider the research possibilities of creative practice through a folio project. It offers the opportunity to work on a project of substantial creative writing in any form. Students will also submit writing for workshop and read a selection of contemporary research and creative works in order to develop an advanced sense of the possibilities within current literary practice.
Engaging with writing practice through the folio project, writers in the class will be supported in developing creative skills. All substantial manuscripts, even the most apparently effortless, are the product of personal fascination with a subject, careful planning, drafing, re-assessment, and revision. At the same time the concerns of potential readers, or in the case of scripts potential viewers, must be taken into account. Fascination with a subject is comparatively easy. Drafing and re-drafing may be demanding. The process can be quite isolating and daunting. If the writer is lucky this reassessment is inspired by the advice of astute readers.
Our class will perform the role of astute readers of one another’s work. At the same time, we will be considering creative practice more generally. We will be discussing questions of creative methodology, both on a conceptual level and in the context of the work of established writers. We will analyse creative work for narrative technique and linguistic structure. How might an experienced writer manage research imperatives within an engaging narrative? How far can we push an experimental approach? How might an adept writer bend the rules of continuity within a narrative? How might a writer position a political or socio-cultural message within their work? What can be learned from other writers, within and outside the class? In this course we will be sharing our reading experiences, as well as our writing, in order to develop sustained creative work.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

All teaching contact is via weekly seminars (two hours), which incorporate workshopping and creative exercises alongside theoretical discussions. I expect all students to work with me in this space to develop a workshopping environment based on trust and familiarity with each other’s projects, approach, and creative thinking. The seminars will be supported by the provision of readings and tools on the LMS to facilitate the easy sharing of work and feedback.

Unit Structure

Teaching in the unit is weekly, with seminars commencing in Week One. A hiatus in Week Two will allow for preparation for the first workshop in Week Three, when seminars resume.
We will develop a detailed schedule of workshop presentations in the first seminar (Week One), allocating a week to each participant. Workshops will also make use of material provided via the LMS and Unit Readings, as a support to both conversation and presentations. Students are expected to read the week’s material before each workshop. Failure to do so will be reflected in your final grade for participation.

Participation

Assessment of participation will be spread across two outcomes: active engagement during seminars and the completion of related seminar activities in the LMS.
It is expected that students will miss no more than two seminars over the course of the unit. Any more (without special consideration being approved) will result in an automatic fail being awarded for the participation component of assessment (30%).
Should any students experience difficulties with participating online, an alternate method for the assessment of participation can be established, based on the completion of the related seminar activities. 

Referencing Requirements

All sources cited (creative, critical or popular) must be referenced. Students are expected to use the MLA style of referencing for all submitted work. A guide to the MLA style can be found here (http://guides.library.uwa.edu.au/friendly.php?s=mla). All assignments will be submitted electronically by LMS. All other aspects of academic policy are to be followed as per University guidelines.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
