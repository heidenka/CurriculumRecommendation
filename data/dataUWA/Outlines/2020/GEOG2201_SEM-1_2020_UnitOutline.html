Introduction
Everything on the earth exists in some place and time. Location is an important part of understanding the nature of a phenomenon and its relationships with other aspects of a system. Geographic Information Systems (GIS) are now widely accepted as a technology for examining objects and events in space and time, and spatial analysis is an important skill which is required in many disciplines. This unit is designed to introduce students to the fundamentals of spatial analysis and the application of GIS through lectures and practical problem solving in labs exercises.
Goal
This unit provides an introduction to the fundamentals of spatial analysis and the application of GIS to examining spatial phenomena and spatial relationships in a real world context. Applied examples will cover a range of human, physical and environmental geography. Students will be learning about geospatial science through interactive discussion seminars and will build fundamental GIS skills through using QGIS (a desktop platform).
Broad Learning outcomes
Students are able to (1) recognize different types of spatial data and identify how geographic information is related through space and time using core GIS concepts such as interactivity and interdependency; (2) operate QGIS software to analyse different types of geospatial data; (3) apply a range of spatial processing techniques, including the use of spatial statistics, to assess both vector and raster datasets; and (4) communicate geospatial information using clear and informative mapping approaches that follow fundamental principles of cartography.
Unit-specific prerequisites
There are no prerequisites for this unit. However, a basic understanding and interest in Geography as a broad topic is presumed for all students undertaking this unit. Students are not expected to have used GIS before, and whilst the unit uses spatial statistics, students do not need previous experience in this field. A strong interest and patience in learning new computer software skills, is however necessary.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

All classes for this unit are conducted in person. Materials which are provided online are on LMS to support the class content. Tutors with high level GIS experience will be available to support the teaching coordinator in computer lab sessions. For students at Albany campus, you will undertake your seminar and computer lab during your extended lab session with a highly experienced GIS tutor.
Expectations of students
Students are expected to (i) Attend all seminar and lab sessions; (ii) Prepare well and contribute to class discussions; (iii) Complete a total of 150 learning hours; (iv) Complete all unit assessment to a professional academic standard; and (v) Reflect well on formative feedback to enhance your learning.
Expectations of teaching staff
Teaching staff are expected to (i) Provide you with all information needed for unit completion; (ii) Guide your learning with their expertise in GIS; (iii) Help you develop key GIS technical skills and knowledge; (iv) Welcome your questions, ideas and suggestions; (v) Provide clear formative feedback on unit assessment; and (vi) Make you think, be critical, and learn about a new subject!

Seminars
One one-hour seminar session per week. Attendance is compulsory.  These classes will comprise of interactive discussions for which you are expected to prepare (read the assigned texts, complete tasks and watch podcasts) in advance and actively participate. You can find an overview for the seminar content you will need to complete on LMS.
Computer labs
One two-hour computer lab per week. Attendance is compulsory. Labs are allocated through OLCR and will be filled up according to total student numbers registered for the unit. The help lab sessions for catch-up / assignment sessions are not compulsory.

Participation

All classes are compulsory and full participation is expected. You will have to be prepared for this unit and undertake the assigned seminar preparation PRIOR to each seminar, coming prepared with your notes that answer the tasks assigned (see LMS for full details).

Referencing Requirements

It is expected for you to undertake reading and reference academic literature in your assignments. Please ensure you follow the correct format to achieve this and use the Harvard referencing style. Use this library guide to help you with correct formatting for in-text citations and your reference list.

Texts

Readings for this unit are listed downloadable unit outline on LMS are are available via the UWA Library Reading List. Search for GEOG2201 and you will see two folders are provided - one with the compulsory tutorial readings, and the other with optional readings.  Feel free to supplement your learning with additional reading resources – use your own initiative to develop your knowledge further to help develop a deep understanding of GIS. In most journals within your discipline (geography, agriculture, zoology, human movement, etc.) you can find a number of articles using GIS. However, there are also a number of journals that deal specifically with the science and technology of GIS, the most notable being the International Journal of Geographical Information Science. Although not necessary, if you want to purchase a GIS introductory text, then we recommend the following book:
Heywood et al. 2011. An Introduction to Geographic Information Systems (4th Edition). Prentice Hall

Other

Basic computer literacy is necessary to undertake this unit. Students will be using GIS software throughout the unit and as such engaging with computer skills. You need to have patience and a learning drive to use computer software and acquire new technical mapping skills.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
