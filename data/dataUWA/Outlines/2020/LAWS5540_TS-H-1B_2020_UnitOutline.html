Academic Information

Content This unit examines key concepts in environmental protection law in Western Australia, in its national and international contexts. It focuses on the law relating to pollution control and environmental impact assessment. The unit analyses a range of legal techniques and principles applied to environmental protection through six key themes: (1) the key definitions, institutions and objectives, (2) policy and information instruments, (3) controls on particular activities, (4) environmental impact assessment, (5) criminal liabilities, and (6) civil liabilities.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

The circumstances of 2020 require some adaptation of our established learning and teaching strategy. In previous years, this has comprised three stages: (1) pre-class reading and assessment exercises; (2) primary class contact by attendance at an intensive workshop over three days addressing the six themes (or topics) identified in the Unit Information; and (3) a post-class research paper.  
In 2020, stages 1 and 3 will be largely the same but we will not hold the stage 2 intensive workshop, scheduled for the last week of May. Instead, class delivery will be entirely on-line and conducted over a longer period during May. Recorded lectures for each topic will become available during the first three weeks of May. In the week commencing Monday 25 May, we will have a series of shorter interactive on-line sessions, including some sessions with expert guest contributors. The Unit Schedule below indicates the approximate timing of the interactive on-line sessions. There is more information below and more details will be on the LMS site by 27 April. 
For now, here is the broad outline of the three stages of the learning and teaching strategy.  In the context of 2020, "pre-class" means before the on-line interactive sessions in the week beginning 25 May. 
(1) The pre-class reading and assessment exercises will be available on the LMS site by Monday 27 April. We assume no prior knowledge of Environmental Protection Law to enrol, so it is  invaluable for you to read some core materials, including important legislation, and to learn about central concepts in Environmental Protection Law; concepts such as "emission", "pollution" and "environmental harm" and the principles of "ecologically sustainable development".   The pre-class assignments will also introduce  you to some of the essential elements of Environmental Protection Law.  You should read selectively, according to your interest in the relevant material, sufficient to enable you to complete the pre-class assessment exercises.
(2) The on-line material will include some recorded lectures uploaded for independent review before and during the last week of May, and some interactive on-line discussion exercises and panel sessions with guest contributors (public and private sector experts) in the week commencing Monday 25 May. The aim of the on-line lectures and interactive on-line sessions is to explore the key legal materials and concepts pertaining to each of the six topics by working through the reading guides and reading materials pertinent to teach topic, including key legislation; such as, the Environment Protection Act 1986 (WA) with the Environmental Protection Regulations 1987, and the Environment Protection and Biodiversity Conservation Act 1999 (Cth).  The reading guides and reading materials are arranged so as to link references to the legislation and case law with the headings on each topic. There will also be private and public sector non-degree registrants participating in the interactive sessions; they often bring unique practical experience and understanding to the operation of the legislation.
(3) The post-class research paper is the opportunity for you to build on the knowledge gained through stages (1) and (2) by selecting a problem or question that you wish to research.  You will be asked to submit a research proposal within 10 days after stage 2 finishes (by Monday 8 June). In light of feedback on the proposal by Monday 15 June, you should complete and submit your research paper by the due date, 13 July (see assessment information below).  In proposing and preparing your research paper, you are encouraged to aim for publication following the completion of the unit assessment.  Publication options can be discussed with the Unit Coordinator, especially after completion of the assessment. 
Note that there will be continuing professional development points available for participation in each of the sessions addressing the six topics.

Unit Structure

The unit is structured in three parts around the three key elements of Environmental Protection Law and the six topic themes:
Part 1 - Environmental Governance: covering Topic 1 on the key definitions, institutions and objectives of Environmental Protection Law;
Part 2 - Environmental Regulation: covering Topic 2 on the policy and information instruments for managing cumulative harm, Topic 3 on the controls on particular activities causing pollution and environmental harm, and Topic 4 on environmental impact assessment; and
Part 3 - Environmental Liabilities: covering Topic 5 on criminal liabilities and Topic 6 on civil liabilities for causing pollution and environmental harm.

Referencing Requirements

There is a strong preference for you to apply the Australian Guide to Legal Citation, edition 4. There is a succinct summary of AGLC4 provided by the UWA Library: http://guides.library.uwa.edu.au/AGLC4. If you believe that you cannot master the AGLC4 footnoting and referencing techniques, you may use the Harvard system of in-text references and bibliography so long as you also give a pinpoint page or paragraph citation for the sources of propositions referred to or quoted in your case note and research essay.

Texts

A reader designed for the unit will be available through the Learning Management System. The main legislation we study is: Environment Protection and Biodiversity Conservation Act 1999 (Cwlth),  National Environment Protection Council Act 1994 (Cwlth), Environmental Protection Act 1986 (WA), Environmental Protection Regulations 1987 (WA), and the Contaminated Sites Act 2003 (WA).

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
