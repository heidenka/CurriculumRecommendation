Academic Information

Content This unit develops students' knowledge of geotechnical engineering and explores the application of geomechanics principles to the solution of problems related to shallow foundations, slopes, embankments, retaining walls and ground improvement. Students learn how the mechanical characteristics of soils, as inferred from laboratory and in situ tests, can be applied in geotechnical design. Topics include site and soil characterisation, laboratory tests in soils, bearing capacity, earth pressure theory, retaining wall design, road cuttings and embankment design, ground improvement and slope stability.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

The course aims to equip students with the skills entailed in the application of the principles of geomechanics to the solution of commonly encountered problems in geotechnical engineering. Students apply material covered in lectures in the practice sessions. There are also a large number of example applications covered at lectures and further examples are posted on-line. Laboratories are conducted in small groups and provide hands-on experience to improve understanding of earth pressure theory and bearing capacity. The reports written by individual students on their laboratories provide much needed experience in report writing (which is an essential skill for all engineers).
On satisfactory completion of the unit, students will:
•	be proficient in the application of soil mechanics principles to the solution of engineering problems related to the stability of foundations, retaining walls and slopes;
•	have gained experience in interpreting experimental data and writing a corresponding engineering report;
•	have improved interpersonal skills by working in groups;
•	be in a position to extend soil mechanics principles to the solution of previously unpractised geotechnical problems;
•	be capable of interpreting  geotechnical laboratory and field ground investigation techniques; 
•	be able to apply new solution techniques, including bound theorems and the finite difference method; 
•	have developed increased awareness of construction practices; 
•	be aware of accuracy requirements and limitations in Geomechanics;
•	understand the role of a geotechnical engineer within the general construction industry


Unit Structure

Lectures held on Mondays and Wednesdays will vary in length from 45mins to 90mins; all are recorded.
Many lectures are devoted to ‘example applications’ and problem solutions for the main course material
The first 30mins of assessed sessions (4 No.) is compulsory and students are required to attend their designated venue. 
Allocated ‘Free’ sessions may be used for additional lectures and practice sessions. Students will be provided with at least 1 week’s notice if a change is made.
Students attend one session for Lab1 and one session for Lab2; these sessions must be pre-booked by all students.
Specific Course Content
1.	INTRODUCTION & BASIC SOIL MECHANICS (Weeks 1 & 2)
Overview of course and its applications in geotechnical engineering
Soil characterisation
Overview of strength and stiffness of geomaterials
2.	SOIL PROPERTIES FROM LABORATORY TESTING (Week 3)
Shear box, triaxial, 1-D compression, ring shear 
Interpretation of soil strength
3.	EMBEDDED RETAINING WALLS (Weeks 4 & 5)
Rankine and Coulomb earth pressure theory
Influence of wall friction, wall verticality and backfill slope
Treatment of water seepage
	Cantilever and propped walls
4.	BEARING CAPACITY THEORY (Weeks 6 & 7)
Upper and lower bound theorems + applications
Limit equilibrium
Bound theorem applications
General bearing capacity formulations
	Gravity retaining walls
5.	MID-SEMESTER EXAM (Week 8)
6.	SLOPE STABILITY (Weeks 9 & 10)
Translational slips
Circular slips
Slope stabilisation
7.	GROUND IMPROVEMENT, SETTLEMENT PREDICTION & GROUND INVESTIGATIONS (Weeks 11 & 12)
Finite difference solution for consolidation
Improvement by surcharging, reinforcement, drains
Elastic settlement prediction
Borehole and in-situ testing ground investigation

Participation

It is essential that students prepare in advance for all sessions, including listening to pre-recorded lectures. 
Attendance at the four assessed in-class practice sessions is compulsory (see schedule on Unit structure above)
Attendance at the ‘non-assessed in-class practice sessions’ is recommended but not compulsory. Students should attempt to complete these example problems with the assistance of the facilitators.

Students are allocated specific time slots for their labs; changes to this slot may be made by emailing Lisa.Larkin@uwa.edu.au or calling to the admin office on the 2nd floor in the Civil & Mech Bldg.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
