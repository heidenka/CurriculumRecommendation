Unit Information

Welcome to this year’s unit on human and organisational factors in managing work health and safety. This unit is coordinated and taught by Dr Lisette Kanse, whose background is in human performance management, specialising in human and organisational factors and work design characteristics that influence people’s work practices and performance in regard to productivity, reliability, safety, and health.
This unit offers you an organisational psychology perspective on managing safety, health and well-being at work, not only at the individual level but also at the work team and organisational levels. Insights in human performance management and organisational behaviour are invaluable for a role in safety management. As you will discover in this unit, many of the techniques used in safety management stem from the behavioural sciences, with a safety twist put on them.
Insights from the domain of industrial and organisational psychology, in particular what is known regarding behaviour, cognition, perception, and factors that influence these, can make, and have made, major contributions to work health and safety.
A range of topics is covered, including human error types and mechanisms, human and organisational factors that influence individual and team performance; well-being and mental health; near misses and threat and error management; safety culture, climate and the impact of leadership styles and behaviour; organising for high reliability/resilience; task analysis and procedure development/compliance; behaviour-based safety; and effective, personality based safety communications.
Learning outcomes:
Students are able to (1) explore a wide range of behaviour, cognition or perception-related factors that have been found to influence work health and safety; (2) become familiar with ways to analyse and manage these factors; (3) decide which human/organisational factors-based work health and safety approach suits which organisation/situation; and (4) demonstrate their understanding of how to implement these human/organisational factors-based approaches.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

Educational principles:
In this unit, you will be encouraged and facilitated to develop the ability and desire to:
• Critically interpret scientific publications relating to the topics addressed in the unit;
• Contribute to group discussions about these topics and publications;
• Apply some of the methods and techniques outlined during the unit;
• Work in team settings as well as on your own;
• Clearly and convincingly present your findings and ideas in oral and written form.
Teaching and learning strategies:
We use a flipped classroom approach in this unit, where students are expected to work through learning content on LMS every week to prepare for the interactive workshop-style class session in that week. The preparation involves reading scientific publications relevant to the topics addressed in this unit, watching video materials/lecture recordings, and completing online quizzes. This preparation enables us to run the class sessions in workshop-style, focussing on practising selected methods and techniques in various case studies. Such practising takes place either individually or in small teams – the latter especially for those techniques that benefit from team input. Students will be given feedback regarding their learning as part of the class exercises and discussions.  Occasionally we will have a guest speaker to talk to our class about his or her first-hand experience with developments in the field concerning the week’s topic and to participate in discussions. The weekly workshop-style class sessions are delivered twice: once via a virtual meeting for the online delivery mode, and once in a physical classroom setting for the face-to-face delivery mode.
All the in class case studies, the individual case study analysis assignment, and the work health and safety management initiative team assignment concern application of what you learn in this unit to real or realistic cases. For the team assignment, students can choose from a variety of cases suggested or provided by the lecturer. Alternative suggestions by students themselves are welcome too, for example if you can draw upon your own work experience, but please make sure it is suitable by discussing it with the lecturer first. The aim is to tailor your learning experience to suit your own background and/or interests.
Most activities are scheduled during regular class times, although groups may require additional out-of-class meetings to co-ordinate their team’s class presentation. Students are invited to provide feedback on each other’s presentations and will play a role in marking their peers.
Students are encouraged to read widely beyond the required reading, and attend classes well prepared to join in discussions. Next to the required reading, references will be given to recommended further reading materials, websites, and so on.
If you have a question regarding a topic relevant to this unit, feel free to ask. If I don’t have an immediate answer, I will strive to get back to you with a response by the next session.

Use of student feedback:
I am keen to obtain your feedback regarding any concerns you have about the unit, about aspects you felt were lacking, as well as parts you particularly liked. Over the past few years such feedback has been very useful for further development of the unit. Over the last years, some major revisions have been done; some as a result of changes in UWA assessment policy, some based on recent research undertaken and other current developments in areas relevant for this unit, and some to reduce overlap with other units. My aim is to continue to improve the unit, its contents, and how they are delivered, so please feel free to send me any comments and suggestions you may have, via e-mail, LMS, or in a face-to-face discussion, any time. Feedback early on in the semester can even help to shape the remaining part of the unit. Your input is much appreciated.

Preparation work for interactive class sessions:
You will get the most out of the interactive class sessions if you come prepared. For most sessions this means you should have studied the required readings and any other materials provided, as well as completed the online quiz, prior to coming to class.

In-class discussions:
There will be in-class discussions every session about the required readings, if any, and other matters relating to that week’s topic. Please come to class prepared to participate in those discussions. This is your opportunity to make the topic more relevant to you.

Workshop-style practice opportunities:
Wherever possible and applicable, time during the class sessions will be dedicated to practising the application of methods and techniques presented in this unit.

Student presentations:
The team assignment for this unit involves presentations by all the students. The class audience is encouraged to provide feedback to those presenting and will be involved in rating the presentations.
During class sessions where exercises are done in smaller groups, and/or where small group discussions are held, students are expected to take turns in presenting their group’s findings to the entire class. Note that such presentations are informal and ad hoc – no need to prepare slides or rehearse speaking notes. This is an opportunity to practise your skills in providing clear, verbal summaries that reflect your group’s learning, as well as an opportunity for feedback.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "honest, ethical and responsible scholarship". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.

Special Considerations

If your study has been adversely affected by illness or other significant circumstances outside of your control, you can apply for special consideration. Significant circumstances may include but are not limited to:

  • serious illness or death of a member of the student's immediate family or household or of a close friend
  • serious injury
  • being a victim of a crime
  • breakdown of relationship
  • sudden loss of income or employment
  • serious disruption to domestic arrangements

The full policy and information on applying for special consideration is available at in the UWA Policy Library

Compulsory online modules

UWA has a wealth of resources available to support your learning. These online modules are compulsory for many students:

  • Academic Conduct Essentials (ACE) introduces you to the basic issues of ethical scholarship and the expectations of correct academic conduct at university. All students are required to complete ACE in the first semester of their course.
  • Communication and Research Skills (CARS) aims to assist you to develop communication and research skills in an academic context and is compulsory for all new coursework students.
  • Indigenous Study Studies Essentials (INDG1000) introduces you to the shared learning space that UWA embodies. This learning space includes both western and Indigenous knowledge systems. INDG1000 is compulsory for all students completing a first year undergraduate course.

More information about these units can be found on the in the UWA Handbook
