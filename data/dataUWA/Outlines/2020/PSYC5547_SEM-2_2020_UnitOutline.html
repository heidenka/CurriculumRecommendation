Unit Information

INTRODUCTION
This unit is designed to develop the students understanding of the responsibilities of working as a professional in the area of industrial and organisational (IO) psychology, whether this be in the role of a registered psychologist, organisational psychologist, consultant, or internal practitioner. 
We will introduce you to the regulatory framework for registered psychologists, and explore the ethical, societal and legislative expectations associated with the practice of psychology in Australia. Throughout this unit we focus on key pieces of legislation, ethical frameworks, and guidelines that underpin the practice of psychology. We focus on the Health Practitioner National Law Act, the National Practice Standards for the Mental Health Workforce, and the Australian Psychological Society Code of Ethics. We explore the role played by professional associations (such as the Australian Psychological Society and the College of Organisational Psychologists) in maintaining integrity and public trust in the psychology profession. 
Throughout the unit students will be exposed to a range of scenarios that have been designed to support the development of skills relating to navigating professional and ethical issues in IO psychology. This process is designed to teach you different ways to scaffold decision making in the ethical and professional landscape to develop confidence in managing issues as they arise in the workplace. It is also intended that this process will help to clarify the role of the organisational psychologist across a broad range of settings. 
This unit will introduce the scientific method used in psychology through an organisational psychology lens, and begin to explore the professional skills required by students to translate psychological science to the work context in a safe and competent manner. Continued development of these skill sets is a focus of professional learning through Placement 1, 2 and 3. 
Towards the end of semester we will introduce you to the process of placement and will discuss expectations, placement management, placement process, and the role of placement in developing the student’s professional identity. 
By the end of the unit the student should have an excellent understanding of what it means to be a professional working in IO psychology today. We expect the student to have considered and developed strategies for unpacking professional and ethical issues, including identifying how to obtain support and peer guidance, in order to be confident in their ability to practice as an IO professional.  
LEARNING OBJECTIVES OF THE UNIT
At the end of this unit, you will:
1.	Have developed your understanding of the ethical and regulatory frameworks which set the standards of conduct and professional practice expected for Registered Psychologists.
2.	Be able to apply the National Practice Standards, APS Code of Ethics, and other relevant legislation to a range of contexts to make appropriate decisions within the work context. 
3.	Be able to confidently discuss the professional responsibilities and standards expected of a Psychologist operating within the IO landscape. 
4.	Understand how placement activity, and the process of administering your placement, is a key aspect of demonstrating your ability to practice safely and competently.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

LEARNING METHODOLOGY
This unit will run flexibly to facilitate maximum discussion of the main challenges facing IO Psychologists. Students will be required to do independent reading of relevant National Practice Standards, APS Code of Conduct and associated regulations, and Health Practitioner National Law in order to complete the NPS Assessment, participate in class discussions, and the complete the individual case study facilitation.

Participation

CLASS PARTICIPATION
You are expected to attend every class. The structure of learning is workshop style, integrated learning that is highly interactive. Lecture capture system may not be available at the location. For the last half of semester we spend time introducing you to placement activity and you commence your group supervision within class. This supervision has been carefully structured and is a mandatory requirement for meeting your overall placement goals. 
I assess class participation by giving you a rating each week. At the end of each week I will consider the criteria below and assign you a 0, 1, 2 or 3 based on your contribution during class. The addition of these ratings over the semester (and conversion to a mark out of 20) will form your class participation grade.
I will give you a ‘0’ for contribution if you did not attend class. You will receive a ‘1’ for “presenteeism”: if you are physically in class but not “really there” either because it is evident that you have not read or prepared before class, or if you are not attentive to what is happening in class (e.g., working on your computer or phone on unrelated things). I will give you a 2 or 3 depending on the degree to which I see evidence that you have come to class adequately prepared, that you have considered and integrated class content with your prior experience and knowledge, and that you have been able to develop insight and new ways of thinking about professional issues based on class readings and the ensuing class. 
If you have something to contribute, you need to speak up. Having important experiences, insights or viewpoints locked away in your head may provide learning for you but it does not provide learning for the rest of the class. I seek to facilitate each class in a way that provides numerous opportunities for individual contribution and for those who don’t habitually speak up, however it is then your responsibility to ensure that you use these opportunities.
Should you feel that you require assistance to learn how to speak up in class, the Student Services website provides workshops on this topic and a drop-in service to discuss learning skills. See:

This unit forms a core element of your eligibility to apply for registration as a psychologist at the conclusion of your degree. As such, attendance at class is compulsory.  

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
