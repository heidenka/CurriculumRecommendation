Academic Information

Content The aim of this unit is to gain a quantitative knowledge of hydrologic processes across a range of space and time scales. Topics covered include (1) physical laws governing the catchment water balance at large scale, catchment energy balance and conversion to mass balance, calculation of evapotranspiration and estimation from satellite data; (2) introduction to runoff processes—the flood hydrograph and flood frequency analysis, variable source area runoff concepts and calculation; (3) unsaturated zone hydrology—Richards equation, unsaturated flow and transport parameter identification, scale issues and preferential flow; (4) groundwater flow—general principles and estimation of recharge and discharge, groundwater-surface water interactions, identification and quantification of fluxes in groundwater-dependent ecosystems; and (5) introduction to ecohydrology and quantification of vegetative feedbacks in hydrologic processes.

Unit Information

This masters-level course will introduce the basics of quantitative engineering hydrology, with applications to urban and agricultural systems.  The aim of this unit is to gain a quantitative knowledge of hydrologic processes across a range of space and time scales. 
The topics covered include: 
(1) Quantifying water in the environment.  How much water do we have?  How should we answer that question when water might be present as snow, or as water vapor, or in the soil?  How can we measure water in the environment?
(2) How can we make predictions about what water will do in the environment?  The answer is "we use conservation laws" - especially conservation of mass.  How do we start with the simple idea that water cannot be created or destroyed, and use it to come up with equations that we can solve to predict what water will do?
(3) How and why does water flow in the environment?  Can we do better than "water flows downhill"?  How does the answer to this question change depending on whether water is present in a channel, or in an aquifer, or in unsaturated soil, or even in a plant?
(4) What conditions should we design for?  How do we assess the risks posed by hydrological systems?  What engineering methods are used to relate the risks associated with the weather to risks posed by water flow on the landscape?  When are these methods appropriate - and what are their limitations?
(5) How do we do design?  Given a goal, equations, and data - how can we use these tools to answer questions about the kinds of infrastructure we might build to manage water flows?
The learning outcomes for the unit are:
1.	Quantify water in different phases and environmental states.
2.	Use conservation of mass to derive and solve governing equations for water flow and storage in different states, and connect these equations and solutions to engineering design
3.	Conduct engineering design accounting for the variability of rainfall and its translation into variable runoff.
4.	Work collaboratively to quantify water availability, apply mass balance approaches, and/or explore the behavior of water resources and their consequences for society.
5.	Communicate technical findings and decision-making in an engaging and professional way.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

Schedule Information
Class will meet from 10am - 1pm on Thursdays in the Cheryl Praeger Lecture Theatre (G17 in Maths -- it's a nice room!).
Formally, we have 2 hours of lecture and 1 hour of practical class.
But most weeks it's going to be more like 1 hour of instruction and 1.75 hours of in-class work.
That remaining 15 minutes?  
That's when you'll be taking a break, going outside for fresh air, moving your legs, and having a snack.  Please do plan on bringing some food to keep you going - 3 hours of work will be plenty without us getting hypoglycemic!  I will join you outside with my muesli bar.
Outside of class, I will encourage you to bring questions you have to my office hours: Monday 2-4pm in 225 Maths and Stats.
Class structure
In class, most days, I will deliver some lecture material, and you will get a "worksheet" to practice what we learn.  
The worksheet will actually take the form of a JuPyter Notebook (in Python). It will integrate code with questions, and give you a chance to do everything from data analysis to simple modeling.
The worksheets aren't assessed (but they're a great example of what you might see in an exam...).  
I expect you to work on the worksheets with friends, and to ask for help from me.  I might interrupt the work sessions to share questions and ideas that other students bring up with me.
Once we get the semester underway you'll also have two kinds of assessments that we'll work on during class:
(i) Guided design projects.  These will be like the other worksheets, but they will take you through the process of design.  You can work on them with friends, but at the end you must submit your own design report individually.  There will be 3 of these.
(ii) A midterm examination - this will be held in Week 7 on April 9th. Plan to be here.
If you haven't gotten the point -- your life will be waaaay better if you come to class.  Its intended that you could get most of your work done in this 3 hour period each week, and have your friends, and your instructor, available to support you.  Video-recordings of the lecture will not be a good substitute for this experience.

Unit Structure

We will have class every week during the semester.
One class (Week 7, April 9th) class will include a Midterm Examination.  This is the day before Good Friday.  
I know that is a bummer if you have plans to travel for Easter, but please, do not plan to miss this class.  If you have a travel conflict that cannot be avoided, you must let me know by March 19th so that I can work with you to make arrangements to take the examination early.
I will also organize one field trip (date TBA, as early in the semester as possible).  This field trip will be organized outside of class time and will not be compulsory (but it will be worth attending!).  We will visit 3 sites around Perth that are relevant to our guided design projects so that everyone can see examples of these systems in operation.

Participation

You will be required to attend the midterm examination which will be administered in class on April 9th.  This is the Thursday before Good Friday, so please do NOT plan to travel on that day.
Otherwise, there is not a participation grade.  However, I strongly recommend attending class.  Each week we will work on in class worksheets that will contain the kind of questions you need to be able to answer in the examinations.  It will also prepare you for the guided designs.  
You will also, in class, be able to work in groups on the guided design assessments.  Each has a worksheet that will guide you through a design process, with an individual report to be written at the end of it.  If you don't come to class, you will be working through those worksheets alone, and will be disadvantaged relative to people who come to class.

Referencing Requirements

I expect that you will fully reference and cite any claims you make in written documentation, and that you do so inline.  
That is, it will not be sufficient to claim that the world is flat in your writing, and then list a reference in the bibliography.  I expect to see: "the world is flat (Flat Earther Society, 2020)" appearing in the text, and then the Flat Earther Society 2020 reference provided in the bibliography.  
I would strongly recommend the use of a citation manager such as EndNote to help with consistency in how you format and present citations and reference.

All this said, this unit will not have a large dependence on references, so I don't think this will be a huge issue for you all!

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
