Unit Information

Welcome to SCOM1101- Communicating Science
What is scientific knowledge, how does it develop and how can it be more effectively communicated? The purpose of this unit is to provide guidance on how to be more effective at both inward-facing (to scientists) and outward-facing (with non-scientists) science communication. In addition to you becoming a more successful scientist, giving equal consideration to improving your communication in both directions will help to make you a more valued one as well. 
In this unit you will use a variety of communication modes to develop dialogue around scientific research to a variety of audiences. You will also work within your tutorial group to organize and run an a poster event that aims to develop dialogue around a scientific topic. 
Peer feedback is used throughout the unit and you will be expected to provide feedback, as well as receive it.

Lecture Capture System

In accordance with UWA’s policy on lecture capture all lectures* in enabled venues will be recorded and made available to stream and download by enrolled students via the Lecture Capture System (LCS) for the duration of the unit (including Supplementary and Deferred Examinations period).

* Please note that in some limited circumstances lectures may not be recorded due to:  ethical and/or security related issues; the use of commercially sensitive material; the use of third-party copyright teaching material; or culturally sensitive content. Students will be advised, in advance, when this applies.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

In terms of learning and teaching strategies this unit will make use of:

(1) traditional face to face lectures (on line due to COVID 19)
(2) online prerecorded lectures
(3) a range of online activities
(4) face to face (FTF) lab classes (on line due to COVID 19)
The FTF lab sessions will not introduce any new material, but are an opportunity for students to practice and reinforce what they have learnt in the lectures and via the online materials/activities, creating a safe environment for discussion and dialogue. They are also an opportunity for students to get to know one another and the University, and serve as a forum for them to receive any further information that they require, especially with regards to assessments. 
Peer feedback is used throughout the unit and students will be expected to provide feedback, as well as receive it.
In the last weeks of the unit, groups of students in each lab class will have to organize and run a poster presentation that aims to develop dialogue around a scientific topic.

Unit Structure

Every week there will be:
 (1) a Tuesday face to face lecture (Octagon Theatre 14:00-14:45); on line due to COVID 19
(2) a 'Wednesday' released prerecorded online lecture
(3) a practical activity that can be either on line (weeks 1,3,7,9,11), or face to face (now on line via Zoom due to COVID 19) (weeks 2,4,5,6,8,10)
In week 11, each lab class is expected to self organise and make further preparations towards their poster presentations that will be held in the last week of semester (i.e. week 12).

Participation

This unit requires your attendance, participation and practice to learn and benefit from the opportunities presented. 
It is good practice to attend all lab sessions as it will positively impact your overall grade. Most labs will incorporate class discussions and activities that are obviously more valuable if you participate in person. The labs will not be recorded. It is important for you to attend the session to which you have been assigned. If you are unable to attend a lab associated with one of the assignments, you will be required to apply for special consideration through the Science Student office. 
If for some reason you are unable to attend your assigned lab and wish to do a single lab swap for the same week, you should check the lab schedule on the LMS and request permission from your unit coordinator and demonstrator via email to swap the lab for another lab within that week.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
