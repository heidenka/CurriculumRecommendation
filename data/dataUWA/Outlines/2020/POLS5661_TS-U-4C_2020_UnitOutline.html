Unit Information

This unit examines the theoretical foundations of the discipline of international relations (IR), to provide a basis for you to successfully complete other advanced Masters of IR or Honours units. The course is devoted to examining the four major paradigms (i.e. 'clusters' of similar theories) in IR, namely, realism, liberalism, constructivism, and the critical theories. We will examine both 'inter-' and 'intra-paradigm' debates with reference to a number of key IR research agendas and contemporary issues in international politics.
The purpose of the course is to provide students with a detailed understanding of how applying different theories can provide very different explanations of international events and outcomes; it will be up to you to decide which paradigm is ‘best’, whether you consider one paradigm is ‘best overall’ or perhaps only that certain paradigms are better at answering certain questions than others. In addition, understanding the fundamental differences between paradigms enables you to critically assess the various competing claims made by scholars and commentators on world events.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

The unit will be taught in six three hour seminars. As contact hours are limited, attendance is mandatory for the seminars and will be taken. A stream of online seminars will be offered if and as required, for students unwilling and unable to attend on-campus classes. 
Students will be able to (1) understand core debates in international relations theory; (2) understand a variety of different research methods in international relations; and (3) apply various international relations theories to contemporary events.

Unit Structure

Seminars will take place from 5-8pm on Monday nights. Each week Alex will give an interactive one-hour lecture to start each seminar, detailing the key points of the theories in question. This will be recorded and can be watched offline for those students taking the course offline. 
In the second hour, we will go through the readings and discussion questions listed below. In the third hour, we will do a research activity, aimed at applying theory to a case study. Each of these activities are designed to build your skills for completing the assessment tasks. 
Online seminars will be provided to students who are unwilling or unable to travel to campus due to the coronavirus.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
