Academic Information

Content This core unit introduces students to the literature of chemistry, important aspects of occupational health and safety relevant to the chemical laboratory as well as understanding occupation health and safety legislation. Furthermore, an essential portion of lectures will introduce key scientific writing techniques, in particular learning how to compile and write laboratory reports. Lectures will also cover how to plan and design an experiment and solve problems in the laboratory. Chemistry terminology will also be covered in lectures and the laboratory.

Unit Structure

The following is the list of topics that will be covered in the lectures for the course.  The exact order of the material will be tailored to meet the objectives of the course.   Prof McKinley and some external lecturers will deliver most of the course and Prof Baker will deliver the " Communication of the results of chemical experiments.  Aspects of sustainability, green chemistry, and polymers." sections.  Prof Baker will be in charge of Experiment 1 and 2 in the laboratory course and Prof McKinley will be in charge of Experiments 3 and 4.

Topic                 {number of lectures}
Introduction to Occupational Health and Safety (OH&S).  {2 lectures}
Historical background and modern trends. Case studies of selected chemical hazards in the workplace,gender specific aspects.
OH&S Operational Details.  {4 lectures}
UWA safety procedures and structures. Physical hazards in the laboratory.  Chemical hazards in the laboratory (toxicity, flammability and explosive capability). Case studies of typical laboratory incidents. Waste disposal. 
OH&S Resources.   {2 lectures}
The literature of chemical laboratory safety. Hard copy resources and resources  available online. Web sites for West Australian, Australian and International databases. MSDS sheets and the need to ensure that all laboratory personnel have access to chemical safety data.
OH&S Regulations {3 lectures}
The legal responsibilities of professional chemists and laboratory personnel in general. West Australian legislation and Australian legislation. Special enactments (e.g. for radioactive substances, carcinogens and teratogens, chemical weapons precursors, precursors to drugs of addiction, agents that could be misused by terrorists ). Legal responsibilities of management, staff and students in teaching and research laboratories. Case studies of legal consequences of accidents involving chemicals.
OH&S in Industry {2 lectures}
Occupational health and safety in industrial and other chemical employment.  The role of Worksafe WA in regulating OH&S issues in WA workplaces.  A summary of the legal requirements in current and harmonised Australian legislation.  
New trends and risks from an OH&S perspective. {2 lectures}
Impact of new technologies e.g. nanotechnology, sustainability.
Communication of the results of chemical experiments.  {2 lectures}
How to complete a written report of a laboratory experiment.  What purpose does a report serve.  What should be included. How should it be set-out.  Summary of ethical considerations.  Common mistakes made.  A comparison between a laboratory report and a journal publication.  
Aspects of sustainability, green chemistry, and polymers. {6 lectures}
In this section considerations of the sustainability of chemical industrial processes will be discussed. Approaches such as green chemistry to reduce the impact of chemical processing on the environment will be explored.  The manufacture, properties and applications of polymeric materials will be examined.

Review of material covered and the format for the examination. {1 lecture}

Participation

Laboratory classes are compulsory to attend and all are assessed.  The first two laboratories run for two weeks and the 3rd and 4th laboratory sessions are for 1 week each. There will be three reports submitted on lms for laboratories 1, 2 and 3 and the 4th laboratory will be marked in the laboratory.  Laboratories 1, 2 and (3+4) have equal weight.  For Lab (3+4) the weighting is 2/3 Lab 3 and 1/3 Lab 4.
The expectation is that all students will attend the poster session.  In the event of an unavoidable clash students will be given an opportunity to present the work either earlier on the day or the next day but the poster must be submitted and displayed during the poster session.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
