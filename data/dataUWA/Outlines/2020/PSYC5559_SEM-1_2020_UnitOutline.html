Academic Information

Content Leadership is a key variable affecting individual, group, and organisational performance. In this unit students will explore major concepts, models and theories of leadership, including both conventional and alternative perspectives. Through critical discussion of leadership research and its practical applications in a range of organisational contexts, students will develop the knowledge and skills required to be an effective leader. In the unit, exercises, self-assessments, and case study analysis will be used to develop personal and interpersonal leadership skills and capabilities. Students will work together on a small group project to deepen their understanding of various leadership topics and practice shared leadership. Students will also be tasked with the development of plans and actions to improve leadership competencies beyond the unit.

Welcome to PSYC5559 Leadership!
Leadership is one of the most popular topics in business psychology and management research and it has captured the minds and hearts of countless practitioners. There are numerous books, articles, podcasts, videos, etc. on leadership. Leadership development is a massive industry worth billions of dollars. McKinsey estimates that US companies alone spend almost 14 billion US dollars annually on leadership development. However, despite decades of research and practice, the overall quality of organisational leadership remains low. Nearly every individual will readily provide an example of ineffective, at best, and toxic, at worst, leader. 
In 2016, Centre for Workplace Leadership at the University of Melbourne released a report on the state of Australian leadership. Their key findings indicate that leadership matters for workplace performance, innovation, employee engagement and talent management. However, they found many Australian workplaces to under-perform and report low levels of innovation. Unsurprisingly, they also found that many Australian leaders are not well-trained for the job, while too many Australian organisations under-invest in leadership development, especially at the frontline.The need for better leadership and leadership development has never been so high.
In this unit you will explore different approaches to leadership and leadership development. You will learn to think about leadership critically, while pondering questions like: What is 'effective' leadership? What are the qualities of 'effective' leadership? Is leadership a property of an individual or a group? How can leadership be developed? Is 'effective' leadership always positive? Is followership an attribute of leadership or a separate process? and What is the future of leadership?

Learning And Teaching Strategy

This unit follows a flipped learning model. This student-centric approach to learning places the onus of learning on the student, empowering them with greater control over their learning experience.
In practice this means that instead of delivering a 'lecture', the lecturer will facilitate in-class activities aimed at deepening and broadening the understanding of the assigned topic. Examples of such activities are in-depth discussion, group discussion, case study, role plays, etc. The students are tasked with preparation for these activities by reviewing the assigned materials. This preparation is crucial for the success of the in-class session.
To learn more about flipped learning, see http://www.worldclasseducation.uwa.edu.au/eeu/flipped-classroom
The assessments in this unit are designed to supplement the preparatory and in-class activities by deepening your understanding of leadership in yourself and others. To ensure adequate preparation for the in-class activities, four content knowledge quizzes will be administered throughout the unit. Working in a small group you will be tasked with delivering an interactive session on a selected leadership topic, which will deepen your knowledge of the topic, but also allow to observe and practice leadership in a  group setting. Individually, you will work on three short critical reflections and a personal leadership development plan.
Notes on assessments: 
This unit uses a stacked assessment approach, meaning that instead of submitting one larger assessment piece, you will be required to produce many smaller components throughout the semester. Please make sure to familiarize yourself with the deadlines! 
Formative feedback: Student will be provided with feedback on their assessments in the first 5 weeks of the unit. Feedback on the first critical reflection will be provided within the 2 weeks of the submission (i.e., week 4). Feedback on the first content quiz will be provided immediately after the submission deadline (i.e., week 3).
Word count: For all written assessments in this unit, the stated word count excludes references. Appendices are not allowed.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.
