Unit Information

This unit provides an introduction to hydraulics (or fluid mechanics). You will be introduced to and become familiar with all relevant physical properties and fundamental laws governing the behavior of fluids and you will learn how to solve a variety of problems of interest to civil and environmental engineers. While there will be a chance for you to put your mathematical skills to use in this subject, the emphasis is on developing a physical understanding of why a fluid behaves the way it does. The aim here is to combine your extensive, existing intuition for fluid mechanics with formal analytical methods.
The topics covered include fluid properties, conservation of mass and momentum in bulk form, the Bernoulli equation, dimensional analysis, experimental design, pipe flow, pipe networks, open- channel flow, solutions to the Navier-Stokes equations and hydrostatics. There is also an introduction to fundamental concepts of fluid mechanics such as boundary layers, turbulence, waves and drag.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

Students are expected to go through all lecture, tutorial and lab sessions. There are six modules in total. In addition to examples given in the lectures, practice questions for the content covered in each module will be provided and discussed in the tutorial sessions. There will be no assignments in this unit. Instead two in-class tests will be conducted to constitute as formal feedbacks to your study. 

Unit Structure

Lectures: Three online lectures (recorded) per week. Students are strongly advised to go through the lectures as they form the backbone of the unit with plenty of hand-on examples.
Tutorials: There are two parallel sessions (two hours each) each week, starting from Week 2. Students only need to attend one of the two sessions but welcome to attend both. Session 1 is Monday 2PM - 4PM and Session 2 is Tuesday 9AM - 11AM.
Labs: Laboratories begin in Week 5 of semester. They are held online. There are 3 laboratory sessions:
• Conservation Of Energy (COE)
• Swan River Model (SRM)
• Transition To Turbulence Before the lab session
Instructions for each of the three labs are posted on the unit website. You must read through the instructions before you watch the recordings, as there are pre-lab questions that must be completed. Although no lab report is required, a two-hour lab test will be held in Week 11 of semester. This test will assess your understanding of the experiments and your ability to analyze the data. Note that the labs are quite closely related to the lecture material, but may involve concepts that are not covered in class. Each set of lab instructions will contain questions for you to answer in preparation for the test.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
