Academic Information

Content This unit is designed to equip the student with a foundation for understanding labour law. It explores individual and collective aspects of employment in Australia, particularly in Western Australia. It falls into four main parts: (1) an examination of the contract of employment and the individual employment relationship, focusing on the formation, operation and termination of contracts of service including, in particular, various common law duties of employers and employees; (2) the federal and state legal systems of industrial relations that provide for the regulation of conditions of work including a discussion of arbitrated awards and collectively negotiated agreements, and an examination of how these industrial instruments affect the contract of employment and the individual employment relationship; (3) an examination of how the law endeavours to deal with and regulate industrial conflict, with particular attention given to the existing rules concerning protected industrial action; and (4) an overview of the law of occupational health and safety, including the principal methods of compensating employees injured at work (especially workers' compensation).

Unit Information

EMPL5555 – Employee Relations Law is about the law that regulates the relationship between employers and employees.
This unit is designed for postgraduate students. It is expected that you are coming into this unit with a basic understanding of employee relations law that was acquired through your undergraduate studies and/or industry experience. The depth of knowledge and practical skills required to complete this unit will be more demanding than those needed at the undergraduate level.
This unit will cover the following topics:
1.     the employment contract and the employment relationship;
2.     the difference between contracts of service and contracts for service – and the legal consequences that flow from mischaracterising employees as contractors;
3.     the jurisdictional sources of power that underpin employee relations laws;
4.     the court and industrial tribunal hierarchies that operate in Western Australia;
5.     the National Employment Standards (Federal system) and the Minimum Conditions of Employment (State system);
6.     the Truck laws, overpayments, and underpayments;
7.     the award system (Federal system and State system);
8.     enterprise agreements (Federal system and State system);
9.     industrial action;
10.   general protections laws;
11.   unfair dismissal laws (Federal system);
12.   breach of contract;
13.   post employment restraints; and
14.   occupational safety and health laws in Western Australia.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

The Business School has adopted a set of broad goals and specific outcomes that its courses seek to achieve. Those goals and outcomes will be fostered in this unit through the following teaching and learning outcomes:

1. Knowledge and skills
During this unit, you will study the laws that govern the employment relationship. This will involve a lot of reading and thinking. The assessment tasks in this unit are designed to test your scholarly knowledge and your ability to apply that knowledge in a practical manner. To pass this unit, you need to demonstrate that you understand the course content. How well you do will usually depend on the depth of your understanding.
 
2. Cognitive skills
You will not learn everything that you need to know to do well in this unit by simply showing up to the seminars. As a postgraduate student, there is an increased expectation that you will learn independently. Outside of the seminars, you will need to dedicate some time to reading (and reading widely), thinking about the course content, and rationalising how to apply the legal principles in a practical and logical manner.
3. Communication skills
This unit will require you to develop and demonstrate your verbal and written communication skills. 
Verbal skills: During the seminars, you will be required to orally present information to the class. This may seem daunting. However, it is to assist you with building your confidence and oral presentation skills. The in-class activities will go towards your participation mark  
Written skills: You need a strong command of written English. It will be very difficult to do well in this unit without it. Your ability to communicate in a clear, concise, and logical manner is key to doing well in this unit. You will lose marks if you do not communicate effectively and accurately in English.

4. Interpersonal skills
The seminars will involve group work. Each week, you will be working with different people in class. This is to encourage you to network with your peers, to build your communication skills, and to develop your ability to adapt to changing circumstances. 

5. Reflective learning skills
The final exam is heavily weighted. The final exam is intended to test your ability to recall and apply the information and skills you acquired throughout the semester. It would be difficult to cram the necessary information required to get through the final exam in a short amount of time. This means that you will need to spend considerable time during semester reviewing what we covered in previous lessons.  
6. Social and ethical awareness
Employment relationships involve people. That ingredient makes practicing in industrial/employee relations exciting and challenging. You will never cease to be amazed by the difficult circumstances that employers and employees tend to find themselves in. In navigating employment issues, you need to have an awareness of ethical, cultural, and social issues that exist at a workplace level, an industry level, and at a legal level. We will broach on these issues as we move through the semester. Students who find a healthy balance between passion and compassion are more likely to do well in this unit. 
Other information:
You will need a good understanding of word processing software in order to participate in this unit. The neat and tidy formatting of assessment documents is just as important as its content. 
You will need to be able to access legal databases and decisions so that you are able to research the law and how it has been applied by the courts and industrial tribunals. The Law Library offers regular classes to improve legal research skills.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
