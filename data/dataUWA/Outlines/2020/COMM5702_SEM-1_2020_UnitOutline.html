Academic Information

Content Digital Media: Theory and Practice offers students the opportunity to familiarise themselves with the technical aspects of digital technologies in order to consider how they shape communications strategies. Finding the right platform and the right tools is central to a successful communications campaign. This unit therefore considers the different technologies and social media platforms available to organisational communicators including websites, Facebook, Youtube, Twitter and Instagram and reflects on how each of these tools shapes content and information sharing in distinctive ways. Students are also introduced to the importance of web analytics and search engine optimisation in monitoring and analysing trends in contemporary media audiences. This is a hands-on unit that will see students learn how to use a wide range of digital tools as part of developing a communications campaign.

Unit Details

COMM5702 - Digital Media: Theory and Practice - SEM-1-2020 - 2020 - Face to face / Blended learning

Unit Information

Digital Media: Theory and Practice offers students the opportunity to familiarise themselves with the technical aspects of digital technologies in order to consider how they shape communications strategies. The unit provides students with theoretical depth and hands-on experience in critically analysing, designing and managing digital media for campaigns; most specifically websites, social media and digital storytelling production. Students are also introduced to the importance of web analytics and search engine optimisation in monitoring and analysing trends in contemporary media audiences as part of developing a communications campaign.

Important Information
Team Assignments
To model the collaborative way that digital media is produced in various creative media industries, a large proportion of the work and assessment in this unit involves working in teams. Teams are strongly advised to exchange reliable contact information with each other as early as possible, maintain continuous contact throughout the semester. Organising yourselves professionally and effectively is the team’s responsibility. If serious teamwork related problems arise, you should contact your tutor immediately, but students should make every effort to communicate with their teams and plan and execute their productions efficiently to avoid difficulties wherever possible. Team Contribution Forms may be used where required.
Filming and Editing
Students are encouraged to primarily film with ADL equipment and to edit in the allocated ADL editing suites. If teams would like to film with alternative equipment (smartphones, DSLR cameras, drones etc) they should choose a technology or digital form that is most suitable to their levels of technical ability as the ADL team is not required to provide technical assistance for non-ADL equipment used. If groups own editing software or a version of Adobe Premiere Pro and would like to edit off campus, they should first seek approval from the ADL, to avoid non compatibility with on-campus software. Please note: the ADL team may not assist with any issues that arise from external editing.
Copyright
The production, distribution and use of media content are subject to Intellectual Property and Copyright laws. In your Digital Story project, you are allowed to use audio-visual material created by people other than yourself/your team (this material must be appropriately credited) under the following conditions:
audio: you can use an unlimited amount of sound, music and sound effects which are in the public domain, or under creative commons licence; or, if you want to use material under copyright you must obtain written permission from the rights holder. All such material must be appropriately credited.
video/image: you can use a very limited amount of any image or video (a matter of a few seconds on screen; you must consult with your tutor), but you can use this material only if it is in the public domain, or under creative commons licence, or, it is under copyright, but you obtain written permission from the rights holder. All such material must be appropriately credited.
If groups fail to meet these copyright rules, the Digital Story can only be used/screened in an educational setting.
Contacting academic & ADL staff
Please use your UWA student email for all university correspondence.
Questions related to thematic elements or the quality of your project must be addressed to the Tutor or Unit Coordinator. This includes things such as:
narrative, script/dialogue, direction, plot development, character development, timing/flow/pace of film, elements of story that could be deleted, elements of story that need to be added, location and setting, casting, mise-en-scene (props, wardrobe, makeup, composition, performance, lighting), genre-specific tropes.

filming techniques, the use of filming equipment, cutting, colour grading, syncing audio and video, adding and fading music, adding sound effects, adding special effects, adding titles.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
