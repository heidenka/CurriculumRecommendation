Academic Information

Content This unit gives students an understanding of how legislation is made and, once in force, how it is to be read, understood and interpreted. The unit has an emphasis on interpretation in practice. The topics discussed include the legislative process, drafting, interpretating common law and legislative principles, the relationship between statutes and the common law, how to use extrinsic materials, the effect of amendments, the relationship between a statute and its delegated legislation, and comparisons to the interpretation of non-statutory instruments, such as contracts. Presentations from members of the legal profession on drafting and interpretation are incorporated.

Unit Information

Statute law is of considerable importance to legal practice. There are now very few areas of law that are not significantly regulated by statutory provisions. To be properly prepared for legal practice, or any work related to law, it is essential that you have a sound understanding of how to read, interpret and apply legislation, and of the role of statutory interpretation in our legal system as a whole. 
Many eminent members of the legal profession have highlighted the importance of this area. For example:
-->Hon. Robert French (former Chief Justice, High Court of Australia): “It is of fundamental importance that all law graduates, whether going into practice or other forms of law job, should be aware of the central significance of statutes in the Australian legal system...…They should be able to apply the provisions of a statute to a particular fact situation and be able to make inferences about rights, powers, duties and liabilities which may arise out of that application.” [‘Bending Words: The Fine Art of Interpretation’ (Speech delivered at Legislation in Practice Guest Lecture Series, Law School, University of Western Australia, 20 March 2014, 17].
-->Justice Leeming (Court of Appeal, Supreme Court of New South Wales): “What is commonly thought of as ‘common law’, namely, the various bodies of judge-made law…taught in law schools and written about in law books is and always has for the most part been sourced in statute and is unintelligible without reference to statute.”   [‘Theories and Principles Underlying the Development of the Common Law – The Statutory Elephant in the Room’ (2013) 36(3) UNSW Law Journal 1002, 1004].
-->Hon. Michael Kirby (former Justice, High Court of Australia): “Interpretation…is an activity that most lawyers are regularly engaged in. If you don’t enjoy resolving problems about the meaning of words, you should probably think of a career swap…law is expressed in words…words and their meaning constitute the essence of it.”   [Foreword, P Herzfeld and T Prince, Statutory Interpretation Principles (Thomson Reuters, 2014) v]
This unit addresses statutory interpretation law. In the context of learning about the concepts of text, context, and purpose, as well as more specific interpretative principles, the unit also highlights the broader role of statutory interpretation in our legal system. Because, as well as being pivotal to practice, the function of statutory interpretation is critical in determining the boundary between the judiciary, the legislature and the executive. 
This unit can be a challenging one for some students, especially in the early part of the semester, for three main reasons. First, it necessarily approaches the law in a way that may seem different to the legal studies you have engaged in so far, which often focus on formulating the rule of a judicial decision. Second, while most of the concepts in statutory interpretation law are not conceptually difficult, a precise, uniform method of application is not available. Statutory interpretation involves a weighing and balancing of interpretative factors. Third, it will take us the first few weeks to cover the foundation concepts, which interact and provide the cornerstone of the law.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

The tuition in this unit consists of one weekly two hour lecture (Tuesdays 9am to 10.45am) AND a fortnightly two hour tutorial. The first half of the lectures will comprise a more traditional style lecture and the second half will (to the extent possible depending on student attendance) be more interactive, using exercises with students.
LECTURES
It is essential that students keep up to date with lectures on a weekly basis. Statutory interpretation law consists of areas that build on each other. Putting together this law is like assembling building blocks, one at a time. It is not like some other areas of substantive law where there are discrete and separate topics. Therefore, to effectively study, you must engage with each topic weekly as subsequent areas of study will cumulatively build on prior areas.
TUTORIALS
Please note that tutorials commence in the first week of semester.
Please ensure that you have been allocated a tutorial group through the class allocation system (CAS) [ https://www.cas.uwa.edu.au/student ] by the start of semester. It is important that you attend the tutorial group that you have been allocated by CAS. If, due to an unforeseen event or special circumstance, you need to attend another tutorial group for one tutorial then please contact the Unit Coordinator to arrange this.
Tutorials will primarily take the form of ‘hands on’ workshops applying the law addressed in lectures. There will also be opportunities to discuss some particular principles in depth, given their importance and relevance to our system of government. It is extremely important that you prepare for, and attend, tutorials. Lectures can only provide you with the foundation - they are to guide you in your study, not to provide comprehensive information. The tutorial readings and exercises are to enable you to engage with the law and ideas we address in the lectures more meaningfully and in greater depth. Tutorials provide students with the most significant opportunity to put what we are learning into practice through hypothetical problems, case studies and analysis of principles and theories. Becoming proficient in statutory interpretation requires ‘doing it’ and practising that ‘doing.’

Participation

There are six tutorials over the semester. Students are expected to attend ALL SIX tutorials. There is NO 'grace' tutorial for non-attendance.
Students will be given a mark out of 10 for each tutorial. This will be converted to an overall mark out of 10 at the end of semester. Students will be awarded a mark on the basis of their participation in tutorials in accordance with the following criteria.
HD (8 to 10 marks): Student shows excellent preparation for all tutorials; exceptional standard of participation with contributions being clear, substantive and insightful; student is punctual, involved in discussion, and listens respectively to others; student shows excellent command of subject matter with articulate, well-constructed arguments.
D: (7 to 7.5 marks): Student is well prepared for tutorials; contributions to class discussion are clear, relevant and well considered; student is punctual, involved in discussion, and listens respectively to others; student shows good understanding of subject matter and arguments are informed and thoughtful.
CR (6 to 6.5 marks): Student is adequately prepared for all tutorials; attends and is punctual, shows interest, contributes to discussion and contributions are generally correct and mostly clear.
P (5 to 5.5 marks): Student is not adequately prepared for most tutorials; attends; contributions to discussion are mostly adequate but reflect little understanding of the issues raised by the tutorials.
F (0 to 4.5): Student attends but may be late, shows little or no interest, contributions or preparation are inadequate; or student does not attend. Students who do not attend a tutorial will be given a mark of 0 out of 10 for that tutorial unless there is special consideration granted.
*NOTE: Students may perform at a level above or below some of the elements of each level descriptor. In that case, the mark will be allocated on an overall weighing of how the student performs.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
