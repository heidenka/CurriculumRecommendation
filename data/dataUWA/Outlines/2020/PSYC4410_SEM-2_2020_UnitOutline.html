Learning And Teaching Strategy

The unit will employ a variety of teaching and assessment techniques. Lectures will be accompanied by 'flip-classroom' style Q&A sessions and students will give brief ‘elevator talks’ about their Honours projects in seminars. Students will also attend the School of Psychological Science colloquia series and critically evaluate the presentations. Finally, students’ presentation at the annual Honours Conference is the main item of assessment.
Learning Outcomes:
(1) Students are able to understand how to present their research in an informative and interesting way to a range of audiences. 
(2) Students are able to critically evaluate oral research presentations.
(3) Students are able to communicate their understanding of the research area in which they will be conducting research to an audience with expertise in this area.

Unit Structure

There will be three pre-recorded online lectures in semester weeks 1, 2, and 5; these will be uploaded at the beginning of each week. Accompanying each lecture, there will also be a live online flip-classroom style "Question and Answer" session on Fridays at 10am in weeks 1, 2 and 5. In these sessions students can ask any questions that arise from the pre-recorded lecture material. It is expected that you will listen to the online lecture promptly, as lectures will contain important assessment information. Doing so will allow you to apply the lecture content to your own research and to develop questions for the live Q&A sessions. 
There will be one compulsory seminar, during which you will give a short “elevator talk” on your honours research project (i.e., a '3-minute-thesis' talk using only 3 slides in 3 minutes). These will be marked via a combination of peer-assessment and marking by the unit coordinators (worth 20% of the grade). Students sign up for one of six available seminars in week 4: Thursday, Aug 20 (8-10am, 10-12noon or 12noon-2pm) and Friday, Aug 21 (10-12noon, 12noon-2pm or 2pm-4pm). Use CAS to select your classes: www.cas.uwa.edu.au 
You also need to attend at least two of the four School of Psychological Science colloquia series. If face to face colloquia go ahead, students will need to sign up online (via sign-up link on LMS), but please note there will be a cap on how many students can attend any given face to face colloquium due to venue capacity.  Otherwise colloquia will be held online via zoom. The four colloquia will take place on Tuesdays, 4-5pm - dates are Aug 4, Aug 25, Sep 15, and Oct 13 (dates may be subject to change).

Written assignments should be APA formatted. Format of oral presentations will be discussed in class.

Other

Assessment Details 
Oral presentations at seminar (“elevator talk”) and at honours conference: You will be required to give a 3-minute presentation on your honours project. The focus should be on creating interest, so you should explain the project's aims and significance, while only touching on methodological details. You can refer to results if they are available but this is not expected. This presentation should be designed for a general lay audience, and you can only use three slides. You can look at example presentations online (e.g., http://threeminutethesis.org/). 
Written evaluation of School of Psychological Science colloquia (two semester-2 colloquia): Colloquia are research presentations usually by visiting academics. These are 45 minutes plus 10-15 minutes question time. Students attend 2 colloquia during Semester 2. Students may need to sign-up for specific colloquia, as there will be a quota for face to face sessions, i.e. a maximum number of students allowed at each colloquium. Questions to the speaker are encouraged. Students evaluate the two attended colloquia on a standardized form, provided by the unit coordinator at the beginning of the semester. APA formatting rules apply. Submission via turnitin. 
Finally, you will be required to formally present your Honours project to your peers and academics attending the Honours Conference. Presentations will be marked using a variety of criteria by two markers. Marking criteria will be freely available and will be discussed beforehand in class. There will be a strict time limit (e.g., 12 minutes), plus time for questions (responses to questions will not be marked). Supervisors can only provide feedback once on the Honours Conference presentation—that is, there can only be one "dry-run". Conference talks will be recorded. 

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
