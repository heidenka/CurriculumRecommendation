Academic Information

Content The past two decades have seen an increasing amount of attention given to the importance of environmental policy and planning in promoting a sustainable future for the planet. This unit considers environmental policy and planning in the context of Australia, exploring the role of government, business and community, the success or otherwise of policies and plans, and the contemporary issues that require policy and planning solutions. The unit also develops tools for policy and planning assessment. It is suited to students interested in careers in environmental management and planning.

Unit Information

This unit examines the development, negotiation and implementation of environmental planning and policy, with a focus on topical, contemporary issues in Australia and overseas. Environmental policy is a broad field that spans topics as diverse as the management of ecosystems and species, the agricultural sector, resource sectors (such as minerals, energy, timber, water and fisheries), and pollution and waste management. The content of the unit reflects this diversity, and caters for students from a wide range of interests and disciplinary backgrounds, with a particular focus on planning and geography,  natural resource management, conservation biology and environmental management. This unit focuses on environmental policy and planning frameworks within Australia at the Commonwealth, State and local levels, and introduces students to environmental policy and planning issues at the international scale. It considers the complexity of urban and rural environmental problems, the economic and social dimensions of these, and the ways in which policymakers and planners respond. It is suited to students with an interest in pursuing careers in the broad fields of environmental management, policy and planning.
The goal of this unit is to improve students’ understanding of the complexity of environmental problems and the difficulties involved in developing policy and legislation to address these. Students will learn practical and analytical skills that will enable them to critically analyse contemporary issues in environmental policy and planning, and identify a range of policy responses to address these issues.
Learning outcomes
On completion of this unit, students will be able to:
1.	Appreciate the role of different tiers of government in Australian environmental policy and planning.
2.	Understand the role of different stakeholders in shaping policy and planning decision-making and outcomes.
3.	Understand how environmental policies and plans interact with social processes, economic development and the biophysical environment.
4.	Develop basic skills in the analysis of environmental policies and plans and in the selection of the most appropriate policy tools to address particular environmental issues.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

The teaching and learning strategy for this unit is multi-mode, with pre-recorded lecture content, and face-to-face tutorials. In 2020, there will also be a fully online mode available for students who are unable to attend campus in person. Face-to-face practical sessions take place each week (two hours) in a group environment and form a cornerstone of the unit, with many sessions dedicated to learning skills and conducting group work that are essential to completing the assessments.
The unit is taught in Crawley and in Albany.

Unit Structure

Lectures are pre-recorded for this unit in 2020. Tutorials will run for 2 hours per week (four streams in Crawley including three face-to-face and one online; and one stream in Albany) during most weeks of the course in Semester 2. Please refer to the Class Allocation System (CAS) page from 20 July 2020 for the final scheduled times for Semester 2, 2020. 
The tutorials are strongly aligned with the assessments for the units, focussing firstly on the policy brief, secondly on a research report in environmental planning, and finally on negotiation of international environmental policy to aid with the exam. 
The lecture content for the unit comprises three modules, each of which last for four weeks. 1) Principles and concepts in environmental policy and planning 2) Australian environmental and natural resource management policy 3) International environmental policy. A detailed indicative unit schedule will be provided on LMS.

Participation

Tutorials run for 1 hour 45 minutes in most weeks of the course. Students are expected to attend all tutorials, whether these be face-to-face, or online interactive. Absence from a tutorial session must be explained in writing (e.g. via email) to the unit coordinator or via the Special Consideration process. Students are expected to prepare for and actively participate in all tutorial sessions; details of preparation required for activities will be made available prior to each session where necessary. The sessions will assist the student in the preparation of the Policy Brief and Research Report, which form two of the major assessment items for this unit. In particular, tutorials in teaching weeks 5 through to 10 include compulsory group work and a group presentation that form part of the research report assessment. Students who miss one or more of this set of classes without: (1) approved Special Consideration; OR (2) prior satisfactory explanation to the unit co-ordinator and/or their group members (as appropriate), will incur a marking penalty of 5 per cent of the total possible mark for the research report per class missed.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
