Academic Information

Content This unit provides students with an understanding of the governance structure of planning systems in Australia, and in particular Western Australia. It introduces students to the political and bureaucratic frameworks that underpin planning; the role of different tiers of government—federal, state and local; the links between planning, the business sector and non-government organisations; the dynamics of power, conflict, and decision-making in democratic planning systems; and the complex interplay of social, political and economic processes within planning systems and policy decision making more broadly.

Unit Information

Welcome to PLNG5403. Planning and Governance, a core unit for the Postgraduate Diploma/Masters in Urban and Regional Planning, is led by Dr Clare Mouat. Professional planners and policy analysts need an appreciation and understanding of the ‘political and institutional landscape’ of planning, policy-making, and planning governance.  PLNG5403 provides a foundational introduction to the complex interplay of processes, structures, and concerns of planning and governance as the democratic basis of urban and regional development. A core aim of this unit is to sensitise students to tensions between planning as a technical profession and a political activity. Unit activities interrogate and explore the relational dynamics, character and nature of the 3 P’s that underpin professional planning practice: Power; Policy; and Politics. Blending theory and practice about ‘real world’ planning and governance, the unit regularly takes fieldtrips, and invites guest lectures from a mix of representatives from the public and private development sectors that are held on campus and around the city.
Students completing this unit will gain critical understanding – theoretical and practical - of the policy and political machinations of planning/policy processes by surveying key concepts and theories within urban planning, politics, governance, and decision making. Planning and policy decisions are informed by technical expertise and value judgments expressed by democratically elected officials, planners, and other stakeholders such as local communities, the development industry and environmental groups. PLNG5403 exposes how the actions and interplay of these different actors are influenced by wider structural processes – globalisation, capitalism, patriarchy, racism and class – and ideological beliefs. Through guided and independent study, students will translate meaningful observation and strenuous analysis of ideal and real planning and governance into a professional planning capacity in and beyond the classroom. 
*Unit Goal
Develop professional capacities in understanding the governance structure of planning systems in Australia, and especially Western Australia by:
1.	Introducing students to the political and bureaucratic frameworks that underpin planning; the role of different tiers of government: federal, state and local; the links between planning, the business sector and non-government organisations; and the complex interplay of social, political and economic processes within planning systems and policy decision-making more broadly. 
2.	Creating sensitivity to the tensions between planning as a technical profession and a political activity within the dynamics of governance within and beyond the spheres of government.  
*Unit Learning Outcomes 
At the end of the unit, you will be confident and competent in your ability to demonstrate that you can:
1	Critically distinguish key theoretical underpinnings of planning and governance	1 and 2
2	Develop an appreciation of the practical and operational facets of the ‘institutional, policy and political landscape’ of planning and governance within WA/Australia.	2 and 3
3	Be prepared for planning practice by exposing you to the political and bureaucratic machinations of planning decision-making and governance at the state and local government levels	1, 2 and 3

Learning And Teaching Strategy

The unit is taught in an online studio-style (“Learning Lab”). In teaching weeks shared interactive classes are scheduled for Mondays 1-3pm live online (in an Urban Cafe Session via Zoom). Students are expected to prepare prior to the Urban Cafe sessions by undertaking the nominated independent remote learning (Anytime online) activities as nominated on the LMS.  During teaching weeks, there will be a common co-working hour / open office hours session on 10-12pm. Advance notice will be provided when this morning session will be used for guest lecturers. Links and instructions for the Zoom meetings are found on the LMS. At anytime before semester starts, please (1) orientate yourself to the LMS and unit using the learning module found under the menu "Welcome - Start Here"; then (2) complete the pre-semester "Pulse Check" worksheet for discussion in class during week 1 as advised.
PLNG5403 is delivered via a series of (i) short academic learning modules that will focus on key conceptual and theoretical aspects of planning and governance including short lectures, videos and readings, (ii) a series of presentations by professional planner/developers on key practical and operational aspects of planning and governance, and (iii) interactive/independent studio activities. There are several ‘fieldtrips’ planned for PLNG5403 where we will virtually attend the offices of different planning/development stakeholders or they will visit us and attending local government council meetings – see schedule for details. These activities are designed as professional development to boost your future employability and introduce you to peer and industry networks. Time set aside for compulsory out-of-hours activities are offset by a corresponding reduction in scheduled meetings - see schedule for details.
The online format of this unit involves a range of static and/or captured videos including lecture capture and interactive online meetings. All key materials and links for captured session will be available in the LMS. 
Online class meetings will be set up ahead of time to allow us to simply start and join Zoom meetings via a join link that is displayed on the LMS module for each week we meet and to share cloud recording links where they are recorded. Please note: all attendees will receive a pop up box informing them that the meeting is being recorded. Lectures are recorded with your active consent for reasons of equity and education so you must press Continue; otherwise you have the option to opt out by pressing Leave Meeting and then you can watch the recorded version later.  
Students are expected to prepare by working through the weekly package independently and prior to attending the formal lecture sessions and the (virtual) fieldtrips.

Participation

Participation is expected in terms of compulsory attendance (labs and fieldwork), reading, and preparation for the assessments. Participation activities are designed to guide students whether they are familiar with Australian planning and governance or this is their first experience.  To help students learn in class and with their peers, students are expected to prepare for labs and assessments by sourcing high-quality readings (provided and found independently) and reading widely and deeply about theoretical, practical, topical, and historical aspects of planning and governance in comparative perspective. Guidance will be given as part of the teaching and class discussion. However, student-led learning is expected including 1. preparatory notes and 2. MAP diaries (neither are for credit but are formative feedback for your assessment and professional development). 
1. Preparatory notes: these are expected to be submitted weekly to the unit coordinator: students must email the unit co-ordination at least two hours before class with their preparatory notes of about 250 words in length. These notes are a way mainly for you to gather your thoughts and secondarily for me to get a sense of what is on your mind before class each week. This important but low-effort task will help ensure the labs balance individualized student learning with the scaffolded unit learning and individual student development. Preparatory notes are a way mainly for you to gather your thoughts and secondarily for me to get a sense of what is on your mind. You might write a brief reaction paper; pose some questions; share your observations on a striking formal feature, passage, or pattern; describe a connection between the primary and secondary readings; or explain a connection between the reading and your own life, another class you’re taking, or contemporary popular media. I will respond with a brief email before class, perhaps to encourage you to share your idea in class, follow up with me during office hours, or pursue your thinking in some other way. Prep notes submissions will start from week 2.
2. Each year, students are expected to independently develop their understanding of planning and governance through a media, academic and policy (MAP) diary - this diary must also include a visit to a council meeting (other than the City of Perth) in the student's local area: The City of Nedlands visits are scheduled for those that wish to attend as a group. In 2020, these will likely be livestreamed.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
