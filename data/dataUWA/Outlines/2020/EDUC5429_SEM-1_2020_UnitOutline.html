Academic Information

Content This unit will provide an introduction to a range of perspectives and understandings that will help students work effectively with Indigenous students, youth and families within the Australian/Western Australian education system. The unit examines identity and worldview and the wider society's attitudes towards Aboriginal people and what people mean when they speak of 'Aboriginal education'. Students are asked to critically reflect on how they locate themselves, their world views and perceptions within Aboriginal Education. The unit will examine historical relationships between Aboriginal and non-Aboriginal Australia and the impact of colonisation, governmental policies and practices on all aspects of life and, in particular, education. 'Aboriginal education' is considered in the context of social justice and Indigenous and human rights issues which continue to affect Aboriginal people and their relationships with the wider Australian community. The focus of this unit is to help develop cultural understandings that will enable teachers to embed Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander perspectives and draw on strategies that are culturally responsive within an educational context.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

The Teaching and Learning Strategy of this unit based on a variety of current teaching and learning strategies which will assist in preparing pre-service teachers to teach in diverse and technological educational spaces. The unit design follows principles of the ‘flipped classroom”. You can access this link for more information on ‘Flipped Classrooms” https://www.adelaide.edu.au/flipped-classroom/about/.
Online preparation tasks, seminars, workshops and learning on Country are the learning designs in EDUC5429. It is expected that you will develop and enhance your knowledge by engaging in the learning opportunities throughout the unit. The engagements will reflect the values as expressed in cross-cultural learning environments in which a key component is critical reflection based on preparation and willingness to share what is known at that moment. Attention is focused on acquiring knowledge through engagement in online and face to face activities, discussion and sharing. 
These learning strategies are an opportunity for you to develop your professionalism, especially in the context of working in schools. It is important for you to practice maintaining high standards of professional conduct and commitment throughout the unit, this will be reflected in your attitudes to learning and behaviors as you engage in the learning offered.

Complete the ISE module before week 1 workshop as an introduction or a refresher (ISE link is on LMS) (approx. 1-2hours to complete). The ISE module is an introduction or reflection of some essential Indigenous understandings which will assist in familiarising you with the context of this unit. 
Online Preparation Tasks - See Modules on LMS
Weekly online pre-workshop preparation tasks which include: a review of previous week’s seminar content in the form of a self assessed quiz; guided reading components (article extracts or a full article); guided viewing components (each video ranging from 10mins to 45mins duration); diagram or notes pertaining to reading/viewing to bring to the workshop for the workshop activity; discussion board post based on a set question connecting with the guided reading/viewing (approximately 100 words).
Seminars
Weekly seminars will be presented by professionals/educators from Aboriginal Education sectors in education who are knowledgeable in the specific topic. The aim of using seminars is to move beyond a lecture of content and to stimulate critical thinking and raise questions in order to increase learning about concepts offered in each seminar. In preparation for each seminar please analyse weekly course materials and note any queries to bring to the presentation. Opportunities to ask questions will be offered and a dialogue will support the directions the seminar will cover. Completing the Essential Readings/Viewings will enable you to gain deeper understandings of seminar concepts. 
Workshops
A dialogic and transformative learning approach is fundamental to the workshop design of this unit (for you own information http://citeseerx.ist.psu.edu/viewdoc/download?doi=10.1.1.1002.9078&rep=rep1&type=pdf). Workshops will be interactive using both activity based and dialogic approaches. To be able to engage in the classroom dialogue and activities students will need to reflect on and analyse their understandings the content/points in unit materials provided in online preparation tasks on LMS and in seminars. A dialogic and transformative learning approach requires students to reflect on and share understandings and perspectives about the unit content/points being raised. Critical reflective components of the activities and the dialogic approaches are designed stimulate students’ transformative learning and facilitate deeper understandings.
To be able to engage fully in your learning ensure you prepare for your workshops by accessing the weekly workshop guide and by completing the Online Preparation Tasks on LMS.

Learning on Country - Field Trip
Learning on Country is a significant component of learning for Indigenous students/peoples and also for non-Indigenous students/peoples’ learning. Country encompasses more than just the physical environment, it is knowledge, the ancestors, kin/people and spiritual. Learning on Country is a way of learning through connecting with Country and the knowledge within Country.

RESUBMISSION RULE
Failure to pass an assessment item within a unit (MTeach)
15. Students must pass all components of a Master of Teaching unit to pass the unit overall. Students who fail an assessment item in a unit may resubmit it if—
(a) this is their first request for a resubmission in the unit;
(b) they contact the unit coordinator by email within 5 University working days of the release of the result and formally request a resubmission.
(c) A resubmitted assignment that is deemed to be a ‘fail’ by the unit coordinator, will receive the original failing mark for the component.
(d) Where resubmission is approved, the reassessed mark is capped at the assessment pass mark, unless an application for mitigation is approved in accordance with the University Policy on Assessment: Special Consideration (UP11/23).
(e) A resubmission is normally due one week after being approved by the unit coordinator.
(f) Assignments that are failed on the grounds of lateness or academic misconduct will not normally be considered for resubmission.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Special Considerations

If your study has been adversely affected by illness or other significant circumstances outside of your control, you can apply for special consideration. Significant circumstances may include but are not limited to:

  • serious illness or death of a member of the student's immediate family or household or of a close friend
  • serious injury
  • being a victim of a crime
  • breakdown of relationship
  • sudden loss of income or employment
  • serious disruption to domestic arrangements

The full policy and information on applying for special consideration is available at http://www.student.uwa.edu.au/course/exams/consideration

Compulsory online modules

UWA has a wealth of resources available to support your student learning. These online modules are compulsory for many students:

  • Academic Conduct Essentials (ACE) is compulsory for all new students about ethical scholarship and the expectations of correct academic conduct that UWA has of its students.
  • Communication and Research Skills (CARS) is compulsory for all new undergraduate students which aims to assist students at UWA to develop communication and research skills in an academic context.
  • Indigenous Study Essentials (INDG1000) is compulsory for all students completing a first year undergraduate course and introduces you to the shared learning space that UWA embodies. This learning space includes both western and Indigenous knowledge systems.

More information about these units can be found on the Academic Conduct Essentials website

Useful Contacts

Student Guild

The Guild represents all students enrolled at UWA and can help you in a number of ways. For financial, academic or welfare support please email Guild Student Assist at assist@guild.uwa.edu.au. For all other queries email hello@guild.uwa.edu.au or visit the UWA Guild website.

University Library

Visit one of UWA's six libraries or the University Library online to access a wide range of services, study spaces, information and help, including:

  • Access to textbooks and readings for your units
  • Individual and group study spaces
  • Wireless internet and your student account
  • Research, referencing and copyright support
  • Printing and scanning services

Student Experience

There is a wealth of material provided by UWA Student Experience that can help you settle into university life and help with many other issues you may encounter as a university student.  Contact details for departments within Student Experiences can be found on their website.

Study Smarter

STUDYSmarter provides free academic advice, support and resources for all undergraduate and postgraduate students at UWA. We can help you to develop the writing, research, English language, maths and stats skills you need to excel in your university studies. Get personalised advice at WRITESmart Drop-in and (ma+hs)Smart Drop-in, and find out about our extensive range of on-campus workshops, and online study guides, videos and tips on the STUDYSmarter website.
