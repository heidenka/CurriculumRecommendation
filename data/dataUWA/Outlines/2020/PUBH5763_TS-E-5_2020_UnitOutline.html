Academic Information

Content This unit examines key theories, concepts and approaches to leadership and management in health organisations. An important premise underpinning the unit is that leadership is needed at all levels of an organisation, and that leadership takes many different forms. The need for health leadership to be flexible and responsive to different contexts in a rapidly changing world is explored with students. Across the unit leadership is considered from an individual, team, organisation and health sector perspective. Students will examine contemporary leadership issues and challenges facing the health sector, including ethical leadership, leading change, leadership in times of crisis or uncertainty, and the growing imperative to measure and demonstrate outcomes. A feature of this unit is the integration of real world examples and case studies to explore contemporary issues and challenges in health leadership.

Unit Information

Welcome to Leadership & Management of Health Services (PUBH5763).
PUBH5763 is an elective unit in the Masters of Public Health degree program, and a compulsory unit in the Master of Pharmacy degree. 
Overall Aim	This unit aims to develop student understanding of the contemporary theories of effective leadership and management and how these apply to the health sector and to health professionals within health services. 
Content	This unit examines theories and develops key themes to facilitate effective leadership and management of health services. These include the importance of vision, the techniques of strategic planning and prioritisation, the art of communication and negotiation, and insights into health system complexity and policy development. 
The unit provides a working knowledge of topics that are useful to health professionals holding administrative positions in the health system and those working in the governance of not-for-profit community organisations. A feature of the curriculum in this regard is the opportunity afforded to students to explore contemporary issues in the health system and wider context at the social, organisational and personal levels.
Learning Outcomes	At the time of successful completion of the unit, the student should be able to:
(1) Discuss and critique key theories, concepts and principles of leadership and management relevant to health organisations and services
(2) Distinguish different dimensions and contexts of leadership, including self-awareness, and leadership at the team, organisational and wider health sector level;
(3) Demonstrate an understanding of challenges and obligations related to governance, accountability and ethical leadership in the health sector; 
(4) Apply evidence based principles to facilitating and leading change and to leadership in times of uncertainty or crisis;
(5) Critically analyse contemporary issues in health leadership and management.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

This unit is taught through a series of face-to-face lectures and supporting tutorial and group activity sessions. 
The  teaching  philosophy of  the  unit  seeks  to  create  a  diverse  learning  experience,  which  includes  structured  lectures,  tutorial discussions and experiential learning. Lectures and tutorials incorporate relevant examples from the real world to illustrate the application of leadership and management principles to a range of health settings, issues and challenges. 
In this unit you will be encouraged to work both independently as well as collaboratively in group settings. The unit is premised on the adult-education philosophy that each student is an independent and self-motivated learner, with the role of the ‘teacher’ being one of facilitation and direction rather than the source of all knowledge.

Turnitin & Referencing
You will be required to submit your assignments through LMS using the Turnitin software. You will be able to view your similarity score prior to the submission deadline. It is advised that students submit their assignments early to allow for a full review of their similarity score and to ensure appropriate referencing has been undertaken. While all assignments will be assessed, a similarity score above 20% will be flagged to markers for thorough investigation. 
Please remember that using a full sentence or sentences from another source, with minor word adjustments is NOT acceptable and is viewed as plagiarism.  Paraphrasing is permissible if correctly done as per UWA guidelines on academic integrity. “To paraphrase effectively you will need to change words, grammatical constructions and the sentence order of the text you want to paraphrase”  https://www.student.uwa.edu.au/__data/assets/pdf_file/0004/2748118/Paraphrasing-Survival-Guide.pdf
Plagiarism includes presenting someone else's work as your own. Ensure you are familiar with the university's plagiarism policy:  https://www.governance.uwa.edu.au/procedures/policies/policies-and-procedures?policy=UP07/21

Policy on late submission of assessments 
In accordance with the UWA policy on late assignment submission, if you hand in your assessment item after the due date/time a penalty of 5% of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assigned work is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assessment item is due. 
Assessments submitted later than 7 days after the deadline receive a mark of zero, unless an application for Special Consideration is approved. If you believe you are eligible for an extension, your Special Consideration application must be lodged with your Faculty Student Office before the due date, or in unforeseen circumstances, within 3 working days of the due date.
Ethical Scholarship, Academic Literacy and Academic Misconduct
Ethical scholarship is the pursuit of scholarly enquiry marked by honesty and integrity. 
Academic Literacy is the capacity to undertake study and research, and to communicate findings and knowledge, in a manner appropriate to the particular disciplinary conventions and scholarly standards expected at university level.  
Academic misconduct is any activity or practice engaged in by a student that breaches explicit guidelines relating to the production of work for assessment, in a manner that compromises or defeats the purpose of that assessment.  Students must not engage in academic misconduct.  Any such activity undermines an ethos of ethical scholarship.  
Academic misconduct includes, but is not limited to cheating, or attempting to cheat, through:
•	Collusion
•	Inappropriate collaboration
•	Plagiarism
•	Misrepresenting or fabricating data or results or other assessable work
•	Inappropriate electronic data sourcing/collection
•	Breaching rules specified for the conduct of examinations in a way that may compromise or defeat the purposes of assessment.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
