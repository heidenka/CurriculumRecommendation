Academic Information

Content This unit examines the historical and social impact of some key technological innovations on architecture and design. Particular attention is paid to the synergies and density of relations between technological innovation, aesthetic originality and social formations. Technological innovations discussed include agriculture, writing, the printing press, the lens, oil paint, iron, steel and glass, photographic and filmic technologies, motorised transport, television, biological technologies and digital technologies. The unit addresses subjects from different cultures and periods, and is aimed at a broad range of students from the arts and sciences. The outcomes and assessments are geared to basic generic literacy and research skills which prepare students for a range of disciplines.

Unit Information

ARCT1011 examines important premodern case studies in architecture from a cross-cultural and geographical vantage. Students are introduced to a range of technological, social, and aesthetic developments that inform architecture and its practice. It discusses the key moments that culminate in a history of architecture and also the scholarly domain that studies these moments. 
However, the unit does not solely focus on the main actors and ideas germane to past architectural endeavour and their influence today in a “globalised” world, but also calls them to task. If we have any hopes of designing solutions to very real problems today then alternative pasts matter. Maybe we can take a different starting point to build a different future? 
Students will learn critical analytical skills, assemble an archive of design precedents, and refine cultural and academic vocabulary basic to the course in architecture.

Referencing Requirements

Every time you quote or use images, text or the ideas of another person in your assessment submission, you must provide a properly formatted reference to the source. For ARCT1011 the only acceptable format for referencing and notation is the ‘Chicago’ academic or citation style. Details of this style are widely available but are easily consulted via the library website and notes which have been prepared by library staff: https://guides.library.uwa.edu.au/Chicago_citation 
The most common type of academic misconduct arises from instances where material is incorporated into text verbatim, without quotation marks, and thus represented as a student’s own words. (This is commonly called plagiarism.) Even where foot- or end-notation referencing is provided identifying where the appropriated material comes from, this is nonetheless misconduct if text is reproduced verbatim. Take care and be sure to paraphrase (put into your own words) such material instead, as well as provide a properly formatted reference to the source. 
As well as plagiarism, contract cheating, collusion, falsifying data and representing work that has already been submitted to the University or elsewhere as an original response to an assignment, academic misconduct may include an additional activity or practice engaged in by a student that violates: "...explicit guidelines relating to the production of work for assessment, in a manner that compromises or defeats the purpose of that assessment. Any such activity undermines an ethos of ethical scholarship.” (From the University's Policy on Academic Conduct.) Such activity or practice includes failure to cite sources at all (not only improperly citing them) even though this is clearly required, failure to cite page number(s) for a foot- or end-notation where required, and citing a range of pages (for example, pp 1-169) from a source when a more precise location is clearly required. (In some case it is appropriate to cite a range of pages, say, where you want to reference the content of an entire chapter or section of a source. Quotations, however, typically appear on one or, at most two pages and these should be indicated precisely.) Whether or not such infractions are investigated as academic misconduct they fail to satisfy unit guidelines and will be penalized by a lower mark or a failed mark depending on their severity.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
