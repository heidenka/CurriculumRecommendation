Academic Information

Content Ethnographic data and interpretations of it form the core of social anthropology and qualitative sociology. This unit considers how this data is produced. In a seminar setting, students read and analyse productions of the ethnographic imagination. By immersing themselves in the first-hand experiences of anthropologists in the field, students familiarise themselves with the process of ’doing’ ethnography. The unit may address the following issues: (1) the connection of explanatory theories to methods of research; (2) methods of studying and interpreting everyday life—participant observation (everyday life as field research), questioning, recording, note-taking; (3) eliciting and interpreting accounts of social life—semi-structured interviews, questionnaires, genealogies, family histories, participatory social research; (4) observing and interpreting categories and symbols; (5) using information technology resources to source methods and explanations; (6) engaging with innovative and interdisciplinary research methodologies and issues; and (7) how to use ethnography to engage in cross-cultural analysis.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

The unit is presented in "flipped mode" with the unit content usually presented online followed by weekly workshops designed to consolidate the ideas and knowledge expressed through the content. Some meetings will have formal input from the unit coordinator which will be shared later via the LMS. The workshops are designed to enhance your understanding of key concepts and ideas and to address questions you may have about these ideas and the content. We will also use these meeting times to hone skills necessary to conduct the ethnographic research project that sits at the heart of the unit. This assignment is the major outcome through which all of the unit's learning is consolidated. Active participation in the workshop is vital to successful engagement with the core concepts and practices of ethnography, consequently participation in this important aspect of the class forms a significant component of the assessment for the unit.

Unit Structure

You are expected to attend one ninety minute workshop per week (we usually use the full 105 minutes available). A period of time spanning across the study break will be set aside for data gathering, vital to your final assignment. This means that formal classes will run for ten of the available twelve weeks. You will also be required to attend one 45 minute symposium that is additional to the normal classroom workshops - Assessment Item # below details the requirements for this symposium. You are also expected to conduct a short, sharp ethnographic project.

Participation

A participation and engagement grade will be derived from participation in the weekly workshops. The workshops aim at preparing you to conduct a short, sharp ethnographic study and to then write up your research into "an ethnography". So-called "flipped" forms of delivery aim at shifting the face-to-face engagement between student and teacher away from lectures to various forms of symposia actively engaging students in processes of discovery and consolidation of knowledge. While the flip is often described in terms of delivering unit material using an LMS, the real flip is from teaching to learning.
The expectation is that students are actively engaged in the workshops, drawing from the ideas explored and developed through the online. Your preparedness for the classroom discussions and activities will be monitored and graded at the conclusion of each meeting, the template for which is made available in the first meeting.

ASSESSMENT
Participation and Engagement forms 30% of the unit assessment. 
Engagement with the key ideas of the unit will be facilitated through the workshops. Students are expected to complete key research activities each week, to read articles vital to formation of important insights for the unit and to come prepared to share their own understanding and insight into these ideas as well as to share their own research work with colleagues in the class, to listen attentively and to offer and receive feedback on the work conducted for the unit.
ASSESSMENT ITEM 1
Questions of Perspective in Ethnographic Writing
The aim of this assignment is to get you thinking ethnographically, to get you writing and aware of your preferred research position/stance/perspective AND to give you some early feedback on your writing and the directions you are starting to take in the unit.
You have 500 words (approximately one page of single-spaced writing) to do two things:
1. State your preferred research perspective and the methodological implications of this –explain the ways in which it shapes research practice 
2. Recount and reflect upon an observation or incident from the initial fieldwork you have been doing in the first month of the unit ensuring that you write from the point of view outlined above.
RESEARCH SYMPOSIUM
Shortly after you have completed your fieldwork you will be required to contribute to a research symposium focused on what you have learned about ethnography in the unit so far. Your contributions should be based firmly on what you have read about ethnography as well as your experiences as a social researcher.
Ideally each symposium will comprise six students (±1) and will last for 30 minutes. Your grade will be judged according to how well prepared you are to address the subject and your ability to contribute to the group discussion through the questions you ask and the connections you make with the ideas and insights brought to the table by your colleagues.
*The task is inspired by the title of a paper by Marshall Sahlins, see Sahlins, M (1999) Two or Three Things that I Know about Culture The Journal of the Royal Anthropological Institute, 5(3):399-42.
MAJOR RESEARCH ASSIGNMENT (Max 3 000 words, Due 3 November)
The culmination point of this unit is the production of your own ethnographic account of an aspect of social life. It will emerge from
research you conduct over the course of the unit, starting in Week 1. The assignment will consolidate key aspects of your learning
through the semester, the content of which will be negotiated in class through a series of exercises designed to prepare you to conduct an ethnographic study.
The details of what is expected of you will emerge through the semester; suffice it to say at this stage that the final paper will be written for an ethnographically oriented audience as a submission for publication in an online student journal such as the Journal of Undergraduate Ethnography (see http://undergraduateethnography.org)
The expectations for the submission will be made clearer in due course. The major milestones for the project include:
31 August – Choosing your project (as part of a group researching in a similar domain of activity)
14 September – Project Clearance – including ethical practice clearance
21 September – 5 October – Data Collection Phase
12-16 October – Research Symposium (Assessment Item 2)
19 October – peer feedback on draft
From 23 October – Upload Final Assignment

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
