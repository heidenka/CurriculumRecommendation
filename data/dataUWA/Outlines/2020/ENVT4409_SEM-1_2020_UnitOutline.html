Academic Information

Content This unit introduces students to the concepts and methods of remote sensing of the environment. This includes the use of optical, hydroacoustic and other remote sensing methods to acquire accurate and reliable environmental data, and the processing and manipulation of this information with an appreciation for how those data may be combined with other geospatial data to analyse practical problems. The unit places emphasis on the analysis of remote sensing in a range of contexts, from catchment to coast, including measuring and monitoring environmental processes and applying outputs to scientific and management outcomes. The unit covers topics including principles of remote sensing; the advantages and disadvantages of different remote sensing data sources; pre-processing and geometric correction of data; incorporation of outputs into a geographic information system; quantitative and qualitative analysis of remotely-sensed environmental data, as well as accuracy and errors assessment of derived outputs. Emphasis is placed on practical training and examples in an effort to give the student a solid foundation in the use of remotely-sensed data for a variety of multidisciplinary applications.

Unit Information

The goal of this unit is to develop a practical understanding of remote sensing data and its analysis; develop problem-solving with remote sensing data and methods; 2 acquire knowledge and skills across a range of applications. Lectures cover historical and contemporary applications of remote sensing data and methods, solving problems with remotes sensing data, remote sensing theory and application, sourcing remote sensing data for different tasks, georeferencing, atmospheric correction and development of remote sensing indices, collecting ground truth data, classification and modeling methods, error assessment and incorporating remote sensing data into a spatial analyses workflow, cartography. Learning And Teaching Strategy

This unit introduces students to the concepts and methods of remote sensing of the environment. This includes the use of optical remote sensing to acquire accurate and reliable environmental data, and the processing and manipulation of remotely sensed information with an appreciation for how those data may be combined with other geospatial data in an information system. The unit places emphasis on the analysis of remote sensing in a range of contexts, including environmental processes and management, urban planning, and agriculture. The unit covers topics including principles of radiant energy; the advantages and disadvantages of aerial and orbital remote sensing systems; radiometric and geometric correction; pre-processing of data for incorporation into a geographic information system; quantitative and qualitative analysis of remotely sensed environmental data, as well as accuracy assessment of derived outputs. Emphasis is placed on practical training in an effort to make the student a competent user of remotely sensed data for a variety of geographic and multidisciplinary applications.

Unit Structure

The unit has been developed using mixed learning techniques. The primary contact for this unit is comprised of 10 lecture and lab sessions which occur over an 11-week period (with 1 week of no labs or lectures with study week). During this period, students will work through a series of laboratory exercises accompanied by instructor-led discussions. Finally, each student will plan and complete an independent project (in the form of a research poster as an interactive online Story Map format). The final research project will be due five weeks after the final contact period.
Coarse assessment consists of three assignments a) an in lab multichoice quiz b) a remote sensing project outline c) a final remote sensing project. An in class multiple choice quiz detailing topics covered in the course so far will be given on Friday 24th of April.

The unit coordinator will then provide feedback on this project plan which will hopefully help the student successfully complete their project.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
