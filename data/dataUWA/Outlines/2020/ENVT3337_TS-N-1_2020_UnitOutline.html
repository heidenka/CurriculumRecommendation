Academic Information

Content Students investigate the agricultural production environmental impact nexus, in a real world context by either participating in a field trip to Vietnam or working on a one-on-one basis with an individual UWA academic advisor on developing a case study. For students participating in the field trip, the focus can be at the project management level, working with office based staff; primarily in the field via extension and data collection work; or a combination of both. For students not participating in the field, the case study context is framed around an active project at The International Centre for Tropical Agriculture Vietnam office, which may overlap with some of the field trip based projects, and students still have the opportunity to participate in skype project meetings, and work on real world collaborations, including with Vietnamese CIAT researchers working in partnership with UWA staff. All students, regardless of field trip participation, are exposed to international research organisations and the process of developing a research project, assessing the risks associated with the delivery of that research project, and primary data collection in the field. Through a process of reflective practice, students develop an understanding of the practical challenges of implementing effective environmental and natural resource management policy in a developing country context, and understand the implications of policy changes on rural communities.

ENVT3337 - International Fieldwork Project - TS-N-1-2020 - 2020 - Face to face / Blended learning

Unit Information

This Unit is new in concept and, because of its overseas component, has a special format and teaching period. 
It includes a first part overseas before Christmas and a second part at UWA which starts with semester 1 but finishes early, towards end of April. 
The main costs of the overseas field trip (airfare and accommodation) will be covered through a special grant by UWA's Global Learning Office (GLO). 
The purpose of the Unit is twofold: 1) to give students the opportunity to discover and better understand an Asian country (Vietnam), a goal inherited from the federal government's New Colombo Plan initial funding of this program, and 2) to introduce the students to something they do not typically learn at University: the real-world challenges of implementing projects, programs or policies. In both cases, the main focus will be on the management of natural resources (land, water, forests, air quality, etc.)  
As a result, students will meet and get the opportunity to discuss implementation challenges with external program managers who have been personally involved in trying to makes things happen on the ground. depending on the problem at hand, they can also be asked to compare how similar or how different such challenges are in countries like Australia and Vietnam. 
This Unit does not have a final exam and classes are mostly run like workshops, some with external professionals who share their experience with the students. In all cases, student participation and engagement under the guidance of their teachers will be the focus of their learning experience.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

As this is a totally new Unit and the format is itself bot new and unusual, the way this Unit will be run will in part develop on the go, adapting to student needs and available resources. But the essential idea is as follows.
The initial two-week overseas field trip (in principle taking place in Vietnam) aims at providing the students not only with a new life experience and new friendships, but also with the essential data and information which they will use as raw material in the second part of the course (at UWA) to work on. 
Since the focus of this Unit is on the implementation challenges of policies or programs aiming to improve the management of natural resources, students will first be asked, using a specific problem and/or program, to identify and explain as best as possible the existing implementation challenges, whether past or present. they will then be asked to identify and work on possible solutions, as well as to the challenges their proposed solutions might encounter. The learning goal is to get students to learn how to think about such problems in ways that can lead towards realistic and feasible solutions. Instead of generalities, specific case studies or programs will be the primary information material.   

Unit Structure

This Unit is quite special in that it involves overseas fieldwork, which, except for exceptional circumstances, is mandatory for all enrolled students, as it forms the core part of the Unit. Overseas fieldwork will be in Asia, most probably in Vietnam. The Unit is divided into two "legs". The first is the overseas field trip, and the second is the follow-up at UWA. 
First leg: Because of practical reasons involving different teaching periods between northern and southern hemispheres, the (two-week) field trip will take place somewhere between 20 Nov and 10 Dec., from Saturday to Saturday. This leg will involve activities at both a local (Asian) University and/or at a Research Centre (e.g. CIAT in Hanoi). Students will be involved and associated with real local projects. 
Second leg: The Unit will continue after Christmas and the summer vacation, starting on first day of semester and ending towards the end of April - the length of the second leg being 8 weeks. There will be one 3-hour session per week. This leg will build on information gathered during the overseas field trip, as well as on talks given by external presenters, which will lead to final oral and written assessments.    
The Unit will not involve any work during Christmas or the summer vacation, during which time students will be free from study for this Unit.

Given that all classes will be run as highly interactive workshops, student presence is absolutely essential. 
Absence will not be officially sanctioned, but absent students will in fact be sanctioning themselves, as their absence would put them at a disadvantage and at risk of failing the Unit.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
