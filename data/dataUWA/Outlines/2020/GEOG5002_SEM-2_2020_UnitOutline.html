Academic Information

Content Adopting an applied perspective, this unit introduces students to the range of professional contexts engaged in contemporary international development practice. The core focus is on what it means to do development, in a variety of different contexts, and what knowledge and skills a development practitioner needs to have to address real challenges encountered in the field. It introduces students to key actors in international development and the working cultures of large development institutions (e.g. World Bank, UN organisations; non-government development agencies; aid/relief organisations; aid celebrities, and development academics. This exploration is set against theoretical perspectives that inform values, assumptions, rationales and ethics underpinning development practice. Students critically reflect on their own position in development and develop self-reflexive, technical and communication skills required for professionals working in international development, regional planning, and humanitarian aid.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

This semester, the class meets online. All lectures are pre-recorded (following university policy) and will be available 48 hours prior to class (each Sunday 9pm). Both 90min live workshop slots are Tuesdays, online (via Zoom), 9-10:30am and 11am-12:30pm. These workshops are mandatory and require your active participation and critical thinking. 
At the end of this unit you will be able to:
 (1) articulate the roles in and approaches to development practice, including old and new actors and institutions in the development industry; 
(2) critically assess the differences between different approaches to development practice, and embedded opportunities and pitfalls; 
(3) demonstrate how diverse theoretical perspectives of development practice lead to alternative and competing behaviours and actions; and 
(4) apply reflexive, technical, and communication strategies to address particular challenges and novel ways of doing development in practice.

Practice and Practitioners in International Development

This first week provides a basic introduction to practice and practitioners in international development. Who are these practitioners? What do they do? What skills do they have? What challenges do they encounter? What solutions exist when we engage in development practice and which of these seem to work? We will take a first glimpse at knowledge practices and ‘travelling rationalities’ of a variety of actors involved in development practice and at specific strategies to navigate dilemmas in community development. Moreover, we will test emerging insights into power dynamics between different development actors through an in-class simulation regarding the new Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs).

The Development “Expert”

Crises and Danger

The ‘development bubble’ is not only full of contradictions and ethical dilemmas but also prone to risky behaviour and dangerous situations, within and beyond contexts of crisis. During this week, we explore how development/aid/humanitarian workers deal with danger, consciously and unconsciously, and how danger shapes practice. We also examine the ‘work in crisis’ rhetoric, how it governs the lived experiences of the ‘development persona’, and how it produces institutionalised sets of values and distinct techniques of governmentality.

Details for Analytical assignments (with TWO parts: a and b)
a)	Reading & reflection log (30% of the marks for the unit):
Over the course of the unit, you will read several scholarly papers relevant to the learning objectives. In the online workshops, time will be dedicated to discussing and critiquing these papers in order to enhance your conceptual and analytical understanding of key issues in development practice. Reading critically and reflecting on the meaning of the material, including how it makes you feel, are important skills to have and to nourish, in academia and in development work.
Prior to class, you need to have read the assigned papers and completed a ‘Reading & reflection log’ for 17 articles (not counting Week 1). A log is a one-page (11pt Times New Roman, single spaced) précis and critical digestion of each paper. I am not interested (and you won’t benefit) from summaries or slightly modified abstracts. The logs are your reaction to the paper, based on careful reading and reflective and critical thinking. 
Each reading log should start with the full name of all authors and their affiliations (who are they?) – not counted toward the one page –  and then address the following four parts (all 4):
•	What is the core message/argument of the paper? What is/are the author(s) trying to say?
•	What are the strengths and limitations of the paper (i.e. is it well argued, or are there gaps or weaknesses)? What do you like, and what leaves you confused?
•	How does the article affect you? Does it trigger new insights, does it make you feel angry, depressed, does it inspire you, does it challenge you or your way of thinking to the core?
•	What are the key ideas or questions that you would like to discuss or clarify as part of a discussion in class? This is particularly important for the discussants and in preparation for interacting with our guest speakers. 
Reading & reflection logs need to be submitted to the Discussion Board (Forum) on LMS, no later than 6pm Sundays before class! The discussion leader for individual articles will read over your reading log and incorporate ideas for class discussion from your suggestions. They will also assess how well the four bullet points above are addressed in each log.
Marking criteria (17 pts per article) :
Evidence of clear understanding of the arguments, theories, methods, and limitations of the paper, as presented by the author(s) (8pts);
Ability to raise critical questions, spot ambiguities or novel ideas, and state emotions (6 pts);
Quality of the summary: good grammar, eloquent writing style, perfect spelling (3 pts). 
b)	Development blogging/tweeting and debate (20% of the final marks for the unit)
Blogging and tweeting have enhanced the visibility of voices in international development on the internet and social media. In this assessment, you are asked to follow ONE blog and ONE hashtag from the list below over the course of the trimester, attempt to actively engage in some debates of your choice (either via posting a blog/tweet or commenting on it), reflect on your virtual encounters, and share your experience with your peers in class.
Over the course of ~ 6 weeks (from 15 September 2020 onward), you will individually follow and participate in one blog and one hashtag. We will ensure that you all follow different debates. Then, in teams of two, reflect on the following five bullets and prepare a 20min long presentation of your respective experiences for presentation in our online workshop the last day of class (20 October). I will provide more details before the Study Break (Week 40). Your team member will function as peer-evaluator for your blogging and tweeting throughout the ~6 weeks. 
•	Who writes/blogs/tweets and on what themes? Consider gender, profession, status, organization, age, country of residence and/or origin;

Submission Details and Late Penalties 
Submit your written assignment (critical essay) on the due date, on time, through the LMS portal. Your assignment must be uploaded as .doc, .docx file. 
In accordance with UWA’s assessment policy, a penalty of 5% of the possible mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays), after which the assigned work is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due. For example, if you submit an assessment item late by three days, and it was scored a mark of 45 out of 50, you would receive an actual mark of 37.5 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day as a penalty). 
Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached. The same rule applies for an assessment under the required word range.
If you believe you will have trouble submitting before the deadline due to reasons beyond your control, please apply for Special Consideration through the Faculty of Science (personal reasons or illness). If you are experiencing difficulties specific to the academic requirements of the assignment, please make an appointment to see the unit co-ordinator.

Student Complaints Protocol
The Department of Geography and Planning operates a carefully designed protocol with respect to student complaints. These apply to the delivery of any unit with the prefix GEOG or PLNG. All matters are followed up, whether these are made in person, via email or in writing. Strict confidentiality and anonymity is applied at all stages. 
Student complaints relating to administration, teaching, supervision or the conduct of either staff or students should be directed to the relevant Head of Teaching and Learning within the Department. Student complaints relating to any other matter should be directed to the Responsible Officer as detailed at https://www.web.uwa.edu.au/university/complaints/students/complaint-policy-and-procedures
The Departmental Heads of Teaching and Learning are currently Dr Sarah Prout Quicke (sarah.proutquicke@uwa.edu.au) for undergraduate units and Dr Linda Robson (linda.robson@uwa.edu.au) for postgraduate units. In the event that the complaint involves either Head of Teaching and Learning, the complaint should be directed to the other Head. 
Any complaint from a student will be acknowledged in writing within 2 working days of receipt. 
Complaints received by the Head of Teaching and Learning will be followed up by an invitation to the student to meet with the relevant Head of Teaching and Learning, together with the Head of Department (currently Dr Julian Clifton). This discussion will be an opportunity to explore the background to the complaint and discuss it in more detail, not to determine the validity of the complaint. The student is welcome to bring along a friend or other supporting individual if s/he wishes. Staff may take notes during this meeting but these records will be strictly confidential and not divulged to anyone outside the meeting.
The Head of Department and the Head of Teaching and Learning will then hold a separate meeting with the member of staff identified within the complaint. This will explore the staff perspective on the complaint. The identity of the complainant will not be revealed, nor will any material be used which could identify the complainant. 
Both of the above meetings will be held within 10 working days of acknowledging the complaint.
Within five working days of the above, the Head of Department and the Head of Teaching and Learning will reach a decision on the complaint and communicate this via email to the student and member of staff involved. The student retains the right to appeal this decision. 
Further information on the official complaint procedure is available at https://www.web.uwa.edu.au/university/complaints/students/submit

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
