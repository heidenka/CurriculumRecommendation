Academic Information

Content The project is a capstone experience in which students integrate and apply knowledge and skills acquired in earlier units to a substantial real-world design challenge relevant to their discipline. Students work in teams, typically with four to six members, and are supervised by an academic and/or industry mentor. Teams are responsible for managing their project as well as reporting against milestones and preparing the necessary design documentation. The academic objectives of the project are to lead students through a systematic engineering design and synthesis process and to better prepare them for professional practice as engineers. The focus of the unit is on teamwork skills and the design process.

Unit Information

Design 1 is a capstone unit in which students work in teams on a design project similar to those completed by professional engineers. All teams work on the same design brief. The process, teamwork and reporting are all important. Design 1 is preparation for Design 2 when each team works more closely with the project partner who offered their design brief. The units have been carefully designed in collaboration with experienced engineers. Many graduates report that the design units are highly relevant in their work.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

In the Design units students work on authentic engineering design projects in teams. Disruptions, ill-defined problems, messy data, and constraints are intentional, authentic features of the projects. Students must consider stakeholders in defining the problem and requirements and they learn to use engineering processes, communication, and teamwork. To complete their projects, students need to learn technical topics they have not yet learned, and make professional judgements within time and knowledge constraints. They learn to practise professionally, ethically, safely, and sustainably, earning the trust that society places in engineers.
The unit has up to two hours of lectures per week, some of which are given by highly experienced industry members. Student teams work on the design project throughout semester. The team's supervisor attends a one-hour team meeting each week.  In Design 1 the students also meet the project partner, who offered the project, during lectures. 

Unit Structure

Lectures are held every week except weeks 9 and 12.
Team meetings attended by supervisors are held in weeks 2 to 12.
Students work in their teams during the week.

Participation

Students are expected to work with their teams for 7 hours during the week. Collaborating together face-to-face is most effective. 
For students to write their reflections, they will be expected to refer to learning in lectures, as well as in their teams. The lectures are designed for face-to-face attendance. Although recordings are available afterwards, many of the lectures involve interaction. The lectures scheduled for meetings with the project partner are  for students to ask technical questions. All of the lectures are most valuable when students attend and participate. 
Weekly student team meetings attended by the supervisor are compulsory for students. Students are assessed on professionalism during the meetings. 

Referencing Requirements

The unit has templates as used by professional engineers. These include a generic document template, technical query template, technical query register template, risk register template, agenda and minutes templates, and a design review meeting template. IEEE referencing style is used. 

Texts

The unit does not have a textbook. It has recommended reading, listed in the Unit Readings on the learning management system. Students also access standards and costs guides to complete their design projects.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
