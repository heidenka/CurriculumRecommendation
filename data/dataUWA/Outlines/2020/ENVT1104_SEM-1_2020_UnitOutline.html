Academic Information

Content Through the exploration of six key thematic areas, this unit introduces students to the drivers and consequences of the most significant environmental challenges of our time, in addition to critically assessing the basis for potential solutions. Essential concepts in environmental physics, chemistry and biology are introduced throughout each module to develop essential knowledge and skills within the broader context of environmental science. This material forms the basis of more focused content delivered in Levels 2 and 3 of the Environmental Science and Geographical Science majors. The unit content is delivered through modules related to: climate change and carbon pollution; catchment development and water resource management; biodiversity loss and conservation; land degradation; coastal systems; and cities. Through these lenses, the unit looks to explain lessons from the past using both local and global examples to ensure students establish the connection between environmental processes and the manifestation of environmental degradation and recovery. Tutorial sessions are included to complement key lectures to encourage students to participate in activities related to problem identification and conceptualisation, and critical assessment of diverse information sources. In addition, each module has a component related to data analysis and the development of written and oral communication skills.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

All lecture material will be available either through our online resource LaunchPad, or as pdf files in Blackboard. You should not attempt to take down everything that is said or presented – they are available online. Instead take note of key concepts and learning outcomes included at the start of each lecture and listen for main points.  Use notes as a guide only – you are expected to use the resource material provided to you to expand on the material presented in lectures – as a tertiary education student you are responsible for keeping up with assessments and pre- and post-lecture reading. Workshop and laboratory classes are designed to complement your learning - these will provide the foundation for your environmental science learning journey. Material for both workshops and labs will be made available - where specified you should ensure you have undertaken any pre-reading required.

Unit Structure

Lectures will take place once a week, Thursdays at 11am in the Woolnough LT which is in the Geography and Geology Building. Lectures will generally take the following format:
1.	Main content lecture delivered by the module lecturer
2.	Guest lecture from a staff member of the UWA School of Agriculture and Environment (SAgE) or from an industry person with environmental science expertise
Each lecture session will run as follows:
•	Content lecture: 11-11.45am
Short break – 15 minutes
•	Guest Lecture: 12noon-12.45pm
The main content lecture will closely follow the material from our assigned textbook – Environment: Science, Issues, Solutions by Manuel Molles and Brendan Borrell. This textbook will be provided to you via our online portal (Blackboard with a link to LaunchPad for the ebook). Details of how to access the ebook and associated resources are provided in the unit guidebook. The campus bookshop will have hard copies of the text if you wish to purchase this, however it is not necessary as you will have full access to the ebook. Hard copies will also be available in the library for those who wish to read a paper textbook. Please note this unit will be run as much as is feasible as a paperless unit.
The second component of each lecture will be a guest lecture on a key research or industry topic that is related to the main content lecture. Both these components will be recorded, and slides made available online where applicable. We encourage you to attend as many lectures as you can to get the most out of your time at University.
Workshops, laboratory classes, industry engagement and field trips complement and enhance learning in this unit - attendance at these classes is highly recommended - in addition to supporting material covered in lectures these classes will also cover assessment in the unit.

Participation

It is recommended that you attend as many lectures as possible. While lectures are recorded and you can download and listen to them at other times, many lectures incorporate class discussions and activities that are more valuable if you are present to participate. In addition each lecture will feature a guest speaker from either the academic staff of the University or from an Industry body – these guest speakers will highlight recent developments in their field and again will incorporate Q&A sessions as part of their presentation.
On-campus workshop sessions will take place each week as outlined in the unit guidebook. You are expected to attend and attendance records will be taken at each workshop session. The format of each workshop will vary from week to week and in general workshop sessions have been designed to support your understanding of the material delivered during the lecture sessions, in addition to providing a pathway to successful assessment grades. Details of each assessment item will be provided in workshop sessions as well as support to complete these assessments successfully. Absence from workshop classes and failure to submit assignments without good reason are likely to result in failure of the unit.
Please note you will be required to attend to workshop classes specifically related to your unit grade as follows: (1) oral presentation assessment during your normal workshop class in week 6. If you cannot attend you must apply for special consideration from the Faculty Office and an alternative session must be attended. (2) Workshop class in week 12 of semester where there will be a compulsory practical class quiz – this is a face to face quiz and will not run online. If you cannot attend you must apply for special consideration from the Faculty Office and an alternative session must be attended.

Late Penalties: A penalty of 5 per cent of the possible mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays), after which the assigned work is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due. For example, a student, who has submitted a piece of work for an assessment item late by three days and was scored a mark of 45 out of a possible mark of 50, receives after penalty a mark of 30 out of 50 (a mark of 5 is deducted per day as a penalty for each student from the mark obtained). Where there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied holistically to the assessment item after all tasks have been completed.
Assessments submitted later than 7 days after the deadline receive a mark of zero, unless an application for mitigation is approved in accordance with the University Policy on Assessment: Special Consideration (UP11/23).
Word Count: Where an assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached. The penalty for exceeding word limit for an assessment item/task in a unit must be clearly communicated to the student.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
