Academic Information

Content This unit provides students with hands-on experience in field research, group work and leadership with an explicit focus on geographical and planning problems. The unit requires students, working independently and in small groups, to undertake a field-based research project on a geography/urban planning-related issue in a designated location. Students are required to (i) undertake background research on the field site; (ii) design and implement appropriate research methods/tools to collect relevant primary data; (iii) analyse and interpret all primary and secondary data collected; and (iv) present their research plan/strategy and results in a written report and/or presentation.

Unit Information

GEOG3302 is a capstone unit for students completing the BA Human Geography and Planning, and, the BSc Geographical Sciences). It builds on GEOG2202 (Reading Landscapes: People and Processes) as well as other units you have covered during your studies at UWA, especially units taken in Semester 1 of your final year. 
GEOG3302 provides you with an opportunity to put your academic and technical knowledge, ideas and skills into action via developing and executing a research proposal within the fields of human geography/planning, geographical sciences; and/or, environmental science. 
As a research project-based unit in 2020, GEOG3302 is an opportunity for students to demonstrate an ability to think and act (i) independently, (ii) collaboratively; and, (iii) analytically in terms of:
(a)	developing a scoping report/literature review and research proposal;
(b)	collecting and analysing data;
(c)	communicating research results via an oral presentation and written report; and,
(d)	developing time management and people skills and understanding the importance of the need to work both independently and collaboratively to complete assigned tasks.
For 2020 the unit involves a series of interactive seminars (Monday) and workshops (Tuesday) that will: (i) examine key aspects of the research process; and (ii) provide students with space and time to meet and collaborate with one another in order to develop their research proposal and complete their project.
In order to get the most of out of the unit in terms of developing your ‘soft’ and ‘hard’ research skills, effective time management and successful completion of all assignments you will need to attend all classes, especially tutes, on a regular basis.
 Classes on Monday will be content-driven and interactive in nature; they will be held in-person and streamed/recorded via Zoom for those unable to attend in-person due to social distancing issues relating to COVID-19. Classes on Tuesday are workshop-based and require students to work in and with their research project team members. The small group-based and interactive nature of these classes means that they will be held face-to-face. 
In an effort to maximise social distancing Tuesday’s class has been split into 2 x 1 hour sessions in an effort to have a 50/50 split in terms of student numbers in each session.
The overall research theme for the research projects this year is: Sustainability, Vibrancy and Liveability: A Geographic/Environmental/Planning Approach
The unit requires students to work INDEPENDENTLY and COLLABORATIVELY in order to develop a critical appreciation and understanding of the key issues and challenges associated with designing and implementing a structured and manageable research project.
In simple terms, geographic/environmental/planning research involves a number of key steps/tasks: 
1. ‘Searching, reading and reviewing’;
2. ‘Planning, designing and scoping’; 
3. ‘Implementing and collecting’; and
4. ‘Collating, analysing and interpreting; and 
5. ‘Communicating’.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

There will be a series of interactive seminar-based classes highlighting the key aspects of the research process. These will act as the foundations for you to develop an understanding of how to approach the development of your scoping report/lit review and research proposal.
The workshop-based classes on Tuesday are primarily to be used for students to work/collaborate in their respective project teams.
Students are required to work in small teams comprising between 2-3 members - absolutely no more than 3 members per team! – for all assignments in this unit: (i) scoping report and research proposal; (ii) research project presentation; and, (iii) final report.
Dr Paul Maginn will provide overall strategic research advice and guidance to all students throughout the unit/semester. In addition Dr Maginn will also directly supervise those students that conduct projects within his areas of interest/expertise within Human Geography/Planning. Those students who conduct a project related to geographical science/environmental science will receive supervisory support from other academic staff within the geography/planning discipline.
In summary, the teaching and learning strategy of this unit is to develop your critical appreciation of the challenging, dynamic and rewarding nature of designing and implementing a research project.
Course Aims
The overall learning objectives are:
(1) To enhance your academic and/or policy literature search and review skills; 
(2) To enhance your data collection, management and analysis skills;
(3) To communicate your research ideas, proposals and findings in an thoughtful, analytical and creative way via written/textual/oral/visual expression; and
(4) Demonstrate an ability to work independently and collaboratively and enhance your time and team management skills.

Unit Structure

Classes for GEOG3302 are held on Monday (2.00-4.00pm) and Tuesday (4.00-6.00pm).
Monday's classes for the first half of the semester will be content-driven and will cover key issues in the research process. Classes will be face-to-face and simulcast via Zoom for those students who are unable to attend in-person due to issues relating to COVID-19. In the second half of the semester, Monday's classes will effectively be used to provide a dedicated time/space for students to meet with their project team members.
Tuesday's classes are more tute-based and will involve group-based discussion and other activities that directly relate to your research project. Classes here have been split into 2 x 1 hour sessions in an effort to maximise social distancing. Ideally, all students should attend the relevant Tuesday session that they are assigned too. 
Please do advise me if attending classes in person will be a major issue for any student.
In addition to the scheduled teaching sessions for GEOG3302 I will also organise a series of virtual drop-in sessions for students to discuss their research projects/assignments. Details to be confirmed after Week 1 of teaching.

Referencing Requirements

All assignments require that you use a diverse mix of reference materials - academic, policy, media and grey literature - to inform your thinking and analysis. Any materials that you use to either directly and/or indirectly inform your assignments should be duly noted using standard academic referencing methods in terms of (i) in-text references/citations; (ii) end-of-text reference lists; (iii) direct quotes from sources; (iv) data from official sources; (v) photography and/or any other visual imagery sourced from the internet, books, magazines and/or other forms of media.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.

Special Considerations
