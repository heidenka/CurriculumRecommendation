Academic Information

Content This unit introduces a variety of imaging and analytical methods used in the characterisation of materials/biomaterials and associated cells and tissue, with a particular focus toward biomedical engineering applications. It will complement the BMEG4002 Biomaterials unit. Topics are likely to include (1) imaging bulk materials and surfaces using light and electrons (light microscopy, confocal microscopy, super-resolution optical microscopy, scanning and transmission electron microscopy); (2) crystal structure analysis using electrons and X-rays (electron diffraction, X-ray diffraction); (3) element analysis using electrons (energy-dispersive X-ray spectroscopy, electron energy-loss spectroscopy). Students gain insight into how these technologies are used in materials/biomaterials characterisation and to understand the interaction between these materials and living tissues. Content includes theoretical principles of the techniques, as well as practical data analysis, critique and interpretation.

SCIE5516 - Materials Characterisation for Bioengineering Applications - SEM-1-2020 - 2020 - Face to face / Blended learning

Unit Information

The unit is designed to introduce students to the capabilities and applications of a range of imaging and analytical techniques useful for the characterisation of the structure and composition of materials used in Biomedical Engineering, including 2D imaging techniques (light microscopy, scanning electron microscopy, transmission electron microscopy), 3D imaging techniques (light microscopy, Focused Ion Beam), crystallographic techniques (atomic resolution imaging, electron diffraction, X-ray diffraction), and elemental analysis techniques (energy-dispersive X-ray spectroscopy, electron energy-loss spectroscopy). Content will be delivered through a series of introductory lectures (Monday & Tuesday) and interactive tutorials (Thursday afternoon).

Lecture Capture System

In accordance with UWA’s policy on lecture capture all lectures* in enabled venues will be recorded and made available to stream and download by enrolled students via the Lecture Capture System (LCS) for the duration of the unit (including Supplementary and Deferred Examinations period).

* Please note that in some limited circumstances lectures may not be recorded due to:  ethical and/or security related issues; the use of commercially sensitive material; the use of third-party copyright teaching material; or culturally sensitive content. Students will be advised, in advance, when this applies.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

The unit structure is outlined below. Each topic will be presented by an expert in that technique from UWA's Centre for Microscopy, Characterisation and Analysis (CMCA). The lectures, which will be recorded, will provide an introduction to each technique and examples of how it can be applied for materials characterisation. The Thursday tutorials, which will not be recorded, will complement the lectures by looking at data interpretation and analysis either in a classroom or computer room environment. The tutorials will be interactive, with students working individually or in small groups to complete data interpretation and analysis tasks, and online polling being used to provide immediate feedback and identify areas that require further self-guided study.
The tutorials will directly feed into Assessments 1 & 2, providing students with the necessary skills and knowledge to tackle each of the assessments, and creating opportunities for feedback. 

Unit Structure

The unit comprises standard lectures on Monday & Tuesday and a 2-hour tutorial on Thursday afternoon. Lectures will be used to deliver background material on techniques and their applications. Each technique will be presented by an expert from the Centre for Microscopy, Characterisation and Analysis. The lecture content will provide knowledge about the techniques to support the tutorials and underpin the examination. There will be no lecture on first day of semester, and we will lose the Monday lectures on March 2 and April 27 to public holidays.
The tutorials will be interactive individual or small groups activities where you will learn how to interpret and analyse data from the various techniques. The data processing and analysis skills covered in the tutorials will be used to complete assignments 1 and 2. All of the tutorials are core to the unit and there are no options. Participation in all tutorials is essential to obtain all of the skills needed to complete the assignments. A more detailed breakdown of the activities each week can be found in the Weekly Outline  and in the sub-sections under UNIT MATERIALS available in LMS.

Participation

Participation in Thursday tutorials is essential to develop the skills needed to undertake Assignments 1 & 2 successfully. Failure to participate in the Thursday tutorials will make it very difficult to complete the assignments and exam.

Referencing Requirements

If students choose to include references in their reports for Assessments 1 & 2, they will be expected to adopt the Harvard citation style.

Texts

The unit covers a broad range of imaging and analytical techniques. There is no single text that covers all of this material. Texts available through the library providing more detailed knowledge of each of the techniques covered in the unit have been identified in the 'Unit Readings' section in LMS. Students are also encouraged to search the scientific literature for research papers describing applications of these techniques if they seek more examples of their application to Biomedical Engineering materials.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
