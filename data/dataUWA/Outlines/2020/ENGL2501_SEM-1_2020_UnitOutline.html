Unit Information

This unit of study in the writer's craft aims to stimulate and guide students' creative writing, while considering the intersection between theory and practice inherent in writing as an act. Ultimately, it is about understanding the manner in which creative writing is a socially-engaged endeavour. The unit engages with key theoretical approaches to the literary discipline in order to understand the relationship between the writer and society, contemporary literary forms, discourses and language. 
Students attend workshops and submit writing in the genre of their choice, working in poetry or prose fiction. The unit workshops will offer students the opportunity to experiment in writing in a variety of different styles and forms, as well as develop draft work before submission of the final piece(s) in assignments. These workshop discussions offer a safe space to extend your skills and test your ideas, as well as honing your theoretical approach. Lectures and workshops both consider a mixture of received literature and students' own works. The unit aims to support all students in developing a piece of writing which, by the end of the course, could be submitted to a publication.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

This unit involves two main learning and teaching activities. 
1) Lectures:
The lectures introduce and explore the theoretical modules that we will work through in the unit. The LMS provides access to the relevant weekly readings, texts which we will interrogate in each lecture as examples of creative practice which might be connected to a specific theoretical context. The different theoretical modules we move through will show the manner in which our contemporary writing is shaped by literary history, whether we are conscious of it or not. As such, it connects an individual's writing to broader social discourse and movements, and suggests to us the need to consider the movements and thinking informing our own work.
2) Tutorial workshops: 
Tutorials are a space to explore the lecture content, experiment with related writing practice and to workshop creative material. The LMS provides access to the relevant weekly readings, and the online space for workshopping - facility to upload your files for sharing with your tutorial, and discussion threads for providing workshop feedback to each piece of work. Workshopping is an important aspect of this unit, and will directly support your development of assessment material. We will be using the Critical Response Process in the workshops, which will be introduced in the first lecture. (Your involvement is also graded as part of your final mark.) Please refer to the Guidelines for Workshopping on the LMS. 

Unit Structure

The unit comprises of:
- One 1hr lecture weekly, moving through a range of writing contexts. Lectures are held Monday 10am, in the Fox Lecture Theatre (G59, Arts building). Due to public holidays, some lectures will be online as podcasts/videos. These will be offered via the LMS. 
- One 2hr tutorial weekly, comprising of discussion surrounding set readings, creative exercises, and class workshopping of submitted creative material. All students will be required to submit and present creative work for class workshop. Tutorials begin in Week One, with a hiatus in Week Two (to allow for the preparation of workshopping) and workshopping commencing in Week Three.

Participation

Students are required to attend (or listen to) all lectures, and attend all tutorials. As the tutorials involve workshopping, and will not be recorded, they are mandatory in-person participation. Attendance will be taken. 
Participation in class, whether it is listening to a lecture or getting involved in other activities, is an important part of the learning process, therefore it is important that you attend classes. More formally, the University regulations state that ‘to complete a course or unit students shall attend prescribed classes, lectures, seminars and tutorials’. Where a student, due to exceptional circumstances, is unable to attend a scheduled class, they are required to obtain prior approval of the unit coordinator to be absent from that class. Any student absent from class without having had such absence approved by the unit coordinator may be referred to the faculty for advice and may be required to withdraw from the unit. 
Our protocol for workshopping will follow the Critical Response Model. This be discussed in the lecture in Week One, and is outlined on the LMS, along with guidelines for the principles of workshopping engagement.
Tutorials commence in Week One. Due to Labour Day falling on Monday 2nd of March (Week Two), there will not be tutorials in Week Two, they will resume instead in Week Three, when workshopping will also commence. You will be expected to prepare for the first workshop during the week off. The lecture for Week Two will still be offered, and will be made available as a podcast/video recording via the LMS. 

Referencing Requirements

Referencing or citing your sources is an important part of academic writing. It lets you acknowledge the ideas or words of others if you use them in your work and helps avoid plagiarism. The dangers are more immediate in the online era, when it is tempting to cut and paste. You can do this, but you need to follow the rules around quoting and citation. 
The requirement to reference applies to creative writing drawing from and/or citing secondary sources just as it does to critical writing in your Folio Outline and/or Creative Folio. If you have any questions about how (and when) to reference within your creative work, consult the UWA Library guides (links below) and/or talk to your tutor. 
For a general introduction to referencing at UWA see: http://guides.library.uwa.edu.au/referencinguwa
Every scholarly discipline has a preferred format or style for citing sources. Our preferred style for citing sources in English and Literary Studies is the MLA documentation style. For basic instructions on how to use this style see: http://guides.library.uwa.edu.au/mla.

Texts

All texts required for this unit are short readings and/or excerpts, and will be made available via Unit Readings. A link to each text will be provided on the LMS. 
You are expected to have read the set readings for each week before the lecture (Monday morning). This will be essential to you following the lecture content, which will regularly be based in textual analysis. We make no apology for plot spoilers, if you haven't read the readings before the week they are discussed! 
Some texts will involve explicit content, and content relating to sensitive themes and ideas. (See 'Explicit Material', below.) If you are particularly concerned about any specific topic, please discuss this with your tutor in advance, and you will be excused from reading any related texts. If you are concerned about any text, you will not be forced to read it, and can leave the class while the text is being discussed. You must, however, attend the class in person to explain this to the tutor, and return to the room at the point the relevant discussion has concluded, or you will lose the participation credit for that tutorial.

EXPLICIT MATERIAL
The unit proceeds on the basis that students are adults and the texts that are explored do contain adult material. All the texts in this unit are freely available on public and commercial services or in bookstores and libraries. If you feel that a text contains material that may be upsetting, please let your tutor or unit coordinator know and an alternative arrangement will be discussed. You are never compelled to watch or read a text if you do not want to for personal reasons. 
Because these issues are personal, it is very hard to predict in advance for all students if certain scenes or themes may be upsetting. We therefore empower students to take responsibility for exercising discretion and encourage you to take reasonable precautions if you feel you are vulnerable; for example, by Googling texts and checking that they are not likely to contain material that you find upsetting.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
