In the Visual Art learning area students develop creative skills, critical appreciation and knowledge of artistic techniques and technologies in visual arts. Study of Visual Art develops students’ sense of personal and cultural identity and equips them for lifelong involvement in and appreciation of the arts. Art is a fundamental part of human life. Throughout history the visual arts have given form to and communicated ideas and feelings, providing a way for individuals to express experience. The Visual Art course of study is an introduction to the practice and theory of the broad areas of art, craft and design. Through developing problem solving skills together with creative and analytical ways of thinking, students will gain visual literacy and communication skills enabling them to become discriminating in their judgements. As teachers of Visual Art we need to understand the complexity of a learning area which at times deals with static, traditional and conventional material and other times with the dynamic, challenging and controversial. The course aims to contribute to a sense of enjoyment, engagement and fulfilment in everyday life as well as promote appreciation for the environment and ecological sustainability. I look forward to sharing your passion for your discipline as we explore ways of engaging secondary students in a rich and stimulating study of Visual Art. 
The Visual Art Curriculum I unit is designed to provide an introduction to the knowledge, skills and competencies required of teachers of lower secondary school visual art courses. It focuses on the specific learning outcomes and implementation strategies associated with the Visual Art Learning Area in the Curriculum Framework. Practical workshops throughout the semester will model the different studio areas in the Visual Arts. The unit begins with a Reciprocal Mentoring Program with a local College, where our students work with both teachers and students in an introduction to exemplarily practice. Students must have Working with Children check completed prior to attending the College. 
You will develop an understanding of how students learn most effectively in Visual Art and be able to use this understanding to plan and implement learning programs. It is important to realise that the outcomes in the Visual Art Learning Area encompass concepts from several disciplines and therefore you may be required to come to grips with concepts and content outside the scope of your degree studies. The unit aims to develop resourceful teachers who are sufficiently flexible and creative to initiate and implement effective teaching programs. It endeavours to make students sensitive to the needs and characteristics of learners as individuals and in groups. It also hopes to develop in you an infectious enthusiasm for the Visual Art Learning Area, an awareness of your own attitudes to teaching and a reflective approach to the effectiveness of your teaching.
Participation in a Reciprocal Mentoring program  in the first few weeks of the unit will require you to have your Working with Children Check completed . Excursions to The Art Gallery of Western Australia as well as The Lawrence  Wilson Art Gallery will assist in enriching your understanding of community links and object based learning.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

This unit is delivered in a face to face model. As far as possible, the teaching and learning strategies adopted in this course are designed to develop the skills and competencies required for teaching Visual Art in Years 7  to 10. It is assumed that you have some background in your degree in at least one of the Visual Art disciplines and therefore, this is not a unit in art, but is about teaching visual art. However, a practical focus has been built into this course to enable you to be better prepared for the busy classroom. It is expected that you attend each lecture/seminar.

Assessment 1: Iona Reciprocal Mentoring Program
Through this program you will spend one full school day within Iona Presentation College's Art Department, interacting with staff and students in a variety of classes and co-curricular activities.  The objective for this program is to provide you with an introductory ‘insight’ into the everyday, working life of a College, prior to any formally assessed school placements.  In recent years, students from the program have been involved in many departmental activities, workshops and have also returned in Term 4 to assist with setting up the Annual Creative Arts Showcase. 
Assessment 2: Lesson Planning
Using an appropriate lesson planning format as outlined in class, you are required to plan three consecutive 60 minute lessons for a class of 30, mixed ability students in years 7, 8, 9 or 10. You may select any Outcomes within the Outcomes and Standards Framework and use the Curriculum Elaboration as a basis for your lesson. 
Assessment 3: Teaching/ Learning Program 
You are required to prepare a teaching/ learning programme for 20 hours of a unit of work for lower school. A unit of work is normally assumed to be for one term that is 10 weeks in duration, or 20 hours, given that most schools timetable 2 x 60 minute lessons per week in Visual Art. You will therefore be planning for a 10 weeks program. You are expected to plan sequentially for the ten weeks. The programme may address one, two or three studio areas (whatever is most appropriate to your unit of work).
Assessment Rules for Initial Teacher Education Courses
All Initial Teacher Education students must pass all components of a unit to pass the unit overall. If you fail any component of a unit, it is your responsibility to contact the unit coordinator to discuss possible resubmission of the component. You must do so within 48 hours of the assignment being made available for collection as notified by the unit coordinator on LMS or by other means of communication. You may only resubmit one assignment and the maximum grade for a resubmitted component is a Pass. For a complete overview of these rules please consult your Initial Teacher Education Course Handbook.
An Initial Teacher Education student who fails an exam component of any unit at 40% or greater will be permitted supplementary assessment. The maximum grade for this supplementary assessment is a Pass and this will be the maximum final overall grade for the unit.
Please refer to Late Submission of Assignments at the end of this document. Assignments submitted late without an approved extension will incur a penalty of 10 per cent per day with the highest grade possible after a week being a Pass. Initial Teacher Education students are advised to carefully plan their workload and commence work on their assignments early because poor time management will not be accepted as grounds for an extension.

RESUBMISSION RULE
Failure to pass an assessment item within a unit (MTeach)
15. Students must pass all components of a Master of Teaching unit to pass the unit overall. Students who fail an assessment item in a unit may resubmit it if—
(a) this is their first request for a resubmission in the unit;
(b) they contact the unit coordinator by email within 5 University working days of the release of the result and formally request a resubmission.
(c) A resubmitted assignment that is deemed to be a ‘fail’ by the unit coordinator, will receive the original failing mark for the component.
(d) Where resubmission is approved, the reassessed mark is capped at the assessment pass mark, unless an application for mitigation is approved in accordance with the University Policy on Assessment: Special Consideration (UP11/23).
(e) A resubmission is normally due one week after being approved by the unit coordinator.
(f) Assignments that are failed on the grounds of lateness or academic misconduct will not normally be considered for resubmission.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
