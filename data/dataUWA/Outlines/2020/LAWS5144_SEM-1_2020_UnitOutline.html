Academic Information

Content This unit entails an examination and analysis of Mining Law and Oil and Gas Law in Western Australia. It includes consideration of: mineral ownership, land open for mining, native title, theories of crown disposition, administration of mining dispositions, applications including marking out priorities, exploration tenements, production tenements, surrender and forfeiture, exemptions, questions of law, judicial review and appeals, commercial arrangements, oil and gas, state agreements, registration and dealings. The key aims of the unit include the consideration and analysis of differing regimes of disposition of publicly owned resources; an understanding of the advantages and disadvantages, and benefits and costs of differing regimes of disposition; an understanding of the relationship of differing regimes of disposition to social and economic considerations, and fundamental principles of commercial and property law; and an understanding of the essential incidental elements of a regime of disposition of resources, including security in title and transactions.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

Mining and Energy Law builds on the legal principles you would have studied in earlier units. The unit traverses a range of different resource industries, transactions and practice areas. As a consequence of the breadth of coverage, one of the overarching challenges within this subject is to avoid an overly superficial understanding of the law and its practical relevance. To this end, the subject is delivered in a manner that seeks to build a conceptual framework for the subject as a whole, to anchor this framework to real life issues and to actively engage students in legal research and reasoning processes - in a similar fashion to the way you would in the real world. Assessment has been designed with these purposes in mind.

Unit Structure

This unit will consist of 12 x 1 hour and 45 minute lectures.  Lectures will be held each week on Thursdays from 3pm to 4.45pm in Law Lecture Theatre 1.06.  The lectures in this unit will be recorded in case you are unable to attend class in exceptional circumstances. However, this unit is offered in face-to-face mode and listening to recordings should not be viewed as a substitute for attending class.  Moreover, as many of the lectures will involve a practical workshop component which may not be adequately captured by lecture recording facilities, you are strongly encouraged to attend all lectures in person. Students should be aware that lecture recordings may fail from time to time for technical or other reasons. Where this occurs it may not be possible to rectify the problem and the class will not be repeated or re-recorded.
There will be 5 x 1 hour and 45 minute tutorials.  Tutorials will be held on the weeks outlined in the teaching schedule below. Please ensure you have enrolled in a tutorial in the class allocation system at www.cas.uwa.edu.au. The class allocation system allows you to select your preferred class time. The class allocation system will then assign you to a class based on your preferences. You may be given an opportunity to change your tutorial time in the class allocation system if an alternative time is available. Unfortunately, the unit coordinator is not able to handle requests from students to be allocated to a specific tutorial or to move students to a different class once assigned in the class allocation system as unit coordinators do not have access to the class allocation system. If you have a question or difficulties with your tutorial enrolment, please contact the Student Office through AskUWA (www.ask.uwa.edu.au) and they should be able to assist you.

Participation

Tutorial participation is worth 10% of your marks in this unit. Tutorial marks will be assessed by reference to participation in tutorials. There are no marks allocated to attendance. The expectation is that students will come to tutorials having completed the pre-reading and having attempted the tutorial questions beforehand. The marking criteria for tutorial participation marks are as follows:
(a) demonstrated prior preparation;
(b) willingness and ability to participate in class discussions and respond to questions; 
(c) quality of contribution to class discussions; and
(d) clarity and effectiveness of communication.

Other

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.
