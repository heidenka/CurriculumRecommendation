Academic Information

Content This unit focuses on the concept that climate change can drive the evolution of biological diversity. It aims to explain the atmospheric processes that regulate the Earth’s climate; the role of climate change in the evolution of the world’s ecosystems; the human-induced changes that are occurring in the atmosphere; how these changes affect the global climate which in turn affects biodiversity; and the challenges of biodiversity conservation under climate change. Emphasis is placed on understanding the links between the atmosphere, climate and the world’s ecosystems; how changes in climate can force species losses, migration and adaptation; what can be learned from vegetation response to past climate changes; the predicted impacts to biological diversity under projected greenhouse climates; and the conservation efforts that are required to mitigate these impacts.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

This unit covers a wide range of topics ranging from past, current and predicted future climates, to species and ecosystem responses to climate change and climate change management. The unit is delivered through traditional lectures by a range of engaging speakers, including some from external organizations such as the Department of Biodiversity Conservation & Attractions and CSIRO. However, due to COVID 19 there will be no live lectures in 2020. All lectures will be either 'old' recorded lectures from last year or new prerecorded 2020 lectures. Students are encouraged to participate and actively engage in weekly Zoom sessions which provide an opportunity to discuss each week's lectures. Every two weeks, students' knowledge and understanding of presented lecture materials is tested via online multiple choice quizzes. These quizzes also prepare students for the final exam, half of which consists of similar style multiple choice questions. 'Labs' consist of a variety of activities and are designed to engage students with unit material in different ways (e.g. group discussions, debates, designing an experiment, computer modelling). Other labs are dedicated (1) to improving students' writing ability in preparation for their large essay assignment, (2) to make them realize how big their own carbon footprint is and how they can change it, (3) to enthuse them for climate change research by showcasing climate change related research projects at UWA. There will also be one field excursion where students will be made aware of our coastal flora and fauna and how it may be affected by climate change. All unit content (i.e. lectures and 'labs') is assessed in a final exam, which is a 'failed component'. This means that if you do not pass the exam (i.e. achieve 50%) then you cannot pass the unit.

Participation

To keep up with the theory presented in the lectures, it is highly recommended that students watch all lectures in the weeks that they are released. Attendance at the weekly Zoom question and answer session on the lecture material is also highly recommended but not compulsory.
Face to face attendance of Tuesday afternoon 'lab' sessions is compulsory, unless you are studying the unit remotely or have acquired special consideration (via the Science Student Office). If you miss a class unexpectedly because of a variety of extenuating circumstances, you can avoid losing marks by applying in time for special consideration with the science student office (SSO)(not with the unit coordinator!).

Other

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
