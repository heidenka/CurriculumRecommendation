Academic Information

Content This unit is open to students with no previous knowledge of modern standard Chinese ONLY. It provides a basic introduction to contemporary spoken and written Chinese (Mandarin or Putonghua). The primary focus is on giving elementary instruction in the various facets of modern standard Chinese: comprehension and speaking, reading and writing. The first few weeks of the semester concentrates on the learning of the Pinyin system (Romanisation). In addition, there is a series of lectures on the key features of both the spoken language and the Chinese script. Successful completion of this unit brings students to the ISLPR Level 1 (International Second Language Proficiency Ratings) or New HSK Level 1 (New Chinese Proficiency Test) or CEFR A1 (Common European Framework of Reference for Languages).

Learning And Teaching Strategy

Language learning is communicative, progressive and accumulative. To succeed in this unit, it is crucial that you regularly attempt to communicate with your classmates with what you have learned in class, and utilise the Workbook for the purposes of reviewing, preparing for tests and oral presentations. I encourage you to listen to the recording, or watching the video everyday for half an hour, build this into your daily routine to benefit your learning process. Reading out aloud and practicing the characters on a daily basis will help you to accumulate knowledge of Chinese language to progress to next level.

Unit Structure

This unit provides four contact hours: one lecture and three tutorials per week. It covers lessons upto page 137 of New Practical Chinese Reader 1 (NPCR, 3rd Edition), every two weeks per chapter. Students are expected to study for at least another eight hours per week to successfully complete this unit. For better results three hours for every contact hour is recommended. This includes your time for preview, review, preparation for tests and working on assignments.
Our LECTURES will introduce you to new vocabulary and grammar every week and talk about the construction of some of the Chinese characters. We will go through the dialogues so you understand the context and content. Translation is not provided in the text book, so it is important you pay attention at/to the lectures and actively tune in and engage. Please feel free to ask questions before during or after lecture. Although our lectures are not compulsory, they are building blocks of this unit. It is important you listen and watch the recorded lectures if you could not attend them.
TUTORIALS (language classes) are where you will be expected to take part in various group and individual learning activities. You will be spending much time reading aloud, talking in Chinese in small groups and sometimes speaking in front of the class. Student centred, task orientated and cooperative learning strategies will be used in tutorials. 
SELF-DIRECTED LEARNING is very important for you to do well in this unit. Make the most of the listening materials provided in the NPCR pack. It is important you utilise the Workbook for the purposes of reviewing, preparing for tests and oral language presentations. I encourage you to listen to Chinese everyday for half an hour. Build this into your routine and you will find it most beneficial. Listen to the dialogues and watch the dialogue video on Youtube. Read out aloud and practice the characters on a daily basis.
FEEDBACK – You will have the opportunity to review your tests after they have been marked. However, according to university rules, you are not allowed to take photographs of your text paper or take them out of the classroom.
FEEDBACK ON TEACHING - I am open to any comments and suggestions and forever ready to improve your learning experience. Please feel free to speak with me or email me at anytime for feedback.

Participation

Participation marks: 
Our tutorials are designed to be interactive in nature, and will provide ample opportunities for all to participate and make a contribution. Each session is graded as follows:
Fail (0-49%) - No show, or completely distracted on social media etc., or asleep.
Pass mark (50%) - Attends passively, no meaningful contribution or evidence of preparation.
Credit (60-69%) - Demonstrates some evidence of prior preparation, participates actively in class discussions to expected level.
Distinction (70-79%) - Demonstrates clear evidence of thoughtful engagement with the learning objectives and tasks set for the tutorial session, active participation and thoughtful contribution to class discussions.
High distinction (80%+) - Demonstrates high degree of critical engagement with learning materials, makes original and highly relevant contributions to class discussions and tasks set for the tutorial session.
Note: Supplementary assessment is not available in this unit except in the case of a bachelor's pass degree student who has obtained a mark of 45 to 49 and is currently enrolled in this unit, and it is the only remaining unit that the student must pass in order to complete their course.

Referencing Requirements

PLAGIARISMBe aware that the work you submit must be your own with no unacknowledged debt to some other writer or source. To pass off written work as your own, whether you have copied it from someone else or from somewhere else (be it a published writer, another person, a TV program, a library anthology, a lecture, a website or whatever) is to deprive yourself of the real benefits of this unit and to be guilty of plagiarism. Plagiarism is a serious offence! University policy is that plagiarism, the unacknowledged quotation of material from other people's work, is a ground for failure. Moreover, your name is placed on a central plagiarism register. If you take notes from other sources (critical articles, background works, etc.) you must quote carefully and accurately, and acknowledge the quotation. Even if you paraphrase, you must still acknowledge that you are paraphrasing.Please refer to the University's policy document for further information:http://www.arts.uwa.edu.au/students/policies/dishonesty

Other

Additional resources and reading
The bookshop and library have a number of useful aids for learning Chinese. They include:
Times Dictionary of Chinese Classifiers. This lists all the major measure words with many examples of their use.
Chinese Radicals (Volumes 1 & 2). Most of the radicals are covered in these two volumes, with clear descriptions of the meanings of each radical and examples of characters in which they are used.
Fun with Chinese Characters. There are now three volumes of these books which cover many of the characters you will be learning in the Unit. They explain the origin and meaning of characters, making them easier to remember.
Video: Hello Beijing (Nihao Beijing). This video series is specially designed for beginners to learn basic Chinese language with stress on oral practice.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.
