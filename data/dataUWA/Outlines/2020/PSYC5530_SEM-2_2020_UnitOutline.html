Unit Information

This unit introduces students to a range of specific research techniques, including methodology, research design and analytic procedures, which are of value both to their research and to their clinical practice. The material is presented mainly through workshops and lectures and includes such techniques as single-case designs, evaluating change over time, survey-based research approaches, and reliable change calculation. For many students, research methods seem far removed from clinical practice. This unit will introduce you to methods that not only can you use in your practice but also the outcomes of which routinely inform clinical practice. This is the stuff you did not learn in your undergraduate degree!
The assessments are designed to help you make the links between research methods and your own practice. In the first assessment, you will design a research study to explore a clinical question that is both of interest to you and of relevance to a placement. We want you to practice the skills you will be using once you graduate and, potentially, come up with a study that will be implemented in that placement. In choosing the question to answer, and the methodology to answer it, you should consider both the clinical need for the study and what you know about the strengths and weaknesses of the method you have chosen, and use these to articulate how the method may contribute to evidence-based practice in your chosen area. In the second assessment, the examination, you will answer questions that test your ability to match the methodology to the clinical/research question across the breadth of techniques taught in the unit.

ASSESSMENTS
Assessment 1: Clinical Research Proposal Presentation
Your task is to design a research project that addresses a clinical issue in a clinical service. This could be in the Robin Winkler Clinic, the Neuropsychology clinic, or from an external placement. It could be using prospective or archival data. It could be about an aspect of assessment or therapy, about individual or group therapy, the design of a program or its evaluation. Use any of the methods from this Unit, or any other relevant applied research method. Feel free to speak to your supervisor about your ideas. If you are on external placement, ask your supervisor if there is an aspect of that placement that they would like to see developed or evaluated. Assuming the proposals will all be of excellent quality (!), once marked (and any substantive amendments made) you are encouraged to share the proposal with your placement. The best of the proposals may form the basis of future Honours or PhD studies.
You will present the proposal to the class. You will be working in small groups, and presentations will be given by the whole group. Marks will be awarded for the overall presentation and the quality of the proposal, and also for your, individual, contribution to the presentation. The Clinical Research Proposal Presentation will be approx. 15 minutes plus 10 mins Q & A. Final time limit will be determined by number of presentations to be given.
In this group assessment, you will be required formally to present your proposed research project to the class. The audience will take the role of the stakeholders within the clinical service, who will be in a position to approve your research idea. The purpose of the presentation will, therefore, be to convince these stakeholders that the research is a worthwhile venture. You will be assessed on your ability to convince the stakeholders to support your idea, so an important part of this will involve addressing the concerns that the audience raises. Marks will also be awarded to audience members for contributing in the form of questions for the presenters (max. 10 percent of the assessment grade). Be constructively critical. Audience members who ask no questions, will get no additional marks!
Assessment 2: Examination
In this individual assessment, you will be required to answer questions that assess your ability to match appropriate designs, methodologies and analysis to specific research questions and to show your understanding of how to apply this knowledge and skills to the development of evidence-based practice.
First formative feedback: Students will be given the opportunity to receive feedback on how they are doing throughout the unit via discussions and workshop activities. There are no grades or points associated with this feedback.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
