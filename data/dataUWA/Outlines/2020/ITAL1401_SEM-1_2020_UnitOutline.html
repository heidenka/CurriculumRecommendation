Academic Information

Content This unit offers an introduction to Italian grammar and vocabulary to allow students to communicate orally and in writing in simple situations. It develops cultural and linguistic awareness and focuses on contemporary spoken and written language used in Italy. Students achieve language proficiency equivalent to Level A1 of the Common European Framework of Reference for Languages (Quadro Comune Europeo di Riferimento per le Lingue). This unit leads to ITAL1402 Italian Studies 2. Together, ITAL1401 and ITAL1402 provide access to Level 2 and 3 Italian units, which not only increase language competency but offer the opportunity to further engage with Italian culture and history (including linguistics, literature and film) and to take advantage of a range of exchange possibilities, including our short-term Bergamo Summer School.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

In this unit we will employ a ‘flipped classroom’ learning approach. Face-to-face classes (tutorials) will be integrated with an online component, which consists of short video lessons and online activities (quizzes, movie clips, forums etc. also referred to as e-tivities). Both in-class and online engagement count towards the final mark. 
In class, the teacher will work with students on different language topics, helping them to discover the structures of the language and practice in an interactive and student-centered way. Students are expected to attend all tutorials; if they miss a tutorial, they are required to work on the topics covered in class before attending the next tutorial. All in-class activities are specified in the unit outline. In each tutorial we will build on the topics of the previous ones, so it is crucial that students keep up with the contents of the unit through regular study.
Students are expected to work on the online activities assigned after each tutorial. The video lessons and the e-tivities will be available for students after each tutorial, and will have to be completed before attending the next tutorial.
The in-class and online activities are designed in order to allow students to work on short but regular practice sessions rather than in intensive sessions at the end of the course. The reason for this is a pedagogical one, as this structure allows an effective and lasting language learning experience. Teachers will provide students with regular feedback on their progress in the course; students are expected to use teachers' feedback in order to improve their learning.

Unit Structure

Unit Structure
•	Teaching period: week 1 to week 12;
•	Classes: Monday/Tuesday (1 hour) & Thursday/Friday (2 hours);
•	Three hours of contact hours per week, from week 1 to week 12;
•	Weekly videos (flipped component), online activities and self-study are an integral part of the unit and count towards the final mark;
•	This unit does not have a final exam in the exam period; the unit assessments are both online and in-class during the teaching period (see Required Assessment below).
 
In-class activities: you will work on activities designed to practise different aspects of the Italian language, both written and oral. The activities are taken both from the textbook and from other sources, and they include an important communicative component. Class attendance counts towards your final mark (cf. Assessment below).
Video lessons: this unit does not include traditional lectures. During the tutorials you will work on different aspects of the Italian language; after each tutorial you will be asked to watch short videos addressing the different topics introduced in class. This will help you to consolidate and extend your understanding of what you have seen in class, and prepare for your next class. You will be able to play and re-play the videos at your pace and as many times as you need, and come to the next class more prepared and confident. The videos are available on LMS after each tutorial. You are required to watch the videos assigned after each tutorial before attending the next class. Videos will be available for you to watch until the end of the course.
E-tivities: the online activities available on LMS are an integrated part of this unit and they will help you practise on a regular basis the different language skills learned in class. You will be required to do listening and grammar/vocabulary practice quizzes, contribute to a communal forum, read facilitated and authentic texts, watch movie clips etc. You are required to complete the e-tivities assigned after each tutorial BEFORE attending the next class.

Preparation for Class
1. Grammar points, exercises, dialogues, readers and related activities from your textbook as indicated in the program must be prepared for the language classes. 
2. Obtain a useful bilingual dictionary for your work. Please note that dictionaries are not allowed for the in-class tests and in the final examinations. The reason for this is mainly a pedagogical one, as you are expected to acquire a basic Italian vocabulary and common idioms, contained in each chapter of your textbook.
3. You are strongly urged to make constant use of the audio-visual material available to you through the online textbook website or LMS for improving your reading, listening comprehension and speaking skills.
4. Each unit of the textbook ends with a section, called autovalutazione (self-assessment), which you can use to assess your progress. You are strongly encouraged to make use of it, even if it is not included in the program. Answer keys are provided in the textbook.
Suggestions and Strategies for Successful Language Learning
Language learning will present you with unexpected situations, especially when listening, speaking and responding. So do not be discouraged but persevere; if necessary, ask others to slow down, do not try to be perfect and above all try to understand and be understood, communicating at your level.
1.	Organise yourself and look ahead in order to find the necessary time to learn and absorb the material as it is presented in class; in this way, you will be ready for the next class. 
2.	For each class, spend at least an equal amount of time out of class on your private learning and preparation of exercises and activities.
3.	Find the learning style best suited to your strengths: 
•	READING and ROTE LEARNING, memorising vocabulary, idioms and rules are indispensable;
•	Actively participate in classwork;
•	Speak with classmates in Italian;
•	Practise repeating aloud the dialogues contained in your textbook.
4.	Be an ACTIVE LEARNER: 
•	Ask questions, and DON’T BE AFRAID TO MAKE MISTAKES.
•	Be ready with answers to class exercises.
•	Find some classmates - a native speaker or students from higher levels - to practise Italian on a regular basis; practise by yourself e.g. record your voice.
•	Exploit the presence of native speakers abounding in the Perth metropolitan area e.g. have a weekly coffee and conversation with those who may be accessible to you.
•	Listen to Italian radio e.g. Rete Italia broadcasting 24 hrs a day on AM 65.7, as well as programs on SBS Radio and TV.
5.	When dealing with mistakes e.g. in tests and homework or in class, try to understand what you did wrong, and how to correct it; write down mistakes and their corrections e.g. next to each other. If you do not this, you are bound to repeat your mistakes; often, such errors involve fundamental rules and points of grammar that need to be learnt at the start.
6.	LEARNING VOCABULARY IS AN ESSENTIAL REQUIREMENT of effective language learning, and makes everything else much easier to manage. Make a weekly list so that you can “grow” it, if you want to become a proficient speaker, even at this level. IDIOMATIC EXPRESSIONS are also very important, so MAKE AN EFFORT to learn them by heart as soon as you come across them.
7.	A suitable dictionary will help you with homework and class preparation. When reading, however, rely also on context to guess meaning but do so intelligently, relying on what you already know e.g. get the general sense of a sentence, and try to make sense of what you are reading.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
