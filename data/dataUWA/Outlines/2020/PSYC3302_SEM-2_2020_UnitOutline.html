Academic Information

Content This unit introduces classical test theory and latent trait theories of psychological measurement. In the context of ability testing, students are taught test construction skills including selecting items for a test, plotting item responses, assessing test reliability and validity, and examining 'latent traits' using correlation, partial correlation and factor analysis. Students then use these test construction skills to create a new test of a different construct (e.g. empathy). The unit also includes applied measurement—how tests are used as individual assessment instruments and/or selection instruments in real life.

Unit Information

This unit introduces classical and modern test theory in relation to the measurement of various psychological dimensions. The unit will introduce students to the methods used to evaluate the quality of measures, such as psychological tests, that are used in research and applied settings by psychologists and others interested in human behaviour. The nature and use of psychological tests, along with their varied types, will be explored. The fundamentals of psychometric theory, in particular reliability and validity, will be introduced and their application and interpretation demonstrated. Finally, the applications of psychological measurement to intelligence and personality testing, in particular, will also be covered. 
The learning outcomes for this unit are manifold: students will (1) acquire key knowledge of the principles underlying psychometric measurement, testing and assessment—this requires an understanding of scaling, psychometric functions, item characteristics, reliability and validity; (2) be aware of best practice in assessment, including ethical issues associated with psychological testing; and (3) become familiar with the analysis of individual differences and statistics in common with those used in test construction—correlation, regression and exploratory factor analysis, for example.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

Learning will take place through a combination of face-to-face lectures (all of which are scheduled to be recorded) and laboratories directed by tutors (one of which will be recorded) to help learn to apply and interpret the various statistical analyses introduced in the lectures. Laboratories will be complimented with detailed handouts and instructions to help facilitate learning during individual study.
The main vehicle for driving learning in this unit will be the weekly lectures which will introduce the theory of psychological measurement. Additionally, for several weeks, there will also be laboratory classes that build on the insights from a previous week’s lecture, by demonstrating the practical application of the principles learned. Students are encouraged to engage with the material by attending the weekly digital lectures (or watch the corresponding recordings of those lectures) and on-campus laboratory classes (or corresponding online educational experience), wherein the laboratory tutors will demonstrate the application of the principles presented in the lectures, and provide a supportive environment to facilitate student learning.

Unit Structure

Lectures take place once a week. Roughly the first two thirds of the semester will focus on the principles of psychometric measurement. These will be presented and discussed in lectures and illustrated in several laboratory sessions. The last several lectures will touch briefly on some advanced psychometric methods and the application of psychometrics to intelligence and personality testing. Laboratory sessions will tend to illustrate the principles discussed in the previous week's lecture. The laboratory sessions will be held on the ground floor of the Main Psychology Building (Room G26) in the School of Psychological Science. A corresponding online experience laboratory session/recording will also be made available to students who cannot attend an on-campus laboratory session.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
