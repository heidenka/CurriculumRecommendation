Academic Information

Content This unit enables students to gain a detailed understanding of practices undertaken to promote the sustainable use and conservation of coastal resources. Through a combination of lectures, practical work and local field visits, students are introduced to conservation and management activities at the domestic and international level taken in response to contemporary human impacts on our coasts. Central themes include measures designed to reduce coastal water and sediment contamination from both point and non-point sources, policies to facilitate the conservation of marine flora and fauna and practices designed to mitigate the physical impacts of sea level rise in an inherently dynamic environment. The success or otherwise of these measures are assessed from a variety of environmental, social and economic perspectives in order to facilitate students' critical understanding of conservation and management in coastal regions. Through studying the unit, students gain a holistic appreciation of the unique threats facing coastal ecosystems and our ability to address these successfully.

ENVT3306 - Coastal Conservation and Management - SEM-1-2020 - 2020 - Face to face / Blended learning

Unit Information

This unit provides a detailed overview and analysis of actions, initiatives and priorities necessary for the long term conservation and management of coastal ecosystems in a context of increasing threat and uncertainty. It should be of interest to anyone concerned with how we can conserve and sustain the ecological and environmental assets and values associated with our coast.
Coastal conservation and management has evolved from coastal engineering, whereby policies were designed to reduce threats to property and infrastructure, to the present day focus on minimizing resource conflict through policies which address values held by multiple stakeholders and reflect the dynamic, unpredictable and varied nature of coastal environments.
The unit includes lectures, tutorials and a field trip aimed at providing students with the skills necessary to successfuly diagnose problems in coastal environments, and suggest appropriate management actions.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

This unit will commence with an examination of how such a management approach reflects contemporary debates over environmental vulnerability and resilience in the context of climate change on the coast. It will then move on to examining the nature of human threats in the context of common coastal habitats in the temperate and tropical regions, illustrating the significance and impacts of these through case studies and tutorial discussions. The unit will then analyse responses to these threats within each coastal ecosystem, considering how managers seek to enhance resilience and minimize vulnerability through a range of policies and measures. The design and management of marine protected areas will form a specific area of focus with reference to tropical environments.
The unit will conclude with a discussion of issues affecting our ability to manage coastal ecosystems in a future dominated by the impacts of climate change. Tutorials will be used to explore this content in more depth through in-depth reading and discussion of key issues and guest presentations.
A field trip will also allow us to understand management issues associated with the Swan Estuary.
We very much hope that this unit provides students with the information and opportunity to become involved in the future conservation and sustainable management of our coastal resources.

Texts

Books
Students will be required to read a broad range of literature including book chapters, journal articles and management reports during this unit. Reading lists will be issued covering the main themes of the unit as the semester progresses. A good overview of relevant topics covered in the course is in the following text which is recommended background reading. 
Kenchington R, Stocker L, Wood D. (2012). Sustainable Coastal Management and Climate Adaptation: Global Lessons from Regional Approaches in Australia. CSIRO, Canberra. ISBN 9780643100275. Copies available in the Science Library.
The following two books also contain material which should be of interest.
Norse EA, Crowder LB (2005). Marine Conservation Biology: The Science of Maintaining The Sea’s Biodiversity. Island Press, Washington DC. ISBN 155963662. Available in hard copy from the Science Library.
Ray GC, McCormick-Ray J. (2009). Coastal-Marine Conservation: Science and Policy. Wiley, New York. Available as an ebook via UWA Library.
Journals
A selection of academic journals publishing material relating to coastal and marine conservation is listed below. Full content of all journals is available via the Library website. It is strongly recommended that all students use these and other journals as a basis for locating references and material of relevance to their group project.
Biological Conservation, Coastal Management, Conservation Biology, Global Change Biology, Journal of Coastal Conservation, Marine Policy, Ocean and Coastal Management
Websites
As with all units, care should be taken in using material obtained from websites with regard to its independence and accuracy. In this unit, there are many useful online sources of information which provide up to date data relating to marine planning and management at the local, regional, national and international scales. A selection of these is given below. 
Department of Environment and Energy: http://www.environment.gov.au/ There is a dedicated ‘Marine’ section to the website at http://www.environment.gov.au/marine which links to sites describing the regional approach to coastal management, integrated coastal zone management and many other topics. 
Department of Parks and Wildlife: http://www.dpaw.wa.gov.au Material relating to marine management and protection is at http://www.dpaw.wa.gov.au/management/marine
The Commonwealth Scientific and Industrial Research Organisation (CSIRO) has sections relating to marine and coastal conservation and management at http://www.csiro.au/en/Research/Environment/Oceans-and-coasts
Marine Ecosystems and Management (http://depts.washington.edu/meam/index.html) is an online news and opinion site focusing on marine ecosystem-based management. It is well worth going through recent issues to find current information on many aspects relating to this unit. 
Open Channels (http://openchannels.org/) is an excellent forum for discussion, literature dissemination and online resources as well as job advertising in the field of marine conservation, planning and management. It is well worth signing up to this forum in order to receive weekly updates of material posted online.
Reports
The Final Report of the Resource Assessment Commission Coastal Zone Inquiry from November 1993 is a useful introduction to the problems facing coastal zone management in Australia. Chapters 2 to 6 are recommended introductory reading for this unit. The archived report is at https://www.environment.gov.au/archive/coasts/publications/rac/index.html
The latest national State of the Environment Report is at http://www.environment.gov.au/soe/ which includes a wide range of data on environmental trends and human impacts on environmental quality. Chapter 6 deals with the marine environment. The next national State of the Environment report was scheduled for publication in 2016 but no definite date has been set.

Other

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 30 out of 50 (a mark of 5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
