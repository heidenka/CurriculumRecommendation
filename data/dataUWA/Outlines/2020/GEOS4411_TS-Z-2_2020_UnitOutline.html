Unit Information

This unit examines Earth's metallic ore deposits in terms of their diversity, geographic location, economic importance, and geological setting within the concept of mineralising systems. The character and origin of ore-bearing magmas and hydrothermal fluids are explored and ancient and modern examples of ore deposits are included. Ore deposits such as gold, copper, and iron deposits are discussed using a mineral system approach with emphasis on the metal sources, pathways and depositional site characteristics. Understanding of mineral systems is particularly important in Western Australia where mineral resources provide considerable employment opportunities.
The unit is taught via a series of lectures and, significantly, two laboratory sessions each week. The latter provides an introduction to sulphides, oxides and elements (such as gold, silver) and ore petrology using transmitted and reflected light microscopy. An introduction to reflected light microscopy will be provided.  Hand samples and polished thin-sections and polished sections of hydrothermally altered rocks of various different mineral systems will be studied.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

Teaching and learning in this unit is undertaken by weekly lectures and laboratory sessions. On successful completion of this unit you should be able to: 
1.	Explain the geological setting of mineral systems including knowing the major sulfide and oxide mineralogy.
2.	Explain the structural control of hydrothermal mineral systems .
3.	Exhibit knowledge of topics such as the sulphide phase equilibria, hydrothermal alteration assemblages, zoning and paragenesis.
4.	Understand basics in fluid equilibria including the application of fluid inclusions, stable and radiogenic isotope geochemistry with respect to the geochemical controls of ore transport in and precipitation of metals in hydrothermal fluids.  
5.	Distribution, origins and characteristics of Orogenic gold, VHMS, SEDEX, Epithermal, Porphyry, Skarn, IOCG, MVT, SEDEX, SSC, BIF mineral systems. 
6.	Exploration methods and issues related to mining and extraction for those major mineral systems listed above.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
