Academic Information

Content This unit aims to introduce students to anthropological and sociological perspectives on human engagements with the natural environment. It draws on case material from societies around the world to illustrate a variety of environmental beliefs and practices. The unit familiarises students with the main theoretical currents in environmental anthropology and provides them with perspectives for thinking about human interactions with the environment. It encourages students to think analytically about the values and knowledge that human communities, urban and rural, hold about landscapes, plants and animals. The unit is relevant to students from a range of disciplines including agriculture and the environmental sciences. It provides students with some insights into the range of skills and background knowledge necessary for applying their anthropological training to environment-related research topics. It encourages students to think about career options, professional development and transferable skills while also providing a theoretical grounding in environmental anthropology and/or sociology.

Participation

Description:
Participation in classes is considered a vital component of this course, and 30% of your assessment is allocated to your preparation for these in the form of notes and questions. 
There are 9 assessable weeks of tutorials. This excludes the first introductory week. There are a potential 3.75 marks per week of in-class assessment. Details of how these are awarded are below.

General Preparation:
You are required to come to class having:
1)	Watched the online lecture(s)
2)	Completed any other components contained within the weekly module
3)	Done the required reading and completed the tutorial preparation (see below). 
Written Preparation (2 items to submit before class):
1)	Students should prepare notes on the readings/learning materials for each week (including the readings). These notes should be uploaded to the LMS before the start of the tutorial. A link will be available in each of the weekly learning materials to facilitate this upload. Please name your journal entry ‘Tutorial 1’ etc, (corresponding to the tutorial number). 
Notes uploads should consist of no more than 500 words (not including your own devised questions see item 2 below). You should first note down answers to the set questions, then cover anything else you want to discuss in class. 
I will be looking at these entries but not offering comments unless something is particularly warranted. Please remember that are notes: they do not need to be mini-essays, so please do not get caught up in making the presentation amazing or trying to construct an argument with an introduction and conclusion. There is no point cramming as much as you can into a single page. These notes are to ensure adequate preparation and provide the basis for class discussion. 
2)	For each week of required reading, you are required to devise 3 questions related to that week’s reading. This part of the assessment is about assisting me to gauge how well you have engaged with the reading, and so should be considered an essential part of your preparation. The questions will be drawn upon during class to stimulate discussion in small and large groups. For the questions that you ask, you should have some idea of the answer, or at least have something to offer in response to it. That is, do not ask an ambit question that is only tangentially related to the reading, or that is as vague as ‘did you like the reading?’ Try to ask something that you have drawn from the reading and that will inspire discussion.
These three questions should be added to the top of your weekly journal entry. Ensure you also have a copy of the questions and notes available to you during class discussion.  
Please note that where students’ work appears with 3 identical questions there will be an assumption that you have collaborated and both students will lose marks for this part of the assessment.
Expectations for class participation – marks
Grade (out of maximum of 3.75 possible marks per class)	Criteria
0	Absent
1	Present, but no notes submitted. Not sufficient to pass this component of the unit.
2-2.75	Present plus submitted. Contributes to discussion. Devised questions demonstrate good preparation and engagement with readings.
3-3.75	Present plus notes submitted, offers analysis and synthesizes ideas. Devised questions and discussion demonstrates excellent preparation and engagement with readings.
Note that contributions need to be exceptional to effectively score 3.75 marks (100%).
Please note: I will not be handing out marks for class participation on a weekly basis, though you will receive some numerical feedback on your notes via an LMS comment (journal entries are confidential and not shared with the group). If you are unsure of how you are going at any time, you are welcome to make a consultation with me.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
