Academic Information

Content Why does industry pollute so much? Can it pollute less? Does it want to? Businesses vary greatly, from the model firm leading in sustainable business practices to the heavy polluter that seems to go only for the quick buck. How can you explain such diversity? Is it just because some are 'good guys' and others 'bad guys', or is there more to it? This unit addresses these questions and asks: (1) Under what conditions will a business want to improve its management of the environment? (2) How can government and society effectively encourage businesses to want to do better? Accordingly, this unit has two parts. Part 1 examines how market forces, government and society interact with a business and influence its management of the environment, and introduces students to the tools allowing them to evaluate and compare a business's environmental management performance: environmental accounting and reporting; environmental investment and finance; environmental risk and insurance; environmental management systems; and life-cycle assessment. Students use these tools (in tutorials) to evaluate the performance of a specific industry of their choice, in any field of activity. Part 2 then focuses more specifically on two companies of choice within the chosen industry and studies how external and internal drivers can explain the companies' environmental management. Students can liaise with the Chief Environment Officer, and guest speakers from industry are invited to come and share their experience with the students. Students in this unit have various backgrounds: Science, Engineering, Business and also Arts (business ethics).

Unit Details

ECON3323 - Business and the Environment - SEM-1-2020 - 2020 - Face to face / Blended learning

Unit Information

Why does industry pollute so much? Can it pollute less? Does it want to? Businesses vary greatly, from the model firm leading in sustainable business practices to the heavy polluter that seems to go only for the quick buck. How can you explain such diversity? Is it just because some are 'good guys' and others 'bad guys', or is there more to it? 
This unit addresses these questions and asks: 
(1) Under what conditions will a business want to improve its management of the environment? 
(2) How can government and society effectively encourage businesses to want to do better (if they themselves want to)?  
This Unit will explore these core questions in three ways: 
(a) By looking at how authors have analysed them and what the different aspects are (theory and methods). 
(b) By studying specific business companies and their strategies (empirical data). 
(c) By looking at how we can understand business people’s decisions and behaviour (behavioural and experimental approach). 

Lecture Capture System

In accordance with UWA’s policy on lecture capture all lectures* in enabled venues will be recorded and made available to stream and download by enrolled students via the Lecture Capture System (LCS) for the duration of the unit (including Supplementary and Deferred Examinations period).

* Please note that in some limited circumstances lectures may not be recorded due to:  ethical and/or security related issues; the use of commercially sensitive material; the use of third-party copyright teaching material; or culturally sensitive content. Students will be advised, in advance, when this applies.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

Mirroring the three approaches mentioned in the Unit Information above, the Unit's three (3) sessions per week will be organized as follows: 
- Session 1 will be a content-based lecture explaining the key ideas and methods needed to address the two core questions above (in Unit Information). Most of the content will be based on the core textbook of the Unit. Each session corresponds to a chapter, which must be read before the session (except for week 1). 
- Session 2 will be in the Computer Lab and involve active searching of targeted information on specific business companies, using dedicated data bases. Students will receive a Task List for each session (as in lab sessions).  
- Session 3 will involve demonstrating how experimental methods can help us better understand WHY decision-makers decide as they do in different situations. This session will be run like a seminar or workshop. It will occasionally use UWA's Behavioural Economics Lab (BEL) situated in the Psychology building. 
Each session aims at a different type of learning:
- Sessions 1 aim at understanding concepts and methods
- Sessions 2 aim at learning how to find and analyse company-relevant data using various professional databases
- Sessions 3 aim at introducing students to experimental methods for better understanding human behaviour
For sessions 2 and 3, students will work in small groups of two or three. 
Student attendance will be essential in sessions 2 and 3, as most of the action will not be able to be captured by the "Lecture Capture System". However, attending session 1 will also be helpful in that the lecturer will focus only on those aspects that appear hard to grasp as indicated by those who are present.  

Unit Structure

The structure of the Unit follows to a large extent that of the core textbook, with a couple of extra topics thrown in. 
On a semester-weekly basis, the topics covered will be as follows:
1/ General introduction + housekeeping information (how the Unit will be run, what assessments, etc.)
2/ Environmental costs imposed on society by business firms (the externalities problem)
3/ Stakeholder analysis (some may benefit while other may suffer from a decision)
4/ NO CLASS  (PROSH on Wednesday 18/03)
5/ Does ethics have a place in business decision making? 
6/ Improving environmental management requires good information: how do firms gather it, when they want to?
7/ How much of their environmental performance are firms willing to share with stakeholders and why?  
8/ EASTER BREAK
9/ What kind of environmental management systems (EMS) do firms use, or not?
10/ How much do businesses invest (or not) in better environmental management?
11/ How much do businesses manage and prevent risks to the environment? 
12/ Does business get value from marketing environmentally-friendly products? 
13/ Revisions and exam preparation

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
