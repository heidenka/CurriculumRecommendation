Academic Information

Content This unit covers the principles and methods of epidemiological research. It introduces the principles of epidemiology as well as higher-level coverage of the major study designs (intervention, cohort and case-control studies); the causes and control of bias, confounding, and effect modification; outbreak investigation; and causation of disease. In addition, it provides an introduction to the newer study designs (e.g. case-crossover). Emphasis is placed on the critical appraisal of published articles in order to enable the student to take an evidence-based approach to health policy and practice.

Unit Information

Welcome to PUBH4403 Epidemiology 1. This unit constitutes a core unit for the Masters of Public Health Degree at The University of Western Australia and is a core or elective unit for a number of other degree programs. This postgraduate unit was originally developed in the 1990s by staff in the UWA School of Population and Global Health. This unit guide was revised in 2020 by Associate Professor Tom Briffa who is the current coordinator for this unit.
In this unit we will cover the principles and methods of epidemiological research. The unit introduces the fundamental principles and core concepts of epidemiology as well as higher level coverage of topics such as the major study designs (intervention, cohort and case-control studies); the causes and control of bias, confounding, and effect-modification; screening; and causation of disease. Emphasis is placed on the critical appraisal of published epidemiological research.  Epidemiologic methods are used to address research questions across all areas of health and throughout the unit we trust you will see the relevance and application to your particular area of interest. 
We trust you enjoy the unit and that it stimulates your interest in epidemiological research.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

Epidemiology 1 comprises three hours of contact per week, either through attendance on-campus or online throughout the semester. Classes will take the form of interactive lectures, smaller group tutorials and discussion forums, driven by students, through the Learning Management System (LMS).  Tutorial sessions will involve discussion and practice of key concepts and/or critique and discussion of epidemiological research papers.  We encourage interaction in the lectures, the tutorials as well as the discussion forums through LMS. PowerPoint slides, tutorial journal articles and additional resources will be available on Blackboard (accessed via LMS at: www.lms.uwa.edu.au; accessed 20/01/2020). Materials for each session will generally be available by the Monday prior to the Thursday class each week.
Epidemiology 1 is delivered as both face-to-face and online. As such the lectures will be audio recorded with screen capture and a link to these recordings placed on the Epidemiology 1 LMS site.
In order to meet the learning outcomes it is necessary for students to pursue each topic in more depth than is presented in the lecture notes alone. Students should do this through additional reading outside of formal classes and through the discussions with your fellow students; be it in-person or online.  We will always assume that the prescribed readings have been read and exercises attempted prior to the related session – in order to facilitate more meaningful student experience.
The unit has been typically structured so that the lecture is provided one week and the tutorial on the related topic is given in the week following. Note, this may not always be possible due to late scheduling changes. This will allow time to reflect on the lecture material, complete additional readings, and to read the tutorial resources associated with the topic. There are at currently two weeks during the semester where no formal tutorial has been scheduled. See Lecture and Tutorial Schedule on pages 14 and 15 for details.
There will be a combination of face-to-face tutorial groups (up to three), and online tutorial support presented from among, Dr Peter Franklin, Dr Charley Budgeon and Associate Professor Tom Briffa. Associate Professor Briffa will have responsibility for monitoring and facilitating LMS activities. However please do not hesitate to email questions you may have at any time of the workweek days. To ensure receipt of your email and a timely reply be sure to insert the unit code PUHB4403 in the subject line. We anticipate answering your questions within 72 hours of the date and time an email is received.
The tutorial questions/tasks will be posted at least one week before the scheduled tutorial. Following the weekly release of the solutions to each tutorial, online students may contact one of the three tutors for the unit, by email or phone, to discuss any matters arising. You will be advised through LMS as to which tutor you should contact should the need arise.  Alternatively, online students are welcome to attend any of the face-to-face tutorials where that is an option. Prior contact with the Unit Coordinator is required to ensure numbers are spread evenly across tutorial groups. It is not possible to record the tutorials as the system cannot adequately capture the discussions that occur in small groups or in the class as a whole. However, we will post the answers to the tutorial questions on LMS, following the tutorial. Opportunity will be provided each week for students to discuss issues / problems that they may have encountered with material from the previous week’s topic.
Importantly students are expected to have read the tutorial resources and attempted the review questions related to the research article prior to participating in the tutorial.
The unit is based on the premise of adult-education philosophy in which each student is an independent and self-motivated learner, with the role of the lecturer/tutor being one of facilitation and direction rather than the source of all knowledge.  Notwithstanding this, students are encouraged to consult the unit coordinator (and tutors related to tutorial content) if they are having difficulty with any aspect of the course.

SPECIFIC LEARNING OUTCOMES FROM LECTURES AND TUTORIALS
Below are the intended learning outcomes for the material presented in the unit for which we will review our progress against as the semester unfolds.
1.	Measures of Disease Frequency and Association
1.1.	Differentiate between ratios, proportions and rates.
1.2.	Differentiate and calculate prevalence, cumulative incidence, and incidence rates (i.e. person-time in denominator).
1.3.	Define other common forms of disease measurement (i.e. morbidity rate; mortality rate; case fatality rate; attack rate; point prevalence; and period prevalence).
1.4.	Calculate an odds ratio and relative risk (risk ratio or rate ratio) and interpret them in conjunction with the confidence intervals (CIs).
1.5.	Interpret absolute and relative effect measures (and their 95% CIs).
1.6.	Differentiate between the method of calculation for direct and indirect age-standardisation and the use of both.
1.7.	Differentiate between ‘attributable risk’ and ‘relative risk’.
1.8.	Interpret measures of ‘attributable risk in the ‘exposed’ and ‘population attributable risk’.
2.	Study Designs
2.1.	Describe and contrast the characteristics of the key ‘types’ of epidemiologic study designs.
2.2.	Explain the relative strengths and limitations of the various study designs.
2.3.	Discuss the factors that a researcher considers in deciding which study design to use.
3.	Methodological issues
3.1.	Identify possible sources of bias in each of the research study designs and explain how these can be minimised.
3.2.	Differentiate between differential and non-differential misclassification (outcome or exposure) and its likely impact on effect measures (including null-referenced interpretation).
3.3.	Explain what is meant by the term confounding and outline how it can be dealt with in both the study design and data analysis.
3.4.	Differentiate between confounding and effect modification.
3.5.	Differentiate between internal and external validity.
3.6.	Explain why the randomised controlled trial (RCT) is regarded as the ‘highest level of evidence’ for assessing the efficacy of health care interventions.
3.7.	Explain what is meant by ‘intention to treat’ analysis and why it is recommended in RCTs.
3.8.	Understand the purpose of randomisation, allocation concealment and blinding in reducing bias.
3.9.	Critically appraise epidemiologic studies including: case-control, cohort and intervention studies.
3.10.	Identify methodological issues related to conducting research in Indigenous populations.
4.	Causation
4.1.	Differentiate between association and causation in disease models.
4.2.	Recognise the utility of the various conceptual models of disease causality, including the causal pie model and causal diagrams.
4.3.	Understand the difficulties in establishing causation and the strengths and limitations of the Bradford-Hill criteria.
5.	Screening
5.1.	Explain what is meant by ‘screening’ for disease. 
5.2.	Discuss the criteria for evaluating the appropriateness of screening tests.
5.3.	Calculate and evaluate measures of the validity of a screening test, including sensitivity, specificity, and positive/negative predictive values.
5.4.	Understand the effect of prevalence of disease on positive and negative predictive value of a test.
5.5.	Outline factors likely to influence the reliability of screening tests.
5.6.	Appreciate the study designs that can be used for evaluation of screening programmes.
5.7.	Discuss the methodological challenges involved in evaluating the effectiveness of screening tests, including selection biases.
6.	Application of epidemiology to health policy and practice
6.1.	Discuss the application of epidemiology to health policy and practice.
6.2.	Differentiate between the concepts of ‘efficacy’, ‘effectiveness’ and ‘efficiency’.
6.3.	Identify the ethical issues involved in the conduct of research involving humans.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
