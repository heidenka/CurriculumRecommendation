Unit Information

Welcome to Strategic Workplace Relations.This unit is a capstone unit, building upon the material which has been presented in other units. You will be provided with opportunities to apply your theoretical knowledge of human resources and employee relations to explore the different frameworks that shape and govern employment in Australia. We will be examining emerging trends in employment and the social, economic and technological factors that produce these issues. Further, students will complete practical workplace projects with representatives of employers, governments and unions.
Successful completion of this unit will be difficult for students who are unable to physically attend face-to-face lectures, workshops, research activities and visits to different organisations.
Unit Content
The content for this unit centres on how different interests approach the workforce and employment in a fast changing world where traditional ideas, systems and structures about jobs, occupations and careers are being challenged by a range of factors including globalisation and technology. These changes also impact on the relationships between the traditional interest groups: governments, employers and unions. Students will be provided with a number of theoretical frameworks that explain the different factors and conditions that influence the choices and actions taken by these groups within the fast moving context of change.
The content will be delivered in various ways including guest speakers from government, industry and unions; academic articles; government and business policies; audio-visual materials and case studies.
Unit Goal
The goal of this unit is to equip students to examine critically how governments, employers, employees and their unions respond strategically to current and emerging work and employment issues and topics, with reference to the practical effects of the intersection of these different interests.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

As a capstone unit, Strategic Workplace Relations, provides students with opportunities to undertake research into current and emerging themes in employment relations. Students will be expected to investigate issues by canvassing different points of view and applying both academic and practical knowledge to understanding and solving issues. Students will be expected to do preparatory work for each seminar. The focus of this unit is how work and employment can be managed strategically taking into account the different interest groups involved.
Teaching and Learning Evaluation
You may be asked to complete two evaluations during this unit: The Student Perception of Teaching (SPOT) and the Students Unit Reflective Feedback (SURF). The SPOT is optional and is an evaluation of the lecturer and the unit. The SURF is completed online and is a university wide survey and deals only with the unit. You will receive an email inviting you to complete the SURF when it is activated. We encourage you to complete the forms as your feedback is extremely important and can be used to make changes to the unit or lecturing style when appropriate.

Unit Structure

This unit requires students, individually and collectively, to be responsible for managing much of their own learning. Over the course of the semester there will be 8 formal three hour seminars which will comprise a combination of lecturing, interactive tasks, guest speakers and discussion. The remaining seminars will be devoted to research projects and final project presentation to industry representatives.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
