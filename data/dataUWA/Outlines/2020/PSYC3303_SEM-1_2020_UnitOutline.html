Academic Information

Content This unit exposes students to the major debates, issues, and controversies in modern psychology. The unit examines a series of contemporary practical or theoretical issues and debates through a set of lectures addressing different social problems. The objective is that students use the skills taught to them in this and other units to appraise the theoretical and practical implications of various perspectives in any debate. The topics vary from year to year as key issues and debates in society and in the field of psychology change. Topics may include climate change, gender inequality, social conflict and educational disadvantage.

Unit Information

Real-world problems can benefit greatly from applying psychological research to their solutions. Yet policy makers have not always made use of this research and psychologists have not always communicated it in a usable form. This results in less effective solutions and under-utilised research.
This unit consists of an exploration of how psychological theories, methods and evidence can be used to assist in understanding and solving contemporary social problems. It is also designed to enable you to develop the skills necessary to present this information to policy makers.
The unit will introduce you to some of the major debates and controversies in modern psychology and in contemporary society. In addition to these major debates, you will also be encouraged to identify and explore some real-world problems that interest you.
The unit will prepare you for further research in psychology with an applied focus and for employment in organisations - including business, government and the non-government sector- whose work requires the application of psychology to policy development.  
It will also ensure that you understand the limits of such research. Although a lot is now known about human behaviour from fundamental, often laboratory based research, there is still relatively little evidence about how these research findings can actually be applied to influence the behaviour of communities and populations.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

This unit takes a blended approach, with lectures, online content, and tutorials. 
Lectures and online content will deal with a range of social problems to which psychological theories, methods and research findings are relevant. The content will focus both on a description and broad analysis of the selected social problems, as well as the application of psychological science to these problems. The aim of the lectures and online content is to advance understanding about complex social problems, help you appraise the key psychological aspects which contribute to this problem, and help you judge which of these are relevant to solving or relieving the problem. Overall, this content will show how scientific psychological investigation can assist in resolving societal debates.
In the tutorials, you will discuss a social problem in-depth, and identify the psychological constructs   that are relevant to understanding the problem and/or to intervening to change the problem. Through individual presentations delivered in tutorials, you will learn to communicate in a way that translates psychological theory into practical application for both lay and professional audiences. In and outside of the  tutorials, you will work together in small groups to design an intervention based on psychological research and theory to solve a social problem chosen by the group. The group interventions will then be translated to presentations that will be given in the last two tutorials. This group assignment will teach you how to collaborate in problem-focused think tanks to devise solutions to real-life problems.

Unit Structure

LECTURES
There will be a lecture each week (except the non-teaching week), on Thursdays from 4:00 to 5:45pm in the Social Science Lecture Theatre (G.130). Most of the lectures will be given by Dr Notebaert, but guest lecturers will also contribute. These guest lecturers will highlight some of the research and applied work that is conducted at the School of Psychological Science in specific problem areas. On overview of the problem areas engaged with in this unit in 2020 can be found in the unit schedule below. 
TUTORIALS
Tutorials will be held most weeks (see unit schedule below) and will last for 1 hour. In the first seven tutorials, you will discuss a social problem in-depth, and identify the psychological constructs   that are relevant to understanding the problem and/or to intervening to change the problem. The tutorials will consist of individual presentations and group discussions.  
Individual presentations: Each tutorial requires you to come prepared to speak to at least one psychological construct relevant to understanding and/or ameliorating a part of the social problem for that week. In each tutorial, three or four students will be selected by the tutor to briefly (in 2 minutes) outline the issue they have researched and explain the relevance of the psychological construct/theory to this issue.  These presentations will contribute to your participation mark. You will be given at least two opportunities to speak during the semester, one of which will occur during the first five weeks of semester. This first formative feedback will give you an idea about how well you are able to describe a social problem and argue the importance of a psychological construct associated with it. This skill will also be critical to your policy report, the group presentation, and the final exam. 
Group discussions: For some tutorials, scenarios are listed that describe a particular challenge relevant to the social problem that is examined that week. You will need to come prepared to take part in the discussion on how to respond to that challenge. Prepared students will refer to the previous week’s lectures and also to relevant papers/readings. Formulating responses to these scenarios will also help prepare you for the final exam, in which application of the knowledge you have gained throughout the unit is similarly assessed.
Note on the policy report word count: The maximum word count for the policy report is 2500 words. This includes the abstract, but not the glossary, table of contents, figure captions, table headings, references, or footnotes.

Participation

LECTURES
Although the lectures will be recorded and made available on Blackboard, it is strongly recommended that students attend. Previous years’ results indicate that students who attend lectures in-person consistently achieve better results than those who do not.
TUTORIALS
Tutorial attendance is mandatory, and you must attend the tutorial that your timetable stipulates. You can miss one tutorial without providing justification without penalty, provided you do not miss your individual presentations and group presentation. You can also arrange a one-off tutorial swap with your tutor and the tutor of the class you would like to swap into for that week.

Referencing Requirements

The policy report assignment does not require the same in-text referencing style as a typical psychology lab report. More flexibility in in-text referencing is allowed as the aim is to present a report that is attractive and easy to navigate. We encourage a numbered referencing method, such as the Vancouver style (https://guides.library.uwa.edu.au/vancouver). However, regardless of what in-text method you use, you need to include a full reference list at the end of your policy report document. This list needs to contain all the information that someone needs to follow up your source. 

Texts

There is no assigned text. Students are provided with selected articles on the relevant research and theory in psychology,current affairs and policy. These are posted as PDFs or links on Blackboard.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
