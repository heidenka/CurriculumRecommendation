Unit Information

UNIT SYNOPSIS 
This outline provides information for students studying GEOG3304 Environmental Change in the School of Agriculture and Environment in the first semester of 2020.
The unit focuses on the scientific basis for predicting the impact of environmental change on global systems. This is investigated by studying the underlying processes. The impact of climate change on global biomes and the human imprint on oceans, the atmosphere and land is considered in relation to past and future climates. The scientific assumptions and limitations in models for predicting environmental change are explored. Concepts introduced during lectures will be further explored in workshops. Both individual and group work will be used to develop understanding of the unit material.
UNIT GOAL
The aim of this unit is to enable students to understand contemporary environmental and climate change issues from a local to a global scale. This will be achieved through discussion of the causal mechanisms, and their impacts on the physical and social-economic world. Students will develop sufficient expertise to participate in the wider climate change debate and to understand the relevant issues for future careers in environmental science, planning and management.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

STUDENT LEARNING OUTCOMES
1.	An understanding of the controls of environmental change processes; 
2.	An understanding of the nature of recent and future climate change; 
3.	An appreciation of how climate impacts on the wider global environment; 
4.	An ability to place the science of future climate change into a societal context; 
5.	An acquisition of skills necessary to examine evidence and interpret scientific data;
 for understanding environmental processes and changes from the local to the global scale; 
6.	An ability to identify and critically evaluate specific questions related to   environmental change; and 
7.	An ability to justify the scientific investigation necessary to answer these questions. 
It is anticipated that after successfully completing this unit students will be able to:
A.	Demonstrate an understanding of the controls of environmental change processes, including and the nature of recent and future climate change.
B.	Describe the scientific techniques available to investigate environmental change and define the circumstances where their use is appropriate.
C.	Demonstrate ability to source, interpret and analyse evidence to explain environmental processes at different scales, including
•	critically evaluating published literature related to environmental change,
•	identifying questions that are unanswered, and 
•	suggesting well-justified experimental approaches for further research.
D.	Demonstrate an ability to collaborate and reflect in small teams to share understanding of contemporary issues in environmental change.
E.	Collaborate in small teams to prepare a comprehensive report that focuses on key 	results of scientific research.

Referencing Requirements

REFERENCING
When completing academic work it is essential that students refer to the sources of their information within the text. Complete details of these works should be arranged alphabetically in a reference list at the end of the essay. The Faculty prefers the use of either the Harvard or the APA reference style. The style chosen must be used consistently throughout any document. These may be accessed at through the UWA Library at http://www.is.uwa.edu.au/information-resources/guides
Text Referencing
There are standard procedures for referring to authors and their work, for example:
1.	Smith and Brown (2006) supported the interpretation….
2.	… when recognising two inseparable criteria of experience; continuity and interaction (Clandinin & Connelly 2000). 
3.	When referring to a specific page or pages of a reference:
Labaree (2006 p. 17), (Labaree 2006 p.17), Labaree (2006 pp.17-18)
4.	When referring to more than one work by the same author(s):
Fink and Brayman (2004, 2006)
5.	When referring to more than one work in the same year:
Hargreaves (2005a), Hargreaves (2005b)
6.	When there are two authors:
(Tilbury & Wortman 2003); Tilbury and Wortman (2003)
7.	When there are three or more authors:
(Leithwood et al., 1999)
Note: in the reference list, multiple authors should be listed in full.
8.	When referring to a publication that has no specific author, but which was written by an organization: (UNESCO 1992).
Reference List
All work referenced in the text must also be included (in alphabetical order by author) in the reference list at the end of the assignment.  This also applies to websites but cautious use of websites is recommended.

PLAGIARISM
The Faculty of Science has a Policy on Plagiarism which is available from the Faculty and School Offices.
It is expected that work submitted for assessment by a University student is the students’ own work, and that any contribution from others (ideas, facts or direct quotations) is given suitable acknowledgement by referring to the source (in a list of references, an acknowledgements section or in some other appropriate way).
If such acknowledgement is not given, the writer is guilty of plagiarism. Plagiarism is the use of published work (book, articles, reports) or the work of other students (present or past, at this or any other University), as though it were one`s own. In some cultures, it is regarded as a sign of respect to describe events or ideas in exactly the same words as an acknowledged expert. In the academic tradition to which this University belongs, it is not; it is thought of as mere copying.
Special care is necessary in the case of group work. Where a single report is required from a group of several students, it will be assumed that all students have contributed.
Students not happy with the contribution of other group members should make appropriate representations to the appropriate staff member. Where each student is required to hand in an individual report based on group research, work of another individual student (e.g. diagrams, photographs) should be acknowledged.
Unfortunately, students have been penalised by many Schools within the University for plagiarism. If in any doubt, discuss the matter with your unit coordinator. 
You can access the UWA plagiarism portal at:

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;
