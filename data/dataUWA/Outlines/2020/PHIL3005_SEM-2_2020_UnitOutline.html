Academic Information

Content This unit explores the work of a number of influential continental philosophers of the nineteenth and twentieth centuries, especially those philosophers belonging to the phenomenological tradition. Thinkers discussed may include Kant, Nietzsche, Husserl, Heidegger, Merleau-Ponty, Gadamer and Derrida. Much continental philosophy can be read as an attempt to find a way between traditional philosophical dichotomies such as subject/object, internal/external, realism/anti-realism. As a result, each of these philosophers has important things to say about truth, meaning, the self, knowledge and our relation to others. Given the profound influence of these philosophers on Western thought, their work is of central importance to all students of the humanities and social sciences.

Unit Information

This unit explores the work of a number of influential continental philosophers of the nineteenth and twentieth centuries, especially those philosophers belonging to the existential/phenomenological tradition. Some of the thinkers discussed include Nietzsche, Husserl, Heidegger, Merleau-Ponty, Gadamer and Derrida. Much of this continental philosophy can be read as an attempt to find a way between traditional philosophical dichotomies such as subject/object, internal/external, realism/anti-realism. As a result, each of these philosophers has important things to say about truth, meaning, the self, knowledge and our relation to others. Given the profound influence of these philosophers on Western thought, their work is of central importance to all students of the humanities and social sciences.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

The unit will be taught by online lectures and a tutorial. It is expected that all students read, at the very least, the essential material prior to each week. 
1. Lectures: The lectures will trace some of the many connections among the seminar topics and will help you to identify the philosophical themes and problems that run through much continental philosophy. The lectures will be available at the beginning of each week via Echo360. The power-point slides will also be available on LMS, however you are expected to take your own, more detailed, notes whilst listening to the lectures. 
 2. Tutorials: The tutorials will form the skeleton of the unit and must be punctually attended and carefully prepared. The current plan is to offer the tutorial face-to-face and online via a live zoom link. This will enable students to choose whether to be there in person or digitally. Details to be confirmed.

Unit Structure

Two one-hour lectures and one one-hour tutorial per week.
The lectures will be pre-recorded and available at the beginning of each week.
The tutorial will be held face-to-face and online via zoom simultaneously. Please nominate how you would prefer to attend in Week 1, so that I can be sure that the room capacity meets Covid19 regulations. If you choose to participate via zoom, the recording capacity will be blocked to protect your privacy. I will project the zoom page, so that you feel more like you are a part of the room. Whilst having video enabled is your choice, it is preferred. This is an experiment in teaching, so your patience will be much appreciated, should we run into technical difficulties. 
The list of tutorial topics contains an essential reading for each topic, as well as suggestions for further reading. Whilst attendance is not compulsory, tutorials and their preparation, as well as essays and informal discussion will be where the solid work should be done in this unit. 
Their is a study break between Weeks 9&10 (28th Sept - 2nd Oct).
There are no lectures in Week 12, however the tutorial is essential to the final topic - Derrida.

Participation

Your assessment for the unit will be based on three marks obtainable from three tasks as follows: 
 (i) One 1200-word summary and critique of an essay by a Continental philosopher 
 (ii) One 2000-word essay 
 (iii) Open book two short essay exam. 
Each of these three items will be given a mark out of 100; your final mark for the unit will be based on their non-weighted average. No significant overlap in content among the three is permitted. 
Tutorial attendance and contribution throughout the semester will also be taken into account when determining the final mark, and may result in an upward revision of the mark awarded for written work. 
The pass grades used nationwide are High Distinction (HD: 80–100%), Distinction (D: 70–79%), Credit (CR: 60–69%) and Pass (P: 50–59%).

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
