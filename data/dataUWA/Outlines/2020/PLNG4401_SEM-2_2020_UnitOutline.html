Academic Information

Content This unit provides students with the opportunity to engage in a real-world research based master planning project for an urban or regional location. Students are introduced to key planning theories, concepts and research techniques required to develop a concept master plan. Students study the traditions and philosophies of planning, planning ethics, plan making, and other planning practice issues from the perspective of public and private sectors.

Unit Information

Welcome to PLNG4401 (Planning Theory and Practice) which is a core unit in the Masters in Urban and Regional Planning.
For 2020 this unit will be led by Assoc. Professor Paul Maginn (Unit Co-ordinator) with additional inputs from a mix of staff and guest speakers that include: (i) Professor Neil Foley (NF); (ii) Andrew Brodie/Kevin Vizzutti/Alysha Saunders (Roberts Day); (iii)   Clay Thomas (Peet); and (iv) Chris Swiderski (Flyt).
This unit is fundamentally concerned with looking at urban and regional  planning through a broad theoretical/conceptual lens and, more crucially, a practice lens via the development of group-based master-plan.
We will examine general theoretical/conceptual issues in relation to urban planning policy, planning research and plan-making. This will be achieved via a mix of: (i) interactive lectures, (ii) workshops/studios; (iii) guided  fieldtrips; and, (v) fieldwork. 
Students will assume the role of a planning consultancy and your overall aims/objectives are to develop planning ideas, understand planning policies and collect/analyse data  that will help inform your your final master plan.
This unit is about three fundamental issues:
•	Understanding and undertaking planning research, the activities and processes associated with collecting and analysing various forms of primary and secondary data and information, that underpin the development and creation of ALL plans;
•	Assembling all data and information and presenting it in a plan, a planning report and a formal presentation so as to convey your overall planning vision and details of your planning proposals; and
•	Providing students with a taste of the realities of professional planning practice and the planning process by working on a real planning/development site.
This taste of planning reality is grounded in the fact that your master plan case study is a real site with real planning challenges and opportunities.  Furthermore, we will be holding classes at the offices of Roberts Day, located on 442 Murray Street, Perth, in order to give you a sense of what a real planning consultancy looks, feels and operates.  Planning practice and plan-making is a team effort with individual planners (and other professionals) bringing different specialist knowledge and skills together to resolve planning problems.
Team-based  work is a defining aspect of professional planning practice.  The involvement of many different actors means that  professional  practice  is  often fraught with challenges and difficulties. Deadlines  must  be  met  and  outputs   must be produced.  Individual team members	need to assume responsibility for their assigned key tasks and deliver outputs in a timely manner and of a sufficient  standard to assist the rest of the group. At times, tough decisions and compromises will have to be made. Individual and collective leadership will need to be demonstrated and project management executed to  ensure  everyone  and  everything is focused.
As postgraduate students therefore you are expected to demonstrate a willingness and ability to work and think independently and laterally, using your own initiative and be critically reflective. Successful delivery of your final master plan outputs rely upon collaboration, co-ordination and commitment to one another.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

Learning Outcomes
1. Develop planning and policy research knowledge and skills via collecting and analysing different forms of primary and secondary data;
2. Develop and enhance report writing and editing skills;
3. Develop creative and confident presentation skills; and,
4. Appreciate the complexities and needs of working independently and collaboratively in group contexts and to time deadlines.
In overall terms, your task is to develop a strategic planning vision underpinned by robust and comprehensive data collection and critical analysis that seeks to create a diverse, vibrant and sustainable town centre for local residents, workers and visitors.
You will need to develop a set of cogent, inter-related and justifiable planning and non-planning related visions, proposals and policies for the overall study site and discrete areas within the town centre.
The outline concept master plan exercise and all its assignment outputs is a 100% group-based assignment. Students are to assume the role of a planning consultancy with responsibility for fulfilling the following simple brief:
•	develop an overall planning (and design) vision for the site;
•	prepare a suitably scaled and zoned planning map and any other  relevant precinct plans/maps to illustrate the location, size and nature of key land uses (e.g. housing, retail, commercial, industrial, transport,  open space, and so on) within the project site;
•	prepare a planning report that sets out in general detail the broad set of conceptual ideas, proposals and/or policies – e.g. physical, social, economic, environmental and/or infrastructural – that reflect your planning vision for the site and take into account relevant planning (and non-planning) policy frameworks and analysis of data on, for example, demographic, housing, retail, economic, environmental issues pertinent to the site;
•	utilise appropriate graphics and imagery, ideally your own, to help illustrate your vision and proposals; and
•	provide a clear rationale and justification for your vision and broad conceptual proposals throughout the planning report.
The aims/objectives of this brief need to be reflected in your:
A. Plan: This should comprise: (i) a suitably sized, scaled and zoned plan/map that illustrates the key land-use proposals (e.g. residential, commercial, community, open spaces, transport etc) that you envisage for the site in overall terms; and (ii) suitably sized, scaled and zoned
 
precinct/neighbourhood plans for the project site. All plans are to form a part of the final report.
B. Report: The report should outline the rationale and justification of the vision and specific proposals contained in your concept plan. In other words, the report is the textual representation of your analytical research and interpretation into the planning and non-planning  policy frameworks underpinning your outline concept master plan. As such, this will necessitate conducting research, analysing data and writing up your findings/interpretations on a number of fronts, including, but not limited to, the following items:
1. site analysis and wider spatial context analysis,
2. key federal, state and local government policies and legislation,
3. demographic and socio-economic analysis,
4. property market analysis – residential, commercial, and industrial;
5. public life surveys
6. land-use surveys
7 built environment assessment
8. infrastructure and services,
9. landownership issues, and
10. public services and community facilities audit.
The report should also present illustrated visualisations of your planning and design vision for the area in terms of any new proposed space/places, buildings, facilities, open spaces, traffic and pedestrian networks and systems, and streetscapes.
C. Poster: This should be an informative and creative visual and textual summary of the overall vision and key aspects of your master plan proposals and report. The poster should be A0 in size and matt laminated.
D. Presentation: You will be required to present your master plan and proposals to a panel of judges made up of practising planners.
Both the poster  (20%)  and  presentation  (20%)  will  be  assessed  by  a  panel  of professional planners comprising up to 3-4 people. The  poster  and  presentation  are  both  due  on Thursday 22nd October and  should  be  submitted  to  Assoc.  Prof.  Paul Maginn.

Unit Structure

PLNG4401 is delivered via 2 intensive blocks of teaching over the course of the day.
Block 1 (9-12.00/12.30 morning session) This block will be used for interactive lecture sessions and/or field visits to the master plan study site. Lectures conducted  during this session are designed to be interactive and reflective in character in order to illustrate the linkages between planning/design theory and planning practice so as to bridge the so-called professional gap that exists between planning theory and practice.
Block 2 (2-5.30 afternoon session) This block has been arranged so as to provide students with a dedicated time and space to work in their ‘consultancy teams’ on the outline concept master plan project.	This afternoon session will generally be more practical in content and focus and is designed to enable you to develop your own knowledge and skills that will enable you to tackle the outline concept master plan project. Particular emphasis will be paid to helping you improve some of your urban design skills, planning policy knowledge and planning research skills so that you can present your master plan proposals in a professional manner via your plan, report, poster and presentation.	This block will also be used to conduct lectures and/or fieldwork as part of your master plan project.
There will be at least ONE organised fieldtrip and TWO fieldwork days for PLNG4401 in 2020 as part of the master plan project. These include:
(a)	an initial guided tour of the project site on Friday 31st July (2.00-5.00pm) with representatives from the LGA that the project site is located; and
(b)	At least 2 fieldwork visits will be used to collect data as part of the public life survey and the land-use survey. It is anticipated that these will take place on: Friday 4th Sept; and Saturday 6th September.
Student groups will also need to conduct their own additional site visits to the actual project site and adjacent land areas in order to develop a more nuanced understanding of the local built environment, business activity, traffic and congestion, parking, infrastructure, sense of community and vibrancy and the wider spatial contexts that surround and impinge upon the project site.
Classes marked Outline Concept Master Plan Group Project Session in the schedule below have been set aside for you to come together in your ‘consultancy teams’ so that you can work and focus on your master plan projects. These sessions will also be used for you to ask advice from staff members and guest contributors. Students will also be required to meet on their own time to discuss and progress their master plan. Hence, you will need to develop a meeting schedule for your groups.
On Friday 11th October all groups are to deliver a draft presentation/ overview of their emerging draft masterplan. This is to see how well you are progressing towards completing your project and also an opportunity to get feedback on your proposals and ideas.A range of key readings – academic and policy - for this course will be posted on the course webpage and/or distributed in class. Students are, of course, expected to source and utilise other readings and materials in order to demonstrate that they are researching independently and actively seeking to develop their intellectual and planning knowledge and skills.

Peer Review
Real world planning practice is dependent upon team work. Hence, for this assignment it is imperative that ALL individual members do their utmost to contribute fully and equally to the workload of each group. This means amongst other things, attending all scheduled group meetings, conducting fieldwork and analysing relevant data, producing written and/or graphical work on time and to a satisfactory quality. In order to award an individual  mark to each student, a student-based peer review process will be conducted at the end of the semester – the activity log will be extremely helpful here in determining the contribution of each team member.
The peer review process requires each student assessing, quantitatively and qualitatively, as well as honestly, the overall contribution of each member (including oneself) to their group master plan project. The peer review process is designed to do two things: (i) recognise and reward individual effort and contribution; and (ii) eradicate and penalise any free-riding. Ultimately, the  peer review process may result in some students’ individual marks deviating, positively or negatively, from the overall  group  mark  awarded  to  each master  plan.  Individual marks may fluctuate by a maximum of 30% points above/below the overall group mark. Ultimately, students who are deemed and subsequently proven to have contributed very little to  their  group project  face the prospect of scoring a fail grade - hence, the importance  of keeping the weekly activity log to record each group members' contribution.
In the event that any group experiences difficulties in terms of one or more  team members not contributing fairly to the project, then please alert the unit co-ordinator as soon as possible so that any issues/problems can be resolved promptly.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
