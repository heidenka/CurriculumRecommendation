Academic Information

Content Topics include revision of reaction kinetics, mechanisms and rate equations; fundamentals of ideal reactor behaviour; design of batch, completely mixed and plug flow reactors for single and multiple reactions; multiple and recycle reactors; nonisothermal reactor design for reversible and irreversible first order reaction; residence time distribution (RTD); RTD modelling of non-ideal reactor behaviour; diffusion control heterogeneous reactions; runaway reactions and safety, and shrinking-core model. In this unit, students develop the ability to (1) apply knowledge of basic science and engineering fundamentals; (2) utilise a system approach to optimise design and operational performance; and (3) understand the need to undertake lifelong learning, and the capacity to do so.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

This unit is very mathematical covering many derivations of equations from first principle. The lecture will be delivered face to face. The best way to learn this unit is to solve as many reaction engineering problems as possible so as to attain a good fundamental understanding of this unit. 

Accuracy of the answers or calculations is extremely important for a professional design and process engineer. There are many real
world examples where a simple engineering calculation error resulted in multimillions dollar losses to companies. You as a process
design engineer could be asked by your company to perform design calculations of a process vessel such as a flow reactor which will
be constructed based on your calculations. Hence in this unit accuracy is an important assessment parameter. You are
advised to check and double check all your calculations.

In the weekly 2-hr tutorial session, students will be asked to solve the problems in class. Students are allow to collaborate. Each
problem will be given a specific time to solve and after that the tutor will go through the solution of the problem with the class.
Mathematica software will also be used to solve some of the more complex math problems in this unit.

Your journey toward becoming a Professional Engineer
The Chemical Engineering Programme of The University of Western Australia is fully accredited by Engineers Australia. This Programme will nurture you toward achieving the Stage 1 Competency Standard for Professional Engineer as stipulated by Engineers Australia. Armed with the Master of Professional Engineering degree in Chemical Engineering, students can aspire eventually to apply for Chartered Professional Engineer status (CPEng) with Engineers Australia.
The four Stage 1 Competencies are covered by 16 mandatory Elements of Competency and 3 Programme-specific Elements of Competency. The Competencies and Elements of Competency represent the profession's expression of the knowledge and skill base, engineering application abilities, and professional skills, values and attitudes that must be demonstrated at the point of entry to practice, namely upon the successful completion of the Master of Professional Engineering Degree at The University of Western Australia. More information can be accessed from the Engineers Australia website https://www.engineersaustralia.org.au/resource-centre/resource/stage-1-competency-standard-professional-engineer .

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
