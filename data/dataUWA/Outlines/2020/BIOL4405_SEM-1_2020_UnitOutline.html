Academic Information

Content This is an advanced unit in invertebrate diversity and relationships, and comprises three modules. The first module establishes the relationships between the 30+ phyla and the remaining two investigate the function of invertebrates in functional and practical settings. Teaching is by lectures, discussion groups, practical classes and online learning. The learning integrates across all facets of the teaching. The assessment elucidates not only the content learned but also understanding of the physiological, ecological, phylogenetic and systematic principles involved. The unit provides a strong advantage to those students intending to progress to higher degree studies, and also those intending to seek employment in the conservation and consultation-based industries, both private and governmental.

Unit Information

The unit will cover the “major” invertebrate Phyla (33 phyla based on the textbook) in terms of classification, phylogenetic arrangement, morphological structure, and physiological function. The intent of the unit is to describe the classification and structure (Phylum, Class, Order, …….) for the major phyla, put these phyla in a phylogenetic context (inter-relationships) and establish the patterns of various morphological and functional traits that define (or obfuscate) these relationships. Overall, a traditional classification approach (see textbook; Hickman et al. 2017) will be used to organise invertebrates into similar morphological/physiological groups (e.g. protists, sponges, annelids, molluscs, arthropods). A systems approach (see Pechenik 2015; Kay 1998; Withers 1992) will then be used to cover the important aspects of each classification group, such as tissues/organs, feeding, gas exchange, circulation and blood, iono/osmoregulation, muscular systems and locomotion, sensory systems, nervous systems, and reproduction. Particular topics will be dealt with in discussion sessions (e.g. temperature, metabolism, buoyancy, iono/osmoregulation, gas exchange, circulation, blood).

Learning And Teaching Strategy

This unit presents a range of topics related to Invertebrate Zoology at both an information level (e.g. lectures, “flipped” discussion sections) and a practical level (various exercises selected to give students an opportunity to see and study invertebrates in an experimental context).
This unit requires students to be prepared for both scheduled and independent study at the level appropriate to a postgraduate unit (see unit expectations). 
Unit expectations are that students will:
1.	make a genuine attempt to participate in all aspects of the unit, including lectures, discussion sections, laboratory exercises; this unit is to some extent presented using the “flipped-classroom” model and so student preparation before-hand, attendance at, and participation in, the “discussion lectures” is important for the learning outcome for all students enrolled in the unit;
2.	commit the required time for independent work to keep abreast of material; 
3.	If having difficulty with the academic content or preparation of an assignment, ask the unit coordinator for help in a timely manner i.e. well before the due date so you have time to work on the assignment;
4.	submit assignments on time via the LMS website/Turnitin
Students are expected to have the basic generic skills that would have been acquired through an undergraduate degree equivalent to the UWA BSc with a major in Zoology. Generic skills include
1.	 basic word-processing (e.g. Microsoft Word) for writing of assignments; e.g. page layout, numbering, structure of assignments, formatting text and paragraphs, use of textboxes for embedding figures, etc
2.	spreadsheet skills (e.g. Microsoft Excel) for data analysis and graphical presentation; e.g. storing data, performing calculations, using basic and complex formula calculations, and graphical presentation of results, etc
3.	basic statistical analysis skills using the Data Analysis Toolpak in Excel, or statistiXL (an Excel add-in statistics package for use on PCs [not MACs]; see www.statistixl.com for free 1 month trial); e.g. t-tests, ANOVA, correlation, regression, etc
4.	basic graphical analysis skills, using graphs prepared and edited in Excel to present various kinds of data summaries e.g. line plots, X-Y scatterplots, pie charts, histograms, etc
5.	basic presentation skills (using Powerpoint) for oral presentation of topics; e.g. use of appropriate backgrounds and colour schemes, use of appropriate amount of text and figures on slides, use of audio and video resources, etc
6.	basic phylogenetic-based concepts and analyses (using Mesquite; see http://mesquiteproject.org website); e.g. preparation of phylogenetic trees with node distances (in MYBP), inclusion of categorical and numeric traits (morphological, physiological, etc), use of an outgroup, determination of likely ancestral states, etc

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
