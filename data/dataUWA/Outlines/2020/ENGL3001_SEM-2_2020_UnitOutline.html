Academic Information

Content This unit provides a study of poetry in English from the United Kingdom to the USA and Australia, and draws on the entire period from Medieval times to the present. Poetry is a rather marginalised art in contemporary Western societies, including Australia, but those involved in it tend to believe in its value passionately. This derives from the belief that poetry is the deepest form of human expression, certainly the most profound form that language ever attains. The unit examines poetry and poetic language, covering both technical and philosophical elements of poetry and relating them to shifts in cultural values during the period studied. As a Level 3 unit, this unit aims to further develop students' skills in research, critical analysis and oral and written communication through more focused engagements with specific texts and theories, and via a greater emphasis on independent learning through strategies such as targeted research tasks, small group projects and the opportunity to develop individual essay topics. This prepares students for future studies at honours and postgraduate levels, or as innovative and productive researchers and communicators throughout their chosen careers.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

The three essential elements of this unit are lectures, tutorials and personal reading. One of the recognised study skills in the humanities is speed reading, particularly in subjects where there is a large volume of material to cover. Understanding poetry, and gaining the most out of this unit, on the other hand, depends on 'slow reading' - that is, reading with the ear attuned to the sounds, as well as reading with the eye and the mind. Remember that poems are generally 'open texts,' which allow multiple interpretations, and invite their readers to become co-creators of meaning. As you read, be attentive to the effect of any formal properties, such as rhythm or rhyme, and to the figurative dimensions of language. Give yourself time for re-reading and reflection. 
Another strategy is to read beyond the syllabus, dipping into an anthology or a recognised internet poetry site in order to find poems you like or to get a sense of different styles. 
Readings for most weeks will be found on LMS, but there are three weeks where we have set a book (see under Texts below). Each week there are supporting critical readings to be found on LMS. These are not intended to be definitive, but to introduce you to critical debates. They are secondary to the primary works of the unit, the poems. In addition, the Unit Readings page on the Library website has some excellent resources to enhance your understanding of poetry and poetics.
A recorded lecture-workshop will be available in week 1 to provide an additional lesson on poetic techniques and forms.

Unit Structure

There will be one lecture and one tutorial per week in teaching weeks. Tutorials begin in week 2. In addition to the lecture in week 1, there will be a second lecture/workshop on poetic techniques and forms. Lectures and tutorials run in parallel, with the same texts to be discussed in both each week. After the introductory two weeks, the unit is organised chronologically beginning with Beowulf and ending with the collaboration between contemporary West Australian poets Charmaine Papertalk-Green and John Kinsella. Most weeks focus on a particular poet or text, but others address an important genre or cluster of poets.

Participation

Literature is a subject that benefits greatly from learning through discussion. As a genre, poetry creates productive ambiguity through connotation, symbolism and other techniques. The tutorial is a space where dialogue about different readings is encouraged, and where different perspectives can be developed, weighed up, compared, and explored. Regular engagement in discussion is therefore vital. In addition to general participation, each student will be required to do one short presentation.
With the COVID-19 virus still a factor affecting our lives, we hope to make both on-campus and online tutorial facilities available. Further details about the methods of online participation will be provided in LMS.

Referencing Requirements

Students are encouraged to support their readings by drawing on the work of published critics, and secondary reading lists will be uploaded on LMS for this purpose. When engaging with critics or theorists, whether by quotation or paraphrase, you must document your sources. The preferred citation style in English and Literary Studies is the MLA style: https://guides.library.uwa.edu.au/mla

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
