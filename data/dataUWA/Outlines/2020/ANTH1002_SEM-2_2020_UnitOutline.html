Content What does globalisation mean at local levels? In the face of so much 'progress', why are many people still so poor and others so rich? This unit explores how people's lives have been transformed by colonialism, economic development and globalisation. Focusing particularly on Australia and our region, it examines various social science perspectives on inequality, migration, urbanisation, work, modernisation, globalisation, culture, communication and the environment.

Unit Information

What does globalisation mean at local levels? In the face of so much 'progress', why are many people still so poor and others so rich? In these extraordinary times, this unit allows us to contemplate some of the key issues of our time - racism, gender and class inequality, environmental damage, to name but four. This unit explores how people's lives have been transformed by colonialism, economic development and change, as well of course by globalisation itself. Learning And Teaching Strategy

We are living through extraordinary times, a period when face to face lectures have been "onlined" and learning and teaching in many universities across the globe is requiring unprecedented levels of online engagement. Fortunately, we are able to return to F2F tutorials this semester, allowing many students more immediate contact with peers and teachers - at least for the time being. 
The extensive use of online facilities is not new to this particular unit. Focused very much on active learning, inviting you to participate as fully as possible in the unit, Anth1002 is taught in "flipped mode". Essentially this means placing the unit material into your Learning Managment System (LMS) opening up spaces for informed exchange between students and tutors, primarily through tutorials, but also in a workshop that will be broadcast live online and facilitated by the unit coordinator - Assoc Prof Martin Forsey. 
The exchanges are designed to test out ideas, reinforce knowledge, raise important questions - opening up spaces for you to engage as actively as you can with the unit material with and through your peers, under the experienced, expert guidance of your tutors. 
Research shows this to be highly effective form of teaching - mainly because it is so strongly focused on how students learn. This same research shows that, providing expectations for the unit are clear and the learning processes are well designed, most students respond favourably to flipped learning. Vital to this learning in the social sciences, is the provision of opportunities for students to engage with the unit material in a careful, considered manner, with regular opportunities to test out the learning and to reinforce the knowledge gained in consultation with fellow students and the teachers.  v

These will be further consolidated in the tutorial/workshops, for which students will usually be required to conduct some research into key ideas, concepts, and case studies.

Participation

Tutorials are an essential part of the unit and you are expected to attend all tutorials. Tutorial attendance, preparation, and participation are assessed weekly by your tutor. You must attend your assigned tutorial session and will not receive credit for attending another tutorial. Absences will be excused only through formal special consideration procedures.
Participation entails thoughtful and generous engagement with your peers and tutors with the aim of advancing understanding of the subject matter. Each week you will provided with a tutorial worksheet which must be completed. These are to help guide your thinking on the weekly topic, and they will form the basis of the discussions in class. Demonstrated completion of this written preparation contributes towards your tutorial mark.

Referencing Requirements

It is important to properly acknowledge your sources. In your research essay for this unit, you must properly reference your sources - clearly indicate when you are quoting directly, include citations for all sources that you quote, paraphrase, or otherwise reference, and include a complete reference list comprising all and only those sources that you have cited. Follow the APA In-text Referencing Style - as detailed here: https://guides.library.uwa.edu.au/c.php?g=324904&p=2809542. Please note that you should include page numbers when citing specific passages from written sources. The UWA referencing guide recommends that, 'When paraphrasing text include page number/s in the in-text citation but do not use quotation marks. There is a right and a wrong way to paraphrase. - see the Study Smarter guide at http://www.student.uwa.edu.au/__data/assets/pdf_file/0011/1861319/R4-Paraphrasing.pd'Be aware that the work you submit must be your own. To pass off work as your own, if you have copied it from someone else is to deprive yourself of the real learning benefits of this unit and also constitutes academic misconduct. Such misconduct will lead to academic penalties, which can include failing the assessment, the unit, or your course of study. A Turnitin originality report will be generated for your research essay.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
