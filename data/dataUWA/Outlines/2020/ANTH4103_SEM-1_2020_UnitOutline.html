Academic Information

Content This unit considers epistemology in the social sciences or, simply, how we know what we know in the social sciences in general. It considers theories of knowledge. As such, epistemological questions are central in the social sciences, especially when evaluating differences among various schools of thought. Our traversal of this terrain considers some of the crucial questions in theory construction and validation in the social sciences from an epistemological perspective. Students consider questions such as how can underlying principles be discovered and validated? Can specific theories be proven and refuted? If so, what constitutes evidence for this? How commensurable are epistemological bases of various perspectives? Must we posit a reality outside cultural constructions to present interpretations and explanations? What sorts of epistemological assumptions support a stance of cultural relativism and what sorts deny that position? How does situated knowledge or tacit knowledge challenge claims for objectivity or relativism? What is the role of subjectivity in social analysis? What sorts of representational strategies best convey the epistemologies of others? Students address these questions through the works of Durkheimian sociology, Marxisms, Weberian sociology, interpretive anthropology, phenomenological anthropology, ethnomethodology, practice theory, Foucauldianism and feminism.

Unit Information

There are many ways to think about how we know what we know. At times, it seems that one’s approach influences what one finds. This is no different in everyday life as it is for the social sciences, especially anthropology and sociology. Investigating how we, anthropologists and sociologists and social scientists more broadly, construct arguments to arrive at certain conclusions is often left to a field called the philosophy of social science that studies epistemology, or how “truth” is formed and understood. While influenced by developments in this field, this unit will mostly engage in what is called “metatheory,” or theorizing about theory, which necessarily includes epistemology but will also delve into questions less discussed by philosophers of social science, especially style and politics. What this unit is not is an overview of contemporary social theory. Rather it is an investigation of theorizing. 
To that end, the unit will consist of reading and writing about social theory (broadly defined) not only for content but also with close attention to how claims are made and then substantiated. In other words, this unit is about digging below the surface of social scientific research. 
The unit is structured so that we begin with an overview of the received the classical “canon” shared by anthropology and sociology, which includes Marx, Weber and Durkheim. Unfortunately, due to constraints of time, we will not be able to explore other contemporaneous figures such as Freud, whose ideas we will encounter tangentially. After spending the first few weeks reviewing the classical canon, we will continue by exploring more recent articulations of those traditions. After that, the semester will finish off with perspectives that serve as critiques and/or alternatives to these traditions and their contemporary instantiations.   
Lastly, the unit will be organized to explore certain tensions. These include but are not limited to: 
•	Structure and Agency
•	Subject and Object
•	Ideology and False-Consciousness
•	Culture and Commodification
•	Symbols and Actions
•	Conscious and Unconscious
•	Premodern and Modern and Postmodern
•	Rationality and Irrationality

Learning And Teaching Strategy

By the end of this unit students should be able to:
(1) articulate the conceptual assumptions underlying some of the basic theoretical approaches in anthropology and sociology;
(2) develop the skills to read anthropological and sociological texts in a manner that discerns their underlying assumptions;
(3) evaluate texts in the philosophy of (social) science;
(4) articulate epistemological assumptions and how they are deployed in writing;
(5) develop skills to communicate in both oral and written expression to deal with theory in the social sciences.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
