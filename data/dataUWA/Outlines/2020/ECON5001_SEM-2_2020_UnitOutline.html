Academic Information

Content This unit examines contemporary issues in economic development, mainly in developing countries but with relevance to developed countries. The unit is problem- and policy-oriented. It covers thematic issues/areas of development from five capital perspectives: social, human, technical, natural and financial perspectives, and considers how the economic, social and institutional problems of under-development are interrelated and that require coordinated approaches to find their solution. The thematic areas covered include poverty and inequality, labour markets and migration, education, health, population, infrastructure, technology, markets, trade, microfinance, the environment, conflicts and corruption, and their relationship with under-development and economic development. In addition, a brief history of economic development is covered along with contemporary theories and analytical methods of economic development. Each theme is examined in an integrated and holistic approach to gain insights on success and failure of development policy/intervention. For example, why do poverty traps persist and what can be done to help people get out of poverty? Why does labour migrate and what are its consequences? Why do institutions matter for development? How do environmental protection policies affect local economic growth? What is the role of technology and infrastructure in development?

ECON5001 - Economic Development in Theory and Practice - SEM-2-2020 - 2020 - Face to face / Blended learning

Unit Information

This unit examines contemporary issues in economic development, mainly in developing countries but with relevance to developed countries as well. The unit is problem and policy oriented. It covers thematic areas of (under) development from the perspectives of five capitals: social, human, technical, natural, and financial. It places emphasis on interrelated economic, social and institutional problems of underdevelopment, requiring coordinated approaches to explore solutions. The thematic areas covered in the unit include: poverty and inequality, labour markets and migration, education, health, infrastructure, technology, markets, trade, microfinance, the environment, conflicts and corruption. In addition, the relationships between these themes and economic development or underdevelopment are explored from contemporary theoretical and analytical perspectives, including techniques for impact evaluation. Each theme will be examined in an integrated way to gain insights on success and failure of development policies or interventions. For example, why do poverty traps persist and what can be done to help people for getting out of poverty? Why does labour migrate and what are its consequences? Why do institutions matter for development? How do environmental policies affect economic growth? What is the role of technology and infrastructure in economic development? 
The goal of this unit is to broaden students’ understanding of contemporary issues in economic development and equipped them with concepts and tools to critically examine such issues to find potential policy solutions. 
The objectives of this unit are to facilitate and encourage student to develop the ability and desire to:
1.	critically examine contemporary issues in economic development and their causes and consequences
2.	critically analyse economic development issues using models from economic, social, technical, human and natural capital perspectives
3.	critically evaluate development policy interventions (as case studies) to learn lessons for improving development policy-making
Learning outcomes
On completion of this unit students should be able to:
1.	discuss, explain and review current debates in economics of development such as role of different forms of capitals in economic development 
2.	develop critical thinking in suggesting solutions to development problems such as chronic poverty, youth unemployment, environmental degradation, and institutional and 
    market failure
3.	learn to apply basic economic tools of analysis to address development issues such as poverty, vulnerability and inequality
4.	understand the cause, consequences, and patterns of development and disparities in development across regions and nations
5.	provide critical analysis of real world challenges in development and propose potential solutions to promote development through policy interventions and investment

Learning And Teaching Strategy

Online lecture and tutorial/lab:
This unit is delivered online this semester (Semester 2, 2020). In general the lecture materials will be recorded and posted in advance in LMS. The lecture sessions will be used to discuss the topic or concept in an interactive environment.
For tutorial/lab session, preparatory materials will also be made available in advance through LMS and the session will be used for a range of activities, including discussion on lecture materials and thematic/assigned readings, problem solving, tutorial quizzes, and student presentation.
It is expected that students come to the session with  preparation so that they can contribute to the discussion effectively. Students are expected to read the essential readings assigned for each session to develop conceptual understanding covered in the lecture and also to foster mutual learning through sharing of ideas and understanding during the group discussion.
There will be some assigned readings or case studies each week. Check the detailed unit outline for text book chapters and unit reading for specific papers. In addition, each student will read a number of papers relevant to their assignment topic, which will be assigned once the assignment topic is decided during the first two weeks.
Assessment details
1. Quiz
There will be three online quizzes mostly composed of multiple choice and short answer questions. These quizzes are designed to test your conceptual understanding and the progress, and typically cover the contents of preceding weeks (for quiz 1 and 2). For example, quiz 1 covers the materials presented and discussed during the weeks 1 to 4, while the quiz 2 covers the contents of weeks 5 to 8. The last quiz is a summative quiz that covers the entire unit. Some of the questions in this quiz may demand extended answers. 
2. In-class activity (ICA)
In-class activity consists of three types of assessment items that students do during the lab/tutorial session time or immediately after during the specific week. Tutorial quiz and presentation will be done during the session itself, whereas problem solving part will be given as take-home assignments and student will submit their answers to problem solving question through LMS by specified due date (which will be announced while assigning the problem solving questions). 
Your active participation and engagement in in-class activities are essential to secure the marks and the marks are based on how well you performed the given task.
a)	Tutorial quiz (TQ)
Each week, except the first and last one, there will be a short online based on the assigned reading for that week. You will be asked to read ONE assigned paper for this purpose. The quiz will have multiple choice questions and take only about 10 minutes or so. 
b)	 Problem solving (PS)
Depending on the lecture content of the week, during the tutorial sessions you will do at least one problem solving activity which would relate to conceptual idea covered in the lecture. Such problem can also be based on discussion section of the theme paper(s)/case study(ies) of the week or it can just be a set of clarifying questions about the topic discussed in the lecture. In some cases, you will be given an assignment consists of a set of questions and data to analyse and submit a brief report of your findings on a later date. There will be about three such problem sets. [Note: weekly theme paper(s) is the paper assigned by the instructor for you to read during a specific week].
c)	Class Presentation (CP)
Presentation in the class about specific paper assigned to you is another form of in-class activity. Once a topic for your final assignment is identified within the first 3 weeks, a presentation schedule will be developed. In the following weeks, one of you will give a presentation on the papers you have read as a part of your final assignment-related reading. Students will propose a set of three papers that are linked to their final assignment and the instructor(s) will choose maximum of two papers for presentation purposes. [Note – these papers are specific to individual student and are different from weekly theme paper(s)/case study(ies), i.e. two of you may have different papers to read for the presentation purposes]. One of you will be assigned as a discussant for a given presentation and you are required to critique and provide comments on the presentation made by your colleague. 
The presentation marks will be divided into two parts: presenter 70% (of your own presentation) and discussant 30% (critiquing someone’s presentation).
The marking of your presentation will be based on the following aspects. The preciseness and accuracy of responses to the raised questions also counts.
 How well you have covered the assigned task/topic (6 key points)? It includes:
1.	Identification of the issue or the problem
2.	The economic causes and consequences related to the issue/problem
3.	How the author(s) approached the issue (methodological aspects) and why they did so instead of using other approaches?
4.	What did the author(s) or different papers find?
5.	What are the synthesis messages coming from the different papers, if there are more than one papers? 
6.	What are the policy relevance of these different findings (policy implications)?
3. Final written assignment (or term paper)
Assignment Details
This assignment is not an essay and must not be an essay. It is a different kind of exercise. It is meant to reflect the kind of analysis you would do in the real world for whomever would pay you for it. The problem you will analyse must reflect the content of this module and refer, in a given area or country, to the analysis of economic development and its challenges. 
The assignment topic will be selected in consultation between the student and the instructors within the first three weeks of the semester. During the first week, students will be asked to indicate and explore at least two (up to three) topics in which they would like to work on for the written assignment. Instructors will collect the topics of interest from each student and narrow it down to one for each student, considering the interest of the student as well as the coverage of different topics in the unit which is essential to cover different topics in the weekly presentations. 
From week three, sometime will be devoted to the final assignment during the tutorial/lab sessions. Students will be provided with a checklist comprised of various elements to cover in their assignment. This weekly work on final assignment is a standing-work for the entire semester but incrementally builds each week. There are two parts to the final assignment: submission of the outline and submission of the final version of the assignment (term paper).
a)	Assignment (Term Paper) outline: 
Once the assignment topic is finalised, the first few weeks of work during the tutorial session help you to develop assignment outline. Please refer to the template provided at the end of this unit outline (Table 5) which specifies a set of elements to cover in the final assignment. Use this as a way of thinking and organising your thoughts for the assignment. 
For the outline first few points in the template (1 to 4) are more relevant. How would you put these elements together in a logical order is something we will leave it to you for now. As a way of example, the outline could follow a format that relate to the following dot points. 
•	An informative title
•	Introducing the issue (what, where, why, whom & how?; all from economic perspective)
•	The analytical approach adapted (theoretical/conceptual basis, what sort information would you use and how would you synthesize the information to draw some lessons?)
•	Your expected findings and how they relate to solving or complicating the issue
•	References cited
Specifications for outline: In developing the assignment outline, you are required to cite at least 5 peer-reviewed articles in the text and provide their full citation details following APA Referencing Style (https://guides.library.uwa.edu.au/apa). You need to present your assignment outline clearly and concisely. The outline should be limited to 2 pages only (A4 size, 12 point font, single space) excluding the reference list. Please upload your outline in LMS using the Turnitin link provided by the due date (5:00pm Friday, 11 September 2020), and name your word file in this format: “Surname_First-name_Assignment-Outline.docx”.
BEWARE of plagiarism! Any hint of copying an existing document will immediately result in a mark of zero. Using an existing source but changing the words is still considered as plagiarism if the source is not clearly referenced! 
b)	Final assignment or Term Paper
The final assignment is where you present your detailed analysis drawing from the use of above template. Certainly, the feedback provided on your assignment outline would be an integral part of your final assignment. The structure of the final assignment could consists of different sections provided in Table 6 at the end of this unit outline (BUT bear in mind that the pointers given in each section are for you to think and organise your thought, they are not the title of the sub-sections).
Specifications for final assignment:  The final assignment or term paper should be typed and double-spaced at 12- point font. The word limit is 2500, not counting graphs, tables and references. If you have any, please put graphs and tables at the end of the document after the References section. Use APA (American Psychological Association: An author, date style system) referencing style for in-text citation and to develop the reference list. You can find more information on APA referencing style at this link: https://guides.library.uwa.edu.au/apa
Submission procedure and deadline: Your assignment should be submitted electronically via the Turnitin link provided in LMS with a signed assignment coversheet. Submit your assignment as a word or pdf file. The file should be named following the convention: 
YourLastName_FirstName_StudentNumber_ECON5001_FinalAssignment_2020.docx. 
The deadline for submission is 5:00 pm on Friday 23 October 2020. Failure to submit the essay in time will incur late penalties as per faculty/university policies. 
Assignment grading criteria:
The grading/evaluation criteria for the outline and the final version of the assignment (term paper) will be provided to students as a template at the end of this unit outline (Tables 5 and 6). These criteria are around the quality of the work, including originality, relevance, synthesis of the arguments, information efficiency and presentation.

Monday (lecture session): from 3-4pm delivered online via LMS [Wks 33-39, 41-43]
Tuesday (Tutorial/lab session): from 3-5pm delivered online via LMS [Wks 33-39, 41-43]
The unit material is divided into three modules: basic concepts and tools; the economy and three capitals, and financial and social capitals.First module aims to provide basic concepts and tools required to examine economic development critically and objectively. The modules 2 and 3 focuses on the economy and five forms of capitals that include technical, human, natural, financial and social. The weekly recorded lectures and tutorial/labs and assigned readings will help students to examine the issue of economic development or under-development from these five capital perspectives.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.
