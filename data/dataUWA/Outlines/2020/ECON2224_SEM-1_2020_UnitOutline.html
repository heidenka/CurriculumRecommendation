Academic Information

Content The world currently faces numerous natural resource management problems including air pollution (e.g. climate change), water quality, over exploitation of fish, other fauna and flora, as well as controversies surrounding resource intensive economic activities such as mining and logging. This has resulted in a shift in attitudes to environmental and natural resource management policies. The ability to articulate the causes and identify regulatory and incentive-based solutions to problems is a highly sought-after skill in public and private employment. After taking the unit, students are able to think about environment problems and their solutions in logical and systematic ways.

Unit Information

This is an intermediate level environmental economics unit organised broadly into four modules: basic economic concepts, natural resource economics, environmental economics, and economic valuation. 
•	In the first module, we review the link between economy and environmental/natural resources, concept of economic welfare, market failure, discounting and benefit-cost analysis. The second (natural resource economics) and third (environmental economics) modules focus on application of basic economic concepts into natural resources and environmental management, respectively. 
•	In the natural resource economics module, the focus will be on optimal management of non-renewable and renewable resources and related policies. Mining, fisheries, forestry, ecosystem services and biodiversity will be used as core examples of natural resource management. 
•	The environmental economics module focuses on economics of addressing pollution and externality problems, and relevant policies. Water pollution, air pollution and climate change will be used as core examples of environmental management. 
•	The economic valuation module focuses on the underlying concepts of valuing goods and services that do not have market price. The focus will be on introducing the concept and application of both revealed (hedonic pricing and travel cost) and stated (contingent valuation and choice experiments) non-market valuation techniques. 
•	In modules two, three and four, we will discuss government efforts aimed at regulating or influencing private resource managers through legislation or use of negative and positive incentives.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

•	The unit consists of two one-hour lectures per week delivered online, except first lecture and guest lectures. These lectures will be delivered in short videos and uploaded in LMS to provide key economic concepts to guide your reading and weekly tasks. Obviously, you are required to consult text books and suggested readings to develop deeper understanding of these concepts and their applications. If you need to consult instructor to get clarity on unit contents beyond tutorial or lab sessions, please plan to meet the instructors during consultation hours (see consultation time of each instructor).

Unit Structure

There will be a tutorial session and a computer lab every alternate week. For example, you will have tutorial sessions in weeks 9 and 11; and computer labs in weeks 10 and 12 (see unit schedule for detail). You will attend only one tutorial and lab session per week depending on your enrolment in tutorial and lab sessions. 
•	In the tutorial session, you will read an assigned paper or examine a set of problems in a small group (to be advised during first session) to understand underlying economic concepts and policy approaches discussed in the paper or examined in the assigned problem set. Instructor or tutor will facilitate the tutorial session and clarify any questions you may have on tutorial materials. But it is your opportunity to examine the materials, discuss with your peers, and clarify any questions with tutor/instructor during such sessions.
•	There will be a pre-tute task for you to complete on a bi-weekly basis, starting from tutorial session 2. You will complete the task online in-advance [i.e., prior to the tute session), which will be marked.
•	In the computer labs, you will be practicing problem-solving questions relevant to the topics covered in the weekly lectures/tutorial sessions. Instructor or tutor will provide you the guidance on how to solve the problems, but it is your opportunity to learn and apply what you have learned in the class. You will work in a group of two in solving the problems, so there will be peer-to-peer sharing as well. You may choose a partner to work with you in the lab sessions during the first lab. 
•	The works you do in tutorial sessions and computer labs are linked to the assignments you do in this unit. These assignments are individually based, given the feedback from past years. This means you work in a group or with another student in computer lab, but when it comes to working in assignments, you work and submit individually (for details on assignments see unit schedule and assessment mechanism).

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
