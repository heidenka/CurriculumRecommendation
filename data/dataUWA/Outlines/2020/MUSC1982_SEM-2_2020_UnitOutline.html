Building upon the exploration of self-reflection upon musical experience in MUSC1981 Music Ensemble 1, the primary focus of this unit is the exploration of expressiveness through musical performance.
By participating in a music ensemble, students develop instrumental/vocal skills to levels of assured and convincing public ensemble performance.  Such participation results in one or more public performance/s toward the end of semester (either within the University or the wider community).  Music ensembles may be subject to audition (e.g. Symphony Orchestra, Wind Orchestra, Con-Cantorum, Winthrop Singers), or may be the same ensemble previously attended in MUSC1981*, or be new to the student.  
A series of short videos focus upon the development of skills and approaches that can be applied to examining and interpreting expressiveness through musical performance, and students apply these to a portfolio of written work and video presentation. 
This unit is an elective unit in the Bachelor of Arts degree. It is also open to students enrolled in other degree pathways who fulfil the prerequisites and who have an interest in music.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

The unit comprises a blend of directed learning (i.e. ensemble rehearsals and performance), and self-directed and self-paced learning (i.e. personal practice of musical part, watching of videos, development of Portfolio components). 

The Participation and Performance components are devised and assessed by each ensemble's Director/Conductor, and expectations and requirements will be outlined in the first rehearsal.

The Portfolio components have been devised to be completed at any time, and allow for self-paced learning and balancing workload with deliverables for other units.  They do not necessarily need to be linked to ensemble rehearsal and performance experience/s within this semester.

Unit Structure

The unit comprises two main components: 
1. Ensemble Participation and Performance
Frequency, quantity, and duration of rehearsals and performances differ greatly between ensembles each semester, according to each project.  As a guide, you should expect approximately 30-40 contact hours (typically 10-12 x weekly rehearsals of 2-3 hours duration), and concluding with one or more public performances (typically during weeks #10-12).  The amount of personal preparation required will depend upon your proficiency with your instrument/voice, repertoire, style, etc., but as a very broad guide will usually require about one hour per week.  As the public performance approaches, some ensembles will typically arrange additional rehearsal (e.g. sectional rehearsals, a dress rehearsal/final venue run-through).
2. Portfolio
2.1. Proposal:  200 words.  The Proposal requires you to choose three or four works for analysis, a methodology, and reasoning for these choices.  As a guide, you should expect to undertake your reading, listening, and watching early in semester to allow you to develop this proposal (e.g in weeks 1-3 gather resources, in week 4 develop the proposal, and in week 5  finalise and submit - which allows a one week fudge factor).  Submit by the end of week 4.
2.2. Essay:  1,500-1,800 words. The essay presents your cogent argument supporting your choice of musical performances, analysis choice/s, reference materials, and summary or conclusion.  As a guide, you would develop this concurrently with your Video Presentation.  Submit by the end of week 11.
2.3. Video Presentation:  5-minutes.  The video incorporates your summary PowerPoint slides (or similar), with you presenting to camera as if being recorded while delivering a 5-minute seminar presentation.  As a guide, you should develop some basic video recording and editing skills if you do not already have these (recommended during weeks 1-4), start developing your slides concurrently as you develop your proposal (recommended during weeks 1-4), commence applying your analysis (recommended during weeks 3-7), develop your presentation script (recommended during weeks 6-8), record and edit your script for submission (recommended during weeks 8-10).  Submit by the end of week 12.

Participation

Ensemble rehearsal attendance
Ensemble work relies upon everyone participating fully to the best of their ability. 100% attendance is required for all rehearsals unless a student has been granted approval for any excused absence by the Unit Coordinator, or special consideration in writing by their Faculty Student Office. Each unexcused rehearsal absence (i.e. no approved special consideration) will result in a penalty of 10% reduction in the attendance/participation component. Students with more than two unexcused absences will be deemed to have unsatisfactory attendance/participation and this will result in the award of a mark of 0 for the unit.
Ensemble attendance and participation assessment is based upon the following elements: attendance; meeting the expectations of the ensemble director (as provided to each ensemble at the first rehearsal of the semester by the ensemble director); rehearsal preparation; individual contribution to the ensemble; responsiveness to direction; and interaction with other members of the group.
In 2020 only: Those who are located offshore will have special online participation requirements - please clarify these with your ensemble's director.

Referencing Requirements

Some students enrolled in this unit may not yet have been required to apply academic referencing.  In short, academic referencing requires you to apply a consistent referencing style (or citation style) - a set of rules about type faces, sizes, indenting, referencing, etc.  It creates better readability, and promotes your written work as being serious.  It IS a big deal.  
For this unit, "Chicago 17" citation style is preferred, but "APA" citation style is acceptable. Other citation styles may be used (e.g. "Harvard", among others), but only if this is discussed with the unit coordinator before commencing the essay.
If you don't understand referencing, or are unsure, please either check with the key academic/research staff within your home faculty and discipline, or seek assistance from a librarian.  Those experts are always willing to help.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
