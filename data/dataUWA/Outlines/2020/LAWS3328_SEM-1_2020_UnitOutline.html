Content This unit is an introduction to the legal framework for international trade and explains the relevant international and domestic laws which affect that activity. Emphasis is placed on the treaties and conventions which are crucial to international commercial transactions. The four major areas of study are international sale of goods; settlement of commercial disputes; carriage of goods (by sea and air); and payment in international trade. Students gain a better appreciation of the legal complexities associated with international transactions. Students who intend to work in any area involving international trade or commerce would find this unit very useful and relevant.

Unit Information

This unit is an introduction to the legal framework for international trade and explains the relevant international and domestic laws which affect that activity. Emphasis is placed on the treaties and conventions which are crucial to international commercial transactions. The five major areas of study are international sale of goods; settlement of commercial disputes; carriage of goods (by sea and air); payment in international trade; and marine insurance. Students gain a better appreciation of the legal complexities associated with international transactions. Students who intend to work in any area involving international trade or commerce would find this unit very useful and relevant
Upon successful students will have a basic knowledge of (1) the issues that are raised when two parties from different countries enter into an international transaction for the sale of goods; (2) the laws that are applicable in relation to the contract for the sale of goods between two parties from different countries; (3) the laws that can ensure a proper resolution of disputes between parties from different countries; (4) the different methods of resolving a dispute; (5) the rules relating to transportation of goods by sea and air; and (6) the rules relating to insurance of goods being transported. Students also have skills in (1) identifying the issues that arise when entering into a transaction with a foreign party; (2) understanding and using the relevant Conventions and laws in resolving disputes that can arise between buyer and seller, shipper and carrier, insured and insurance company in an international transaction; (3) legal method and writing
(assignment on the use of International Conventions and other legal sources); and (4) oral communication (participation in tutorials on fact-based questions).

Learning And Teaching Strategy

This unit will be taught through a combination of formal lectures and weekly small group meetings (‘tutorials’). The formal lectures will outline the key legal principles applicable to each of the listed lecture topics and will also indicate the sources of law from which those principles are derived. Students should supplement the lectures by reading the prescribed chapters of the textbook, and any written notes that are provided by the lecturers on LMS. In tutorials students will spend time discussing a number of hypothetical legal problem questions. The purpose of tutorials is to enable students to revise and contextualise the materials they have been learning and, at the same time, to help them develop the ability to construct principled arguments as to how those materials can be applied to the facts of a given legal problem question. The problem questions that students learn how to analyse and answer in tutorials will be very similar to the problem questions that appear in the Final Exam.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.
