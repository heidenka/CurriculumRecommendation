Unit Information

This unit is a core unit for students commencing the Master of Hydrogeology and a conversion unit for students commencing the Master of Environmental Science. 
This unit provides exposure to key concepts in hydrology, covering topics related to water movement and redistribution in the landscape, and catchment management. This includes a specific focus first on learning core concepts in the field of hydrology to develop a process understanding of controls on the catchment water balance, with exposure to a range of application-specific topics relevant to water management. A focus is given to understanding and quantitative descriptions of the surface and subsurface components of the hydrological cycle allowing students to appreciate the fundamentals of the disciplines of both surface hydrology and hydrogeology. Students gain an understanding of a range of biophysical processes through exposition of fundamental theories and process descriptions, combined with analysis and interpretation of observed data. This unit also introduces fundamental mathematical concepts and methods including elementary algebra, probability and statistics, vectors and matrices, and calculus as tools to understand and describe environmental systems. These concepts are presented in the context of environmental dynamics case studies, allowing students to be able to develop simple environmental models.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

The unit is structured as a series of sequential modules, with one running each week. The first 5 modules set the foundation by providing the fundamental underpinnings for understanding the water cycle, with accompanying quiz and practical report assessments. Once students have mastered these core concepts, the remaining 4 modules look at more environmental modelling and water management topics to develop the students critical thinking and problem solving ability. 
Each module is made up of essential theoretical content and example practical exercises to help students master concepts and develop awareness and experience with common techniques used by environmental consultants and catchment managers. In addition to lecture content, assignment-based activities, computer-based data analyses, field and laboratory work exposes students to practical applications and approaches to making hydrological measurements and assessing water-resource related problems.  
This unit utilizes a flipped classroom approach, where learning materials are provided to students so that they can engage with the material on their own time. Time spent in class is then utilized for participatory learning activities. In general, students are expected to under-take self-directed preparation (e.g. online video and/or reading material) each week, prior to attending the Monday/Tuesday workshop session. Within this workshop session, we undertake a hybrid between a traditional lecture and a hands-on tutorial / activity session. Students must attend these each week to be able develop the required level of skill in assessing concepts and relevant environmental data. As skills and knowledge are built upon from module to module, it is essential that students keep up to date with the content and assessments each week. Most weekly modules also include a practical activity in the allocated Wed session, which is either a hands-on activity, a field visit, or a data-based activity.

Participation

Students are required to attend every workshop and actively participate in discussions and learning activities. Students are expected to engage with unit content outside of class-time so that they arrive at each workshop with an adequate understanding of the topic content for that week. Student understanding will be assessed through quizzes which will be completed during or soon after the workshops. 
The workshops vary from week to week and include laboratory exercises, field work and desktop based tutorials. 
Workshop class time is also allocated for development of the individual and group project assessments that carry a significant fraction of the course mark. Absence from workshop classes, and failure to submit practical exercises for assessment without good reason are likely to result in failure of the unit.

PLAGIARISM The Faculty of Sciences has a Policy on Plagiarism which is available from the Faculty and School Offices. It is expected that work submitted for assessment by a University student is the studentsâ€™ own work, and that any contribution from others (ideas, facts or direct quotations) is given suitable acknowledgement by referring to the source (in a list of references, an acknowledgements section or in some other appropriate way). If such acknowledgement is not given, the writer is guilty of plagiarism. Plagiarism is the use of published work (book, articles, reports) or the work of other students (present or past, at this or any other University), as though it were one`s own. In some cultures, it is regarded as a sign of respect to describe events or ideas in exactly the same words as an acknowledged expert. In the academic tradition to which this University belongs, it is not; it is thought of as mere copying. Special care is necessary in the case of group work. Where a single report is required from a group of several students, it will be assumed that all students have contributed. Students not happy with the contribution of other group members should make appropriate representations to the appropriate staff member. Where each student is required to hand in an individual report based on group research, work of another individual student (eg. diagrams, photographs) should be acknowledged. Unfortunately, students have been penalised by many Schools within the University, including this one, for plagiarism. If in any doubt, discuss the matter with your tutor or the unit coordinators. You may also access the plagiarism portal at http://www.studentservices.uwa.edu.au/ss/learning/studying_smarter/jump_start/referencing. Please note that plagiarism detection software may be used to check assessments.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
