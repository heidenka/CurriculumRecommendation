Academic Information

Content This unit introduces students to the anthropology of Australian Aboriginal visual art, focusing on various anthropological approaches to understanding the productions of individual artists within their respective sociocultural contexts. Frameworks derived from the sub-disciplines of visual anthropology, cultural anthropology, and the anthropology of art provide analytical structure for approaching an understanding of Australian Aboriginal art within the broader Australian postcolonial setting. Topics addressed may include art and its relationship to place; cultural creativity and the maintenance of religious knowledge; colour and aesthetics; contemporary art; regional perspectives and stylistic values; appropriation, copyright and intellectual property.

Unit Information

This unit introduces students to the anthropology of Australian Aboriginal visual art, focusing on various anthropological approaches to understanding the productions of individual artists within their respective sociocultural contexts. Frameworks derived from the sub-disciplines of visual anthropology, cultural anthropology, and the anthropology of art provide analytical structure for approaching an understanding of Australian Aboriginal art within the broader Australian postcolonial setting. Topics addressed may include art and its relationship to place; cultural creativity and the maintenance of religious knowledge; colour and aesthetics; contemporary art; regional perspectives and stylistic values; appropriation, copyright and intellectual property.
Students are able to:
(1) demonstrate an understanding of key concepts within the
anthropology/sociology of art and Aboriginal art in particular; 
(2) demonstrate understanding of analytical approaches to an anthropology of art; 
(3) demonstrate an ability to understand the role of art in the transmission and maintenance of cultural knowledge and values and the emergence of new ideas, and apply this insight to the role of art as a marker of cultural process; 
(4) demonstrate an ability to understand the nature and characteristics of regional perspectives and stylistic values and apply this insight to the categorisation of styles in Aboriginal art; and 
(5) demonstrate an ability to communicate anthropological and sociological ideas, principles and knowledge to specialist and non-specialist audiences using a range of formats (written, oral, visual etc.), and to understand issues of appropriation, copyright and intellectual property as these pertain to Australian Aboriginal art.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

Due to COVID-19, this unit is being taught in a ‘flipped’ online unit format. Every week, you will be able to access a ‘lecture’ in the form of a combination of written and recorded modules. This, with required readings and prescribed online viewing (documentaries and so on) comprise the online learning materials for this unit. These will be released on a weekly basis.
Every week, you will be required to participate in a tutorial.* The assumption of that tutorial is that you have viewed the lecture materials and read the required readings.
The emphasis in this unit is on learning through a deep engagement with the learning materials, and 40% of your marks are related to your weekly preparation and participation (see assessments below). Given this format students are required to contribute a small amount of written work to the discussion board on a weekly basis that engages with the unit readings. 
*In Crawley, tutorials are online. In Albany,  they are face to face; the Albany tutor is Yann Toussaint.

Unit Structure

This unit is being taught in a flipped format. That means that lectures will be pre-recorded and available online through the LMS. You are able to watch these in your own time but the expectation is that you watch them before the weekly online tutorial/workshop. Many if not most of these lectures will be broken down into a series of smaller connected lectures.  You are required to watch and/or listen to these and associated materials prior to attending the the online tutorial/workshop. There are 12 lectures and 11 scheduled tutorial/workshops over 12 weeks. We have one virtual visit planned to the Berndt Museum of Anthropology.

Participation

Participation is considered a vital component of this course, and 40% of your assessment is allocated to your contribution. For Crawley students, these tutorials/workshops are online; for Albany students, they are face to face. 
There are a potential 4 marks per week over 10 assessable weeks (excluding the first introductory week, when there is no tutorial, and the last review week). 
What is required:
You are required to come to class (whether online in Crawley or face to face in Albany) having done the required reading and having viewed the lecture and associated materials. 
For each week there is a set of questions related to the readings that you are asked to discuss in class. These questions appear in the supplementary unit outline.
All students are also required to make at least one posting to the discussion board per week. This might be an original contribution in the form of a) considered responses to the questions associated with the week’s reading (and/or audio-visual materials); or b) a considered reflection on the week’s reading (and/or audio-visual materials), or c) a considered response to someone else’s posting. Postings to the discussion board need not be long and indeed, should not be too long (a paragraph or so): the essential thing is to demonstrate that you have engaged with that week's readings (and viewing, where relevant). Please note that assessment of your contribution to the discussion board will be made on this basis (i.e. on the quality of engagement, not on the length of your posting or on the number of postings made). What you post to the Discussion Board can also form the basis of your contributions to the tutorials/workshops. 
You are required to submit your postings to the discussion board within three days of each tutorial, after which the discussion board for that week will be locked.

Referencing Requirements

The referencing system you should use is APA referencing. There is a link to how to use this referencing system here: https://guides.library.uwa.edu.au/apa
Make sure you reference to page number as well as to author and year, not only when directly quoting, but when you are paraphrasing as well. Remember that the point of referencing is to be able to go back and check someone’s sources. A page number will precisely identify where the specific ideas drawn on can be traced back to.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
