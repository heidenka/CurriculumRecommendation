Unit Information

This unit explores how we write about art by looking at alternative and experimental approaches to the discipline. We will interrogate what this contested field of ‘art writing’ is and how it relates to art history; what it means to write about art; and, what different art writing practices might look like. The practice and study of art writing involves creative, philosophical, critical and theoretical approaches to writing about, writing with, and writing as art. Drawing from disciplines including visual art, art criticism, art history and literature, this interdisciplinary field is as much about creative production as it is concerned with critical discourse.
Each week students will reexamine or rework drafts of their work according to different art writing methods with the aim of finding their own voices as writers. From Chris Krauss to Maggie Nelson, we will close read a mix of contemporary art writing in order to consider how to put the visual form into words; and, we will grapple with the tension between writing creatively and forming a coherent argument.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

Students are expected to come prepared to discuss the set readings each week. Additionally, each student will be required to select a week to deliver a presentation related to the readings. These presentations should last at least 30 minutes and will be followed by a discussion of the material. 
For four out of our six weeks you will also be asked to complete a short writing assignment (approx. 500-700 words) that relates to the major text for that week. These assignments are intended to be experimental and you are encouraged to think creatively. They will be submitted via the weekly discussion forum on Blackboard by 11.59pm on the Sunday prior to our Monday seminar. 
Your final paper (5,000 words) will be an extended analysis of the text that you chose for your class presentation. This final essay will be submitted via LMS. 

Unit Structure

Please refer to Class Allocation System (CAS) http://www.cas.uwa.edu.au/ (http://www.cas.uwa.edu.au/) for the unit’s allocated day, time and venue.
This unit compromises 6 x 3-hour in-class seminars, which occur every two weeks.  
Each of these 3-hour weekly workshop will focus on a set reading and include in-depth group conversation and class presentations by students. In addition, students complete a series of assigned weekly writing tasks over the four middle weeks of the semester, as well as a final essay at the end of semester. The first of the 3-hour seminars will contain an introduction and discussion of assessments and how students will use LMS to submit their weekly writing tasks.

Interdisciplinary writing and scholarship

The past fifteen years has seen an explosion in interdisciplinary and cross-disciplinary writing practices that established relations between a variety of disciplines, from creative and critical writing and literature, through art practice and art history to feminist theory, queer theory and performative studies. We will look in particular at how Maggie Nelson writes about the works of artists such as Chris Burden, Gina Pane, Andy Warhol and Ana Mendieta by contextualising and yoking them to works of literature, theatre, poetry, theory and media studies under the same ordering theme of ‘violence’. 
KEY TEXT
Maggie Nelson, The Art of Cruelty: A Reckoning
ASSIGNMENT
Weekly writing: Consider how the interdisciplinary writing of Maggie Nelson creatively brings together multiple critical and theoretical approaches to art writing. Think about how this effects the way that Nelson writes through the various art objects in The Art of Cruelty and in approximately 500-700 words, rework a portion of your current project with a similar methodology.

31/08/2020
Autocriticism / fictocriticisms / autofictions

Autocriticism or fictocriticism or autofiction is often described as writing at the ‘very edge of fiction’, where the difference between art and life is deliberately blurred and where the subjectivity of the author is placed at the centre of the work. Understandably there is a broad spectrum of works within this category and each of the labels are contested. This week we will look at how and why an author might deploy strategies of autobiography to write about art.
KEY TEXT
Maggie Nelson, The Argonauts 

ASSIGNMENT
Weekly writing: Consider your own subjectivity as a writer to experiment with describing an artwork, collecting evidence, and/or creating a narrative. This week think about your own biography and how you might borrow from it to adapt a portion of your current project (approx. 500-700 words). This project can be highly experimental.
4 
14/09/2020
This week we will look at how and why an author might deploy strategies of fiction to talk about art or use art to populate their worlds of fiction. The Flamethrowers is filled with stories, anecdotes and set-pieces that lean into art writing but simultaneously deny it. How might we as art writers borrow from other forms of writing such as fiction (as well as poetry and short stories) to experiment with style and form?
KEY TEXT: 
Rachel Kushner, The Flamethrowers 
ASSIGNMENT
Weekly writing: Consider how other forms of writing use visual art as a way to experiment with narrative, style and concept. Using a text from this week creatively adapt a portion of your current project (approx. 500-700 words) to showcase a different formal approach to your writing. This project can be highly experimental.

Contemporary pop criticism 

What does contemporary popular art criticism look like? What is its function in the contemporary art eco system and the population more broadly? This week will ponder these things while also thinking about the effect of the internet on art writing and publishing. If art history is long form, peer reviewed research than the kind of criticism we are looking at this week is journalistic, regular, responsive and accumulative. It’s about developing a voice and opinion, and culture around that.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
