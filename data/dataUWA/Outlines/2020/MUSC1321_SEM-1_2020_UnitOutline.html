Academic Information

Content This unit is the first in a four-semester sequence that develops core theoretical, musical and perceptual skills. The unit comprises two mutually supportive components—(1) harmony (2) aural. These components comprise an integrated whole in which strong interconnections are defined in both content and assessment. The broader aim of this four-semester program is to support the acquisition of musical fluency and the capacity for independent learning. This unit lays the foundations of students' understanding of musical grammar, both on paper and in their creative imagination. On completion of the four-semester Music Language program, the course aims to produce musically literate students who are able to apply the skills they have acquired in any professional situation. This unit is a complementary unit in the Music Studies major (degree-specific major) in the Bachelor of Arts degree. It is also open to students enrolled in other majors and degree pathways who fulfil the prerequisites and who have an interest in music.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

Teaching and learning strategies are practical and interactive, and will include: the use of instruments and the singing voice; the use of keyboards; the use of conducting gestures, clapping, and Kodaly hand-signs; listening analytically to audio and video examples; study of notated examples; the writing of notated assignments both in class and between sessions; and verbal explanations of musical concepts.

Unit Structure

UNIT COMPONENTS:
COMPONENT A: HARMONY (50%)
This component aims to develop an understanding of the basic tenets of tonal harmony, from the fundamentals of pitch and rhythm to analytical techniques which reveal the hierarchical nature of tonal music and inform interpretation and performance.
 Learning Outcomes:
Upon completion of the Harmony component, students should be able to:
-demonstrate a thorough understanding of pitch/rhythm notation conventions and scale degree names
-identify and/or construct any major or minor key signature
-identify and construct any major or minor scale, interval, triad and seventh chord in any inversion
-demonstrate an understanding of the principles of melodic writing through two voice counterpoint
-begin to develop fluency in realising a figured bass
-identify and construct chords in various musical textures
-begin to develop an understanding of tonal hierarchy including the roles of tonic, pre-dominant and dominant, embellishing tones, cadences, and the phrase model
-demonstrate an understanding of the principles of melodic and harmonic reduction through the creation of a basic melodic reduction and second level analysis
COMPONENT B: Aural & Keyboard (50%)
This component sets out to develop the skills in aural discrimination, recognition and musical memory essential to University-level study in music, and to complement these in support of musicological study and harmonic understanding through the development of keyboard skills.  All students take two sessions each week in, respectively, ‘Aural Lecture’; and ‘Aural & Keyboard Tutorial’.  The Lecture is taught weekly to the whole year group; Aural & Keyboard Tutorials are delivered to the cohort divided into three classes.
The ‘Aural Lecture’ develops the capacity for self-directed practice and learning through peer interaction.  Work is essentially practical, creative, and focused on acquiring experience of musical language through active engagement and leadership opportunities.
‘Aural & Keyboard Tutorials’ develop the traditional aural skills associated with being able to analyse rhythmic, melodic and harmonic structures, and transcribe what is heard into music notation; and develop complementary keyboard skills that support University-level independent study in Music. 
COURSE CONTENT Over the semester, the following topics will be explained and experienced, and will form the basis of Semester 1 assessment.
In Aural:
• Scales, their component degrees and the intervals between them (major; minor, both harmonic and melodic) and the degrees of consonance and dissonance these present; • Chords taken from the major and minor scales, their arpeggios, and all inversions and voicings in both open and close position; • Modes (Dorian, Aeolian, Ionian, Lydian); • Rhythms in 4/4, 3/4, 2/4 and 6/8 time, and involving both simple and ‘tuplet’ divisions of beats, syncopations and accentuation, durations and rests; • Voice leading: parallel motion; contrary motion; suspension; accented and unaccented passing-notes; • Melodic analysis and notation; • Analysis and notation of music in two parts, and of melody over a drone; • Analysis and notation of harmonic progressions in the major: chords Ia, IVa, Va, VIa; Ib and Ic; V7a and V7b.
Classes will involve singing, in order to develop tuning, discrimination and reading skills as well as knowledge of the repertoire (e.g. the Chorales of Bach). It is vital that students focus in their practice on developing fluent, confident and well-tuned singing, so that this skill can support other aspects of their studies, and in preparation for the Oral assessment, which will be conducted as a performance examination. Students should also factor regular and systematic aural practice into their regular instrumental and vocal practice routines.
In Keyboard
• Scales and arpeggios, both major and minor • Standard chord progressions, including cadential formulae • The fundamentals of reading figured bass • The basis of transposition, and reading in unfamiliar clefs • The use of the keyboard to study orchestral and choral scores. Students are also encouraged to bring instruments to some sessions in order for the class to transcribe from a range of timbres and styles in performance and/or interact with keyboard performance.
ASSESSMENT
As this is a skills based unit, consistent attendance is essential. Marks will be given based on attendance and participation at lectures and tutorials, for which a register will be maintained.
Harmony (50% of Unit total):
1) Assignments - 5%
Just as making progress on your instrument requires consistent practise, so does developing fluency in your musical analysis. To this end, weekly work will be assigned, and solutions discussed as a class in the tutorials. Assignments will be checked for completion at the start of each class - no credit will be given for a partially completed assignment. Students are expected to come prepared with completed assignments and make corrections in class based upon the group discussion. This collection of corrected assignments will form a useful study aid at the end of semester. Students with an unsatisfactory record of assignment submission may not be permitted to sit for the final examination.
2) Mid-semester written test - 15%
The mid-semester written test will be conducted during the normal lecture time in Week 6. Students are responsible for all content covered from Weeks 1-5. 
3) Final Exam - 25%
The final exam will be scheduled during the exam period at the end of semester. Students are responsible for all content covered from Weeks 1-12. 
4) Attendance/Participation 5%:
As this is a skills based unit, consistent attendance is essential. Marks will be given based on attendance and participation at lectures and tutorials, for which a register will be maintained.

Participation

Both Harmony and Aural are taught in a manner that requires practical responses and musical interaction.  Physical presence in the classroom is essential. Students are partly assessed on the quality of their participation in class.
It is anticipated that students will be able to make links between what is studied in Harmony and Aural in this Unit and the musical experiences available to them in other aspects of their study at UWA.

Referencing Requirements

Very little work requiring referencing is required in this Unit, in which the foremost medium of written exchange is music notation. However, where reference is made to existing material (either scores or books, chapters or articles) this should be carried out in a familiar format such as Chicago or APA.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
