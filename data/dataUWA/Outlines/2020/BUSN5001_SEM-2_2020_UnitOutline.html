Academic Information

Content Blockchain and distributed ledger technologies (BDLT) are fundamentally changing traditional industries, business structures and consumer behaviours. While the media often focus on Bitcoin and other crypto currencies, valued at USD 825 billion in early 2018, these are only the tip of the iceberg in the context of BDLT's full potential. While the technology is still evolving, its transformative impact on businesses and societies is often misunderstood, feared or altogether unknown. UWA's Blockchain and Distributive Ledger Technologies in Business is one of the few academic courses in the world offering an introduction to the BDLT and a hands-on application of this knowledge to developing and managing innovative enterprises in the new ecosystem.

Unit Information

This interdisciplinary online unit introduces students to blockchain technologies and their business applications. Blockchain has been recognised as one of the greatest technological, economic and social revolutions since the invention of the internet. Blockchain technologies are strategically adopted by leading global corporations, major economies, governments, and more importantly innovative startups in such areas like the latest hyped DeFi (Decentralised Finance) movement. Blockchain skills are the most sought after by companies in 2020 according to LinkedIn. All of this has started with bitcoin: an open public protocol, world's most secure and fastest decentralised computer network and a new form of internet money. Bitcoin's innovation has changed how we understand money, value, governance, truth and how we relate to each other. The innovation inspired further exploration of blockchain and distributed ledger technologies (B&DLT) that have had significant impact on diverse kinds of innovative enterprises. Students will learn how to appreciate the opportunities and be aware of the challenges of B&DLT in a business context and beyond. The theoretical knowledge will be then applied to creating practical B&DLT team projects. No technical and programming skills are required. 
The first step involves learning why this revolutionary technology came to be and how it works. While understanding the fundamentals behind technologies like Bitcoin and Ethereum is the departing point, this unit goes beyond cryptocurrencies and the speculative hype around them. Students will learn how to harness the disruptive potential of the new technology and apply it strategically in business and in their own career path development. Students will also take advantage of understanding the disruptive innovation of B&DLT in the broader interdisciplinary context of regulatory, political, economic and social frameworks to fully capture the new ecosystem and to critically assess the opportunities it offers.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

This is a hands-on unit with interactive seminars and team-based projects that conceptualise the use of B&DLT for all kinds of enterprises (for and not-for-profit). No technical skills and coding are required nor are used in this unit. This unit is taught online. Please see LMS for further details on the online teaching mode delivered via Zoom. 
This unit allows students to meet the required 150 hours of learning time needed for a 6-credit UWA unit. Self-study time and work on a team project outside classes are essential for successful completion of the unit. Traditional readings are accompanied by interactive multimedia (movies, podcasts, weblog, social media) as well as short video-lectures and video interviews with professionals from the B&DLT industry.
Students will be required to engage with some of the learning material and complete their introductory profile/video before classes start. More detailed content and instructions are to be available on the LMS.

Unit Structure

The unit is structured around the following modules:
* Technological foundations: Understanding Bitcoin and Ethereum and the differences between open public decentralised blockchains and distributed ledger technologies. 
* Strategic application of B&DLT at the enterprise level: diverse use cases
* Macro economic, political, ethical, cultural and societal implications of B&DLT - exploring the new world order.
Students will be encouraged to form teams in the first seminars and start working on their project systematically as we progress through the content of different unit modules.

Participation

Participation in class, whether it is listening to a lecture or getting involved in other activities, is an important part of the learning process, therefore it is important that you attend classes. More formally, the University regulations state that ‘to complete a course or unit students shall attend prescribed classes, lectures, seminars and tutorials’. 
Regular and timely participation of all students in team work for the completion of the team assignment is vital. Students are encouraged to keep any evidence (e.g. emails, communication on social media, submitted contributions) of their regular participation and contribution to their team project throughout the semester.  The team project is a self-managed activity, i.e. the teaching staff does not intervene or assist in the team formation and team work. However, any sustained absence in participation or a misconduct of any team members should be timely reported to the unit coordinator by any of the other team participants. 
Where a student, due to exceptional circumstances, is unable to attend a scheduled class or timely complete an assessment, they are required to apply for special consideration at the student office. Omissions of this rule will negatively affect student's marks. 

Referencing Requirements

Referencing and formatting It is important that the referencing of any sources used in your written work is done properly, if only to substantiate the points you are making in your assignment or project. The Harvard style is the preferred used unless otherwise stated by the unit coordinator. There are some notes for guidance which have been prepared by the library staff: ‘Citing your Sources Harvard Style’ (http://guides.library.uwa.edu.au/harvard). The EndNote software package is a really good system for building up a database of references. Not everyone will want to invest the time in using this system but you should consider it if you intend to build up resource materials or plan to undertake extensive research in a particular area. The library staff have also developed a tutoring package: ‘A Quick Guide to Using EndNote’ which provides the basics for using EndNote with an essay http://guides.library.uwa.edu.au/endnote 
Please use standard font size 12 and 1.5 line spacing in your written assignments. Make sure text is evenly adjusted to right and left margins. +/- 5% of word count difference (excluding references) is accepted for major written assignments. Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
