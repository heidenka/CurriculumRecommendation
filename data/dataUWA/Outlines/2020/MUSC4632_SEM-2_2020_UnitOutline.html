Unit Details

MUSC4632 - Secondary Music Curriculum 2 - SEM-2-2020 - 2020 - Face to face / Blended learning

Unit Information

This unit comprises a detailed study of the practices and procedures for teaching class music at lower and upper secondary levels. The class work is supplemented by a practicum in a Western Australian secondary school, which allows for students to connect the theoretical frameworks and examples they have discussed in seminars to a practical situation.
MUSC4632 reflects the following APST standards as follows: 
3.3         Include a range of teaching strategies.
Teaching strategies include lectures, seminars led by the lecturer, seminars led by students, and responses to web-based material. These continue to develop musical leadership skills.
 
4.2         Demonstrate the capacity to organise learning activities and provide clear directions.
Students are required to present in class examples of activities on which teaching will be based; present and discuss planning strategies for a range of teaching contexts; build a portfolio of self-devised teaching materials; and share in class examples of how they have reflected on their experiences.
 
 
5.1         Demonstrate understanding of assessment strategies, including informal and formal, diagnostic, formative and summative approaches to assess student learning.
A project on assessment and its moderation forms part of the examined content of the unit. Assessment material and reflection upon it is required as part of the Teaching Portfolio.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

This unit is taught through modelling real-life pedagogical scenarios and actively engaging in musical teaching and learning as the basis for discussion, reflection and evaluation. 

Unit Structure

The unit builds on the knowledge gained throughout MUSC4631 and the first teaching practicum. It prepares students for their second practicum, normally occurring in the middle of the Semester. As in MUSC4631, MUSC4632 is taught through a sequence of seminars that deal with both the theory and practice of teaching, and provide the opportunity for further practical development. These seminars address the following areas of knowledge;
(1) curriculum studies in secondary music, with an introduction to the Year 11 and 12 ATAR Music courses; and
(2) arranging and composing for school instrumental ensembles. 
Students will reflect on how and why we teach Music at Secondary level. They will continue to develop core practical skills for music teachers and students in the classroom: including arranging and composing, conducting, rehearsal techniques, and ensemble management in the context of school-based music ensembles. 
The unit continues to construct a framework for understanding how the teaching of music responds to, and represents, a Curriculum. The Australian Curriculum (Music), and the Western Australian Curriculum (Music) are critically examined in the light of international research and practice, and taken as a reference point for developing Lesson Plans, Rubrics for Assessment, Programmes of Study and associated teaching materials and resources in preparation for teaching practice experience and subsequent application for employment. Attention is paid to the historical and philosophical issues involved in curriculum design for Music, so as to provide a basis for independent and critical thinking suitable for entrance to a career in which responsibility for Departmental development is often achieved at an early stage. For the composing/arranging task, students select a from the options provided and complete a performable work that can represent part of their portfolio for applying for teaching positions.

Elaboration of the Assessment Tasks (further information available on LMS)
    The Assessment Moderation Task (APST 1, 5, 7) builds on work commenced in class at UWA and involves tracking and recording assessment processes while on Prac.
    The Teaching Resource Folio (APST 1, 2, 3, 4, 5) will be made up of samples of your own work (plans, reflections, materials), and will include documentation of your contribution to sessions at UWA prior to Prac, including a Programme Plan and Assessment Rubrics presented in class at UWA.
    The composition or arrangement (APST 1, 2) needs to be written with school resources and capabilities in mind, and should be retained for possible inclusion in submissions for employment.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
