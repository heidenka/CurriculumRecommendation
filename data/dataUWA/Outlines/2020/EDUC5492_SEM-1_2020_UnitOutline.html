Unit Information

How do teachers and school psychologists diﬀerentiate between students who engage in antisocial behaviour because that is their goal and those who present with similar antisocial behaviour because they cannot help it? Should the management of these students be the same? This unit is an introduction to addressing this dilemma and to understanding the behavioural problems commonly encountered in special and mainstream school settings. Students will be involved in weekly practical DVD based exercises to become familiar with a range of general behaviour problems and their management in classrooms through to severe conduct problems often associated with ADHD, oppositionality  and conduct disorder.
Students will develop a theoretical and practical understanding of behavioural issues in childhood and adolescence, including a range of disorders commonly encountered by school psychologists, special educators and mainstream schoolteachers. Following an introduction to the general and frequently occurring behaviour problems (mild to severe) encountered in mainstream schools and how they are (and should be) managed by teachers, the constructs of risk and reputation will be examined. This will cover school-aged students who deliberately engage in antisocial behaviour and the motivations underpinning why they do so.
Following this, the focus switches to school-aged students who engage in antisocial behaviour, but the underlying mechanisms for which are seemingly diﬀerent. These students present with conditions such as ADHD, Oppositional Defiant Disorder, Conduct Disorder and Psychopathic traits. Throughout the unit the various risk factors for school-aged students will be examined. The two assignments are also incorporated into each weeks teaching throughout the ten weeks, with guided instruction from the coordinator. Numerous practical group and individual exercises - primarily based on DVD cases – are presented each week to encourage familiarisation with the constructs introduced.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

This unit utilises a blended teaching model with face-to-face lectures, group and independent problem solving exercises via DVD dramatisations and DVD based case studies. Students will be expected to participate in group activities during class times. Online readings will be provided. The unit coordinator will teach all sessions and be available from 9am until 2pm for the duration of the unit. All materials relating to the unit will be provided by the unit coordinator. It is expected that students will attend lectures from 4pm until 6pm and submit assignments by the designated date.

Case Study 1
Robert is a young boy diagnosed with a range of developmental psychopathologies “caused” by several factors. A number of persons (e.g., biological mother, grandmother, teachers, therapist etc.) discuss Robert’s situation and life history at diﬀerent times of his development.
(i)	What factors do you believe have the greatest impact on Robert’s behaviour at home and at school? What do you observe that support your opinions?
(ii)	What observations support Robert’s diagnosis of ADHD? Are there any observations that question the reliability of such a diagnosis?
(iii)	Highlight in dot point format the on-going management strategies you would recommend for Robert.
This should be no more than 1,500 words in total and should not include academic journal and/or book references. It is your observations and interpretations that are important
Marked out of 65
Case Study 2:
Vinnie is a high school adolescent. You will notice that several persons are involved in his daily schooling and functioning (e.g., Principal, Deputy Principal, mother, teachers, peers, and counsellors). Present a single page pictorial representation of
(i)	the major underlying motivating mechanism(s) for Vinnie’s behaviour. Include examples from your observations supporting these mechanisms
(ii)	In dot point format suggest the on-going management strategies you would recommend for Vinnie.
This should not include academic journal and/or book references. 

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
