Academic Information

Content This unit investigates the rapidly changing conceptions of “Matter” or material innovation in design, through digital fabrication and 1:1 prototyping. Within this context, students will engage and apply new digital or robotic fabrication methods and technologies, prototyping methods, and experimental material development, culminating in 1:1 prototypes or assemblages exhibiting bespoke properties. Students will research the relation between material properties and specific prototyping methods (CNC, 3D printing, robotic fabrication, etc.) and apply this research towards ‘learning by making' to design, develop and prototype functional or “responsive” material assemblies. Project development and outcomes will explore and apply contemporary topics relating to material craft, digital fabrication, and emerging technologies such as robotic fabrication and smart and responsive materials. The overall academic objective of the unit is to develop a critical rethinking of “Material,” towards a new approach to design and making, combining both analog and digital craft. Material innovation and prototyping topics and projects provide a critical and practical foundation for students to engage twenty-first century design practices, whereby the designer is involved in the development of new tools, processes, materials and prototypes.
Unit Learning Outcomes demonstrate an awareness of current material innovation in design practice |Report, Prototype|*|synthesise research findings to design and produce full scale functional prototypes and responsive material systems |Prototype, final assembly|*|demonstrate an advanced level of understanding of the iterative process of drawing and making |Prototype, final assembly|*|develop critical and practical skills to interrogate and advance the making of physical prototypes|Final assembly|*|

Learning And Teaching Strategy

This unit is taught in a "blended" workshop or lab format, with lectures, case studies and tutorials integrated with specific topics of investigation, material development, or hardware and software integration. Students are expected to work both independently and in small teams, to develop understanding and ability to utilize project specific materials, prototyping tools and processes, and associated software or hardware.
Flexibility, Self-Initiative and Motivation to pursue novel, experimental material development processes, incorporating varied tools, hardware and software, are essential  for a successful learning experience and project outcomes. The unit coordinator will introduce varied topics, processes and methods for the development and prototyping of bespoke material assemblies, ranging from 3-Axis CNC to Robotic Fabrication. 
Teaching Strategy Note No. 1
This semester we will minimize the need to incorporate electronic components (Arduino, Sensors, etc) in the development of project workflows. 
The topic will be introduced, however to provide the option to  incorporate these components in Digital Crafting workflows, for teams interested in this aspect of contemporary material-process or craft.
Teaching Strategy Note No. 2
The use of 6-Axis (UR5) Robot Arms for student project development, is contingent on the availability of these machines in the UWA School of Engineering. 
Material-Process workflows and associated lectures, workshops, etc. will be adjusted accordingly, to support project outcomes, based on limitations regarding the availability of the robot arms during the semester.
Teaching Strategy Note No. 3
Students are expected to understand and apply basic methods and principles introduced in lectures and tutorials, and develop additional, individual expertise (both digital and material-process) as part of the project development, extending and developing basic methods or instruction towards processes that are developed and refined as needed to prepare initial prototypes and final outcomes.
Learning objectives will require the ability to work both independently and in small groups, to accomplish the following project related outcomes:
•	Research of contemporary Digital + Robotic Crafting methods, examples and case studies, and associated software, hardware or material requirements.
•	 Digital + CNC or Robotic Crafting of Material, combining a variety of tools, software + hardware workflows, minimizing the necessity for manual crafting, emphasizing economy of material in the development process.
•	Integration and Refinement of Digital + Material Processes and initial prototypes, towards precise, well crafted final assemblies and outcomes.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
