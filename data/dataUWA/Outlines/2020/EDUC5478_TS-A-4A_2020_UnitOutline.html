Academic Information

Content This unit develops skills and understanding in school psychology so students are ready to work relatively independently in a variety of professional tasks. Students develop sensitivity to the needs and characteristics of children, adolescents, parents and colleagues, both as individuals and in groups. They also develop knowledge and skills relevant to school psychology, and develop an awareness of their professional attitudes. Students undertake self-appraisal of their professional performance. Topics include managing critical incidents, behaviour (externalising and internalising), aggression, learning problems and social development.

Unit Information

Welcome to School Psychology 8478.  This course is intended to enhance your theoretical knowledge through practical application as it relates to psychology in schools.
This unit is intended to prepare psychology graduates for employment as school psychologists within government and non-government education systems.  The course will expose students to a range of relevant theories, processes and practices appropriate for the provision of psychological services to schools and students.
On completion of this course students will:
1.	Be aware of the objectives of school psychology as a student support service in schools including an understanding of the range of service provision as it currently exists.
2.	Be able to demonstrate competency in assessment and analysis of assessment information. 
3.	Be able to demonstrate awareness and competency in different aspects of behaviour assessment and change procedures.
4.	Be able to demonstrate competency in oral and written communications, including interviewing, report writing, appraisal of information derived from a variety of sources, prepare and present materials for groups which is appropriate for context and audience. 
5.	Be able to demonstrate an awareness of the skills and issues in educational consultancy, including stake holders, establishing professional relationships, information gathering, developing service plans, and presentation skills.
6.	Be aware of the role of the school psychologist in pastoral care and student management on a whole-of-school basis. 
7.	Be able to demonstrate an awareness of appropriate aspects of current DET policies, initiatives and strategic directions.
8.	Be familiar with the ethical and professional requirements of the Psychologists Registration Board of WA, the APS and Dept of Education and Training (DET).  Students will be able to apply appropriate professional ethical considerations to situations they are likely to encounter in the field.  Students will develop an awareness of the responsibilities that they face as employees of DET or the non-government education system.

ADDITIONAL DETAILED ASSESSMENT INFORMATION
Assessment 1
Presentations:  Students will prepare and present a 20 minute discussions on a topic relevant to school psychology. The list of topics is attached to this form.  This component accounts for 20% of the course and will be carried out from March 8 until complete.
Assessment criteria/guidelines
Clarity of presentation, breadth of the topic discussed, students apparent knowledge of topic.

Assessment 2
Classroom observation:  (during school psychology practicum) of a student identified as a behaviour problem during a formal and an informal lesson. The student will observe the student on two occasions for a total of 40 minutes making detailed notes on what is observed.  The student will then write a report, on the behaviours observed, which will include an analysis of the cause of the behaviours, the impact of teacher and other students on the behaviour, the frequency of some of the behaviours, and recommendations for the management of the behaviour.  The report should not exceed 2000 words.  This component of the course accounts for 30% of the course and is due by Friday 2nd October.

Assessment 3
Report writing:  Students will be given developmental, social, psychological, educational and behavioural information on a child with a ‘problem’.  From this information, students will compile a report of not less than 2500 words detailing the child’s problems and making appropriate recommendations.  This component accounts for 50% of the course and is due by Friday 16th October.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
