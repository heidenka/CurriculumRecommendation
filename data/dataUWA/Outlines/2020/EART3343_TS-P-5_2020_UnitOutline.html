Academic Information

Content The paradigm of plate tectonics encompasses both plate margins and intraplate areas, explaining the styles and distribution of deformation in the crust and the mantle through geologic time. This unit examines relationships between plate movements, large-scale crustal processes and deformation, flow of fluids through the crust (including those creating economic resources), and the geological structures that are produced and both plate boundaries and intraplate areas. This information is then used to interpret the tectonic evolution of ancient geological terrains, with particular emphasis on the Precambrian rocks and resources of Western Australia, and case studies of multi-scale scales. A compulsory field trip examines deformation and related metamorphism on the south coast of WA, and always runs in the period before semester 1 starts (compulsory; charges: cost of food and accommodation is borne by the student).

Unit Information

Students must have passed EART2231 Earth Materials, EART2322 Field Geology and EART2234 Earth Processes, or equivalent approved units. We assume from Week 1 that all students are familiar with the content from these units. You will find it useful to revise some of this content prior to the field trip.
The three modules in this unit present students with an overview of 1) Major tectonic environments/Planetary tectonics, 2) Precambrian and Phanerozic tectonic environments and 3) Case studies. This unit presents the major structural and tectonic environments on Earth by consideration of their geometry and kinematics in relation to Precambrian, Mesozoic and Cenozoic plate movements. Modern and ancient orogenic belts are dissected and presented in at various scales in an attempt to place individual structural belts in their broader tectonic framework. The principal objective is to introduce students to concepts of multiply deformed regions, and to place these in their regional structural and tectonic context. 
In addition this unit features a field trip which is completed prior to the start of Semester 1. The field trip to Hopetoun and Bremer Bay, on the southern coast of WA, takes a tour through the Albany-Fraser orogenic belt, starting with sediments and low-grade metamorphism, and going through various deformation and related metamorphism grades, ending with exposures from the base of the crust. This immediately gives students a reference frame from which to further apply learned concepts to understanding complex tectonic environments.
By the end of this unit student should: (1) demonstrate understanding of the origin, formation and development of tectonic plate boundaries and how to recognise them in the geological record; (2) demonstrate understanding of dynamic plate systems and how tectonic structures develop in each system; (3) apply these concepts to analysis of geological terrains, both ancient and modern; and (4) demonstrate practical skills using techniques in structural geology and tectonic analysis.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

The unit is run as a series of 2-hour workshops, 2 per week, which are designed to mix theory and practical through a series of interactive exercises. Workshops will involve an integrated series of lectures/exercises during the semester, including short problems to longer projects.  The purpose of these exercises is to develop skills in structural and tectonic interpretations using a range of techniques (including digital data analysis) and datasets that can be used to constrain larger-scale tectonic problems. Readings done outside of class time are designed to encourage new information into our existing knowledge structure, and requires reflection, designed to help the students engage with material in a deeper way and construct new meaning from it. There is ongoing reference to an analysis of the knowledge acquired during the field trip.

Unit Structure

The unit contains a compulsory field trip that departs 10 days prior to the start of semester 1 each year, with a compulsory safety briefing 11 days prior to the start of semester each year.
Field trip duration is 7 days (6 nights, 3 at Hopetoun and 3 at Bremer Bay). Students pay for their own accommodation and food. Accommodation is camping, with tents and cooking facilities provided. Students camp and cook in groups.
The unit has 3 modules based on Major Tectonic Environments/Planetary Tectonics, Precambrian and Phanerozoic tectonic environment, and Case Studies. There are 2 x 2 hour workshops each week. Usually these will be the same days each week. However, disruptions from public holidays (there are a number in semester 1) and PROSH may mean classes in some weeks are moved to other timetabled slots. 
Module 1 usually occupies 6 weeks of the semester, with modules 2 and 3 occupying 3 weeks each. Each module has one main assignment. The reading reflections continue across all modules.

Participation

Given the advanced level of the unit, and its location (for workshop purposes) in a laboratory (non-recorded) venue, we expect all students to attend all sessions. The workshop format means that theoretical information and technical tasks are merged, and often build week-on-week, especially for the assessed tasks.

Referencing Requirements

The nature of the assessments in this unit are practical and reflective, minimising the requirement for referencing.

Texts

The bulk of reading in this unit is from published literature, which is provided on the LMS site. There are also related sections of the main text book from the prerequisite unit that cover background information.The main text referred to, which most students will already have purchased in 2nd year is:Structural Geology of Rocks and Regions, 3rd edition, David et al., Wiley press.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
