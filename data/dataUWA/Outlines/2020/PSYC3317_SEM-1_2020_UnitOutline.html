Academic Information

Content This unit bridges cognitive science and cognitive psychology on the one hand, and biology and neuroscience on the other. It is critical to understanding the link between the mind and brain. Cognitive neuroscience investigates all mental functions that are linked to neural processes and ranges from experiments performed in the laboratory to computer simulations, and can involve humans and animals. The unit consists of an integrated series of lectures, laboratories, reading and assessment and aims to provide a sound understanding of fundamental concepts and contemporary issues in cognitive neuroscience. It introduces the field and techniques of cognitive neuroscience and covers key concepts in vision, attention, action, memory, audition, language, emotion, development and executive functions.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

METHODS OF COGNITIVE NEUROSCIENCE (Weeks 1-4). 
• The first section of this unit concentrates on the methods of cognitive neuroscience. Examples of how these methods are used are taken from all fields of psychology, with a focus on person perception. This focus is because (i) most of the methods of cognitive neuroscience have been used to understand this computationally difficult but socially important skill, and (ii) the unit co-ordinator has expertise in the cognitive neuroscience of person perception. Examples from other fields are in the textbook and will be covered in later lectures. 
• Lectures 1-4 and Chapters 1-5 of the textbook will cover the methods of cognitive neuroscience, with Chapter 6 covering face recognition (and other aspects of vision).
• A multi-choice quiz in week 5 (online) aims to:
- provide early formative feedback, especially of your understanding of the Methods of Cognitive Neuroscience. Understanding the methods forms a foundation for the rest of the unit.
- these chapters will then NOT be assessed again in multiple choice questions in the final exam
- Feedback (e..g, your score on the test) will be available in the first half of the unit. 
• Practice multi-choice questions can be found here: https://routledgetextbooks.com/textbooks/9781138490543/quizzes.php
• The questions on the practice tests and the LMS Quiz in Week 5 will be of a similar nature to what can be expected on the final exam. 
• The first three labs (Weeks 3, 4, & 6) also have focus on demonstrations and methods. The lab report is a written report of an experiment which you will view during the lab class. You will also have the opportunity to sign up to participate in the experiment where we collect electrophysiological data (see sign-up on LMS). 
APPLYING THE METHODS OF COGNITIVE NEUROSCIENCE (Weeks 5-12)
• The second section of this unit builds on the first section, examining how core aspects of psychology have been illuminated via the methods of cognitive neuroscience. Readings from the textbook, and other associated readings, reinforce and expand on the material presented in the lectures. Lab 4 (Week 10) is also connected to this material. 
GROUP PRESENTATION
• A group presentation in the final lab synthesizes both sections, asking you to examine cognitive neuroscience in the media. This assessment is designed to:
- encourage you to think about what questions in cognitive neuroscience interest you and give you the opportunity to examine one in detail. It will also give you the opportunity to think about the techniques used in cognitive neuroscience. And finally, to examine science communication and mass media. 
- think critically when reading research - in both the media and in published papers. Just because something is published doesn’t mean that it is good science or that there are not alternate points of view. Questions that you should think about when reading the paper and writing your presentation include: what question/s were the researchers trying to answer? How was the experiment designed to answer the question? Was it designed well? What did they find? What does this actually mean?
- develop your skills in conveying complex information to others verbally. In addition to giving a presentation, you will also have the opportunity to ask and answer questions and you will also provide written comments on others presentations.
- work with others. Not always easy but a skill which employers value.
Overall, it is our hope that students come to appreciate the role of the brain in psychology!

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
