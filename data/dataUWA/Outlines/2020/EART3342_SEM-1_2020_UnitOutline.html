Academic Information

Content This unit focuses on the geochemistry, petrology, mineralogy and textures of igneous and metamorphic rocks. This unit provides an introduction to basic thermodynamic concepts applied to the petrogenesis of igneous and metamorphic rocks from a variety of tectonic environments. The unit also provides an overview of the mineral system framework to understand the genesis of magmatic and hydrothermal mineral deposits, and an introduction to sulfide and oxide mineralogy. Practical unit material includes extensive use of the conventional microscopy, an introduction to reflected-light microscopy, phase diagrams, application of basic thermodynamic concepts, and identification of major rock-forming minerals and rock textures. Conceptual and practical emphasis is placed on the interpretation of sources and processes in the formation of igneous suites and metamorphic facies.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

Students are expected to attend lectures and labs, where unit content will be divulged in the form of workshops and discussion of relevant literature. Practical work associated with laboratories is crucial to fulfill the learning objectives of this unit. Learning in this unit is heavily focused towards application of concepts and knowledge, with practical skills, to help students develop a deep understanding of petrogenetic processes that occur on Earth and other planets in the solar system. Lectures and workshops provide a blend of conceptual information and interactive work in class to generate a knowledge framework.

Unit Structure

The unit is subdivided into 4 sequential modules, which are interconnected and provide the students with a comprehensive view of the key petrogenetic processes that occur on our planet. The first module (Metamorphic geology and fluid flow) is coordinated by Dr Tony Kemp. The second module (Ore-forming processes and mineral systems) is coordinated by Dr Steffen Hagemann. The third module (Mantle melting processes and the petrogenesis of mafic and ultramafic rocks) is coordinated by Dr Marco Fiorentini. The fourth module (Petrogenesis of felsic magmas) is coordinated by Dr Tony Kemp.

Participation

You are strongly encouraged to attend as many lectures and workshops as you can. Attendance and participation is expected at one 3-hr practical class per week all semester starting in the first week. Note that classes start on the hour - please be punctual so that you do not miss the lab introduction. Practical classes are held in Robert Street Building Third Year Geology Laboratory (located on the first floor). Wearing covered footwear in the lab is a UWA safety rule. Please keep this in mind on the days you have lab classes!! You will not be allowed into the lab without appropriate footwear. Your campus card will provide you with access to this lab out of class time.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
