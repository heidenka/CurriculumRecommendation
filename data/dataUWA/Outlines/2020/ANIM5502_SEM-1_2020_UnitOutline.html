Academic Information

Content There are three primary academic objectives of the unit: (1) to provide experiential learning in a research group atmosphere. Students become part of the Centre for Evolutionary Biology (CEB) and bring their ideas to fruition through discussion with the members of the group and their mentor. They participate in fortnightly CEB meetings where established researchers present their data and answer questions from the group. The students see how science is a collegial and constructive process; (2) to learn the generic skills of scientific thinking, hypothesis testing, data handling and scientific communication; and (3) to develop specialised knowledge of the practical considerations of research in their area of study—learning (but not limited to) techniques such as biometry, behavioural analysis, meta-analysis quantitative/molecular genetics/genomics or comparative analyses. The areas of theory covered depend on the project but could include natural/sexual selection, sperm competition, immunity, conservation biology and population/quantitative genetics. Projects that focus on a range of study organisms, including terrestrial, marine and freshwater vertebrate and invertebrate groups may be available for practical work.

Unit Information

This unit is a research led practical exercise, delivering an opportunity to work closely within the Centre for Evolutionary Biology (CEB), a world leading research group based in the School of Biological Sciences. You will be surrounded by a research atmosphere that you will not experience in other units; you will attend regular CEB meetings and will conduct a research project under the supervision and mentorship of a member of the Centre’s staff. The rationale behind the unit is to expose students to the environment of a research group and ultimately for you to produce research that you can be confident fits within the sphere of a world leading group. The CEB is a diverse group with research into cognition, immune function, sexual selection, sperm competition, alternative mating tactics and population and quantitative genetics

Learning And Teaching Strategy

y attending CEB meetings you will gain an understanding of the nature of research and engage in scientific discourse with a range of evolutionary biologists. In the meetings you will learn how to communicate science effectively and will be afforded the rare opportunity of being part of an active research environment. Experiencing this atmosphere will help you deliver your own science. The practical (research) project will help you develop research skills, including experimental design, data collection, statistical analysis and interpretation. You will present your research in the two main scientific forums: as a short manuscript and as an oral presentation to CEB members. The manuscript will be ‘reviewed’ within the CEB in the same manner as a scientific paper and you will have the opportunity to address the reviewers’ comments in order to improve your manuscript. Hence you will learn about how to write a scientific paper and also experience what it is like to produce a manuscript for publication.
On completion of this unit you should be able to:
1.	understand the subject area of your research topic
2.	access and interpret the primary literature 
3.	collect, organise, analyse and interpret data
4.	critically evaluate evidence (data and primary literature)
5.	communicate clearly and concisely, demonstrating your comprehension of the topic

Unit Structure

Lecture - There will be one compulsory introductory lecture in the first week (Wednesday February 26th, 2pm, Conference Room, ground floor of the Zoology Building) where we will describe in more detail what is expected and what to expect. There is no content delivered at this stage because the content is determined by the project that you choose. Following the introductory lecture you will be asked to list your ranked preference for projects from the list we provide. Projects will be allocated based on students preferences, with every attempt being made to allocate each students highest or next highest preference.
CEB meetings - CEB meetings are scheduled fortnightly throughout the unit on Wednesdays at 1pm-2pm in the Zoology Building Conference Room. Your attendance is assessed.
Discussion groups - You are expected to meet with your project supervisor for one hour per week to discuss the progress of your laboratory work and your interpretation and presentation of this work. The time and place of the meeting will be negotiated with your project supervisor.
Research project work - Your research project work will take place in the circumstances (i.e. the lab or the field) dictated by the project and at the direction of your project supervisor. Our expectation is that you will accumulate approximately 80 hours of ‘research’ time over the course of the unit

Participation

This is a face to face practical unit that requires full and active participation. All components are compulsory. Your attendance at CEB meetings will be monitored and you will be expected to keep a lab book, in which you document your 80 hours of research activity. Conducting research will require you to be flexible with your time.
1. Attendance at each CEB meeting will attract 1% to the maximum of 10% for the 10 meetings. No attendance no mark.  
2. You will submit a research paper formatted as a Brief Communication for the Journal of Evolutionary Biology. Papers are to be submitted to Prof Leigh Simmons via e-mail and must be no longer than 2500 words excluding references, tables and figures and their legends. The manuscript will be assessed by ‘peer reviewers’ (members of the CEB) as if it was a submission to the journal. The peer reviewers will grade the initial submission and return comments for suggested improvements to the student. The student will then have the opportunity to address the comments to improve on the initial mark.
The improvement possible following peer review depends on the initial mark. A score of 50% allows the student to subsequently gain up to 50% by fully addressing the reviewer’s comments and preparing a perfect second draft (but they will have a mammoth task). An initial mark of 90% allows for an improvement of only 10% (but there will be less to do). Clearly the best strategy is to do well first time around and not rely on trying to fix all of the suggested changes. This rubric reflects the peer review process whereby one tries to get the paper ‘in first time’. Note, your mark will be incremented based on both the revised manuscript and on your formal response to the referees comments. You will be provided with a marking rubric so that you can see where you performed well and what needs to be improved.
3. Your research seminar will be marked by 2 members of the CEB  following the rubric available on LMS.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
