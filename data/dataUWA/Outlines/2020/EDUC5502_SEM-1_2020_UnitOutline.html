Academic Information

Content This unit is delivered as a series of interactive workshops with an emphasis on developing students' mathematics content knowledge and pedagogy, and improving attitudes towards mathematics. Taking into account the development and characteristics of children, emphasis is placed on a range of teaching strategies that use concrete materials to engage children in mathematical learning, along with the use of storybooks (hard copy or electronic) to assist children to engage with, and make sense of, mathematics. Students become familiar with the three content strands (number and algebra, measurement and geometry, and statistics and probability) and the proficiency strands of the Australian Curriculum: Mathematics. They also become familiar with the place of mathematics within the Early Years Learning Framework. Students begin to develop a resource file and professional library as a means of developing their own mathematics content knowledge.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

Various learning and teaching strategies are incorporated into this unit. These include:
- Concrete materials such as pop sticks, straws, counters, dice, unifix cubes, MABs, ten frames, dominoes, tape measures, jigsaws, pattern blocks, building blocks and number lines. Natural items such as gum nuts, leaves, bark, twigs and stones are also used.
- Storybooks are used to engage children and help them make sense of mathematics. These may be hard copy books, big books, electronic books or YouTube stories.
- Technology is utilised through a range of iPad apps, calculators, Beebots and YouTube clips.
- Games are presented as a means of practicing numeracy skills and to engage children. These can be bought games or handmade games.
- Songs and rhymes with actions are used to practice numeracy skills, engage children and to use the whole body to experience mathematics.
- Puppets and felt characters are used to present stories, songs or rhymes; practice numeracy; and engage children.
- Problem solving is introduced through group activities to extend children's thinking and to illustrate the different ways to solve problems.
- The natural environment is used as a context and a resource for teaching and learning mathematics.
- Individual, small group and whole class activities are used as appropriate.

Unit Structure

There are 10 weeks that are face-to-face. Each session lasts 3 hours. The sessions will generally comprise a 45 minute lecture followed by 2 hours of hands-on activities. In Week 4 students will be going to Hollywood Primary School to collect data from Year 1 children for Assignment 1.

ADDITIONAL DETAILED ASSESSMENT INFORMATION
Assignment 1 - Case study
In order to determine where to start teaching, it is important to find out what children already know and understand about an aspect of mathematics. The purpose of this assignment is to provide you with the opportunity to find out children’s prior knowledge of numbers through the use of think boards, and then develop appropriate activities to address an area of misunderstanding. For this assignment you will be interviewing one Year 1 child from Hollywood Primary School. Please see LMS for a full description of this assignment, which is summarised below.
When interviewing the child you will firstly demonstrate how to use a think board. You will then complete three think boards with the child using three different numbers: one that is known, one that may be known and one that is challenging. Information from the think boards and the child's conversation will be used in writing the assignment.
For the written assignment you will critique think boards, use the think boards to report what the child knows about number and where there are areas of misconception or gaps in knowledge, locate the child on various curriculum documents (First Steps in Maths - Number, WA Curriculum - Number and Place Value Foundation or Year 1, Counting principles), make recommendations for the next area of development based on the child's main area of misunderstanding or gap in knowledge and describe two sequential activities that could address these, provide a referenced rationale to justify why you have chosen these two activities, and write a personal reflection.
Assignment 2 - Planning documents
Stories can be used to assist children to develop mathematical skills and knowledge in a meaningful way. This assignment will give you the opportunity to select and analyse a story book that has the potential to be useful to reinforce understandings of number concepts by young children. Please see LMS for a full description of this assignment, which is summarised below.
There are five parts to this assignment: choosing and describing a book that has the potential for children to develop a number concept, making a manipulative that assists in the development of the focus number concept, critiquing the use of manipulatives in the classroom, writing two sequential lesson plans that would teach the chosen number concept using the book and the manipulative, a class presentation to receive formative feedback on your ideas for this assignment, and an e-portfolio entry that addresses a given Graduate Teacher Standards.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
