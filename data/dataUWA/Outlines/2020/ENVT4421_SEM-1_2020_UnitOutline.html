ENVT4421 - Fundamentals of Environmental Management - SEM-1-2020 - 2020 - Face to face / Blended learning

Unit Information

This unit introduces students to the underlying concepts and practice of environmental planning and management. The unit traces the emergence of environmental planning in Australian and international contexts, and critically reflects on the economic, social and regulatory forces shaping planning and management practice. Students are then introduced to the process of planning and strategies for environmental management. This includes an introduction to core concepts and techniques of environmental impact assessment (EIA), adaptive management, community-based natural resource management, and other models and methods of environmental management. The classes provide a conceptual and applied background from which students conduct independent research on a variety of topics concerning environmental planning and management. 
The unit emphasises an understanding of the socioeconomic issues relating to the environment as well as wider human-environment interactions, connections and impacts. In this context, students are able to demonstrate an understanding of the relationship between the sciences and social sciences; develop a working knowledge of a number of biophysical and social issues; and research and analyse particular aspects of environmental sustainability, planning and management in a critically rigorous and independent manner.
The unit caters for students from a wide range of interests and disciplinary backgrounds. Students are encouraged to tailor aspects of their assessment to suit their interests and professional development needs.
Goal
To develop students’ understanding of the key methodological and conceptual themes in environmental planning and management, together with an appreciation of how these concepts can be applied to analyse and mitigate real-world problems. Students will learn practical and analytical environmental management skills that will enable them to examine environmental issues at a range of geographic scales.
Learning Outcomes
On completion of this unit, you should be able to:
(1) 	Demonstrate an understanding of the core approaches to environmental planning and management 
(2) 	Evaluate the impact of economic, social, cultural, and political processes which shape environmental planning and management practice 
(3) 	Apply an understanding of core environmental planning techniques including environmental impact assessment, community engagement and adaptive management 
(4) 	Develop and integrate scenario analyses into environmental decision making for a selected natural resource management topic

Participation

Students are expected to read the prescribed materials for each weekly topic.
Practical classes (tutorials) run for 1 hour 45 minutes in most weeks of the course. Students are expected to participate and complete all practical sessions. If you do not attend the session in person or via Zoom, you should email your work to Marit or Ana for checking. Not completing a session must be explained in writing (e.g. via email) to the unit coordinator or lecturer, or through the submission of a Special Consideration application as appropriate to the circumstances.
Many of the practical sessions contribute towards the assessment items for this unit. It is therefore important that you work through all materials. Students who miss two or more (≥2) of the practical sessions without: (1) approved Special Consideration; OR (2) prior satisfactory explanation to the unit co-ordinator and/or tutor, will incur a marking penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the individual write-up of the scenario plan. This penalty functions in the same manner as the late submission penalty. For example, if a student’s scenario plan mark was 70/100, and the student missed three of the required classes with no explanation, the final mark for that student would be 60/100 for the assessment (i.e. you can miss one class with no penalty).
In Week 17, all students will attend a fieldtrip to an environmental management body in Perth. The date for this fieldtrip is Wednesday 22 April and will be scheduled as an online forum via Zoom. Preparation for the virtual 'field' trip will be compulsory and is part of Assessment 2. You must apply for Special Consideration if you are unable to join this field trip, and notify the unit coordinator.

Brief description of the assessment items (please refer to the LMS for detailed assessment briefs). Word limits do not count references or appendices, but do include captions and tables.
1. Comparative analysis of EIA frameworks
Each student will be required to prepare a 1,500 words comparative analysis of the environmental approvals process (including requirements for Environmental Impact Assessments) for a hypothetical development (for example, a hydropower dam for electricity generation, or an open cut mine for iron ore extraction) located in 1) Western Australia AND 2) ONE international jurisdiction. There will be accompanying lecture content and practical exercises associated with this assessment. A detailed assessment brief and rubric will be available for this assessment and posted on the LMS. Please refer to the detailed assessment brief when completing this assignment.
2. Scenario plan for environmental management
Each tutorial class will be divided into groups of ~3/4 students to work jointly on preparing a water management plan under different future scenarios. Groups will work together to conduct independent literature research, collect data during fieldwork, and draw up a range of potential future scenarios. The group-based component of the work will be assessed through submission of a summary poster of your scenario analysis and a brief group presentation. Each individual will submit their own individual scenario plan, in a concisely written, illustrated format suitable for dissemination to the broader public (think ‘summary for decision makers’). As for the other assessments, a detailed assessment brief will be available that contains further details on the assessment. Please refer to the detailed assessment brief when planning and writing your work.
UPDATE: Groups are still available for students to work together on the assignment, however the presentations and reports will all be based on individual work. Please see the Detailed Assessment Description on the LMS.
3. Reflective diary
UPDATE: This assessment will be updated to adhere to social distancing recommendations. Please refer to the LMS for an updated Assessment Description 
Each student will prepare a <2,000 words (maximum) reflective dairy on the role of community-based organization in environmental management. Your reflections should draw on the literature discussed during lectures, and be based on attendance of at least two informal meetings or events organized by a community-based organization. Examples of community organisations you could examine will be provided. Your diary should be written in the LMS, and reflect your personal experience as well as critically evaluate the organisation’s role in environmental management. A detailed assessment brief and rubric will be provided in Week 2 of semester; please refer to the complete assessment brief when preparing the briefing note.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
