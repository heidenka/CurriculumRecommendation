Academic Information

Content The unit teaches through four modules, reviewing four major issues in science—biodiversity loss; energy, resources, and pollution; whale and fish stock conservation; and water, carbon and climate change. Students learn from the perspective of practising scientists, and so are involved in data acquisition, data analysis, and the communication of formal scientific information. The unit focuses on statistical and numerical analysis applied to core topics in the natural sciences, and how to present the results of formal analysis effectively.

Unit Information

Human society faces many big challenges and science plays a major role in finding ways to deal with these challenges.  The unit ‘Science, Society, and Data Analysis’ will teach you techniques to solve major scientific challenges through four modules: Biodiversity conservation; Energy, resources, and pollution; Fish and marine conservation; and Climate change mitigation and Water resources.
In this unit, students will learn about the nature of the challenge in each area, and also learn about objective ways of managing and organising data relevant to understanding the key issues associated with each challenge.  Basic data presentation and statistical data analysis skills underpin scientific study in almost all subsequent scientific units.  Consequently, this unit focuses on mastering basic approaches to data presentation and statistical analysis, which will provide a platform for subsequent detailed scientific inquiry at higher level units.  The four themes considered also highlight areas for future possible research focus in later years of study at UWA.
Broad learning outcomes
On completion of this unit you should be able to do the following:
1)	Data analysis skills:
   •	Be able to determine the most appropriate approach to visually display different data sets
   •	Be able to fit a trend line to data and interpret the trend
   •	Be able to conduct a structured analysis of a data set
2)	Computer software skills:
   •	Use computer software to effectively manage data sets and calculate summary statistics
   •	Use computer software to create effective visual plots that highlight important features of a data set
   •	Use computer software to test different hypotheses about population means
   •	Use computer software to conduct both parametric and non-parametric t-tests and multiple group comparison tests.
3)	Module-specific knowledge:
   •	Understand what is meant by biodiversity, how to measure it, and why it is important
   •	Be familiar with the value of energy, and the risks and consequences of using energy from different sources
   •	Understand the importance of global fish stocks and the challenges to effective fisheries management
   •	Have an understanding of the complex interactions between science and society within the context of carbon emissions and water allocations
4)	Interpretation and reporting:
   •	Report results using the IRMD format for reporting scientific data analysis results

Learning And Teaching Strategy

This unit consists of weekly online lectures and face-to-face practical classes. Students are expected to:
1) Watch the online lectures prior to the tutorial/computer lab
2) Attend a weekly 2-hr small-group class during which the tutorial works and computer lab will be discussed. The majority of these classes will be delivered face-to-face. However, an online workshop is available to students who are unable to attend campus because they reside overseas, in remote regions, or due to medical reasons.
In addition, students will be expected to:
3) Read preparatory materials distributed via the LMS
4) Complete the tutorial materials for each class, and submit the computer lab solutions each week as stipulated in the study guide
5) Devote time to each assignment preparation outside the dedicated class hours. Details of each assignment will be posted on the LMS and will test your ability to apply the activities taught in the practical classes
6) Devote time to studying for the two online quizzes. Information about these quizzes will be posted on the LMS and will test your understanding of the lecture content and tutorial + computer lab information.

Other

Software requirements
The computer software used during the course switches between MS Excel and the open source platforms R and the R Studio interface. Additionally, you will need to be familiar with MS Word (or other appropriate software) to present your work. The university computer labs run PC machines and the course is based around the PC versions of MS Office and R. There can be slight differences between Mac and PC versions of software.
Why MS Office and R?
The MS Office suite of products is used in every government department and almost every private sector company. It therefore makes sense to spend time working with MS Office products and learning how to use these neatly and efficiently.
There are a wide number of proprietary software packages available for statistical analysis. These platforms are, however, expensive; and employers will not readily purchases new software just because a graduate they hire would like a specific software platform. The R statistical software package is open source, and will therefore be available in all workplaces. If you learn the basics of R, you will be able to work in any workplace. R is also a common platform for research, and is possibly now the most common software platform used in advanced studies. The R platform allows the creation of publication quality graphics, and this is a valuable skill.
Is there a textbook or a set of notes?
Textbooks are expensive; and for this course there is no one book that covers both the theory material and the how to use R material. Because we don’t want to ask students to buy two expensive textbooks (one to cover the theory content, and a second on how to use R), Ass/Prof James Fogarty has written his own book that covers the essential material for this introductory course. This has been a major undertaking, and we should all applaud him for providing this textbook for free! The book is released under the creative commons licence, so you can take whatever you want from the book and do whatever you want with it, provided you acknowledge the original source.
References, resources, and reading materials
For statistical test related matters and reference theory material, the course textbook provide sufficient detail for the course. More generally, the notes represent a relatively comprehensive treatment of basic concepts and go beyond the content covered in the course. The textbook can serve as a basic reference going forward.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
