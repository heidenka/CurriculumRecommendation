Unit Information

This unit covers mechanics, electricity and magnetism, waves, early quantum mechanics and experimental method. Physics 1030 is a comprehensive introductory physics course that covers the major topics of classical physics and touches briefly on some topics in modern physics. It aims to cover as much of the Year 11 and 12 ATAR Physics course as is possible in 12 weeks. The course caters to students who did not complete physics in high school but need it to advance further in their university studies. It is an ideal course for aspiring engineers, as well as those who wish to sit the Graduate Medical School Admissions Test (GAMSAT). But given the monumental role science has played in forming our modern culture, in building our technological society and in shaping our worldview, a certain amount of literacy in physics is an excellent investment in one’s education, making this course a great choice as an elective for those wishing to increase the breadth of their degree. As the most fundamental of all the sciences, physics provides us with a deep and unique perspective on the universe like no other subject can; covering topics from atoms to galaxies and everything in between. Physics is not only learned, but done; both content and reasoning skills are equally important in Physics 1030.  Apart from learning about the discoveries in physics,  you will  also employ the tools of physics; there is a strong focus on problem solving and conceptual understanding.

Lecture Capture System

In accordance with UWA’s policy on lecture capture all lectures* in enabled venues will be recorded and made available to stream and download by enrolled students via the Lecture Capture System (LCS) for the duration of the unit (including Supplementary and Deferred Examinations period).

* Please note that in some limited circumstances lectures may not be recorded due to:  ethical and/or security related issues; the use of commercially sensitive material; the use of third-party copyright teaching material; or culturally sensitive content. Students will be advised, in advance, when this applies.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

The course is delivered by video lectures. These are supported with on-line practice classes (2 hours per week), offering a high level of individual support for students. Learning is reinforced with laboratory studies. These activities are scheduled via the Class Allocation System (CAS) with 5 sessions of 2 hours' duration spread throughout the semester.

Unit Structure

The course consists of: 24 lectures, given via recorded videos available from the beginning of the semester. The animated powerpoint slides are also available to augment the recorded lectures.
The lectures are reinforced by weekly assignment (available via LMS) and are supported by practice classes, 2 hours per week. These are conducted on-line via Zoom and are scheduled by CAS. Please consult your timetable for your individual allocation.
There are 5 laboratory studies pdf 2 hours', spaced during the semester and allocated via CAS.

Participation

The course content is delivered by means of 24 recorded lectures and these are reinforced by weekly assignments, where each assignment refers to two lectures. Students should view the corresponding lectures before attempting the assignment.  The recorded lectures are supported by the original powerpoint slides, which were used to record them and these are available via LMS. A PDF version is also available, having smaller file sizes but lacking the animation feature of powerpoint.
Students are encouraged to attend the on-line practice classes, which are intended to provide support for the lectures and also assist with completing the assignments and preparing for the tests.
Completion of the laboratory studies is strongly recommended as they constitute a significant part of the final mark. 

Referencing Requirements

References and citations are not required for any submitted assessments, whether tests, assignments or laboratory reports.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
