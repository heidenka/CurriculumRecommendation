Academic Information

Content This unit builds on the progress made in ITAL1403 Italian Studies 3 and aims to extend grammatical knowledge and communicative skills, both written and oral. It further develops the students' ability to communicate in Italian by providing intensive practice in oral and written language, and enriches the students' understanding of the culture of modern Italy. The unit leads to ITAL2405 Italian Studies 5. The academic objectives of the unit are to (1) develop skills in reading, writing, listening and speaking in the Italian language; (2) develop skills in reading written and visual texts relating to contemporary Italy with reference to their social and historical context; (3) acquire knowledge of aspects of Italian culture; (4) increase awareness of the structure and use of the English language; (5) develop independent learning skills; and (6) develop interpersonal communication skills in spoken and written Italian and English.

Unit Information

The language component of Italian 1404 is an intensive course, at the intermediate level. It builds on the work done in Italian 1403 and aims to extend grammatical knowledge and communicative skills, both written and oral.  As in the first semester, language classes will be conducted in Italian, and students will be encouraged and helped to use the target language during these classes, to improve confidence and proficiency in oral expression. 
OUTCOMES
On successful completion of this unit students will: 
1	be able to speak, listen, read and write in Italian about a good range of topics;
2	be able to use language as appropriate to context;
3	be able to express themselves with a good degree of grammatical correctness;
4	have a good understanding of the Italian grammar;
5	be able to read, watch and comprehend texts and movie from contemporary multimedia sources; 
6	have the ability to address critically aspects of the Italian cinema and society;
7	have an increased awareness of how language works in general (metalinguistic skills);
8	have interpersonal communication skills including the ability to work effectively in pairs and small groups;
9	be able to work independently.

	Structure of language classes
	
	The three weekly lessons are structured in a cycle, as follows:
	
	Tuesday 9-10 (TUTORIAL 1)
	
	iMovie project & Cinema (exploring idioms of contemporary Italian as seen in movie clips, together with the basics of visual literacy to analyse and dub short CGI silent animations) 
Wednesday 9-10 (TUTORIAL 2)
	
	Oral and written activities, including grammatical exercises.	
Wednesday 10-11am (TUTORIAL 2)
	
	Communicative activities to practice language features
	
	
	
Tutorial 1
This is a group project which consists in creating a screenplay to dub one of six CGI (Computer Generated Imagery) silent movies made available using iMovie. Each group will explore features of contemporary Italian language related to the videos and learn about elements of visul literacy. The two class assessments are a PPT plan presentation (week 4), and the final dubbing (week 11-12). The project follows a flipped learning approach: students will be required to watch Francesca’s short videos and do activities beforehand. Class time will be used for discussion and further practice. In addition, Italian movie clips will be screened and discussed in class.
TUTORIAL 2 (first hour)
This language class is designed for the active use of the new (and old) structures and vocabulary through a variety of grammatical exercises and communicative activities.
TUTORIAL 2 (second hour)
In this language class, as a group and individually or in pairs, students will work on texts, videos and or authentic documents to further practice their communication skills. 
It is essential that students prepare in advance the exercises for class work (both homework/grammar exercise and watch the iMovie videos) as indicated in the programme. Class attendance, performance and two movie assignments will form part of the final assessment (10%).

Participation

Students are expected to attend all tutorials and prepare for class by writing down the answers to the exercises as per programme. Students should make every effort to participate actively in the class. The Participation mark is based on both attendance and quality of contribution to class activities. A doctor’s certificate must be supplied in the case of illness. Failure to attend at least 80% of all classes may lead to a letter sent out by the Course Coordinator in the first place, followed by marks deduction.
Two assignments need to be submitted as part of the participation marks: students will write about two movies of their choice in weeks 5 and 9.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.

Special Considerations
