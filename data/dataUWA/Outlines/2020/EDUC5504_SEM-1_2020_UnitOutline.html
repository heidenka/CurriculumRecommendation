Academic Information

Content Students examine language and literacy development and learning in young children, as well as a range of key literacy teaching practices including effective strategies for teaching reading and writing, speaking and listening, and viewing. Students also learn about key foundational skills such as phonological awareness and phonics knowledge as well as exploring a range of resources that are important in early childhood literacy learning and teaching contexts, including children's literature, informational texts, multimedia and digital texts, rhymes and songs.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

This unit is mainly taught face-to-face, although a 'flipped classroom' approach will regularly be used, whereby you will be expected to watch lecture videos and/or complete readings and tasks before the seminar. There will be two off-campus activities - one in the UWA Early Learning Centre and one at Telethon Speech and Hearing in Wembley.

Unit Structure

This unit runs over 10 weeks, with 3-hour seminars once a week. You are expected to have completed the required readings and online activities prior to each class so that when we meet, we can engage in activities that allow you to further explore and apply your new knowledge. We will be using technology a lot during classes so it will be advantageous for you to bring your laptops or mobile devices. We will be using a class set of iPads but if you have your own, you may like to bring it to class. There will be some off-campus excursions and flipped classes.

ADDITIONAL DETAILED ASSESSMENT INFORMATION:
Assignment 1 - digital resource and rationale (30 marks)
a)	Create a multimodal e-book or other digital resource to be used by a teacher to support young children's learning of a specific phonological awareness or phonics concept. The resource should be designed to help a small group of children with similar needs in either Kindergarten, Pre-primary, Year 1 or Year 2. 
If your resource is an e-book (most pre-service teachers will choose this option), it should be approximately 8 – 12 pages (screens) long, and created using an app such as Book Creator. If you prefer, you may use PowerPoint or Keynote. 
b)  Write a 1,200 to 1,500 word rationale (justification). In the rationale, you should: 
1) Briefly describe the digital resource, its purpose and its features. Clearly state the specific learning goals. 
2) Describe how the digital resource will be used alongside non-digital resources to help the children learn the specified literacy concepts. What will the teacher do? What will the children do? How will the teacher know that the planned learning has occurred? 
3) Make reference to current research literature to justify the design of the digital tool and the way that it will be used. Your rationale should also include a brief description of how the digital resource should be used to ensure safe and ethical use of ICT in early childhood settings.
Write the word count at the top of your assignment. The word count does not include the end-references. Please see the rubric for this assignment.
*The purpose of this assignment is to help you learn about the content (what the children need to learn), the pedagogy (how they can learn/be taught the specific content) and how digital technology can be used to improve the pedagogy. This assignment aligns with Unit Outcomes 1, 2 and 3.
Assignment 2 - planning task
40 marks
Part A
a) Design and make a literacy learning centre, station OR take-home literacy pack for a particular age group of children, focusing on specific literacy needs. This should preferably be suitable for a group of children or an individual child in your upcoming professional practice class. See your tutor if you are not doing a professional practice this semester. Please do not select the same learning goal/s or year group that you chose for Assignment 1. NB. You will not be handing the resource in for marking but will describe it in the rationale.
b) Write a 2,000 - 2,500-word description and rationale (justification) to explain how the use of the station, centre or take-home literacy pack should help children learn the specified literacy concept/s. 1) Describe the station, centre or pack and its features. You should include photographs of your resources in this description. Clearly state the learning goal/s. 2) Describe how the station, centre or pack is intended to be used to teach the specified literacy concept/s to the children and to assess the children's learning.  Include a section on how you plan to involve parents in the children's learning and why this is important. This section constitutes a teaching plan.  3) Justify the design and planned use of your teaching station, centre or literacy pack, making reference to journal articles, not just textbooks.  Write the word count at the top of your assignment. The word count does not include end-references.
Part B
If possible, implement the station, centre or take-home pack during your professional practice. After the children have used the station, centre or the take-home pack, post a reflection in your e-portfolio and upload a copy of it, or a link to it, on LMS. See the guidelines and rubric on LMS for more details. The reflection should be linked to the Australian Professional Standards for Teachers taught in this unit. You may also get a chance to implement your e-book or digital resource during your professional practice class. If so, you may wish to post a reflection on this also (optional). *Even if you do not use your resources on professional practice, you are still required to post a reflection about the resources you have created to your e-portfolio.
* This assignment is linked to Unit Outcomes 1, 3, 4, 5 and 6.

RESUBMISSION RULE:  
Failure to pass an assessment item within a unit (MTeach) 
15. Students must pass all components of a Master of Teaching unit to pass the unit overall. Students who fail an assessment item in a unit may resubmit it if— 
(a) this is their first request for a resubmission in the unit; 
(b) they contact the unit coordinator by email within 5 University working days of the release of the result and formally request a resubmission. 
(c) A resubmitted assignment that is deemed to be a ‘fail’ by the unit coordinator, will receive the original failing mark for the component. 
(d) Where resubmission is approved, the reassessed mark is capped at the assessment pass mark, unless an application for mitigation is approved in accordance with the University Policy on Assessment: Special Consideration (UP11/23). 
(e) A resubmission is normally due one week after being approved by the unit coordinator. 
(f) Assignments that are failed on the grounds of lateness or academic misconduct will not normally be considered for resubmission.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
