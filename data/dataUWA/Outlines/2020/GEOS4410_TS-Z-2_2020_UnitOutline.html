Academic Information

Content How can geoscience contribute to Australia's wealth and prosperity over the next 100 years? This unit focuses on the evolution of the Australian continent during the last four billion years, ranging from crustal formation, tectonic assembly, and paleogeographic history up to recent geological times. This geological history provides fundamental information about secular changes on Earth as a planet, and a framework in which to understand the formation and distribution of Australia's important resources, e.g. mineral deposits, groundwater, petroleum, and other energy sources. The unit draws on a broad range of geological, geophysical and geochemical datasets to provide an overview of the Australian continent and its remarkable record of critical points in Earth's history ranging from early life to present day. Unit material will also focus on current research topics. Scientific literacy skill development is a key component of this unit including critical reading of scientific articles, effective scientific writing and oral presentation.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

Teaching and Learning in this unit is undertaken via workshops with a weekly theme to explore the geological record and evolution of the Australian continent.  Evolution is about changes through time, so our themes are based on geological time periods and will emphasise WA examples of important geological regions.  The workshops  are research-led and combine conceptual knowledge (e.g. current understanding) with opportunities to engage with researchers actively working in these fields to develop deeper understanding of critical time periods and how processes and systems are connected.  Wednesday and Thursday workshops focus on the geological theme with details provided in the Unit Schedule. You may find it helpful to refer to the one page schedule that is provided on the Welcome page.  These workshops involve presentations by researchers and whole-class and/or group discussions to focus on key questions and evidence for interpretations, reconstructions and models.  Preparatory reading is provided in LMS prior to the week's theme.  
Tuesday workshops will focus on developing skills in scientific literacy with emphasis on critical reading, writing and oral presentation, using material relevant to weekly themes and the unit overall.  These skills underpin our ability to communicate our results and interpretations and understand those of others in contributing to important debates about Earth evolution and understanding this history to predict future changes.  Assessment work aims to provide direct feedback to help you improve your skills during the unit.
Teaching staff in this unit are:  Dr Alan Aitken, A/Prof. Annette George, Dr Nico Thebaud and Dr Ian Tyler.  All of these people are active researchers in the School of Earth Sciences at UWA, as Academic staff or Honorary/Adjunct staff.  Dr Tyler has recently retired from a very senior position in the Geological Survey of WA where he worked for several decades.

Unit Structure

This unit is composed of weekly workshops as shown in the UWA timetable, held on Tuesdays, Wednesdays and Thursdays from Week 9 (starting Monday 24 February).  Workshops are up to 3 hours and commence at 2 pm on each of these days.  Please refer to the unit schedule at regular intervals to ensure you are up-to-date with required reading for each theme.   In the last two weeks of the unit, there are no formal classes so that time is available for you to undertake the project and prepare your presentation and abstract, all of which are due in the last week of the unit.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
