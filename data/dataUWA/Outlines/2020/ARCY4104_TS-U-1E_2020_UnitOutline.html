Academic Information

Content This unit transfers qualitative and quantitative research skills that are essential core competencies for all archaeologists. Topics include: radiometric dating techniques, sampling strategies, geoarchaeological methods, statistics and quantitative research methods, qualitative methods and archival analysis, archaeozoological, botanical and lithic analyses, interview techniques, principles of data management, and lab safety. Experts in the field explain and demonstrate the latest archaeological research methods and participants gain competency in these methods.

ARCY4104 - Advanced Methods in Archaeology - TS-U-1E-2020 - 2020 - Face to face / Blended learning

Unit Information

This unit transfers qualitative and quantitative research skills that are essential core competencies for all archaeologists. Topics include research ethics, understanding sampling strategies from perspectives of spatial and temporal context, statistics and quantitative research methods, lithic and other specialist analyses. Experts in the field demonstrate the latest archaeological research methods and participants gain competency in these methods through individual and group workshops.
Archaeological research is frequently interdisciplinary and draws upon a wide range of research methods.  These methods are determined both by a theoretical famework – and the material/problem at hand.  Opportunity is given to explore specialist methods (e.g. historical archaeology, marine archaeology, geoarchaeology, conservation), geophysical survey and receive feedback on project design and research strategies relevant to individual archaeological topics of interest. The course is taught in intensive mode over six 3-hour seminars for 6 weeks. These hands-one seminars involve individual and group problem-based learning, literature analysis, practical application of research methods, and peer interaction.  Students are required to participate in seminar discussions after having read the week’s required readings, on which there will be a short quiz.  
Students are required to complete weekly online quizzes, in-seminar exercise, and write a fully referenced critical analysis (2000 -2500 words) on one or more methods relevant to their proposed Honours research.  There are no exams.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

The unit uses a weekly three-hour session comprising both a one-hour lecture and a weekly individual or group in-class exercises to allow students to engage with each of the topics relevant to advanced archaeological methods. The lectures provide broad overviews of the respective topics, including new technologies, as well as methodological and ethical considerations.
Whilst not comprehensive, the unit introduces methodologies and scientific techniques that archaeologists use to understand the past and its temporal and spatial context. Students learn how artefacts, sites and other evidence for human behaviour are identified, dated, analysed and interpreted, as well as exploring the strengths and weaknesses of different approaches and methods.  Some of these methods may be used by the students in their own Honours research.

Unit Structure

This Unit will be conducted as a combined seminar and in-class workshop themed around six different aspects of advanced methods in archaeology.  Each week there will be a quiz associated with the reading material each worth 2% (2% x 5 = 10%), and an in-class workshop designed to be either individually or as a group each worth 8.33% (8.33% x 6 = 50%).  At least an hour each week will be provided in-class to complete the assessment for each week.  The final assessment (worth 40%) will be a review of one or more advanced methods that is relevant to each individuals Honours topic. 
This unit is designed to be helpful, informative and a little bit challenging but ultimately aimed at aiding the process of thesis preparation and writing.  As an Honours unit, you will be expected to perform in-class and produce some quality work.
Topics for the six classes are as follows:
1.	Introduction and methodological approaches to understanding the past.
2.	Statistics
3.	Spatial context
4.	Temporal context
5.	Materials analysis - bones
6.	Materials analysis - lithics

Referencing Requirements

The approved UWA referencing practice recommendations follow footnote, author-date and numeric styles, and include: a) Australian Guide to Legal Citation (AGLC) b) American Psychological Association (APA) referencing style (author-date style) c) Chicago Style Guide (footnote and author-date style) d) Institute of Electrical and Electronic Engineers (IEEE) style (author-date style) e) Vancouver referencing and citation style (numeric style) f) MLA (author-page style of in-text citation). Late Penalty - University Policy on Assessment
Per the University Policy on Assessment, a penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assigned work is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due. Assessments submitted later than 7 days after the deadline receive a mark of zero, unless an application for mitigation is approved.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
