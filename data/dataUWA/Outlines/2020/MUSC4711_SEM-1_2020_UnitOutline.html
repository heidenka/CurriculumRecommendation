Learning And Teaching Strategy

The unit is taught through practical sessions that model studio teaching and ensemble direction contexts in order to provide experience of: planning; repertoire selection; warming-up; instruction; evaluation; assessment; direction; and the record-keeping on which reflection depends. Informed attendance is compulsory, and students are required to prepare for sessions in which they will be involved in performance and leadership roles.

Teaching strategies include lectures, seminars led by the lecturer, seminars led by students, and responses to web-based material. Many of these involve practical experience of musical leadership.
Students are required to present in class examples of activities on which teaching will be based; present and discuss planning strategies for a range of teaching contexts; and build a portfolio of self-devised teaching materials.
Sessions deal with: the nature of assessment and its role in motivation; questioning styles and their purpose; the design and application of assessment materials; and investigation into how assessment is carried out in Practicum schools

Unit Structure

This unit includes the psychological and pedagogical aspects of instrumental and vocal teaching and performance; rehearsal techniques; conducting school ensembles; philosophy and psychology of instrumental teaching and learning; and materials, repertoire and methods. At the core of the unit is the teaching apprenticeship requiring a minimum of 20 hours of supervised teaching.
Learning outcomes (see above) are accredited by the Teacher Registration Board of Western Australia following national guidelines. Students can review the Australian Professional Standards for Teachers online at: www.teacherstandards.aitsl.edu.au/OrganisationStandards/Organisation.
The unit breaks down into phases as follows:
Weeks 1-4   Working with young voices and choirs (Nicholas Bannan)
Weeks 5-10  Studio Instrumental Teaching and Ensemble Direction (Dale Pointon)
Assessment Structure and Elaboration
Item Title 	Description 	Additional Information 	Assessment criteria
Arranging (40% total). Two arrangements are required to be submitted. They are of equal importance (20% each).
1a Choral arrangement
 Students are required to arrange an existing work of their choice for High School choir (SATB) a cappella. Notation should be in Sibelius or Finale. Advice may be sought on appropriate sources. 	The arrangement should be of about 3 minutes' duration 	Credit will be given for: stylistic appropriateness to the medium; educational potential; likely attractiveness to target performers; musical fluency in voice-leading and notation.
1b Arrangement for instrumental ensemble
Students are required to arrange an existing piece of music for an appropriate instrumental medium. The score of the existing piece will be provided. Credit will be given for: taking into account the technical potential of High School instrumentalists; fluent and expressive employment of the medium; appropriate notational conventions

(40%) 	
Students should write an essay of 2,000 words on a topic related to the field of vocal and choral education. A selection should be made from one of the following titles:
(a) What are the pros and cons of Kodaly education in the classroom?
(b) What strategies can promote effective vocal development in reluctant singers?
(c) Can writing songs encourage students to develop their vocal performance skills?

(20%) 	This reflective log will illustrate the opportunities to develop your skills and understanding in the field of ensemble direction. Experience can be built with ensembles at UWA or in the community during the first 10 weeks of the course; and develop further as opportunities arise during Prac 1. The log should set out works studied, observation made of other directors, and self-reflection on specific directing sessions, identifying skills and experience acquired.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
