Academic Information

Content Over recent decades there has been growth in government and an increasing diversification and complexity in government service provision. There has also been, for the most part, a concomitant rise in the number (and reach) of accountability agencies, so much so that some commentators now talk of the emergence of a fourth branch of government, the ‘integrity branch', to sit alongside of the executive, legislature and judiciary. These accountability agencies have responsibility for overseeing, investigating and educating the public sector in relation to matters such as corruption, misconduct, good decision making, and avoiding conflicts of interest. Such agencies play a vital role. Governments make decisions on a daily basis that directly shape many aspects of individual and corporate existence. With such regulation comes the need for transparency, responsibility and accountability. In the words of former Commonwealth Ombudsman Professor John McMillan, "In short, the power of government is pervasive, and accountability is an indispensable check on how that power is exercised."

Unit Information

As this unit is run intensively, classes are not recorded.
Broadly speaking, in this unit students will explore:
• the place of government in the modern state, and the how and why of government accountability;
• the broad accountability framework (with reference to traditional administrative law, political structures, the media, academia and non-government organisations); and
• the theoretical underpinnings, development, evolving law and practice, and regulation of various accountability or ‘integrity’ agencies.
The Western Australian accountability regime, widely regarded as a progressive one, will be used as an accessible example. This practical local focus will be supplemented by national and international comparison at various points.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

This unit is taught in an intensive format across five days.  It combines both theoretical and practical explorations of the subject matter – drawing on presentations from high-level statutory officers, interactive exploration of the theoretical underpinnings of contemporary government accountability and the integrity sector, and workshop style analysis of broader evolutions and contemporary issues in administrative law.  The assessment is comprised of a class engagement component, a research proposal and a final research assignment.  Each item of assessment is designed to build towards the stated outcomes for this unit.  The engagement exercise builds students’ capacity to understand and assess accountability frameworks and advanced administrative law issues, and to apply and critique the content learned.  Students are expected to undertake some pre-reading on certain topics.  The research proposal focuses students’ efforts on the effective identification, collation and use of key resources in the planning of a project.  These projects will be largely self-driven, with teaching staff providing advice and support in topic selection and parameter setting – and ultimately written feedback on research and project design.  The final research assignment requires students to draw these various capacities together in the production of a focussed and topical substantial research assignment.  Staff will remain available for consultation as projects develop and evolve – and ultimately will provide detailed written feedback on the papers.  The over-arching goal of the unit is to employ a range of teaching formats to engage students in a complex, dynamic and very contemporary field of law.
Units running intensively do not use the lecture capture system. As this unit is run intensively and involves various guest speakers using their own materials, classes are not recorded.

Unit Structure

The unit runs 29-31 January 2020 and 3-4 February 2020 and the venue is the Law Lecture Theatre, room 1.06, first floor, Law Building.  Classes in this unit will run over five full days (with regular breaks and a weekend break in the middle to facilitate reading and preparation).  The bulk of classes will be led by the coordinators, with guest presenters joining the discussion at various points throughout the period.  Students will have the opportunity to question and mingle with these high-profile guests.  Some sessions will focus on broader theory and long-term evolutions in law and policy, others will focus on recent case law and contemporary trends.  Guests will provide a very practical insight into the operations of their respective offices.  Students will have ample time to discuss the issues under consideration.  The final session will involve a Q & A style panel, with guests contributing their thoughts and experiences on some of the critical contemporary issues in law and government.

Participation

A student’s attendance and engagement through the teaching period will be worth 10% of the total marks in the unit.  In general terms, this assessment component will take account of: a) regularity of attendance; b) the extent of preparation apparent from discussions; c) ability to identify relevant issues in case studies and interactive discussions; d) understanding and application of the principles and concepts involved; and e) understanding of and critical engagement with broader issues.  Class discussions will be managed to provide opportunities for all students to contribute.

Referencing Requirements

The research components of this unit require diligent, individual and original work - in project planning, research and writing.  Students are required to work beyond class discussion and materials, and all resources must be fully and accurately referenced.  Students should avoid relying too heavily on particular commentary, should work from primary sources as well as secondary sources, and should be discerning in their selection and use of materials. 
Written papers should conform to the Australian Guide to Legal Citation.  Links to the latest edition of the AGLC and supporting materials can be found on the library website: https://guides.library.uwa.edu.au/AGLC4.  You must use footnotes where appropriate. A bibliography is optional.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
