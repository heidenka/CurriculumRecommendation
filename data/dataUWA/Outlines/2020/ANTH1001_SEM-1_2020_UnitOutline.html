Academic Information

Content We live in a multicultural world where there are many ways of 'being human'. At the same time, all people share some experiences in common—all learn, share and communicate beliefs, create and maintain social relationships, adapt to local environments, and make a living through particular modes of production. This unit explores the world's rich diversity of human experience and practice, looking at cross-cultural examples of it in domains such as religion, identity, subsistence, social inequality and communication. It also considers the variety of ways that anthropologists and sociologists conduct their research.

Unit Information

In this unit, we will be exploring what it means to be human, and the various ways that anthropologists and sociologists have sought to understand the cultural and social dimensions of human life. We discuss our shared human capacities and the wide variety of ways that these are realised through diverse ways of life. 'Being Human' is an introduction to both anthropology and sociology; both disciplines explore how ordinary people organise their lives, interact through cooperation and conflict, develop and share ideas and values, and how people identify themselves and others according to various social and cultural categories. In this unit, we are concerned with both culture and society, and the relationship between these ways of understanding what it is to be human.
Lectures
This unit includes a weekly, two-hour lecture held on Tuesdays from 3pm to 5pm in the Social Sciences Lecture Theatre (SSCI G130).
These lectures introduce and explain important concepts and examples which will be vital to help you understand the readings, tutorial activities and assignments. They are also an opportunity to ask questions directly of the unit coordinator and guest lecturers. For Albany students, and those who miss a lecture, recordings will be available from the Lecture Recordings link. The lecture slides will also be available in the topic folders.
The first lecture is in Week 1 - on Tuesday, February 25 at 3pm.
 
Tutorials
The weekly tutorial discussions are a vital learning activity for this unit; these are organised as 45 minute sessions, coordinated by a tutor, addressing the topic from the lecture the previous week. It is very important that you attend these tutorials having completed the assigned readings and tutorial questions. Tutorials are a required component of the unit, and you will be assessed based on attendance, written preparation, and participation (15%).
Tutorials begin in Week 2 of the semester, but these first tutorials address the material from 'Topic 1'. Make sure that you have completed the readings and the tutorial activity sheet for this topic in preparation for the first tutorial.
   
Readings
Each week, you will be expected to complete a set of readings (and sometimes videos) associated with the topic, as listed in the topic folders. Tutorial discussions will centre around engagement with these readings, which will usually comprise 1-3 book chapters, academic journal articles, and/or documentary films. You can find these resources through the Unit Readings link under 'Student Tools'.
For the first 8 weeks, this will include reading through the book In Search of Respect: Selling Crack in El Barrio by Philippe Bourgois. This classic work of urban ethnography examines the lives of drug dealers in a Puerto Rican neighbourhood in East Harlem, which we will be using to illustrate and explore the different dimensions of society and culture that we address through the unit. While specific chapters are associated with particular topics, you are encouraged to read ahead. However, please be aware that this book contains content which some readers may find distressing, including accounts of sexual violence and rape. The book can also serve as one of the major ethnographic examples that you can use in writing your research essay. (We will discuss the meanings of 'ethnography' in more detail in Topic 2; for now, 'an ethnography' can be defined as an extended social scientific study of a particular social or cultural group, and especially the written product of such a study).
The second edition of In Search of Respect is available to purchase from the Co-op Bookshop/Booktopia, and it should be fairly easy to obtain either new or secondhand copies from other sellers as well. The UWA library provides access to an electronic version of the book, and also has several physical copies available to borrow.

Participation

Tutorials are an essential part of the unit and you are expected to attend all tutorials. Tutorial attendance, preparation, and participation are assessed weekly by your tutor. You must attend your assigned tutorial session and will not receive credit for attending another tutorial. Absences will be excused only through formal special consideration procedures. Participation entails thoughtful and generous engagement with your peers and tutors with the aim of advancing understanding of the subject matter. Each week you will provided with a list of tutorial questions. These are to help guide your thinking on the weekly topic, and they will form the basis of the discussions in class. Demonstrated completion of this written preparation contributes towards your tutorial mark.

Referencing Requirements

It is important to properly acknowledge your sources. In your research essay for this unit, you must properly reference your sources - clearly indicate when you are quoting directly, include citations for all sources that you quote, paraphrase, or otherwise reference, and include a complete reference list comprising all and only those sources that you have cited. Follow the APA citation style as described in the UWA library APA guide (https://guides.library.uwa.edu.au/apa). Please note that you should include page numbers in your in-text citations when paraphrasing or quoting specific passages from written sources.

Other

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
