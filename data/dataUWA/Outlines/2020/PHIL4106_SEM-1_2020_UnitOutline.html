Academic Information

Content Analytic philosophy is a philosophical tradition practiced by a diverse array of figures, and has given rise to–or otherwise been at the heart of–a number of intellectual movements and revolutions throughout human history. This unit explores key topics in the analytic tradition. Topics discussed include, but are not limited to, ontology, epistemology, meaning and reference, modality, epistemology, classical and non-classical logics, naturalism, decision theory, feminist approaches to science and knowledge, and action theory. Key thinkers discussed include, but are not limited to, Quine, Putnam, Anscombe, Carnap, Wittgenstein, Haslanger, Lewis, Priest, Restall, Divers, Nolan, Maddy and Bennett. The unit deploys a range of critical, formal, informal and historical methods.

Unit Information

Evolutionary game theory, originally developed to study biological evolution, has found rich applications in the social sciences and in philosophy. Mathematical models within this tradition have been used in attempts to explain the evolution of cooperation, altruism, and justice, as well as meaning, convention, and social norms. More recently such models have been used to study the social dynamics of science. In this unit we will read a classic of the field (Brian Skyrms’ Evolution of the Social Contract) followed by a recent application to issues in social justice (Cailin O’Connor’s The Origins of Unfairness). As the unit continues, we may add or substitute additional readings depending on interest.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

Our meetings are organized as a reading group. This means that there will be little or no lecturing. Rather, our meetings are used to enrich our shared understanding of the reading material through interactive discussion. It is therefore vital that you (a) read the material in advance and (b) come to class prepared with some questions, especially about any points from the reading that were unclear to you.

Unit Structure

We will meet on Fridays (10:00-12:00 in seminar room G03 in the Arts building). We will go through the readings at a pace of two chapters per week. A detailed unit schedule will be uploaded and updated separately on LMS.
As a general rule, we will use one hour to discuss one chapter from the books we will be working through. For each such hour, there will be one person leading the discussion. Each student in the unit will do this two or three times; I will do whatever remains. To lead the discussion, prepare a summary of the chapter (aim for 20-30 minutes) as well as some discussion questions to get us started. Again, I recommend focusing your questions on aspects of the reading that you would like to clarify your understanding of. Note that leading discussions is a required component of the unit. When it is not your turn to lead the discussion, you are still expected to prepare and participate.
The essay is due on Friday 15 May at 15:00. For the essay assignment you are given two options. You may either write a philosophical essay (i.e., taking a view and defending it through philosophical argument) on a topic discussed in or closely related to the unit, up to 4,000 words. Or you may run your own simulation study by taking one of the models discussed in the books and adding a small tweak (I recommend keeping it simple here). In this case you should hand in your code and a brief discussion of your findings of up to 3,000 words (but may be significantly shorter). Detailed requirements will be made available on LMS soon.
There will also be an examination. More information on this will be made available in due course.
In order to mitigate the effects of implicit biases and other sources of unfairness in marking, the essay and test will be graded anonymously. To make this possible, it is vital that you put your student number but not your name on your essay and test.

Participation

Per the general guidelines for the Philosophy Honours degree, attendance for all seminars and at least one presentation for each seminar is mandatory for each unit. Students are expected to show up having done the reading and with a hard copy to refer to in discussion. If a student attends fewer than eight out of ten seminars for any unit, OR if a student fails to give a scheduled presentation, their overall mark for the unit will be reduced by 10%.

Referencing Requirements

In your essay, you should include an in-text reference whenever you are either directly quoting someone else or describing a view or argument in your own words. This reference should give the last name of the author, the year of publication, and a page or section reference - nothing more and nothing less. Example 1: "inequity is the expected outcome of basic cultural evolutionary processes" (O'Connor 2019, p. 3). Example 2: O'Connor's book aims to show that unfairness can be explained through cultural evolution (O'Connor 2019, p. 3). Your essay should include a bibliography that lists all (and only) those sources explicitly referenced in the text of your essay, using a consistent bibliographic style, e.g., Harvard or Oxford.
In the examination and during class discussion, there are no formal referencing requirements. Still, it is important that you distinguish clearly between your own ideas and those you have derived from a scholarly source (e.g., one of the readings). Use phrases like "O'Connor claims..." or "O'Connor argues" to indicate when the latter is the case. Plagiarism is a very serious issue, and it's in your best interest to avoid creating the impression that you are using others' ideas without acknowledging this.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
