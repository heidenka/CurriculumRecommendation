Unit Information

This unit is designed for preservice teachers who plan to teach secondary school English. It provides a theoretical framework for teaching, learning and assessment. Familiarity with the current curriculum documents is developed. Through active participation in workshops, preservice teachers acquire strategies for effective teaching and learning, gain skills for planning and programming, learn about instructional materials and resources, and develop an understanding of issues relevant to English teaching. 
Welcome to this unit and to the beginning of your career in the English teaching profession. This unit introduces the knowledge, skills and competencies required of a secondary school English teacher (years 7-10). It addresses the preparation of preservice teachers for the graduate level of the Australian Professional Standards for Teachers (APST). These standards will be a tool for your continuing career development. Throughout the unit, you will be expected to reflect on your attitudes and values, your previous experiences of studying English and to use these ideas to develop your personal philosophy for teaching English.
As part of this process, you will explore language development, the teaching of literacy and approaches to literature. We will examine teaching and learning strategies designed to promote language development, critical thinking and a range of literacy and literary competencies. You will be introduced to the Western Australian Curriculum strands of Language, Literature and Literacy and develop knowledge of the Cross Curriculum Priorities and the General Capabilities, as they apply to subject English.
Assessment in this unit is through assignments designed to develop the skills and competencies required for teaching English, but which are also intended to produce materials (programs, lesson plans and curriculum resources) for your practicum and first year of teaching. You will also have the opportunity to practice your English teaching.
This unit assumes that students have already developed certain basic skills, such as an adequate command of:English and related communication skills – students are expected to understand and follow the principles of accepted expression, style and good argument; an understanding of content, language and critique skills in at least some of the following areas: literature, film and television, linguistics and other content areas; the skills and abilities to acquire new content knowledge in areas where this does not already exist; technology skills; and research skills.

Lecture Capture System

In accordance with UWA’s policy on lecture capture all lectures* in enabled venues will be recorded and made available to stream and download by enrolled students via the Lecture Capture System (LCS) for the duration of the unit (including Supplementary and Deferred Examinations period).

* Please note that in some limited circumstances lectures may not be recorded due to:  ethical and/or security related issues; the use of commercially sensitive material; the use of third-party copyright teaching material; or culturally sensitive content. Students will be advised, in advance, when this applies.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

This unit is based upon workshops and an Active Learning approach. In this unit, students will experience a range of learning strategies that are both designed to engage post graduate students and also to model strategies and activities for the English classroom. The types of strategies to be included are concepts based learning, collaborative learning, close and active reading, direct instruction, experiential learning, modelling, and visible thinking and cognitive approaches.

Unit Structure

There are ten workshops of three hours duration each. The workshops are intended to be practical and to build your 'craft' as an English teacher. It is really important that you come to workshops prepared by doing the reading(s) and also any preparation activities.

Participation

PARTICIPATION ACTIVITIES
a)	Group presentations and teaching in action (7 marks)
Curriculum sessions assist you to understand subject English, develop practical strategies for implementation in English classes and provide opportunities to develop teaching and learning centred resources. In a student centred environment, the best way to learn is to do.  Teaching in action will allow you to try implementing a learning strategy, suitable for use with secondary school students, while helping each other to process the readings and concepts in your own tertiary study.
Working in groups of two or three, you will prepare a 10 minute activity to introduce a selection of the weekly readings or review the readings in some way. The purpose of this task is to:  a) provide you with an experience in implementing a learning strategy; b) provide class members with the experience of observing and participating in a learning strategy; c) provide a summary sheet to help others in use of the strategy; d) assist with the processing of the concepts in the reading; and e) to encourage a collaborative enterprise.
 
Ten minutes is not very long and you will inevitably over plan. A great learning experience can be short. Keep your ideas very focussed.You will be asked to nominate a partner or two, select a session and nominate a reading. Participation is strongly encouraged. This provides you with a valuable learning experience. There is no formal assessment of your presentation and it is opportunity to develop your skills in a non-threatening environment.
Please see the Teaching and Learning strategies folder for ideas.
b)	Online discussions (3 marks)
There is an opportunity for an online weekly discussion. Please take part in three discussions with entries of approximately 200 words and aim to engage and challenge your colleagues. You may wish to use examples and references to support your points. A mark is awarded for each entry to a total of three marks (one mark per entry). A full mark is awarded for an entry of 200 words.
c)    Online test (Pass/Fail)
The Western Australian English curriculum places a strong emphasis on language. As a new English teacher, you will inevitably have a strong command of the language in that you are a practitioner. You will need to become familiar with key language concepts in the curriculum and this online test will help you to measure your own skills in this area.

ASSIGNMENT ONE – LESSON PLANNING
You are required to develop 3 consecutive lesson plans for a year 7, 8, 9 or 10 English class, each of approximately one-hour in length. You will be provided with a preferred format.
Please include in your lesson plans: a) organisational details (Year level, day, time, room, topic and focus) b) a brief outline of where the lessons might fit in a program e.g. first lesson of a new topic; c)   links to the curriculum. These include (where applicable) content descriptors, skills, concepts, general capabilities, achievement standards and cross-curriculum priorities where applicable; d)   specific objectives, written in behavioural terms and derived from the Curriculum Links to provide a focus for each lesson; e) the prior knowledge and skills expected of the students; f)  the materials and resources required, including PowerPoints, (and when and how they will be distributed); g)  a detailed series of lesson steps showing the sequence of input, activities, explanations and instructions including an introduction/motivation, and conclusion; h) a copy of any content notes you would need for teacher input on an attached sheet; i) a list of resources referred to in the lesson plans along with copies of all the material such as worksheets, maps, diagrams or cartoons that  you will distribute to students during the three lessons. Resources must be presented in a professional manner ready for use in the classroom and their origin clearly referenced; j)  estimated timing of lesson steps; k) how the objectives are to be assessed – this relates to the learning activities; l) space for evaluative and reflective comments.
Your lesson plans must contain all of the following: a) organisational details (Year level, day, time, room, topic etc.); b) title or lesson focus; c) links to the Western Australian curriculum; d) specific objectives that help to implement the curriculum; e) an introduction; f) a motivational activity/task; g) sequence of learning activities that engage students in active learning tasks; h) conclusion; i) timing; j) a list of resources prepared, selected and/or adapted resources, k) questions to stimulate reflection on the lesson.
The lesson must be a) presented in a functional format, containing all the necessary information; b) There must be a professional standard of presentation (including accurate language, grammar, punctuation and headings); c) Be referenced correctly using APA style; d) Links to relevant curriculum documents are clearly identified; e) Are clearly expressed in student behavioural terms; f) Are directly linked to learning activities and resources.
Please see LMS for additional information
 
ASSIGNMENT TWO – MICRO TEACHING
You will deliver 7-10 minutes of a lesson and may build upon the lessons plans prepared for the previous assessment. This means that you may create a lesson segment to take place before or after your segment of three.  Please create an original lesson segment plan. The lesson segment should focus on a concept from the middle school curriculum and may be a lesson aspect such as a general introduction, activity opening, introduction to group work or a lesson evaluation. You will work in small groups and wherever possible your segment will be recorded for your use only. Please take advantage of the opportunity for formative feedback.
Details
The purpose of this microteaching activity and assessment is to give you the opportunity for some hands on practical experience in English teaching before heading off on your first practicum.  You will deliver a 7-10 minute microteaching session in groups of 4-5.
Select an aspect of teaching middle school English. You may build upon an aspect of your lesson plans or you can create something entirely new. Work with a concept such as symbolism, persuasion or identity.
 
You need to provide a 30 second explanation of your microteaching session before beginning with details about: a) The year level; b) The topic; c) he ability level if relevant; d) The topics/activities that need to be covered.
 
After you teach, your colleagues will provide constructive feedback on your micro-teaching by describing and evaluating what worked for them and ways in which the teaching could be improved. Micro-teaching provides a safe and valuable set of experiences for learning to teach. Watching others teach can provide a springboard for discussion and reflection.
You must bring all materials you need, including copies of pictures or text. You can expect your learners to have paper and pencil, but you should be able to provide everything else you need for your lesson. Please submit your plan and resources on LMS.
 
Reflection
After you co-teach your lesson, you are encouraged to write a 500-word reflection. Your reflection should discuss the following: 1) A description of how you developed your lesson. Give us a sense of what you found hard and what you found easy in designing this lesson. What surprised you? What did you learn?; 2) The thoughts, feelings and emotions you had while you were teaching, and their impact upon the way in which you related to your students; 3) Explain your successes and what worked well; 4) Things that happened during the lesson that stay with you. What about the lesson was still in your mind the next day? Of what significance is this?; 5) Plans for the future – what do you need to work on in the future? What have you learned from your course, such as from your English Curriculum and DTL studies that might help you in the future?
                                     	
 ASSIGNMENT THREE
You are required to prepare a program of work based on the Western Australian Curriculum, for a year 7-10 class. The program should be for approximately 8 hours, or 2 weeks with 4 x 60 minute lessons per week.  A program is a document that outlines the teaching and learning that is planned for a period of between 2 weeks to a whole term. It is based on some unifying concept – a collection of skills (e.g. Formal speaking skills), a topic (gender issues/youth culture/war), a concept (point of view) or text (short stories/genre study/a particular film or novel). It provides direction and a time frame to assist with the planning of daily lessons.  Programs are written firstly for the teacher, but are important documents of accountability that may be presented to Heads of Learning Areas and other senior staff, the School Curriculum and Standards Authority, parents and students. Your program conveys your philosophy and approaches to teaching and learning, to students and assessment. You will be provided with a school and class context on which to base your program development or you may use the school and a class you are working with during your observation.
Your assignment should include: a) a description of the context for which the program has been constructed – the school context and the particular class – school type, SES, number of students, gender, ability level etc); b) a personal philosophy, outlining your core values, pedagogical orientation both generally and in relation to the subject English (300-500 words – please use references); c) a rationale to justify the choice of theme, topic, text etc (200-300 words – please use references), and how this choice relates to adolescent development (social, emotional, cognitive, physical); d) links to the Western Australian Curriculum; e) specific objectives for student achievement; f) learning activities in sufficient detail for the program to be enacted by another teacher. (Consider including key questions or issues where discussions are involved). Learning activities should incorporate literacy and numeracy strategies and evidence of the use of ICT to support and enhance learning experiences; g) a list of resources to be used by students and teacher; h) texts, including visual texts, (appropriate to age and syllabus requirements); i) assessment tasks, including the assessment criteria to be used; j) questions to focus future evaluative comments and reflections; and k) approximate timing – number of hours or number of periods.
Remember that this assignment is part of your own learning process. It requires considerable time to locate resources, think through the issues, organise and develop your ideas. You are likely to find it challenging. Please ask me for assistance as you work through this assignment.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
