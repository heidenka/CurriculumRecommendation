Academic Information

Content Evidence is about the adversarial trial which is our society's primary, ultimate mechanism for determining disputed facts for the purpose of assigning legal liability. The study of the rules of evidence is an examination of procedure and substance. The unit looks at a set of rules which determine the process of a trial and which embodies theories of knowledge and morality—How best do we arrive at a truth? How best do we balance truth and fairness? At a trial, two conflicting stories are told and after hearing the stories, a fact-finder (jury, judge or magistrate) must say 'yes' or 'no' to the story told by the prosecution/plaintiff. It matters, in determining that 'yes' or 'no', what information the fact-finder is allowed to hear, what use they are allowed to make of what they hear, and even how sure they need to be in order to say 'yes'. It is the rules of evidence which determine these things. Students learn the major rules of evidence governing a trial in Western Australia and make comparisons with rules from other jurisdictions. They extend skills of case analysis, legal reasoning and statutory interpretation by considering hypothetical scenarios—Is certain evidence admissible? If so, how can it be used and by whom? Is there enough evidence? Can it be countered? Students extend skills of legal reasoning and critical analysis by exploring the concept of a fair trial as it appears in specific principles and underpins the system of evidence rules as a whole.

Unit Information

All societies have a variety of structures and institutional resources for resolving disputes. For example, in Australia, Aboriginal
societies have for thousands of years resolved disagreement through organised structures and principles of law, and continue to
do so, alongside non-Aboriginal institutional structures. The law degree you are undertaking is primarily a study of Australian
Western law, and this is the case in this course too. Non-Aboriginal legal structures for resolving disputes range from relatively
informal mechanisms such as Negotiation and Conferencing processes to relatively formal mechanisms such as Arbitration and
the trial. The adversarial trial is non-Aboriginal Australia's most formal - its ultimate - dispute resolution mechanism. It provides a
means of determining disputed facts in order to assign legal liability, which is then the basis for an exercise of State (societal) 
power. The Evidence course is about this adversarial trial. The study of the rules of evidence is an examination of both substance
and procedure. The unit looks at a set of rules which determine the process of a trial and which embodies theories of knowledge
and morality, for example., how best do we arrive at truth? What is the relationship between 'truth' and 'fairness'? At a trial, two
conflicting stories are told and a􀈅er hearing the stories, a fact-finder (jury, judge or magistrate) must say 'yes' or 'no' to one of the
stories - that of the prosecution/plaintiff. It matters, in determining that 'yes' or 'no' what information the fact-finder is allowed to
hear, what use they are allowed to make of what they hear, and even how sure they need to be in order to say 'yes'. The meaning of
stories can differ significantly depending on what information the fact-finder is allowed to hear or see. It is the rules of evidence
which determine these things. In this course you learn the major rules of evidence governing a trial in Western Australia and make
comparisons with rules from other jurisdictions. You extend skills of case analysis, legal reasoning and statutory interpretation by
considering hypothetical scenarios. Is certain evidence admissible? If so, how can it be used and by whom? Is there enough
evidence? Can it be countered? You extend skills of legal reasoning and critical analysis by exploring the concept of a fair trial as it
appears in specific principles and underpins the system of evidence rules as a whole.
The course will provide students with an understanding of the common law of Evidence and the main pieces of legislation dealing 
with the law of evidence. The main Act is the Evidence Act 1906 (WA). Reference will be made to the 'Uniform Evidence Acts', which
have been enacted in most Australian jurisdictions. However, although it is likely this uniform model will be enacted in the near 
future, it is not currently the law in Western Australia. Therefore, emphasis in this course is on the common law, Evidence Act 1906
 (WA) and various other Acts that together are the source of evidence law in this state.
To date, in your other law subjects, you have generally looked at the elements of an offence or cause of action. In Criminal Law you
considered whether the elements of an offence (usually created by Criminal Code (WA)) have been satisfied. The elements may
seem to be satisfied on the facts in your problem but in Evidence we go one step further. We look at the types of Evidence that can
be adduced at trial, for example, real evidence, documentary evidence and oral testimony.
Now, just a word of warning. Evidence is tricky. It is a large subject. There is a lot of information to take in. At times you will feel a
little overwhelmed. It is one of those subjects that can be compared to a large jigsaw puzzle. All through the semester we will try to
put the pieces together and it may not feel as though you are getting the full picture. However, suddenly (hopefully) at the end of
the semester everything should fall into place. On the brighter side, Evidence is really very interesting, topical and, dare we say, an
exciting subject.

Participation

The objectives we seek to promote with the participation mark (10%) are to promote:
•	engagement, 
•	preparation, 
•	understanding,
•	reflection; and 
•	responsible tutorial membership.
Engagement will most obviously be in speaking but a student might also be engaged whilst listening. This will be important to note for students who are by nature quieter. They may be engaged, though speak less often.
Preparation might be evident in notes the student has come with or in the quality of answers and comments in discussion.
Understanding of the issues raised in tutorials will most commonly be demonstrated though a student’s overall contribution to the tutorial discussion, including small group discussions within a tutorial, whether by way of answering questions, asking questions, making observations, or listening and responding to other students. 
Reflection on the issues (and preparation) might be evident in the student drawing on material outside the material directly related to the tutorial problem, perhaps from other topics in the course and in questions asked during (or even after) the tutorial seminar;
Responsible tutorial membership refers to the respect given to other student’s comments and refraining from attempting to dominate the discussion.
 
Participation is about the quality not necessarily the quantity of contributions (where ‘quality’ means considered and reflective contributions – you are allowed to make mistakes!). The aim of including this component is to encourage unselfconscious discussion amongst students, not an atmosphere of assessment.
          Grade	Descriptors
HD (80% or higher)	Student is well prepared for every tutorial; demonstrates good understanding of and insightful and/or original reflection on the issues raised in tutorials throughout the semester; critically engages with the subject matter of each tutorial; and consistently demonstrates responsible tutorial membership.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
