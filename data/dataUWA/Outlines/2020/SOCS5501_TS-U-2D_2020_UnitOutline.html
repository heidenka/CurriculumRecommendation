Academic Information

Content How do you know whether a policy, project or program has worked? Evaluation involves a structured process whereby the success of a social policy, program, or enterprise, can be assessed. Best practice requires that all projects, whether government, NGO or industry funded, include an evaluation component. This unit provides students with the skills and knowledge needed to assess the effectiveness and efficiency of social policies, programs and enterprises. It covers a range of tools and techniques, including program evaluation, rapid rural appraisal, participatory action research, photovoice and more. Students are taken through the process of planning evaluations, from determining what measures are used (e.g. quantitative and qualitative), and what is taken to constitute success, to reflexive learning based on outcomes. It covers formative and summative evaluation. The unit is useful for anyone working in community development, international development, policy development, governance, service delivery, criminology, public health and social research more generally.

Unit Information

A warm welcome to SOCS5501 Evaluation.
This unit is taught by Dr Rick Cummings. My background is in Anthropology, Evaluation, Public Policy, and Teaching and Learning. I have over 30 years of experience in designing and conducting evaluation studies of public programs and policies, particularly in Western Australia but also nationally and internationally for the World Bank. I am a Fellow of the Australian Evaluation Society and an Emeritus Professor in public policy at Murdoch University.  
I am teaching this unit on behalf of the regular coordinators - Farida Fozdar and Jeannette Taylor.  This Unit is based largely on the structure and content they have used in previous years with some additional input from me.  Thus you have the benefit of the combined research, experience writings and ideas of the three of us plus the wide range of our colleagues in diverse disciplines and countries.   This will provide a rich body of different examples to illustrate the variety of ways in which evaluation can be conducted and used. 
Evaluation is a structured process whereby the specific questions/issues related to the implementation and success of a social interventions, be they a project, program, policy, or organisation, can be assessed. The process involves identifying in discussion with stakeholders the key evaluation issues or questions, systematically obtaining data to answer these questions, analysing this information in order to provide evidence, findings and recommendations to these stakeholders to inform their decisions. These decisions are usually related to summative questions, i.e., judging the worth or merit of the intervention (accountability), or formative questions, i.e., assessing how well the entity is performing (improvement).  
Best practice requires that government, NGO or industry funded interventions include an evaluation component but this may vary considerable for each intervention and depending on the timeline for the intervention. This unit provides students with the skills and knowledge needed to assess the effectiveness and efficiency of social programs. It covers a range of approaches to evaluation, including formative and summative evaluation as well as other approaches. Students are taken through the process of gaining an understanding of the logic underlying a particular program or policy as well  planning, conducting and reporting evaluations, including identifying stakeholders, determining key evaluation issues, and deciding what data (e.g. quantitative and qualitative) will be collected by and from whom, and how it will be analysed and presented to stakeholders.  The unit will focus on the key issues of program improvement and accountability, what is taken to constitute success, organisational learning and evaluative thinking.  The unit will be useful for anyone working in community development, international development, policy development, service delivery, criminology, public health and social research more generally.
I want this Unit to be meaningful and useful to you. Students will come to this Unit from different disciplines and with interests in different issues and methodologies. I have tried to offer you opportunities to pursue these within the context of a broader understanding of evaluation approaches and methods.
I are looking forward to teaching the unit and getting to know you. Best of luck with the Unit and we hope you enjoy it.
Prof Rick Cummings

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
