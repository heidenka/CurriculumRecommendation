Unit Information

This unit is taught as an intensive short course and examines land open for mining in Western Australia, and native title; the constitutional framework; public and private ownership of minerals; history, theory and systems of resource disposition; the disposition regime of minerals with respect to exploration and production; review of warden's and ministerial decisions; dealings in resources titles; private royalty interests; state developer agreements and environmental aspects. The unit entails a comprehensive examination of the Mining Act of Western Australia. 

* As the unit is taught in seminars, none of the sessions are recorded.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

The unit is taught by means of a combination of interactive workshop seminars and tutorials and a variety of instructors. The workshop seminars provide the foundation for an understanding of the area, for which detailed materials are provided beforehand. The tutorials are then conducted by members of the legal profession who practice in the area of mining law. The tutorials further develop the skill in applying the principles of law expounded in the workshop seminars to practical problems.

Unit Structure

The unit provides an intensive course of study on Mining Law in Western Australia. It is designed to be a comprehensive review for lawyers, for professionals and business people, non-government organisations and government administrators working in or with Mining Law. 

It will comprise six sessions covering the range of topics indicated in the program. There will be a session each morning and afternoon comprising a workshop seminar(s) followed by a tutorial discussion of a problem raising the issues of law presented in the workshop seminar. Instructors and tutors will be drawn from experts in mining law from: the Centre Mining, Energy & Natural Resources Law and AMPLA (WA) .AMPLA is largely composed of members with a special interest and expertise in mining law.

Sessions are: 8:30am to 12:30pm and 1:30 to 5:00pm

PRE-READING:

The Course Reader is available for pre-reading on LMS four weeks before the commencement of the unit. Hardcopy will also be supplied.

The materials in the Course Reader are substantial, and parts 1 (Land Open and Ownership), part 2 (Allocation of Mineral Resources), and part 6 (Native Title) should be read prior to the commencement of the unit. They are the most relevant parts to the Opinion which constitutes 50% of the assessment in the unit, but can also be the subject of a Research Paper which comprises the other 50%.

Marking criteria:

The Opinion will be assessed upon the basis of: 

– recognition of the legal issues presented by the facts, 

– analysis of the legal issues, 

– application of the principles of law arising from the legal issues to the facts.

The main categories of issues are suggested to the student as a basis of organisation of the answer in the Opinion itself.

The marks are allocated amongst the issues according to the weight of the issues, along with a smaller fraction accorded to an overall assessment of communication, analysis and application of the legal principles.

Research papers are assessed against three main criteria: 

– quality of presentation and communication, (20%) 

– quality of analysis, including recognition of issues, (60%)

The overall mark is a composite of those elements but they necessarily are also interlinked and so the assessment in one element may influence the assessment in another element.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
