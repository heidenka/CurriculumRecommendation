Academic Information

Content This undergraduate unit provides an overview of leadership principles and approaches relevant to the health sector, and with a particular focus on population health. Leadership in this unit is defined broadly, and includes: leading as individuals; leadership in groups and teams; leadership in health organisations; and concepts of shared and collective leadership around priority health issues facing society. Different ways of describing and fostering the characteristics of effective leadership are reviewed. A distinction is made between management and leadership, and factors contributing to effective leadership in different contexts and settings are explored. The important role of interpersonal and communication skills in health leadership is considered, and the unit provides opportunities for student self-reflection and development of skills relevant to leadership. The unit explores a range of contemporary issues that impact on health leadership in the twenty-first century, both nationally and internationally. An introduction is provided to several other substantive topics relevant to health leadership, such as ethical leadership and the importance of good governance, leading change, and the challenges for leadership in the face of a health crisis.

Unit Information

Welcome to Health Leadership (PUBH3301).
This unit provides an overview of leadership principles and approaches relevant to the health sector, and with a particular focus on population health. There is a strong focus throughout the unit on  critically thinking about how to apply to leadership theory and skills taught to 'real world' health issues and contexts. 
Leadership in this unit is defined broadly, and includes: leading as individuals (and the importance of self-insight); leadership in groups and teams; leadership in health organisations; and concepts of shared and collective leadership around priority health issues facing society. Importantly for students, the unit emphasises that leadership is not contingent upon being at the top of an organisation, or one's job title, or being 'a boss' ; we can demonstrate good leadership from the outset of our careers, and in a range of contexts, not only related to where we work.    
Throughout the unit this semester, students will be encouraged to think about and apply leadership knowledge and principles to a range of contemporary issues that impact on health leadership in the twenty-first century, both nationally and internationally. The most recent example of this of course, is COVID-19, which continues to present enormous challenges for leadership globally, in Australia, and in the health sector. 
Learning Outcomes for PUBH3301:
When this unit has been completed, students should be able to:
(1)	Understand key styles of leadership and attributes of effective health leadership
(2)	Critically analyse contemporary issues in health leadership; 
(3)	Apply leadership principles to different health issues and contexts; 
(4)	Understand principles of facilitating and leading change;  
(5)	Understand issues related to accountability and ethical leadership
(6)	Enhance skills in interpersonal and written communication

Learning And Teaching Strategy

The learning approach in this unit is based on the premise of adult-education philosophy in which each student is an independent and self-motivated learner, with the role of the lecturer being one of facilitation and direction rather than the source of all knowledge. Notwithstanding this, students are encouraged to consult the lecturer if they are having difficulty with any aspect of the course. Throughout the unit students are encouraged to develop their ability to work both independently and collaboratively.  This is a third year unit, and it is expected that each student will take responsibility to engage in the unit as an independent and self-motivated learner. 
Part of my teaching and learning philosophy and style is to highlight the 'real world' relevance of the unit content to topical health and/or leadership issues in the news and/or facing society at present. This is incorporated into lecture and tutorial content, and this year I have also created a tab on LMS that will include each week a couple of examples of topical news relevant to health leadership  
The readings set for each week are an important complement to the lectures and tutorials so please keep up to date with these. There are a variety of materials included in the 'readings' for this unit and they have been reviewed and updated for this Semester to ensure currency and variety. Readings include peer reviewed journal articles, topical articles on leadership from online publications such as The Conversation,and links to relevant videos or TEDtalks. 
Another feature of my teaching philosophy and style is my strong belief that a lot of what we can learn about leadership (the 'good and the bad') will not necessarily come from articles or textbooks, but rather from the experiences and role modelled examples of people in health and other leadership roles. Hence the unit content incorporates case studies, vignettes and observations from a wide variety of people working in public health and related sectors, both within Western Australia and nationally. I have also had the recent privilege of interviewing some key people in leadership roles in WA, and asked them about their views and insights on many of topics included in this unit, and encouraged them to reflect on their own career journey and experiences of leadership.  Segments from these interviews have been incorporated into the learning materials for the unit this semester.

Unit Structure

This unit is taught through a combination of lectures, tutorials and readings (with readings including also video and other types of content).All of the content of the unit is examinable. 
As required by UWA for Semester 2 this year, there are no face-to-face lectures, and all lectures will be available on LMS. 
There are 12 lectures in total for the unit. 
There are 10 tutorials in total - most of these are scheduled as face to face (or zoom if pre-determined that a student(s) cannot attend face to face), but at least one tutorial involves an activity that can be done at home in your own time, with students then posting comments on a discussion board.      
The unit assessment comprises tutorial attendance and participation (10%), a major written assignment (40%) and a final exam (50%).

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
