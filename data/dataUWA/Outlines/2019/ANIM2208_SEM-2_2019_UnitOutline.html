Unit Information

This unit analyses critically, through a philosophical approach, the use of animals by human society. In particular it examines the use of animals in scientific research, agricultural production and natural resource management, and considers community attitudes relating to use of animals for food, and in sport and recreation. The unit provides students with the knowledge and skills to conduct a fair assessment of current and future problems arising from human animal interactions. The students examine current ideologies and scientific research that attempts to understand and improve animal welfare.

This course is not designed to teach you what is “right” or what is “wrong”, but to give you the knowledge and skills to conduct a fair assessment of Ethical and Welfare issues.

At the end of the course you will:

    Have acquired the basis of critical thinking
    Have sufficient knowledge of animal physiology, biology and behaviour to assess the well-being of animals
    Have an overview of a range situations where human-animal interactions are involved
    Have examined or re-examined your own and public attitudes to animal well-being
    Have developed an ability to comment and criticise the main aspects of animal Welfare and Ethics

Teaching and Learning Strategy

The unit is based on a face to face exchange during both lectures/workshop and tutorials.

The unit is very interactive and students are expected to express their opinions and debate their views.

During the first 4 weeks, students are coached and engaged in two activities to learn the debate mechanism and critical thinking. These communication skills and critical thinking skills are then practiced and refined during the debates, the group and individual case studies as well as during the lectures/workshops. Detailed feedback and group feedback is given for each activity. The class is engaged in the assessment of most of the activities so students can learn from their peers 

Students are expected to read pre-lecture material available online and discussed the topic during the lecture/workshop.

Assessment details

1. DEBATE

Activity 1: Thinking about animals – in words (5%)

During the first 2 weeks, you will take part in an online discussion group to present your arguments about the morality of human animal interactions. The posts will be reviewed and discussed during a tutorial.

Activity 2: Thinking about animals – live (5%)

From week 2 to 4, you will create a short video to present your arguments about an example of human animal interaction. The videos will be reviewed and discussed during a tutorial.

Activity 3: Debate (Tutorial time – 30%)

The class will be divided in tutorial groups of 18 to 24 students. Each student will be assigned to 2 debate teams (two different topics). You will be given at least two weeks of preparation using library, internet, and interviews of academics, professionals, members of organisations that play roles in the continuous debate on welfare. The debate rules and format will be explained during the first tutorial. Each student will participate to 2 debates (15%+15%) and will assess all the other debate (10%).

Debate format: 5 min per speaker, 3 for affirmative, 3 for negative

The rest of the class plus academic staff will be the audience
Assessment by the audience, an academic or/and an external participant.
Participation to the assessment of the debates (10%)


2. Group case study: This year theme:  Meat-free diet: implications and consequences.

Presenting facts (10%): You will form groups of 3 students and you will give a short presentation (10 min) to present facts about the assessment and improvement of animal welfare. The class will comment on your presentation (content and delivery), each student will be assessed.

Reporting facts and conclusions (15%): The same groups of 3 students will write a report to present facts about ethical and welfare issues related to their case study and a critical analysis. Each report will be assessed by the unit coordinator as a group.

Participation to the assessment of the case study presentation (5 %)


3.  Individual Case Study (take home exam - 20%)

A topic will be emailed to each student and you will have 72 h to return your case study report.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 10 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 30 out of 50 (a mark of 5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
