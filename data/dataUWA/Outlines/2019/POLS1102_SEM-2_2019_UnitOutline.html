Unit Information

This unit enables students to understand the nature of international politics. It looks at the structure of the international system, its evolution, current international political configuration, the role of developing states, and the emergence of various non-state actors in international politics. The ideas of order and justice, and the role of the United Nations, are explored with reference to a range of issues which may include alliances, regionalism, international law, environmental problems, human trafficking, nuclear proliferation, human rights, religion, ethnicity and terrorism. The unit aims to make students familiar with various approaches to the study of international relations and also to give students an appreciation of the complexity of decision making in the current world order. Overall, the unit provides the conceptual and methodological foundations that allows students to begin thinking like an international relations analyst.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

Teaching in this unit is conducted through weekly lectures and tutorials.

Lectures

There are two separate 45-minute lectures each week, totaling 24 lectures over the semester:

All lectures will be audio recorded by Lecture Capture System and made available after each class via the POLS1102 LMS (http://www.lms.uwa.edu.au/). However due to technical issues, some recordings may not be available after the lecture. We do not guarantee that these recordings will be available in every case. 
Attendance at lectures is not compulsory, but you are strongly advised and expected to attend regularly and participate in learning exercises. The object of lectures is to introduce students to key concepts and issues and to provide guidance for the exploration of these topics in the readings and tutorial discussions. 

Tutorials

You will also be taught in weekly tutorial groups of about 20 students. There is a total of 10 tutorials over the semester and you must enrol in one of the weekly tutorials. To enter your tutorial preference times, go to the On Line Class Registration (OLCR) website via Current Students: http://www.arts.uwa.edu.au/students/enrolment.

These groups discuss the tutorial topics listed later in this guide under the guidance of a tutor and provide you with the opportunity to ask questions about themes encountered in your reading and the lectures. Tutorial discussions are based on a combination of the lecture topics from the previous week. Tutorials provide a forum in which students can develop and test ideas, based on prior reading and reflection, in cooperation with their peers and under the supervision of a tutor. As a result of their participation in tutorials, students should enhance their abilities:
•	to respect and comprehend the point of view of others
•	to offer constructive reflection on the contributions of other students
•	to make well organized, well informed, clearly articulated and concise oral presentations
•	to work with a group in identifying issues, exploring alternative approaches to their resolution, and drawing conclusions

Participation in tutorials is compulsory. Please note that absence without explanation from tutorials will result in a penalty. Persistent absence may result in not being eligible to sit the final examination. Tutorials are not only a valuable learning experience, but can be enjoyable, so prepare fully for your tutorials to obtain the maximum benefit from them.

TUTORIAL AND READING GUIDE 

The unit is designed to encourage a close link between lectures and tutorials.  Students are urged to read from the materials under the relevant lecture topic before they attend tutorials. Students are also encouraged to analyze the readings; good students are those who read and think in the light of the reading, the lectures and the tutorials rather than confining themselves to one of these activities. Don’t be dismayed by the number of references provided; they are intended to ensure that each student can find relevant material in the Closed Reserve collection of the library. You are not expected to read all the books and articles/chapters suggested, but you are expected to do the Required Readings ( a minimum of two references), and may turn additionally to the Recommended Readings if they desire to pursue the topic further. 

Required Readings represent what is necessary for an intelligent understanding of the lectures, and to participate in the tutorial discussions.  

Recommended Readings can be used to supplement the required readings, especially if you are preparing a tutorial presentation or essay.  

All readings are located in the Humanities and Social Sciences Reserve Collection in the Library or, in the case of journal articles, are available on electronically through Reid Library via the POLS1102 LMS.


UNIT LEARNING OUTCOMES 

Upon completion of this unit, students are able to (1) identify and describe elements of continuity and change in contemporary world politics; (2) identify the types of actors participating in the contemporary international system and describe their characteristics; (3) demonstrate a basic understanding of how the interaction between these actors is regulated (or not regulated) by international norms and institutions; (4) identify, and explain the emergence of, a range of new issues in the realm of international politics; (5) demonstrate a basic understanding of the evolution of the academic field of international relations; (6) demonstrate a basic understanding of the concepts, theoretical frameworks and discourse conventions of the academic field of international relations; (7) conduct independent research on topics in international relations, demonstrating an understanding of the basic requirements of convincing argument in international relations, including the use of a range of relevant sources; and (8) as a result of 1 to 7, demonstrate the ability to proceed to the acquisition of specialised and advanced knowledge of the various subfields of international relations.

Tutors may call upon you to share your thoughts on the readings and failure to do so would impact on your final grade for tutorial participation.

We also encourage you to actively interact on Discussion Forum for your group, and for the whole class as well.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
