Academic Information

Content This unit introduces students to the principles of environmental research, and provides students exposure to the diversity of investigation methods relevant to environmental science. Students are given the opportunity to experience these research methods being applied to ‘real world' environmental issues. It is delivered through a mix of on-line and a face-to face sessions, which includes field visits. Module 1 focuses on the establishment of context and the formulation of research questions and hypotheses for testing, and examines potential tools available to test these hypotheses. Working in groups with a mentor, students will be exposed to selected environmental research projects, and will be asked to critique these examples in Module 2. Students have the opportunity to conduct their own environmental research project in Module 3 when they focus on data collection and analysis at a selected site, and will learn how best to communicate their findings. In 2019, the field trip will take place in Albany.

Teaching and Learning Strategy

This unit is taught through a mix of on-line and face-to-face sessions, and includes a field trip to the town of Albany on the South Coast of WA in the week before the mid-semester break. Students will undertake a group work (case study critique) and two individual (literature review and scientific report) assignments (assessment items).  For the on-line components, students are expected to work through the resources provided and complete the activities listed as per the recommended unit schedule.  Regular 'announcements' from the unit coordinator will remind students of deadlines and give guidance on what needs to be completed each week.

Unit Structure

The unit consists of three modules - Module 1: Context, research questions and tools, Module 2: Environmental research case studies, and Module 3: Environmental research in practice.  Each module has a number of components which students are expected to work through.  For example, Module 1 has three main components - "An introduction to the environmental research toolkit", "Establishing context - the literature review" and "Using statistics in environmental research".  Each component has a background section, a set of resources such as recorded lectures, reading material and videos, and a list of activities that students have to complete. Modules 1 and 2 have to be completed during teaching weeks 1-7.  Module 3 commences in teaching week 8 and continues until the end of semester.

Participation

Attendance at 'face-to-face' and Collaborate workshops and the field camp is compulsory.  Attendance at the 'hands on' statistics workshop is optional, but highly recommended if statistical skills are weak.

Referencing Requirements

There are no strict requirements on reference formatting. References can be in any style or format as long as the style is consistent. Where applicable, author(s) name(s), journal title/book title, chapter title/article title, year of publication, volume number/book chapter and the article number or pagination must be present.

Texts

There is no specific text for this unit, instead, there are a number of different sources that will be used as appropriate for each module. All of these will be made available to students via the LMS.

Other

Students will need to bring their laptop computers and a USB  'data stick' to the field camp to be held in Albany.  Please can you make sure that you have software loaded that will enable you to undertake some basic mapping and spatial analysis (e.g. QGIS or ARCGIS) and undertake statistical analyses (e.g. the R statistical software).

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 10 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 30 out of 50 (a mark of 5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
