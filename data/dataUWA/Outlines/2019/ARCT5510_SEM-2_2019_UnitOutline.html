Unit Information

This unit aims to develop an understanding of local and international higher density housing, with an emphasis on qualitative assessment, sustainability, adaptability, affordability and the limitations and opportunities arising from prescribed densities, regulations and codes. Students will be equipped with methods for understanding, critically evaluating and communicating qualitative, environmental, spatial and regulatory issues relating to higher density housing design.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

This is a seminar-based elective unit offered to students in the Master of Architecture degree.  It focuses on the transformation of the middle suburbs of Australian cities that is occurring as a result of higher density residential infill.  We will be asking whether and how we can do it better.

Unit Structure

Seminars will run for three hours each week from Weeks 1-11 inclusive. Seminars will consist of a combination of invited guest presentations, individual student presentations and individual student responses. 

Invited guest presentations will take place from 10-11:30 am and will cover individual, organisational and statutory approaches to innovation and good outcomes for higher density housing. Attendance is compulsory. 
RESEARCH COMPONENT ONE: Student seminar presentations and responses will take place from 11:30am - 1:00pm and students will be allocated both a week to present in and a week to lead a response. Attendance by all students is compulsory. Each student will present a considered verbal, graphic and written analysis of a single project to be allocated during Week One.  All completed analyses are to be placed on LMS/Dropbox so that all students can access.  
A nominated response to a presentation from another student is required from each student as a component of the seminar participation.
The projects are to be presented in a standard graphic format which will be outlined in Week One – but, to enable fuller understanding of the project, other graphic material should be used: photographs and drawings of the project and its setting, and any additional graphic tools that you consider will help describe your analysis.  Presentations should be made in Powerpoint or PDF.
Your presentation will be assessed on the basis of the depth and clarity of your analysis of the projects’ characteristics, strengths and weaknesses, and typological applicability to Perth; and the graphic quality of your standard format submission.
Each student will have up to 30 minutes to present their projects.  One student will be nominated as the initial respondent for each presenter, with group discussion to follow.  While each student will be responsible for leading one response, all will be responsible for raising questions and participation in discussion.
Your presentation is to be submitted to the LMS/Dropbox one week after your presentation, allowing you to make modifications in response to seminar discussion.
This work will be a ready-for-publication graphic analysis of your allocated higher density housing project.  
In addition to the common format drawings, students will write a succinct analytical description of the project (a maximum of 1000 words) and complete a simple data sheet for the project that records address, description of context, site area, number of dwellings, dwelling areas, circulation areas, amenity areas, number of cars. 
RESEARCH COMPONENT TWO: The Major Project for the semester asks you to respond to the question: 
How do you propose protecting the liveability of established middle-suburban residential areas while enabling infill?  
Use, as examples to illustrate the points you make, the case study you presented in the seminar, together with two other relevant examples that you select from your colleague’s presentations.  
It is important that you draw on the information presented during the talk series that formed the first part of this unit of study, acknowledging sources in the conventional manner.  
The submission will take the form of an essay with illustrations and analytical diagrams.  The word count should be approximately 3,000.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
