Academic Information

Content This unit consists of a weekly seminar with an honours coordinator. Some of the seminar sessions examine major aspects of developing a research project (in this case the honours dissertation): formulating a research question in relation to existing knowledge; developing a formal research proposal; supporting claims; structuring the dissertation; and advanced academic style. Students have the opportunity early in the semester to receive feedback on their draft research proposal from their peers in a supportive environment.

Teaching and Learning Strategy

This unit is taught through a weekly seminar, addressing social science research skills and especially developing your own Honours research project. To that end, the unit comprises a series of indicative and summative assessments, culminating in your Honours research proposal. The unit thus complements the research support you will be receiving from your supervisor.

Unit Structure

The unit is taught through a weekly 2 hour seminar, including some content provided by the unit coordinator, peer discussion and workshops. In some weeks, the seminar will be replaced by scheduled discipline-specific meetings.

Participation

Seminar attendance and participation in the form of discussion and presentation of work in progress is the essential foundation for this unit. Students will discuss and present their honours thesis proposals-in-progress and other assignments in order to receive feedback on their work from their peers and the unit coordinator. The participation component of the mark will be awarded for your participation in these discussions and workshops, including your response to feedback and the contributions you make in providing feedback for other students' work.

Referencing Requirements

For your written assignments in this unit, you should follow the precise referencing format and guidelines required for your Honours dissertation itself.

At the Honours level, the expectations regarding academic conduct and proper citation of your sources are of a high standard. Academic integrity is defined in the University Poliy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled. Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.

Texts

In addition to reading extensively for developing your own thesis proposal and literature review, specific readings are assigned as preparation for many of the seminars. Several of these are chapters from the book The Craft of Research (Booth et al 2016). The most recent (4th) edition of this book is available from the Reid Library in both hardcopy and as an ebook.

Wayne C. Booth, Gregory G. Colomb, & Joseph M. Williams, Joseph Bizup, and William T. FitzGerald (2016) The Craft of Research 4th Edition. The University of Chicago Press: Chicago.

Other

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 10 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 30 out of 50 (a mark of 5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.
