This unit uses face to face delivery strategy. The lectures, tutorials, and computer labs of this unit are a joint responsibility of the teaching team and students. Students are responsible for managing their own learning progress. The lecture recording and slides are posted on LMS after each lecture. Unit Structure

The unit consist of 11 2-hour lectures, 9 1-hour computer labs, and 9 1-hour tutorials.

1)	Lectures

The unit will consist of one 2-hour lecture per week, which will be delivered by different lecturers (see unit schedule). These lectures are meant to guide students’ reading of the weekly class material. Students are strongly urged to attend the lectures.

The lecture series includes a number of guest lectures, in which students are introduced to practical decision support tools that are being used by policy makers. The guest lecturers are experienced modellers who work with State government departments. Guest lectures demonstrate how models are used to solve real world problems, and will help students prepare for their future career. Students are strongly encouraged to attend the guest lectures, not only to learn but also out of politeness to the guest lecturers!

2)	Tutorials

The unit includes one 1-hour class-based tutorial per week. In these tutorials, we will practice mathematical assignments and discuss literature. The tutorial exercises will help you understand the steps in model development, linear programming, and evaluation of model types in different situations. Students are expected to read the required material prior to class, and to participate in class activities. A weight will be allocated for completion of the tutorial assignments (see Assessment).

In weeks with quizzes, the quiz will be conducted during the tutorials. Please refer to the Unit Schedule for quiz starting times.

3)	Computer labs

The unit also includes one 1-hour computer laboratory session per week. These give students an opportunity to develop and practice their computing and modelling skills.
In these labs, you will practice the techniques learned during the lectures to develop your own decision support tools. These computer labs provide you with useful work-skills needed to complete the individual assignment. A weight will be allocated for completion of the computer labs (see Assessment).

Participation

•	Students are expected to prepare for, and attend, the tutorial sessions. A 5% mark will be given for participation and submission of work in at least six out of eight tutorials. You have to email the preparatory assignment before the relevant tutorial to the lecturer.

These tutorials will receive a grade (pass, attempt or fail). To pass, your answers need to clearly demonstrate that you are prepared for the class. If you do not present meaningful work, you will not receive a pass – even if you handed something in. You are expected to come prepared.

The tutorial exercise will be posted on LMS several days before each tutorial. Solutions to these tutorial exercises are discussed during the tutorials. Even if you are unable to attend a tutorial, to get counted the work needs to be submitted to your lecturer by email before the relevant tutorial. 

•	Participation in the computer labs is compulsory and will help you prepare for the quizzes. A 5% mark will be given for participation in least seven out of nine computer labs. You will have to hand in your completed computer lab after the relevant lab (either physically or by email).

If the student is unable to attend the lab or does not complete an exercise during the lab hours, the work has to be submitted to your lecturer by email before 9am of the day after the relevant computer lab.
Solutions to the computer lab assignments will be posted on LMS after the 9am deadline. If you have not submitted your lab work before then, you will not receive a pass mark.

•	The unit assessment includes two numerical quizzes (worth 25% each) to test mathematical and modelling skills (learning objectives #3 and #4). Each quiz will be one hour long. Please note the starting times in the unit schedule on pages 11-12.

•	A final modelling project is a major component of this unit. In this project, students will evaluate an environmental management problem and build a simple decision support tool about their chosen issue. A report will be due on the 1st of November.

Details of the project and the final report will be posted in a dedicated folder on LMS. You have a chance to discuss your final project ideas in weeks 9 and 10.

Other

Adjustment of marks

Quizzes, labs, and tutorials contribute 60 percent of the final unit grade. Processes have been established that seek to achieve consistency in marking approach across markers. However, a final moderation and scaling process exists to address marking anomalies. Additionally, the School Board of Examiners meeting has the power to request changes to the mark distribution for units. The grounds for intervention are typically a high fail rate in a unit; the proportion of High Distinction and Distinction marks is above the mandated policy setting; or a lack of dispersion in the mark distribution. The unit moderation process, and the role of the Board of Examiners means that your final mark can, and generally will be different from a calculation based on the raw marks that are posted on the LMS.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 10 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 30 out of 50 (a mark of 5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
