Academic Information

Content This unit explores and critically analyses the international politics of Africa, drawing on historical developments to examine contemporary dynamics. The first part of the unit examines key historical and conceptual contexts—including the slave trade, colonialism, the Cold War and the global political economy—that have shaped and help us to understand contemporary African states and their regional and international relationships. The second part explores key relationships between African states, the African Union, and major external actors like the US, EU, China and the UN, examining key themes including regional integration, economic development, natural resources, counter-terrorism and peacekeeping. The third part examines the positions of African actors, and their impacts, on key issues on the global agenda, including development, trade, climate change, and global governance reform.

Teaching and Learning Strategy

There is no set textbook in this unit. You are however required to have read the three ‘Required Readings’ (journal articles/book chapters) under the relevant topic before you attend corresponding tutorials. As Level 3 students, it is expected that you would have the capacity and motivation to read and make sense of three weekly readings. Required Readings are necessary to enable an active and intelligent discussion in tutorials. ‘Further Readings’ can be used to supplement the required readings, especially for assessment preparation. For the Research Essay, it is expected that you will locate and consult additional sources through your own research. All weekly Required Readings will be available online via the unit’s LMS page.

As lecturer and tutor, my aim in the classroom is always to present a clear and comprehensive understanding of topics. I consider teaching and learning as one of the most effective processes of social progress. Therefore, I provide my students with a clear understanding and appreciation of the central political interests and dynamics at play in the social world. Students are thus encouraged to make their own judgements about the real world. I do this in a way that develops my students’ ability to be independent thinkers.

I believe a good teacher should be able to tell the relative strength of students and devise means of making students achieve the optimal experience in the teaching and learning process. Accordingly, I have a non-hierarchical approach to teaching and learning. A friendly and freer classroom environment is created for effective teaching and learning when the teacher learns (or is seen to learn) from students as much as students learn from the teacher.

I also identify the strengths and interests of my students and combine generalized and personalized teaching and learning methods to ensure that none of my students feels left behind. I tell stories, use analogies and strike everyday examples to bring my class in sync with the topic of the day. This means all students leave the classroom haven participated in and benefitted from sessions, irrespective of their strengths and or social and academic skills. Where necessary, I refer students who require extra support to available students learning and support services.

Importantly, my teaching has a social and people-centred agenda, from a neutral and balanced perspective. With my primary expertise in African politics and international relations, I bring my students to appreciate how interconnected the world is, and how a positive impact anywhere yields positive dividends everywhere. My classroom is, therefore, a place where I inspire students to learn, research, work and engage in the field of Political Science and
International Relations, with an eye on making the world a better place.

Teaching should also be carefully organised and coherently structured, with clear aims and a plan for achieving desired results. Assessments must be carefully considered and appropriately linked to other components of teaching. I believe that classes should be informative, interesting and inspiring, with relevant and contemporary subject material examined. Students should also be exposed to a diversity of political, cultural and social positions and theories when considering topics to enable a more holistic understanding of social and political phenomena.

As a result of participation in tutorials, students should enhance their abilities:

Participation

Tutorial preparation and participation: In addition to the required readings, each student should also find, download, print and bring to class one copy of a recent, short online news-media item relating to the particular tutorial topic for that week (for example, it could be a BBC, al Jazeera or African media story on a new development in a particular African conflict that the African Union is addressing, etc). Be prepared to introduce this example or development into the tutorial discussion as one of your contributions to that class, suggesting how it relates to the broader topic under discussion (this will be a little more indirect for some of the initial weeks, which examine historical contexts). The articles will, in turn, help the class to relate the broader themes of the unit to contemporary issues and developments on the African continent. This will also count towards your tutorial participation mark. Please submit the news item, with your name hand-written on the top, to your tutor at the end of each tutorial.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 10 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 30 out of 50 (a mark of 5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.

Special Considerations

  • being a victim of a crime
  • breakdown of relationship
  • sudden loss of income or employment
  • serious disruption to domestic arrangements
