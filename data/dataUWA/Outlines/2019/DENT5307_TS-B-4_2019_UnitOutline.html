Unit Information

The modules in this unit equip students with the theoretical knowledge and clinical competencies for comprehensive management of complex patients.

Learning outcomes
Students are able to (1) formulate an initial diagnosis and initial case assessment; (2) evaluate treatment options and discuss these clearly with the patient; (3) formulate a definitive treatment program; (4) implement all stages of the treatment program; (5) evaluate the outcome of treatment on completion of the treatment program against the initial stated objective and at developing; (6) implement a future clinical pathway for the long-term monitoring and maintenance of the case; (7) explain aesthetic principles, such as unity and variety, balance, emphasis and focal point, rhythm, scale and proportion; (8) describe the basic colour theories; (9) select the shade for the restorations; and (10) correct extrinsic and intrinsic staining of teeth.

 There are 2 modules described in this unit Complex Oral Rehabilition 2 and Dento-facial Aesthetics.  For practical purposes, the teaching and assessments in these 2 modules have been combined.  However on the timetable and lecture schedule, you may notice that there may be reference to both of these modules.

Complex oral rehabilitation 2 specific learning outcomes
Understand and demonstrate comprehensive patient care, formulate a diagnosis from an initial case assessment, apply appropriate adjunctive diagnostic steps, evaluate treatment options and discuss these clearly with the patient.

Recognise when a patient's treatment needs exceed one's own skills/knowledge level and refer appropriately.

Formulate a definitive treatment plan, implement all stages of the treatment plan, evaluate the outcome of treatment on completion of the treatment plan against the initial stated objective

Develop and implement a future clinical pathway for the long term monitoring and maintenance of the case. For this, you will be given a pool of patients requiring comprehensive care and you will be expected to complete their cases over the duration of the third year.

Demonstrate an understanding of restorative techniques and principles of tooth replacement.

Demonstrate a comprehensive understanding of dental materials used for direct and indirect restorations and prostheses, ie; in a patient's comprehensive care.

Explain dental laboratory techniques and methods of fabricating indirect restorations and prostheses.

Evaluate dental laboratory work and identify faults and errors, explaining the impact these have on clinical outcomes. 
Define tooth structure loss, understanding its multifactorial aspects and principles of monitoring and management. Explain the principles of occlusion and how it relates to the restoration of teeth and oral rehabilitation.

Recognise and describe oral dysfunction including problems with mastication, speech and aesthetics. Communicate effectively and sensitively with staff, patients and peers.

Demonstrate effective information literacy skills including an assessment of scientific literature and texts. Demonstrate appropriate record keeping.

Demonstrate legal responsibility and ethical, professional behaviour.

Laboratory and Technical Work By Dental Students
Students are expected to have a level of technical understanding and skill to support the clinical work they are undertaking. Where assistance is required for laboratory and technical procedures this support will be provided by a combination of UWA Dental School/OHCWA staff in conjunction with external laboratories contracted to OHCWA where appropriate. Where technical work is undertaken by individual students, under supervision, priority is on completing the work in a safe manner to a high standard.

 
Progress Monitors and Laboratory Work Authorization
DMD3 Progress monitor forms and laboratory work authorization sheets are a quality check and feedback mechanism to ensure appropriate clinical and laboratory stages are undertaken and to communicate with the laboratory, respectively. Progress monitors record both clinical and laboratory stages of any indirect clinical procedures.

Laboratory work authorisation assessment sheets must be signed in and out of the laboratory by appropriate staff and retained for collation at the end of the year.

It is each student's responsibility to seek signatures, compiling them in a logbook and submitting these forms following completion of treatment or at the end of the clinical year.

No work may enter the clinic until this has been assessed as satisfactory by the authorised laboratory staff member.

Failure to submit correctly signed progress monitors and worksheets is a serious breach of protocol and will render loss of credit for the relevant clinical case. This has implications for failure of the clinical component of this unit.

 

Remediation/ Feedback
Feedback is provided by the unit coordinator and/or the module coordinator on a one on one basis or as a group on assignments within four weeks of deadline for submission with the exception of summative examination at the end of the unit. Students choosing to discuss his/her performance should make an appointment to see the unit coordinator within four weeks after the release of marks/grades. The purpose of feedback will be to identify what remediation strategies can help the student to improve.

Remediation in essential generic skills and knowledge may be offered to poorly performing students to specific learning outcomes that have been identified by the unit or module coordinator as not having been achieved as early in the unit/course as possible and student progress is monitored. Remediation can be in the form of supervised clinical work, written assignments, independent study as and when required. Remediation is not a reassessment and does not affect the student's marks.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 10 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 30 out of 50 (a mark of 5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
