Academic Information

Content This unit continues on from INDO1401 Indonesian 1, introducing students to contemporary spoken and written Indonesian. Students develop all four skill areas (speaking, listening, writing and reading) in Indonesian, with an emphasis on oral skills. The unit also aims to familiarise students with some of the ways in which Indonesian culture and ways of living (e.g. eating, addressing people, using the bathroom) are different from those commonly found in Australia. A larger, intangible but very important objective is to kindle an interest in Indonesia, its people and their cultures. The main objective is for students to develop 'basic transactional proficiency' in Indonesian or Level 1 as described in the International Second Language Proficiency Ratings (Elaine Wylie and D. E. Ingram, Griffith University, 1995).

HOME STUDY
Non-contact or home study consists of the following:
Preparation for Contact Sessions
You are required to prepare all the exercises and readings listed in your Unit Outline and announced by lecturers BEFORE each session. It is essential that you prepare for each class in order to participate in and derive maximum benefit from the activities. Your degree of preparation will be noted by the lecturer and will contribute to your mark for participation. Where there are specific exercises to prepare, these are mentioned in the outline. This preparation is assessed.  As you are preparing for class, note down any questions you have that you can ask the lecturer about in class.  A good principle to remember is that if you don’t understand something, you will not be the only one. Nobody is going to accuse you of asking dumb questions!

Study for in class Translation, Writing, Tasks and Quizzes 
This unit is intensive so you have to keep working at it if you want to keep up. It is very important that you study for your translation, writing, quizzes and tests from Week 1. You will need to maintain sound study habits throughout the semester. Language learning requires ongoing, steady learning. Studying in bursts now and then places too much strain on the memory and is, therefore, far less efficient and effective than continuous study.  Remember that your marks in continuous assessment will reflect the effort you make and that it represents a large component of your overall assessment for the semester. All three Tasks (Weeks 4, 7 and 10) and Quizzes (in Weeks 5 and 9)are held in class time. 

Homework 
Every week there is homework set, but most of it is intended to be for your own benefit and is not directly assessed. There are some occasions through the semester when you have to hand in your homework for correction but not marks – apart from the contribution they make to your assessment mark (in Weeks 3, 6, 8 and 11). These are normally writing exercises designed to practice using grammatical forms that you have been learning in class.  
Under the title ‘Listening practice from Purple Book’ (under ‘Weekly Learning Material’) on the unit website I have included a selection of audio files relating to the Asyik Berbahasa Indonesia textbook. I strongly recommend that you listen to these each week because they will help your listening skills and your pronunciation. 

Additional Study and Practice
You need to master the language elements presented in the course. This means you need to be able to understand them in print and spoken form and to reproduce them in writing and speech. To ensure mastery, you will need to do additional practice and study; at university level, this means independent study. The Ellen Rafferty (et al.) textbooks provide additional activities that will help to consolidate the language patterns and vocabulary.  You will find audio files to accompany the lessons in both Ayo Berbahasa Indonesia and Asyik Berbahasa Indonesia at: www.indonesiantextbooks.wisc.edu/ These recordings will help supplement our classroom work, so bookmark it!

Unit Structure

There are four contact sessions as listed above.  There are no lectures as such. All classes are interactive language classes. Where it is necessary to introduce a new language element the lecturer will spend some time talking to the class, but the majority of each class consists of work in pairs or groups doing exercises or role-plays. This is mean to give you the maximum opportunity to practice speaking conversational Indonesian. There are always opportunities to ask questions when something is unclear. 
There is no fixed pattern as far as content is concerned, but the tasks and quizzes do follow a regular cycle. 

Each week you will do a selection of lessons from Let’s Speak Indonesian (volumes 1 and 2 – known as the ORANGE BOOK) and Indonesian Grammar in Context (volume 2 – known as the PURPLE BOOK) 

Tasks will always be held on Mondays while quizzes are always set on Tuesdays. Where there is homework for submission this will always be for submission in the Tuesday classes.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 10 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 30 out of 50 (a mark of 5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
