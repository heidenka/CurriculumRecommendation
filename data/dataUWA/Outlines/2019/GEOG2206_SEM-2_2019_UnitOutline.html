Academic Information

Content This unit further develops key concepts and principles in human geography and planning by examining how socio-spatial differences (in terms of socioeconomic status, ethnicity, gender and age) manifest within urban contexts, and how planners and policymakers respond. It provides students with an opportunity to critically reflect on the core theoretical perspectives in social geography and planning, and how these have been applied in different geographical contexts. Students are also introduced to analytical techniques such as indices of spatial segregation, as well as critical interpersonal tools for effective practice in urban policy and planning.

Unit Information

This unit is concerned with exploring and understanding inequality and diversity spatially. It explores how these concepts emerge and persist between (and within) groups and communities that lay claim to the various social, economic and physical spaces and places. It also examines how planners and social policy officers respond to these inequalities and diversities. 

In the context of an increasingly ‘connected’ and intersecting global society, different communities and (interest) groups often have different needs, wants and aspirations because of certain identity and status markers (e.g. socio-economic status, ethnic background, gender, age, religious beliefs and customs etc). Such differences invariably generate important questions for social geographers and their ‘applied’ fields of practice. In order to address them we need to develop a deeper understanding of the social and cultural world(s) that we, and others inhabit and influence. 

The unit examines three forms of social difference and diversity of interest to geographers: ethnicity, class (socio-economic status), and age. We will examine how difference and inequality within and across these social groupings ‘play out’ spatially in urban contexts. We will explore case studies regarding key issues such as poverty and locational disadvantage, multiculturalism and ethnic concentration, urban Aboriginality, and demographic variation, and ask ‘how should/do planners and social policy initiatives respond to this complexity?’ Students will also be introduced to analytical techniques and demographic analysis.

Teaching and Learning Strategy

This unit includes a mix of key note lectures and laboratory sessions focussed on core social geography and planning concepts and case studies prior to the mid-semester break. Following the break, the unit pivots to focus on the development of a series of real-world technical skills in social planning. Attendance at lab sessions and the fieldtrip is mandatory for the unit and students are expected to attend all lectures in person (except where they have unpreventable timetabling clashes), as the laboratory sessions closely follow and build upon the lecture content. 

The fieldtrip will take place on Tuesday 3rd Sept from 1-5pm.

Unit Structure

The core content for this unit will be delivered in Weeks 1-8 of Semester through one two-hour lecture, and one hands-on laboratory session (which both commence in week 1). In the first 8 weeks, these lab sessions provide case-studies and scenario-based learning to flesh out the key concepts discussed in lectures, and along with lecture content, will be assessed in the mid-semester exam and the major essay. Following the break, lecture and lab content build towards the final major report. You must attend your allocated lab session (there is a cap on the size of these laboratory classes to facilitate effective teaching and learning and attendance will be taken).

Referencing Requirements

Your essay and major report should be submitted through the LMS. Please adhere to the following formatting requirements:
1.	DO NOT include your name on your essay or the electronic files for submission. Instead, save your document with your student number as the file name. 
2.	The maximum word limit includes everything except references, tables and captions. Include a word count directly the below the title on your assignment. See details in the section below for information about UWA’s standard policy for penalties that apply when you exceed the word limit. 
3.	Use 12pt Times New Roman font with single line spacing.  
4.	Proof read and spell check your essay. 
5.	Use direct quotes sparingly (definitely no more than 10% of total word length) and ensure that all direct quotes (or paraphrased sections) should be properly and fully acknowledged.
6.	Include a reference list at the conclusion of the paper. If you also include web-based sources, ensure that they are reputable. Some potentially useful websites and journals are listed under the ‘Unit Resources’ section of the Unit Outline (available on the LMS).  Ensure that your assignments are all referenced using the APA referencing style. A full description, explanation, and examples of the APA referencing style is available on the library's website at: http://guides.library.uwa.edu.au/c.php?g=324904&p=2809540

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 10 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 30 out of 50 (a mark of 5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
