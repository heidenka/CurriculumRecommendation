Academic Information

Content The project is a capstone experience in which students integrate and apply knowledge and skills acquired in earlier units to a substantial real-world design challenge relevant to their discipline. Students work in teams, typically with four to six members, and are supervised by an academic and/or industry mentor. Teams are responsible for managing their project as well as reporting against milestones and preparing the necessary design documentation. The academic objectives of the project are to lead students through a systematic engineering design and synthesis process and to better prepare them for professional practice as engineers. The focus of this unit is on teamwork skills and the design process.

Unit Information

This unit covers the following: mine design process; ultimate pit design and scheduling, design of the mine infrastructure; equipment selection; production capacity, cut-off grades, scheduling; and budgeting and basics of mine economics.
The project is a capstone experience in which you integrate and apply knowledge and skills acquired in earlier units to a substantial real-world design challenge relevant to their discipline. You will work in teams, typically with three to five members, and are supervised by an academic mentor. Teams are responsible for managing their project as well as reporting against milestones and preparing the necessary design documentation. The academic objectives of the project are to lead students through a systematic engineering design and synthesis process and to better prepare them for professional practice as engineers. The focus of this unit is on teamwork skills and the design process.

Participation

Peer review of individual contributions to group assignments
You will be required to provide marks on the performance of your team members. Everyone will be given 100 marks to distribute to their fellow team members (not including themselves). These 100 marks may be split in any way possible, provided exactly 100 marks are distributed. For instance, in a team of 4 students, some examples of the way in which one student might distribute the marks to their 3 team members are:
-	40, 30 and 30
-	45, 35 and 20
-	34, 33 and 33
-	65, 20 and 15
-	Etc.

If every student performs exactly equally, then every student will receive 100 marks, so that is the “average” performance. So each individual’s total “number of marks received” will be used to adjust team marks into individual marks using the following formula:
		
Individual mark = Team mark * Number of marks received / 100

For example, a team receives 70% for their team report. There are 4 students in the team, so a total of 400 marks allocated. 
1.	Student 1 receives a total of 80 marks from their team members, so gets an  individual mark of 70 x 80/100 = 56%
2.	Student 2 receives a total of 100 marks from their team members, so gets an individual mark of 70 x 100/100 = 70%
3.	Student 3 receives a total of 105 marks from their team members, so gets an individual mark of 70 x 105/100 = 73.5%
4.	Student 4 receives a total of 115 marks from their team members, so gets an individual mark of 70 x 115/100 = 80.5%

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 10 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 30 out of 50 (a mark of 5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
