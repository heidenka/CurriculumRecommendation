Academic Information

Content This unit prepares students for a fast changing world of media enterprise and creative labour by introducing them to different ways of thinking about media work and the media and communication industries. The unit examines the different social, cultural, organisational, regulatory and ethical aspects of a changing and 'disrupted' industry landscape. Students undertake a case study or project relevant to their own field of interest and develop critical and practical skills in research design, project management, and other common activities used in the contemporary professional working environment.

Teaching and Learning Strategy

This unit invites you to bring theory and practice together in the context of thinking about the current ‘disrupted’ state of media enterprise and creative labour. ‘Media enterprise’ refers here to different ways of thinking about media work and the media and communication industries. But it also encompasses thinking about your own efforts and transition beyond university. So the unit will also include a component of career planning and strategizing. This double meaning of the term ‘media enterprise’ means that you should ideally put yourself into the subject matter and use the assessments to follow your own areas of interest. Because everyone’s areas of interest will be different this will involve a degree of independent learning and research. 

This unit is delivered in a lecture/tutorial format. Attendance at the lectures will help focus tutorial discussion and students are requested to make special efforts to attend guest lectures, as those professionals give up time to prepare and deliver their presentation. 

The responsibilities of the unit coordinator are to provide contemporary relevant resources to students and to ensure the assessments enhance student learning and unit outcomes. 

The responsibilities of the tutor are to ensure a safe, inclusive, fair and productive learning environment. Their role is to facilitate discussion not lecture students. Their role is also to assess work according to university policy. 

Tutorials rely on productive engagement between participants, so a responsibility of students is to strive towards productive and respective participation, based on a thorough understanding of the learning materials presented. If you have any concerns about the conduct of tutorials please discuss this with your tutor in the first instance or the Unit coordinator. 

This unit operates through close engagement with and reading of texts. Students have a responsibility to remain engaged with the conceptual frameworks underlying the unit. Students must come to class fully prepared, ready to discuss the readings. Careful note-taking is advised.

Participation is assessed through three quizzes in this unit. Failure to undertake any of the quizzes impacts on your score for participation.

Referencing Requirements

Work submitted in the unit is expected to adhere to the Media and Communication Discipline Style Guide which is available on the LMS. As per the discipline style guide: • Use Harvard referencing style. http://guides.library.uwa.edu.au/friendly.php?s=harvard • Use one and a half or double spacing for text. • Text should be 12pts and an easy to read font such as Times New Roman, Arial or Calibri. • Denote new paragraphs by indenting the first line with the tab key. • Do not leave line spaces between paragraphs. • Leave one space after full stops (between sentences). • Use page numbers to ensure that your work stays in order. • Provide word counts where requested. Word counts are firm. There is no 10% lee-way. Please include a word count on your assignment. For written pieces, especially essays, word limit counts should include all quotations and in-text references but exclude footnotes and references/bibliography. Information central to your argument should not be in footnotes and abuse of footnotes (i.e. containing long quotes) will be noted. An essay or report should have more than 10 academic references, drawing on relevant sources and used in a meaningful way. An exegesis submitted alongside a creative piece should have a minimum of 4 academic references, used in a meaningful way. The creative piece should demonstrate your engagement with these sources.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 10 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 30 out of 50 (a mark of 5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
