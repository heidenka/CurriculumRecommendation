Academic Information

Content This unit builds on the Level 2 units BIOC2001 Biochemistry and Molecular Biology of the Cell and BIOC2002 Biochemical Regulation of Cell Function. The topics of protein targeting, signal transduction and the life cycle of cells—growth, differentiation, cancer and cell death—are covered. Mechanisms of metabolic regulation, the role of enzymes and energy generation (which explains how cells cope with environmental changes and stress) complete the theory part of the unit. The laboratory practical component encompasses tissue culture and qualitative and quantitative analysis of changes in gene expression on cell stimulation and/or differentiation. The opportunity to engage in a research project in a research laboratory may be offered (depending on staff availability) to students who are enrolled in a Biochemistry and Molecular Biology major and also enrolled in this unit and BIOC3003 Omics—Global Approaches to Cell Function. Students who have achieved an average grade of 65 per cent or more in their second-year Biochemistry and Molecular Biology unit(s) are eligible to apply. The research project runs for about 10 weeks and replaces the teaching laboratory components of this unit BIOC3003 Omics—Global Approaches to Cell Function. Students undertaking a research project still do the laboratory quiz and the mark for their written report on the project replaces the laboratory report written by students taking the teaching laboratory component.

Teaching and Learning Strategy

The theory part of the unit comprises 2 lectures per week, where the lecture series is divided into themes of 3-5 lectures. This part of the unit is supported by presentations from students of scientific journal articles roughly related to the thematic structure of the lectures. This not only assists students in gaining  a deeper understanding of some aspects of the themes but also gives them experience in analysing, summarising and audiovisually presenting parts of scientific articles. The teaching laboratory component of this unit two blocks of experiments (2 weeks and 3 weeks) focussed on different aspects of cellular biochemistry, which also illustrate different techniques applied in this area of biochemistry and molecular biology. Some students who have applied and been accepted to perform research projects in research laboratories within the School of Molecular Sciences will spend approximately 9-10 weeks on these projects instead of undertaking the teaching laboratory components of BIOC3005 and BIOC3003.

Unit Structure

Lectures: 2 per week in G33 of the Bayliss Building; Tuesdays at 11am and Thursdays at 8am. Material in the lectures will be examined in the mid-semester exam (Lectures 1-6) and the remaining lecture material will be examined in an examination at the end of semester in the examination period in November.
Journal Clubs: In Room 130 of the Bayliss Building; all students to attend one of the sessions on Monday 29th July. Students will be put into groups of 4 (A1-5, B1-5, C1-5, D1-5; see DRAFT Journal Club Timetable). Each group of 4 students will attend a Journal Club Preparation session one week before they give their presentations e.g. Groups A1, B1, C1 and D1 will attend preparation sessions on August 5th at 8am, 11am, 12 noon or 1pm, respectively, students not in these groups do not attend  the preparation session. All students should attend one of the sessions (8am, 11am, 12noon or 1pm) on all five of the days when presentations are timetabled. Each student will be assessed on their own presentation of a section of a set research article.
Laboratory Practicals: Students attending practicals in the teaching laboratory (Bayliss Building 131) will attend from 10am - 5pm on Thursday or Friday in weeks 2-5 of semester and for a 2 hour period as will be timetabled on Thursday 1st or Friday 2nd August in week 1. Students undertaking research projects will spend approximately the same overall amount of time doing practical work as the other students, but the timing of the practical work will be determined in consultation with their supervisor. Note that the research projects form the practical parts of both BIOC3005 and BIOC3003 and thus continue through the semester. The assessments of the laboratory component of BIOC3005 comprise a generic online laboratory quiz (all students) and a lab report (teaching laboratory course) or a research report (research project students).

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 10 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 30 out of 50 (a mark of 5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.

Special Considerations

If your study has been adversely affected by illness or other significant circumstances outside of your control, you can apply for special consideration. Significant circumstances may include but are not limited to:

  • sudden loss of income or employment
  • serious disruption to domestic arrangements
