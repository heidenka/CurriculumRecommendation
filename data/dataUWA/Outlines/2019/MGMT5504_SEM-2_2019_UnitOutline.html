Academic Information

Content This unit introduces data analysis and decision-making tools that students are able to use to manage their own day-to-day work. Students are able to identify situations in which quantitative analysis can support problem solving and decision making. They also gain practical experience in applying statistical and decision analysis techniques and statistical packages (generally Excel) in management contexts. Topics include introduction to modelling of organisations and business problems, measurement, variability, uncertainty, statistical tests and quantitative approaches to decision making. The unit provides a foundation for quantitative techniques used in other master’s degree units.

Unit Information

Welcome to Data Analysis and Decision Making (DADM) MGMT5504. I hope you're as enthusiastic about taking this unit as I am about teaching it. Some of you may feel nervous, especially if it's the first time you've studied a statistics-related topic. This anxiety is perfectly normal but as our journey progresses I'm 99% confident you'll feel more comfortable and see the topic's relevance to your field of study, workplace and everyday decision-making.

Learning data analysis is similar to learning a new language or skill; initially you may feel confused, even frustrated, but eventually through perseverance you'll understand how to apply the statistical tools discussed in class. For those with previous statistical experience, I hope the 'extra' activities developed for the unit will expand your knowledge and introduce applications to assist you to make better data-driven decisions.

I can't stress enough how important it is to keep up to speed with the content throughout the semester, as this is a scaffolding unit, with most topics covered building on the previous ones. To assist with this I've scheduled two in-class quizzes and a final exam. In teams, you will also complete a "Statistics in Practice" presentation, requiring you to analyse secondary dataset and present the findings in a non-technical way. All team members need to contribute to the presentation.  

Therefore, if you regularly read your textbook (I'll advise which sections are particularly relevant) and consistently work through recommended questions, your learning experience will be greatly enhanced. Please don't restrict your reading to your textbook - any article/journal that refers to statistics will add to your learning. If you feel overwhelmed at any stage, please don't hesitate to contact me so that we can work through your concerns together.

All material will be made available in LMS and I urge you to use this resource on a weekly basis. During class, I welcome discussion on how the subject matter relates to your experience. All the very best in both DADM and any other unit(s) you are studying in Semester 2, 2019. I look forward to meeting you soon.

Teaching and Learning Strategy

My teaching philosophy is encapsulated by the following quote - “When teaching, light a fire, don't fill a bucket” - Dan Snow

With teaching, my main goal is to inspire, to ‘light a fire’. I hope that you see real value in the learning material presented, understand the application of this material to your workplace and, as a result, seek more information on the topic. However, a unit such as this presents many difficulties. It may be that you attend the first class with misconceptions of the use of analytics, believing that there is little application for this knowledge in management. Additionally, many of you may have little or no background in analytics and, therefore, are very nervous and concerned about studying 'data analytics'. I firmly believe that engaging and encouraging you to participate in each topic provides a more conducive learning environment than simply bombarding you with information or ‘filling your buckets’. To inspire you, and help overcome your apprehension, I encourage both critical thinking and the linking of theory to real-world examples. In doing this, I present newspaper articles, business reports, reports from the Australian Bureau of Statistics, and case studies to demonstrate the application of certain statistical output. I also have you critically appraise such material. However, to ensure this approach is successful, I present theories, models, and research in such a way that allows you to draw on your own working experiences and relate these experiences to each topic. I reinforce this with a Statistics in Practice presentation involving the analysis of workplace data. You are required to analyse the secondary data provided and communicate your findings as a team presentation. This ‘hands-on’ approach helps to reinforce learning and understanding of each topic. It also provides a useful framework for those who initially may not see the application of business analytics to the workplace.

Unit Structure

Please refer to the Class Allocation System (CAS- https://www.cas.uwa.edu.au/student) page for the lecture timetable.

Several different activities have been included in the DADM unit to make your learning easier, more engaging and stimulating. The aim is to provide you with a "toolkit" for data analysis and decision-making in a range of scenarios, including your field of study, workplace and everyday decision-making. Lectures will include discussion of case studies or reports demonstrating the use of a particular technique, plus practical hands-on sessions to reinforce the elements required for each topic. This, combined with your accumulated knowledge, experience and ideas, will reap dividends when it comes to your project and problem-solving.

Any person operating in a business, management or marketing environment requires as much information as possible about that environment. In everyday life, we're confronted with data - often unknowingly - and make decisions based on that. It's therefore important to know how to correctly interpret the information hidden in data to help make sound decisions. This unit focuses on the processes of data collection, analysis (mainly quantitative data but occasionally qualitative), interpretation, presentation, and how these tasks assist decision-making. The main focus is on interpreting the results of data analysis and developing decision strategies based on these results rather than generating data itself. However, an ability to convert data into meaningful statistical output is a very useful skill. It cannot be emphasised enough that this unit is NOT designed to convert you into a data scientist (we cover very little mathematics), but instead to give you an appreciation of the various uses of readily available data. At the same time, you'll learn about several analytical models and natural “laws” that build the foundations for more advanced techniques encountered in other Business Masters' units. All the very best in both 3D and any other unit(s) you are studying in 2019. I look forward to meeting you very soon.

Software requirements 
Being familiar with statistical software packages, in this case Excel, is a vital part of maximising your learning experience. Having both a competency and confidence using Excel will enable you to analyse large files of data without having to worry about cumbersome mathematical formulae, as well as assist with your "Statistics in Practice" project. This unit will use Excel; a prior knowledge of this software is desirable but NOT essential. The textbook provides instructions on how to use specific features of Excel and regular demonstrations in class will aim to increase students' familiarity with the package. However, students are welcome to use a statistical package (e.g. SPSS, SAS, R, MATLAB) of their choice for analysis, if they have access to it. 

Calculator Requirements
As the unit emphasis is on interpretation of output, there is no need to purchase a sophisticated scientific calculator. A standard, UWA-approved Casio or Sharp scientific calculator is more than enough, in fact for the exam a simple calculator with the basic arithmetic operations will suffice, if needed at all (the exam is practical, so you are expected to use Excel for all calculations). Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 10 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 30 out of 50 (a mark of 5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
