Unit Information

This unit provides an overview of the legal regulation of business in Australia. It gives a basic introduction to the Australian legal system, the legal framework of business regulation and areas of substantive law relevant to commerce with particular reference to business contracts and torts, fair trading (including misleading or deceptive conduct, unconscionable conduct and unfair contract terms) and consumer protection law.

Teaching and Learning Strategy

Lectures and Workshops:

This unit will be taught through a combination of formal one hour long lectures on Mondays; two hours of interactive discussion, quizzes and small group work on Thursdays; and weekly tutorials. On Monday each week the formal one hour lecture will provide a brief overview of the law relating to the topics that have been prescribed for study during that week. Students should supplement this lecture by reading the prescribed chapters of the textbook, the relevant Reading Guide(s), any Lecture Notes or Lecture Slides and any Supplementary Reading materials that are provided by the lecturers on LMS. On Thursday each week, the two-hour workshops will normally commence with a short ‘in-class quiz' to test each student’s grasp of the concepts covered in that week’s readings and lecture. Students will be expected to have prepared their own hand-written answers to the quiz questions prior to the Thursday class each week. Students will be given 10 to 15 minutes in class to discuss the quiz questions with their classmates and finalise their answers to the quiz questions before handing their answers up for marking. The quiz will normally be followed by an interactive discussion session focused on the development of legal reasoning and problem-solving skills. The aim of these sessions will be to provide students with ‘scaffolding’ relevant to the solution of the following week’s tutorial questions and to give students the opportunity to ask questions and seek clarification from the lecturers on any of the legal issues arising from that week's lecture topics and prescribed readings which they found particularly difficult. It is hoped that, in this way, students will be assisted and guided to an understanding of the lecture topics and as to how best to fully prepare themselves for the following week’s tutorial classes. 

Tutorials:

In tutorials students will spend time discussing a number of hypothetical legal problem questions. The purpose of tutorials is to enable students to revise and contextualise the materials they have been learning and, at the same time, to help them develop their legal reasoning and problem-solving skills. Students will be taught how to construct principled legal arguments as to how the law should be applied to the facts of a problem question so as to resolve the legal issue or issues arising from those facts. This is a skill which is valuable in and of itself. But in the context of this unit it is a skill which is doubly valuable since the problem questions that students learn how to analyse and answer in tutorials will be very similar to the problem questions that appear in the Final Exam. Students will be awarded up to 20% of their overall final mark on the basis of their participation in the tutorial classes. However it is important to note that marks will only be awarded for tutorial participation – no marks will be awarded simply for tutorial attendance.

Handwritten Answer Scaffolds Can Account For Up To Ten Tutorial Participation Marks :

Each week students will be expected to prepare and hand up to their tutor handwritten Answer Scaffolds (note outlines) for each of the questions that are set for discussion in tutorials that week. As stated above, a maximum of 20 marks will be awarded for each student’s participation in tutorials. Up to 10 of those marks will be awarded for the cogency of each student’s oral contributions in class and students can earn up to 10 more marks across the semester by each week submitting to their tutor at the start of their tutorial class a copy of their hand written Answer Scaffolds for each of the tutorial questions. These Answer Scaffolds must be submitted in-class in (hand written) hard copy form, and must be on no more than one sheet of A4 paper (NB: writing on both sides of the page is permitted). Students should keep a copy of each of their Answer Scaffolds and must, each week, be prepared to elaborate on and answer questions relating to what they have written in their Answer Scaffold for that week during their tutorial class.

Unit Structure

Students must attend one 1-hour lecture per week (Monday 11am or 2pm); one 2-hour workshop per week (Thursday 10am or 2pm); and one 1-hour tutorial per week. Students will be allocated to a particular lecture stream (‘original’ or ‘repeat’) and tutorial class by the Class Allocation System (CAS). 

NB: Students may not attend any lectures or tutorials other than those to which they have been allocated by the CAS without the written permission of the Unit Coordinators.

Participation

Tutorial Participation:

All students must attend and participate in tutorials. Students will be allocated a tutorial on CAS, and must not change tutorials without the written permission of the Unit Coordinators. Students attend one tutorial per week. Tutorials commence in Week 2 of the semester. Tutorial exercise questions will be posted on LMS one week in advance. Thus the questions for Tutorial 1 (to be discussed during the tutorial classes that are held in Week 2) will be posted on LMS at or before 9am on Monday of Week 1. Every tutorial question will match up with the content covered in lectures in the previous week. Thus, for example, if the lectures in Week 10 are on Contractual Terms; then the tutorial questions for Week 11 will also be on Contractual Terms.

In tutorials students will be expected to discuss and solve various hypothetical legal problem questions. The purpose of the tutorial problem questions is to provide students with an opportunity to revise and contextualise the legal principles they have been learning and to assist students to develop and practice the analytical and thinking skills that are required in order to undertake logical and closely argued applications of those principles to the resolution of legal problems. 

See: "Teaching and Learning Strategy" (above) for more details on how tutorial participation will be assessed. 

Tutorial Participation Marking Rubric:

Students will be assessed on the basis of their participation in the eleven tutorial classes that they will attend across the semester. Students are expected to be thoroughly prepared for each of their tutorials and to actively contribute to the in-class discussion each week. Attendance and participation is the key both to enjoyable classes and to effective learning. 

Tutorial class participation marks will be awarded based on the extent to which students consistently:
•	demonstrate preparedness for tutorials;
•	show respect for their tutor and other students;
•	demonstrate active listening techniques by listening to other students and the tutor in a responsive way and by responding appropriately to comments made by 
        others;
•	understand the issues and principles raised in tutorial/workshop questions; and
•	participate constructively in discussions by:
        (i) answering questions if asked;
        (ii) volunteering a comment, question or answer in a way that is timely and develops the discussion; and
        (iii) not dominating the discussion or using offensive or aggressive language.

Referencing Requirements

The Australian Guide to Legal Citation (4th ed) stipulates the required referencing style for this unit. AGLC4 mandates a referencing style for legal citations that consists of: (i) Citations in the body of the page, using a superscript (raised) number; and (ii) A list of footnotes at the bottom (foot) of each page, for all citations on that page NB: In some circumstances a bibliography will also be required. If a bibliography is required it should be provided at the end of the paper and give details of each source mentioned in the text, as well as details of other sources consulted in preparing the paper.

Other

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 10 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 30 out of 50 (a mark of 5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
