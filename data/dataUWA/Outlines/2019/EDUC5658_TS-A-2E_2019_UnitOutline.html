Academic Information

Content This unit develops the themes of mapping the conceptual terrain of policy, and globalisation and internationalisation. Global trends in education policy (devolution, accountability, privatisation, marketisation, school choice, curriculum, international education) are also covered. This unit also covers other policy domains as identified by student interests, as well as future policy scenarios.
Unit Learning Outcomes
# Outcome How outcome will be assessed
1 define 'policy' and discuss its complex and contested nature as an actively negotiated process which extends between the policy intent of central authorities and everyday practices in schools and classrooms a group presentation, essay
2 recognise and discuss the growing impact of globalisation and internationalisation on education policy and practices a group presentation, essay
3 identify and analyse contemporary education policy trends globally, nationally and locally a group presentation, essay
4 examine and assess the changing nature of policy directions in the domains of devolution, accountability, privatisation, marketisation, school choice, funding, curriculum and international education, and critique the implications of these changes for practices in schools and classrooms a group presentation, essay
5 critically evaluate future education policy scenarios a group presentation, essay
6 select and apply appropriate conceptual tools to analyse educational policies and practices at global, national, state and local levels, focusing on a policy area of personal interest and relevance a group presentation, essay

Unit Information

This unit enables students to understand the changing nature of education policies and the way they are practised in educational institutions. Global trends in education reform set a broad context for the unit. The unit analyses recent education policy developments such as national reform, accountability, testing, standardisation, school funding (including public/private education); and curriculum. Further, the group works together to critically evaluate possible future education policy scenarios. Students critically analyse a chosen policy area relevant to their experiences and interests.

Other

1. Group Presentation: (30%) Groups of 3-4 students will be established and each group will be assigned a reading. In your group, your task is to develop and deliver an oral presentation of no more than 20 mins that: 1) briefly summarises the key issues and arguments in the reading; 2) contextualises the reading and its subject matter in relation to Australian and global policy trends; 3) offers a critical appraisal of the reading; 4) briefly engages the class in discussion about the reading (e.g. by posing questions or developing a short activity). The presentation will be assessed in line with the Assessment 1 Criteria and Rubric (see LMS). There are FIVE weighted criteria. Please carefully consider the weighting of each criterion. The criteria should inform your decisions about how to structure your presentation. The use of PowerPoint is optional and should only be used if the group believes it will help them better address the assessment criteria.

2. Essay: This task requires you to write a report based on policy research you will conduct during the semester. Your report will address a research question that you will develop and refine. You will spend time brainstorming and refining your research question with your peers and your unit coordinator in the seminar workshops. Your research question should relate to a topic that is relevant to education policy in Australia and globally. You should develop a question that interests and motivates you. Potential topics and research questions will be discussed and modelled in seminar workshops. Exemplars of previous reports will be made available on the LMS under the 'Assessment' section. The research you conduct for this report will be 'desktop research'. In other words, you will base your research around existing literature, policies and data/evidence. You must not conduct fieldwork in the form of interviews, surveys or any other kind of empirical research. The report should be no longer than 3,500 words (including 'in text' references; but not including the Reference List). Your research question and project must be 'small-scale' (i.e. capable of being feasibly completed during one semester and written into a concise report). You should carefully consider issues of scale and feasibility so that your research project does not become unrealistic or unwieldy. Your report must include a 'Reference List'. In-text references and the Reference List should reflect consistent usage of an author-date system such as APA or Harvard. You will be assessed in line with the Assessment Criteria and Rubric (see LMS). There are NINE weighted criteria. Please carefully consider the weighting of each criterion. The criteria should inform your decisions about how to structure your report. Further advice about how to most effectively structure your report will be provided in seminar workshops. All assessments must be submitted via Turnitin (on the LMS) via the 'Assessment 2 Submission' link, located in the Assessment section.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 10 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 30 out of 50 (a mark of 5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
