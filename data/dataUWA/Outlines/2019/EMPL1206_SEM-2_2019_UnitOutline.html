Academic Information

Content Work is an experience that dominates human existence. This unit uncovers the nature of this experience by drawing on the insights of social psychology. The unit explores how market globalisation transforms the way work is structured within corporations and public sector organisations. This has had a profound impact on the experience of work. These transformations are explored in some detail.

Unit Information

Work plays an important role in structuring our daily lives and our sense of self. It provides a structure for how we should define ourselves in relation to others and how we should behave. It is a source of fun and enjoyment, but also a source of alienation and conflict. This course will examine some key theories of social psychology and apply them in the context of work and employment. The unit is divided into two sections.

The first section looks at the social psychology of Erving Goffman. Often referred to as dramaturgy, this theoretical framework conceptualizes human interaction as theatrical, whereby "performers" or "actors" play socially constructed "roles" aimed at defining the situation. We will look critically at the rules governing dramaturgical social interaction and then apply them to a workplace setting.

The second section looks at social identity theory and the experiments used to study human groups and social categories. In this context, we will examine the development of stereotypes and prejudices that often result in inter-group conflicts that play out in the workplace.

Teaching and Learning Strategy

This is a "flipped" unit. What exactly does this mean? Traditionally, students came into university to listen to lectures and then went home to do their homework. This course is structured in the reverse. All lecture material will be uploaded onto the LMS. Students are expected to listen to the lecture material and to do the weekly readings in their own time. You will also be expected to attend one two-hour workshop per week, during which time you will be working on assessed in-class activities. Please note that your attendance in the workshops is essential. If you cannot commit to attending a two-hour workshop once a week, then you should not take this course. The tutor for your workshop will play the role of supporter and guide, rather than that of lecturer.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 10 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 30 out of 50 (a mark of 5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
