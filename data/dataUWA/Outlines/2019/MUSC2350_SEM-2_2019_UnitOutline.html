Academic Information

Content This unit provides students with an introduction to a wide range of traditional and contemporary non-Western musics through lectures and participation in a percussive world music ensemble. It aims to develop an awareness of musical traditions, techniques and performance contexts of diverse musical traditions from around the world. Various case studies (e.g. West African drumming, North Indian music and Balinese gamelan ensemble) are used to examine specific musical practices and to generate broader questions about music such as: What is music? How do we learn music? What does musical practice tell us about social organisation? How are instruments iconic? How do musical traditions evolve over time? These are some of the questions that are explored in the unit through discussion and performance using percussion instruments. This unit is an elective unit in the Music Studies major in the Bachelor of Arts degree. It is also open to students enrolled in other majors and degree pathways who fulfil the prerequisites and who have an interest in music. No prior musical experience is necessary for the unit.

Unit Information

This unit provides students with an introduction to a range of traditional and contemporary non-western musics through workshops blending listening, discussion, research and music making. It aims to develop student awareness and promote understanding of diverse global musical traditions, techniques and performance contexts. Various case studies (such as West African drumming, North Indian music and Balinese gamelan ensemble) are used to examine specific musical practices and to generate broader questions about music such as: What is music? How do we learn music? What does musical practice tell us about social organisation? How are instruments iconic? How do musical traditions evolve over time? These are some of the questions that are explored in the unit through discussion and music making using percussion instruments. 

This unit is an elective unit in the Music Studies major in the Bachelor of Arts degree. It is also open to students enrolled in other majors and degree pathways who fulfil the prerequisites and who have an interest in music. No prior musical experience is necessary for the unit.

Teaching and Learning Strategy

This unit will be delivered by a combination of teaching and learning practices:
The workshops include group participation in discussion, listening to musical examples and practical exploration of instruments including marimbas, drums and gamelan. In-person attendance is required for all workshops. 
Please note: In-class tests will take place in weeks 2, 6 and 11. Attendance is compulsory. 
The tutorial time is for private practice of music making explored in the workshops. During this time, the instruments will be made available for student to review and practice as needed. 
Online materials will introduce and discuss concepts specific to each music culture examined in the unit, exploring this from varied perspectives and contexts using video and audio recordings, PowerPoint, formative quizzes and worksheets.
Student’s learning will be supported through LMS, which serves as a resource for course materials and readings, and as a platform for student-student and student-lecturer/tutor online interaction.
Self-directed study will enable students to acquire knowledge and skills independently and autonomously, whilst taking responsibility for the quantity and quality of their learning.

Participation

Workshops provide students with the opportunity to gain a practical insight into the topics discussed by participating in a world music ensemble. Students do not need to have prior music experience to participate fully in the workshops. As music is a collective activity, students must be present to engage in this learning experience. For this reason, attendance at a minimum of 10 workshops is required. 

Group task/showing performance: This will take place at 1pm, 26 September in the Callaway Music Auditorium. The call time for all students is 12 noon. Please bring an audience to your performance!
Note: It is a 'hurdle requirement' that all students attend the concert dress rehearsal and the concert. If a student is not successful in applying for Special Consideration for being absent, the student will fail the unit.

IMPORTANT: DETAILED ASSESSMENT INFORMATION

Research project (40% of final grade) submission details: Written Research Essay
Students must write an 1800-word research essay in formal English on one of the topics provided on LMS by week 4.
Students are expected to submit the research essay with a Conservatorium of Music Assignment cover sheet with an assignment title, your name and student number (Conservatorium of Music template, available on http://www.music.uwa.edu.au/students/forms) The assignment should be submitted as a single file on Turnitin via LMS in either Word or PDF formats ONLY. 

As you prepare your research project, ensure that you understand and will be able to demonstrate the following in your essay:
1. Clear articulation of the chosen topic, its rationale and methodology 
2. Meaningful articulation of focus issues 
3. Evidence of awareness of relevant research literature or other sources

The essay itself must contain:
1. A brief introduction and statement of your research topic and methodological approach (1/2 page, approx. 300-500 words). 
2. The body of the paper, supported by text explanation, musical examples or other evidence to support your argument, arranged in logical sequence with sub-headings and clear paragraphing (approx. 900-1400 words). 
3. Citation of relevant published sources relevant to your assignment using the correct referencing style for the unit (Chicago). 
4. A conclusion with a succinct summary (a paragraph, approx. 200-400 words). 
5. A list of references, appropriately formatted. The final draft should be word-processed, double-spaced and use standard type fonts (e.g. Arial, Times New Roman, etc.), font size 12 with standard margins.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 10 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 30 out of 50 (a mark of 5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
