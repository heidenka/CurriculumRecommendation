Academic Information

Content This unit examines the role of art and visual culture in America during the twentieth century. A range of visual forms, painting, sculpture, photography, film and video, design, new media, architecture, museums and exhibitions are discussed within the societal contexts and historiography of a century known for dynamic political, economic and technological change. Using methodologies such as the social history of art, iconography and the diverse theoretical positions that have framed the art of the twentieth century, the unit explores the development, production and interpretation of visual culture in North America. Key themes that students are introduced to in lectures and encouraged to further examine through tutorial discussion, written analysis and a research essay include the exploration of the visual culture environment; the role of the artist; visual forms of cultural production; and the influence of modernism and globalisation on the development of an American cultural identity.

Teaching and Learning Strategy

The aim of this unit is to provide an overview of the history and interpretation of visual culture and art in America in the twentieth century. The Unit will utilise a combination of lectures, tutorials, and one online activity to introduce students to the history of American modern and contemporary art. The assessment items will expose students to research methodologies used in art museums through the preparation of an acquisition purchase proposal as well as art historical research to produce an oral presentation and an essay.

Referencing Requirements

Every time you quote or use images, text or the ideas of another person in your assessment submission, you must provide a properly formatted reference to the source.  Unless otherwise stated, the School of Design prefers the Chicago Style, and there are some notes for guidance which have been prepared by library staff.       https://guides.library.uwa.edu.au/Chicago_citation

Please be sure to adhere to the academic protocols of essay writing, and most importantly ensure that any portion of your essay that relies on secondary sources is appropriately acknowledged. While you must always acknowledge all sources be sure to assert your own authorship and avoid writing an essay in which other author’s speak for you. 

On the first occasion that you refer to a work of art you must give the year that it was made. Such information should be in parentheses after the work's title, i.e. Symphony in Flesh Colour and Pink: Portrait of Mrs Frances Leyland (1907). Subsequent references to the work do not require the date, however on each occasion that you name a work of art you must italicise or underline the title of the work. It is not necessary to attach reproductions to your essay.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 10 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 30 out of 50 (a mark of 5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
