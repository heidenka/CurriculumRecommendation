Teaching and Learning Strategy

This unit is taught through modelling real-life pedagogical scenarios and actively engaging in musical teaching and learning as the basis for discussion, reflection and evaluation.  Attendance and informed participation are compulsory.

Unit Structure


The unit builds on themes that were encountered in Semester 1 in preparation for Prac, and on experience of the first school placement. It prepares for the Second Placement, which comes in the middle of the Semester. As in Semester 1, the unit is taught through a sequence of Lectures that deal with the theory and practice of teaching, and Tutorials that provide the opportunity for further practical development. Together, these address the following issues in relation to preparation for and reflection upon Practical placement in school: (1) curriculum studies in secondary music; (2) arranging and composing for school instrumental ensembles. In providing experience in these fields that sustains and reflects on their development on Teaching Prac, the unit covers issues related to how and why we teach Music at Secondary level, and the further exploration of strategies for doing so, as well as extending experience in the core practical skills: arranging and composing; conducting, including rehearsal techniques and ensemble management in the context of school-based music ensembles. Students are encouraged to develop their musical skills in directing, composing and arranging in support of their classroom teaching as well as in relation to extra-curricular activities. The unit continues to construct a framework for understanding how the teaching of music responds to, and represents, a Curriculum. The National Curriculum for Music is critically examined in the light of international research and practice, and taken as a reference point for developing Lesson Plans, Rubrics for Assessment, Programmes of Study and associated teaching materials and resources in preparation for teaching practice experience and subsequent application for employment. Attention is paid to the historical and philosophical issues involved in curriculum design for Music, so as to provide a basis for independent and critical thinking suitable for entrance to a career in which responsibility for Departmental development is often achieved at an early stage. For the composing/arranging task, students select a from the options provided and complete a performable work that can represent part of their portfolio for applying for posts.

Elaboration of the Assessment Tasks

    The Assessment Moderation Task builds on work commenced in class at UWA and involves tracking and recording assessment processes while on Prac.
    The Teaching Resource Folio will be made up of samples of your own work (plans, reflections, materials), and will include documentation of your contribution to sessions at UWA prior to Prac, including a Programme Plan and Assessment Rubrics presented in class at UWA.
    The composition or arrangement needs to be written with school resources and capabilities in mind, and should be retained for possible inclusion in submissions for employment.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 10 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 30 out of 50 (a mark of 5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
