Academic Information

Content This unit explores the role of the mass media in the operation of government and the conduct of politics. The media are correctly perceived to have great power in shaping public policy and moulding public attitudes on a wide range of issues. However, while the media's power is undeniable, one should not disregard the influence over the media of other actors, notably the government. The unit examines the interaction between the media and the government in affecting public opinion and policies. It covers key issues such as the nature of, and forces producing, news; media cultures and organisational settings which shape the way journalists report politics; and the relationship of media to the processes of government, including agenda setting, policy formation and policy implementation. It analyses cases from Australia and overseas in order to provide students with a better understanding of how and why the media game is played.

UNIT TOPICS, READINGS, AND QUESTIONS

Wk 1: Media, democracy and public opinion
Question: In most instances, do the media make public opinion or reflect existing public opinion? 

Essential reading
Shiraev E. and R. Sobel. 2006. People and their Opinions, New York: Pearson Longman. Chapter 6, pp.116-142.
Entman, R.M. and S. Herbst. 2001. ‘Reframing Public Opinion as We Have Known It’, In Mediated Politics: Communication in the Future of Democracy, eds. W.L. Bennett and R.M. Entman, Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, pp.203-225.

Wk 2: Agenda setting
Question: How, and to what extent, do the media shape the public agenda?

Essential reading
McCombs M. 2004. Setting the Agenda. Cambridge: Polity Press, Chapter 5, pp. 68-85.
Downs, Anthony. Up and Down with Ecology: The ‘Issue Attention Cycle’. In Classics of Public Policy, ed. J. Shafritz, K. Layne and C. Borick, New York: Pearson Longman.
Iyengar S. and A. Simon. 1993. ‘News Coverage of the Gulf Crisis and Public Opinion’, Communication Research, 20, 3, pp.365-383.

Wk 3: Problem definition, framing and priming 
Question: What are the significance and limitations of framing and priming effects? Discuss, using relevant examples.

Essential reading
Rochefort, D.A. and R.W. Cobb. 1994. ‘Problem Definition: An Emerging Perspective’, In The Politics of Problem Definition, eds. D.A. Rochefort and R.W. Cobb, Kansas: University of Kansas Press. Chapter 1.
Entman R.M. 1993. ‘Framing: Toward Clarification of a Fractured Paradigm’, Journal of Communication, 43, 4, pp.51-58.

Wk 4: Media-government interaction I
Question: Apply Hallin’s model or Bennett’s model (choose one) to analyse the relationship between the government and the media. You can use a few examples or a single case.  

Essential reading
Hallin D.C. 1986. The “Uncensored War” The Media and Vietnam, Oxford: Oxford University Press, Chapter 5, pp.159-210.
Bennett W.L. 1990. ‘Toward a Theory of Press-State Relations in the United States’, Journal of Communication, 40, 2, pp.103-125.

Wk 5: Media-government interaction II
Questions (choose 1): 
•	Apply either Wolfsfeld’s model or Herman and Chomsky’s model (choose one) to evaluate the agenda setting powers of the media relative to the government. You can use a few examples or a single case.  
•	Assess the connections and differences between these models. – Bennett, Wolfsfeld, and Herman and Chomsky.

Essential reading
Wolfsfeld G. 1997. ‘Fair Weather Friends: The Varying Role of the News Media in the Arab-Israeli Peace Process’, Political Communication, 14, pp.29-48.
Herman E.S. and N. Chomsky. 2002. Manufacturing Consent: The Political Economy of the Mass Media, New York: Pantheon Books, Chapter 1, pp. 1-35.

Wk 6: Media, its significance, and limitations I 
Question: Critically analyse the notion of the Fourth Estate or watchdog journalism for democracy. Discuss also the strengths and weaknesses of this model in practice.

Essential reading
Bennett, W.L. and W. Serrin. 2007. ‘The Watchdog Role of the Press’, in D.A. Graber (ed.), Media Power in Politics, 5th edn., Washington D.C.: CQ Press, Ch. 28, pp.326-336.
Louw E. 2005. The Media and Political Process. London: SAGE Publications Ltd, Chapter 4, pp.59-92.

Wk 7: Media, its significance, and limitations II 
Question: What are the factors that can constrain the ability of the media to provide information that is accurate and unbiased to members of the public?

Essential reading
Maheim, J.R. 2011. ‘The News Shapers: Strategic Communication as a Third Force in Newsmaking’, in D.A. Graber (ed.), Media Power in Politics, 6th edn, Washington D.C.: CQ Press, Ch. 35, pp.421-430.
McNair, B. 2011. 'Politics, Democracy and the Media'. An Introduction to Political Communication, 5th edn. Oxon: Routledge. Chapter 2. pp.15-26.
Lippmann, W. 1949. Public Opinion. NY: The Free Press, Ch. 24, pp.226-230

Wk 9: Government and its influence 
Questions (choose 1): 
•	Critically analyse some of the common measures adopted by the government to shape the public agenda or public opinion.
•	Using a case study approach, examine the government’s use of rhetoric to shape the agenda.

Essential reading
Corner J. 2006. ‘Simply Propaganda?’, FlowTV, 4,4, http://flowtv.org..
Street, J. 2011. 'State Control and State Propaganda'. Mass Media, Politics & Democracy, 2nd edn. London: Palgrave Macmillan. Chapter 5, pp.131-158.
Bennett, W.L. 2012. ‘How Politicians Make the News’, News: The Politics of Illusion, 9th edn, Boston: Pearson Longman. Ch. 4, pp. 111-152.

Wk 10: Media, political conflicts and spontaneous events
Questions (choose 1):
•	In what ways have political conflicts or spontaneous events (choose one) affected the relationship between the government and the media?
•	How, and to what extent, has the media serve as a conduit between the government (or terrorists/victims) and the people during political conflicts or spontaneous events (choose one)?

Essential reading
Wolfsfeld, G. 2003. ‘Media, Conflict and Peace’, in P.J. Maarek and G. Wolfsfeld (eds.),Pollitical Communication in a New Era, London: Routledge, Ch. 8, pp. 139-156.
Lawrence, R.G. and T.A. Birkland. 2004. ‘Guns, Hollywood, and School Safety: Defining the School-Shooting Problem Across Public Arenas’. Social Science Quarterly, 85, 5, pp.1193-1207.
He, Xueqin Elaine, and John P. Tiefenbacher. 2008. ‘Political and cultural contrasts in reporting about disasters comparing United States and Chinese newspaper portrayals of bridge collapses’. GeoJournal, 73:133–147.

Wk 12: Media and political elections
Question: Critically analyse some of the strategies used by the government (or political parties) during political elections to shape the media or public agenda.

Essential reading
Young S. 2006 ‘The Convergence of Political and Government Advertising: Theory Versus Practice’, Media International Australia Incorporating Culture and Policy, 119, pp.99-111.
Grant, W.J., B. Moon, and J.B. Grant. 2010, ‘Digital Dialogue? Australian Politicians’ Use of the Social Network Tool Twitter’. Australian Journal of Political Science, 45, 4, 579-604.
Tracey, S. 2017. ‘Trust, Trump, and the Turnout: A Marketers Point of View’. American Behavioral Scientist, 61, 5, 526–32.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 10 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 30 out of 50 (a mark of 5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
