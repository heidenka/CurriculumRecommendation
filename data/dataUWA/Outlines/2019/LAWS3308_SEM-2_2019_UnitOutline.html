Academic Information

Content This unit is available at Level 2 or 3. It traces the development of a marketing enterprise through all stages of the marketing process and considers the legal aspects of each stage. It deals with the legal protection of innovative new products and the prohibitions on other traders ’free riding’ on the reputation and goodwill of an established business, the legal rights and duties of suppliers, customers and ultimate consumers of goods and services, relationships with competitors, proscribed anti-competitive behaviour and product liability.

Unit Information

This is the final ('capstone') unit in the Business Law Major. It builds on and complements the knowledge and skills relating to business and commercial law that students have obtained in the seven other units which comprise the Business Law Major. In this unit all of that knowledge and all of those skills will be called on as students undertake a detailed study of three hugely important areas of Australian commercial law: Intellectual Property Law, Consumer Law and Competition Law. To do well in this unit students will have to demonstrate highly developed legal research and writing skills, strong oral and written communication abilities and the capacity to work cooperatively and productively with up to 10 other students in preparing, and delivering in class, engaging and relevant tutorial presentations.

Teaching and Learning Strategy

This unit will be taught through a combination of formal lectures and weekly seminars (tutorials). The formal lectures will provide a brief overview of and context to the law relating to the topics that have been prescribed for study during the upcoming week. Students should prepare for each week's formal lecture by pre-reading the prescribed chapters of the textbook, reviewing the relevant LMS study guide(s) and the lecture notes and slides provided by the lecturers, as well as undertaking any supplementary readings that may have been prescribed or recommended by the lecturers.

Lecture slides (outlines) and/or (detailed) lecture notes will be posted on LMS at least one week prior to every lecture.

NB: While every effort will be made to conduct this unit in a way that is logical, well organised and clear, the lecturers and tutors can only shoulder part of the responsibility for achieving the learning outcomes prescribed for this unit. Each student’s in-class contributions and their efforts out of class (in particular the depth and extent of their supplementary reading) will be critical to the achievement of those learning outcomes. 

A comprehensive set of supplementary reading materials will be made available to all students enrolled in this unit on the unit LMS page.

Unit Structure

Students attend one 2-hour lecture per week (Friday 9 am to 11 am); and one 1-hour tutorial per week.

Spaces may change daily according to student movement, so students who wish to move to an alternative tutorial must keep checking the CAS regularly.

NB: The CAS will be closed to students, and no more changes to tutorial allocations will be allowed, on or after 9 am on Friday 2 August.

Participation

Tutorial Participation:
All students must attend and participate in tutorials. Students will be allocated a tutorial on CAS, and must not change tutorials without the written permission of the Unit Coordinator. Students attend one tutorial per week. Tutorials commence in Week 2 of the semester. Tutorial exercise questions will be posted on LMS one week in advance. In their tutorial classes students will be expected to discuss and solve various hypothetical legal problem questions. The purpose of the tutorial problem questions is to provide students with an opportunity to revise and contextualise the legal principles they have been learning and to assist students to develop and practice the analytical and thinking skills that are required in order to undertake logical and closely argued applications of those principles to the resolution of legal problems. 

Tutorial Participation Marking Rubric:
Students will be assessed on the basis of their participation in the eleven tutorial classes that they attend across the semester. Students are expected to be thoroughly prepared for each of their tutorials and to actively contribute to the in-class discussion each week. Attendance and participation is the key both to enjoyable classes and to effective learning. 
Tutorial class participation marks will be awarded based on the extent to which students consistently:
	• demonstrate preparedness for tutorials;
        • show respect for their tutor and other students; 
        • demonstrate active listening techniques by listening carefully to comments by other students and the tutor, and by responding appropriately to those comments
	• understand the issues and principles raised in tutorial/workshop questions; and
	• participate constructively in discussions by:
        		(i) answering questions if asked;
                        (ii) volunteering a comment, question or answer in a way that is timely and develops the discussion; and
        		(iii) not dominating the discussion or using offensive or aggressive language.

Other

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 10 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 30 out of 50 (a mark of 5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
