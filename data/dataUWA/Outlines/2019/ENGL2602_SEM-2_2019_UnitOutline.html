Academic Information

Content This unit introduces students to the close analysis of a number of important literary works by Shakespeare and his contemporaries. It enhances students' engagement with the range of literary, ethical and societal issues that Shakespeare's and other early modern works raise support. The unit also encourages students to examine the continuing relevance of Shakespeare's work, and that of his contemporaries, for our time and world, as well as differing critical and directorial interpretations these works have attracted.

Teaching and Learning Strategy

This unit combines lectures, tutorials, presentations, and essays in an incremental teaching approach. In each lecture I will introduce the main ideas that students will then apply in tutorial, first informally in guided discussion and eventually in graded presentations that will each lead to a formal research essay that goes beyond what I have taught to focus on the student's own discoveries, insights, and interests.

Unit Structure

Lectures take place on Tuesdays. Tutorials take place on Mondays and Tuesdays, and relate to the lectures they follow (so if you have a tutorial Tuesday morning it will be covering what I presented the previous week on Tuesday afternoon). This can become confusing, so please get into the habit of using the lecture as preparation for the tutorial rather than vice versa.

Participation

Students are expected to attend all tutorials, having read and understood the assigned materials. Students will be working together in the tutorials to prepare presentations that will be graded; missing a tutorial will be detrimental to the student's group. The tutorial mark is based on the presentations, which includes preparation, the presentation, and perceptive questions during others' presentations.

Referencing Requirements

MLA style is the standard referencing format in English literature and should be followed for all written work (ie the two essays) submitted in this unit.

Texts

Shakespeare's plays and sonnets are widely available in new, used, and electronic versions as single plays and as collected works in one or multiple volumes. A proper scholarly edition with good footnotes will greatly enhance your experience: for single plays I recommend the Arden, New Cambridge, or Oxford editions, and for collected works the Norton, Arden, or RSC editions. I have included links to the Folger and Internet Shakespeare electronic editions in the unit readings for those who prefer to go paperless. Whichever edition you choose, please bring it to the tutorial (this may be on a laptop or tablet -- phones are not ideal for this purpose).

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 10 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 30 out of 50 (a mark of 5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
