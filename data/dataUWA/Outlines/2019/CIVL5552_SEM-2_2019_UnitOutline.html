Unit Information

The project is a capstone experience in which students integrate and apply knowledge and skills acquired in earlier units to a substantial real-world design challenge relevant to their discipline. Students work in teams, typically with four to six members, and are supervised by an academic and/or industry mentor. Teams are responsible for managing their project as well as reporting against milestones and preparing the necessary design documentation. The academic objectives of the project are to lead students through a systematic engineering design and synthesis process and to better prepare them for professional practice as engineers. The focus of the unit is the delivery of outcomes to a professional standard.
The course will convey an understanding of the behaviour of structures and how structures respond to loads in a ‘physical’ sense. This is in contrast to analysis, although some hand analysis will be covered, and to design in the sense of carrying out detailed calculations to check the capacity of elements of a structure. Analysis and design of steel and concrete structural elements have been covered in pre-requisite courses and a knowledge of these is assumed.
Also assumed is the ability of students to read and interpret the Australian Standard loading code (AS 1170.0, .1, & .2), the steel structures code (AS 4100) and the concrete structures code (AS 3600). While these will be referred to illustrate particular points, detailed use of the provisions of these codes will not be covered. Again, these matters have been covered in pre-requisite courses.
An understanding of bending moment, shear force and axial force diagrams will be assumed and their construction by inspection will  be covered.    Sketching of  deflection  diagrams  will  also be covered.

Unit Structure

Each Week, there will be 2-hours tutorial that cover the required material to complete the project. There are 3 modules in this unit as follows:
o	1st module: 5 weeks on steel frame and concrete floors buildings design.
During the first 2 weeks, students will be exposed to the wind loading code AS1170.2, a review of steel design concepts and an introduction of structural behavior of steel frames, and concrete floor and footing during the tutorial hours.  In the tutorial classes, real case studies will be discussed and tasks will be assigned to students to complete their projects. In remaining 3 weeks of this module, more examples of structural performances of steel frame buildings will be delivered in the tutorial classes. The tutorial classes will be used for project supervision.
o	2nd module: 4 weeks on steel truss design. 
During the first 2 weeks of this module, students will be exposed to the theory of the design of truss structural elements and concrete floor and footing.  In the tutorial classes, numerical examples will be offered and discussed.
In the remaining 3 weeks of this module, tutorial classes will offer examples of real case designs. Observations on cost-analysis and standard design practices in Australia will be discussed in these classes. The tutorial classes will be used for project supervision.
o	3rd module: 3 weeks on timber design.
In the 3 weeks of this module, students will be exposed to the theory of the design of timber structural elements.  The tutorial classes will be used for the timber project supervision. All aspects of the timber design code AS1720 for floor beams and roof joists will be covered. A major assignment will be on these learning outcomes.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 10 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 30 out of 50 (a mark of 5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
