Academic Information

Content In this unit, students are provided with a state-of-the-art insight into how drugs affect physiological and pathological systems. This is achieved by applying experimental pharmacological methods to a range of contemporary topics including clinical pharmacology, clinical toxicology, immunopharmacology, respiratory pharmacology, clinical trials, psychopharmacology, neuropharmacology and oxidative stress.

Teaching and Learning Strategy

A 'flipped classroom' approach is used extensively in the Systems Pharmacology Methods PHAR3321 unit.  That is, you will be exposed to many of the theoretical aspects of the laboratory sessions prior to attending the lab, typically through the lectures conducted in the co-requisite Systems Pharmacology PHAR3320 unit and by reading the associated laboratory notes provided on LMS.  Your knowledge and understanding of this prior content may be tested through Readiness Assurance Tests conducted prior to the laboratory sessions.

Unit Structure

LABORATORY CLASSES
Students enrolled in Systems Pharmacology Methods PHAR3321, will attend one laboratory class per week – on either Monday or Tuesday afternoons from 1 pm, depending on your lab group allocation in CAS (or as indicated by the Unit Coordinator). Lab groups will be finalised in the first week of semester. The laboratory work has been designed to complement and extend the lecture material and introduce some of the experimental methodology of modern pharmacology. Laboratory reports will generally need to be submitted within TWO WEEKS of completion of the class.

CREDIT WILL NOT BE GIVEN FOR LABORATORIES THAT ARE NOT ATTENDED
(unless Special Consideration is obtained from the Faculty of Health and Medical Sciences).

Each student will require a laboratory coat and appropriate footwear (no sandals, thongs or bare feet) for each laboratory session. No eating or drinking is permitted in laboratory classes (e-Learning suites or wet laboratories). These laboratory sessions can last more than 4 hours. Note that some laboratory sessions use  laboratory  animals for  provision of  tissues  for  in vitro  use. If you feel that the use of animals will be distasteful to you, then you should discuss the matter with the Unit Coordinator before commencing the unit.

SEMINARS
In Systems Pharmacology Methods PHAR3321 each student is required to present a 10 minute seminar with a 5 minute question and answer session. These seminars will be held in early September - see Unit Schedule below for dates of seminars.

Each student will present a seminar that is based on a recently‐published scientific paper. The paper for presentation will be chosen by the Unit Coordinator from a pair of papers nominated by the student.  That is, by Friday 9th August, each student will need to have COMPLETED THE SEMINAR PAPER NOMINATION FORM ON LMS AND SUBMITTED PDFs OF THE TWO SCIENTIFIC PAPERS they have nominated for consideration via the LMS. During the following week, forms will be returned to students indicating which of the two papers presented for consideration has been selected to be the subject of the seminar presentation.

When selecting the two scientific papers, you must choose papers
•	that report original research (NOT review articles that summarize the findings of numerous scientific papers),
•	that were published recently (in either 2018 or 2019)
•	that are likely to be of general pharmacological or toxicological interest,
•	that have been published in the higher quality scientific journals (Journal Impact Factor greater than 3.0*, see below for explanation)
•	that you have not previously, or will not in the future, present or knowingly discuss in another unit.

We are interested in how you came to select each paper (did you conduct a PubMed search? or did you look at the current journals displayed in the library? etc.) and why you selected each paper (do you have an underlying interest in the area of research? or are you familiar with some of the techniques used in the paper?). Please fill in all the relevant sections of the form.

Make sure you carefully consider which papers you select, taking into account your level of interest in the subject matter and your level of familiarity with experimental and theoretical aspects of the study (choose in haste, repent at leisure!). You are encouraged to nominate a preference for one paper.

Other

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 10 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 30 out of 50 (a mark of 5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.

Special Considerations

If your study has been adversely affected by illness or other significant circumstances outside of your control, you can apply for special consideration. Significant circumstances may include but are not limited to:

  • serious illness or death of a member of the student's immediate family or household or of a close friend
  • serious injury
  • being a victim of a crime
  • breakdown of relationship
  • sudden loss of income or employment
  • serious disruption to domestic arrangements

The full policy and information on applying for special consideration is available at http://www.student.uwa.edu.au/course/exams/consideration
