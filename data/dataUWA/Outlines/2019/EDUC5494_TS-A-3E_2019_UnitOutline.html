Unit Information

This unit focuses on the principles of assessment and evaluation in the context of education and the social sciences. Students explore principles of test construction, administration, scoring, item analysis and technical adequacy from both theoretical and practical perspectives. On successful completion of this unit, students will be able to: Design effective paper-and-pen assessment tasks; Design effective alternative assessments within the cognitive domain; Design and select measures within the affective domain; and Interpret data gathered through norm-referenced tests, including the NAPLAN.

ADDITIONAL DETAILED ASSESSMENT INFORMATION:

Assignment 1 Description (30% of semester mark)

Task: Interpreting Information

Maximum word count: 1000 words.

Assignment 1 comprises a combination of short responses to content questions and short extended responses to show understanding of how to interpret information from assessments.

The short content questions focus on the Guttman pattern, which is a component of the framework known as Item Response Theory. Participants will respond to a series of short answer questions focusing on: the Guttman pattern; interpretation of data analysed using the Guttman pattern; and summarising test data using the framework introduced. The short response questions contributes 10% of the total and is used primarily to provide feedback to participants about understanding of the technical concepts and the framework.

In addition, short extended questions focus on interpretation and use of information from a two-stage assessment method that incorporates student performance exemplars (work samples). The questions require participants to apply concepts assessed in the short responses. Questions focus on use of exemplars in assessment, use of fine-grained information for formative assessment, and understanding the relationship of the Zone of Proximal Development to the Guttman pattern.

Assignment 2 (70% of semester mark)

Task: Provide a ‘What Teachers Need to Know’ Manual on designing good assessment tasks within your own discipline area (or one of them).

Maximum word count: 4000 words.

Part A – Generic Principles (10% of the assignment mark) - Identify the generic principles (e.g., validity, reliability) that teachers should know in designing good assessment tasks.

Part B – Designing Paper-and-Pen Tasks: Selected Response Items (30% of assignment mark) -  Provide a brief description of each of the following types of items (standard MCQ; T/F; Matching; Multiple T/F; short answer; Complex), and suggest learning objectives within your own area that each would be suited to assessing. Discuss advantages and disadvantages of each for assessing these outcomes, and the core principles for designing sound items within each type. Provide example items to illustrate these principles.

Part C – Designing Paper-and-Pen Tasks: Constructed Response Items (30% of assignment mark) - Provide a brief description of the types of constructed response items that are often used within your area. Suggest learning objectives that each type would be suited to assessing. Discuss advantages and disadvantages of each for assessing these outcomes, and the core principles for designing sound items within each type. Provide example items to illustrate these principles, along with any rubrics that would be useful in the assessment of these.

Part D: Designing Alternative Assessments in the Cognitive Domain (30% of assignment mark) - Provide a brief description of the types of alternative assessment tasks (e.g., performance tasks) that you feel would be useful within your area. Suggest learning objectives that each type would be suited to assessing. Discuss advantages and disadvantages of each for assessing these outcomes, and the core principles for designing sound items within each type. Briefly describe some example items to illustrate these principles, along with any rubrics that would be useful in the assessment of these.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 10 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 30 out of 50 (a mark of 5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
