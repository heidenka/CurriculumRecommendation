Academic Information

Content This unit covers some fundamental issues which are central in philosophy and which provide an introduction to contemporary philosophical debate. The topics are organised around three major themes—God, Mind and Knowledge. It covers such issues as whether it is reasonable to believe in God, whether we have grounds for believing in the external world, whether the mind and brain are really distinct and what makes us count as the same person over time.

Teaching and Learning Strategy

The course has two chief goals. The first goal is to teach students how to do philosophy, through the development of problem-solving, critical thinking and research skills. Particular skills include:
 
• Capacity to form arguments. This means not just reporting what famous philosophers have thought, but developing a considered opinion as to whether they are right, where a considered opinion is one that can be backed up with solid argumentation.
• A critical and enquiring attitude to philosophical issues.
• Ability to read high-quality philosophy essays
• Be able to write a better philosophy essay. This is extremely important and has three
components. First, you will be able to express your ideas in a clear and precise manner. Second, you will be able to write a more structured essay and, third, you will learn to formulate the sort of arguments you are expected to master at an undergraduate level.
• You will learn to work effectively in collaborative contexts during tutorials. More specifically in tutorials, you will be expected to participate both in small group tasks, such as schematising one of the arguments from the reading, as well as group discussions.
• Be a better researcher. You will be encouraged to use a range of research methods involving a range of information technologies. You will also learn to identify the most important sources for the sort of essay you are writing, so that you can research both more effectively and more efficiently.
• You will be encouraged to take responsibility for your own learning via the essay and the exam, each of which will require the synthesising of a range of sources in order to formulate a structured and well-argued paper.

In addition to these broad skills, students will also gain an understanding of the course content. This includes:
• Demonstrating an understanding of important historical and 6 contemporary philosophical ideas in areas such as ethics, free will, knowledge and religion.
• Articulating and understanding a number of philosophical problems in relation to religion
• articulating and understanding key issues and concepts in metaphysics and epistemology.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 10 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 30 out of 50 (a mark of 5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
