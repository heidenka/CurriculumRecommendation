Academic Information

Content This unit introduces the language structures and techniques needed to write well-structured programs in the object-oriented paradigm using the Java programming language. In particular, the process of developing appropriate classes, objects and methods to solve simple computational problems underlies the entire unit. Core computer programming topics such as the use of variables, primitive and reference data types, expressions, control structures involving selection and repetition, method decomposition and parameter passing are all covered in this context. Algorithmic techniques such as iteration, sorting, searching along with programming practices such as error handling, testing, debugging and documentation are introduced. The unit also covers advanced topics such as association, inheritance and interface. A strong focus is placed on the practical application of these concepts and techniques to produce working programs in computer laboratories. The rationale for using the object-oriented paradigm, and in particular the language Java, is covered in detail. No prior knowledge of computing or programming is assumed.

Teaching and Learning Strategy

The lectures will introduce the core concepts of OO programming using practical examples.  As well as participating in lectures, students are expected to work through the text book chapters for each topic and to undertake exercises provided in the text book.  The mid-semester multi-choice test and the final written exam will test students knowledge of these core concepts and their ability to apply them in Java.  Weekly labs are designed for students to practice reading, writing, debugging and testing Java programs.  The labs directly support the two programming projects, which will assess students' ability to implement Java classes from specifications and to make effective use of software development tools. The workshop each week will provide extra support for students to ask questions and work through examples.

Unit Structure

Students attend 2 lectures and one two-hour lab each week.  An optional workshop will be held every Friday aimed principally at students who feel they need extra support.  The workshops will not introduce new material and the session will be organised around student questions.  Recordings of the lectures and the workshop will be available on LMS, but classes are designed for face to face mode and so attendance is recommended.  Supervised labs are offered every day from Tuesdays to Fridays (see the timetable for details).  Students are encouraged to attend one or more labs each week as required.  During busy periods priority will be given to students in their timetabled lab.   Two programming projects will assess students' ability to apply the programming practices introduced in the labs and lectures.

Participation

Attending lectures and reviewing the material comprises may be a third of the effort required for this unit. The remainder of the time should be spent reading a text, possibly an online tutorial, and practicing the lab exercises. Students must attend the mid-semester test for CITS1001 in person, but otherwise there are no compulsory attendance requirements at classes.  See the Resources link in LMS for more guidance on how to succeed in learning to program.

Referencing Requirements

In this unit you will complete two programming assignments for assessment. In accordance with the University Guidelines on Academic Conduct policy, you may discuss with other students and teachers the general principles required to understand the assignments, but the work you submit must be the result of your own effort. The following link gives a guide for our expectations on referencing your sources in programming assignments: http://integrity.mit.edu/handbook/writing-code. In particular, note that ``It is fine to use material and code from external sources like StackOverflow, but only with proper attribution, and only if the assignment allows it. In particular, if the assignment says 'implement X', then you must create your own X, not reuse one from an external source.''  Programming assignments will be checked and students suspected of academic misconduct will be referred to the Academic Conduct Advisor.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 10 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 30 out of 50 (a mark of 5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
