Academic Information

Content This unit covers the fundamentals of signal and system analysis, focusing on dynamical systems in time and frequency domains, transforms (Laplace, Fourier and Z) used in the analysis and design in signals and systems, sampling and reconstruction of signals, and analog and digital filters. Applications are drawn broadly from engineering including feedback and control, and communications and signal processing.

Teaching and Learning Strategy

Lectures will be conducted as a seminar where you attend or review the recordings, take notes to guide your learning and develop the motivation and excitement of the learning through the worked examples, demos, and illustrations of concepts presented.

Practice classes will be conducted in flipped classroom style where you are given the opportunity to review the course material and attempt selected problems from the tutorial sheets with the help of roving facilitators and the lecturer.

Laboratories is where you will use simulation and programming tools to analyse a system or signal and compare simulation with theory and extend your knowledge through a deeper analysis or application. You will do this in groups and need to learn to work with your group to ensure you complete all the tasks in the allotted time and can demonstrate and explain your results.

Online quizzes, class tests and final examination are individual written assessment where you are required to calculate, derive, or find an answer to an exercise involving systems and signal analysis, application and possibly design.

Unit Structure

This unit comprises lectures, practice classes, online quizzes, in-class tests, group labs and the final examination. 

Lectures (Mondays 10am-12pm; Wednesdays 8am-9am) will guide students through the relevant course material, highlight key concepts and results, illustrating concepts and going through worked examples or demos. The practice classes (Wednesdays 9am-10am) will have three peer facilitators and go through selected problems from the tutorial question sheets. All lectures and practice class sessions are recorded, but you will need to attend to get the most benefit from the practice class sessions due to their interactive nature.

The laboratories comprise three labs which run over two weeks as per your timetable. You need to attend your allocated session for that lab (one session for each of the three labs). You will also need to complete pre-lab exercises for some labs BEFORE attending your lab session. Laboratories are mandatory assessment. Assessment is completed during your scheduled lab session so it is imperative you prepare before the lab as you would for a test or exam.

The online quizzes will run the week preceding a class test. The online quizzes are multiple-choice questions which either test your understanding of concepts (so you need to read the questions carefully, research and think) or simple drill exercises.

The class test will be held during the 9am-10am Wednesday in place of the usual practice class, The class tests are optional in that if you miss or do poorly in the class test relative to the final exam it will not be counted (see Other for explanation), you should use the class test as preparation for the final exam.

The final examination will be held during the semester 2 examination period and  will assess your understanding, analysis and application of the course material in the same question style as the class tests and tutorial exercises, and source knowledge from the quizzes and laboratories.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 10 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 30 out of 50 (a mark of 5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
