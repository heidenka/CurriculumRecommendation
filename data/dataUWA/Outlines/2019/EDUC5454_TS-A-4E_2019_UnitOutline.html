Academic Information

Content This unit provides a general overview of learning difficulties from a number of perspectives (e.g. medical, neuropsychological, educational) and a range of teaching strategies for students with learning difficulties. It critically examines the specificity and complexity of learning difficulties, including moderate, severe and profound learning disabilities. Although the primary focus is on reading, mathematics, written expression and learning disorders not otherwise specified, other psychopathologies which impact learning are also covered.

Unit Information

Students will develop a theoretical and practical understanding of learning difficulties and specific learning disabilities (LD) across a range of contexts (e.g., school and residential institutions) and the psychopathologies that contribute towards them. The majority of LD covered in the unit will be those most commonly encountered by school psychologists, special educators and mainstream school teachers. Following an introduction to the terms learning difficulties and learning disabilities and the difficulties associated with using IQ as a measure to determine LD status, a range of topics will be covered. For example, Disorders of Written Expression, Language Expression, Numeracy, and Motor Coordination will be examined. Psychopathologies such as Autism Spectrum Disorders, Developmental Language Disorders/Specific Language Impairment, Anxiety and Tic Disorders which effect learning (and teaching) and make learning difficult for children and adolescents will also be reviewed. The strategies used to overcome learning disabilities will be examined. This unit will involve students in practical exercises aimed at familiarisation with the issues schools encounter when managing students with learning disabilities. Assessment will be based on (i) in-class DVD based case study and (ii) communication of factual knowledge.

ADDITIONAL DETAILED ASSESSMENT INFORMATION:

ASSIGNMENT ONE – CASE STUDY (Cameron) (60%)

APST Standard 6 – Engage in professional learning - APTS Standard 7 – Engage professionally with colleagues, parents/carers and the community

In this assignment you are required to watch the 60 minute DVD case study each week in class (10-15 minutes per week) and make notes under the main question/headers. We will discuss key points to the DVD and integrate these into developing an understanding of the most pertinent characteristics and issues in the lives of young people with neurodevelopmental disorders and learning difficulties. In the final session, you will identify the key criteria of Cameron’s neurodevelopmental disorder (Autism Spectrum), his social interactions and how these contribute to his everyday adaptive functioning in school and home. You will also identify how his neurodevelopmental disorder contributes, positively and negatively to his academic prowess, and how the relevant theories (Theory of Mind, Weak Central Coherence and Executive Functioning) underpin his functioning. Finally, you will evaluate the positive and negative aspects of school and home and how they contribute to Cameron’s overall wellbeing. A demonstrated understanding of theories, concepts, and ideas; an ability to objectively observe and interrogate theoretical constructs and criteria; establish clarity around the main issues; and provide a clear answer to the main questions within the time frame will be required. Be particular and specific rather than containing statements that are general. It is important that your case study contains a clear exposition of the issues observed; a clear and detailed description of observations made; a critical engagement and understanding of the issues in question; a familiarity with relevant literature; and an adherence to the conventions of academic writing.

ASSIGNMENT TWO – Fact Sheet (40%)

APST Standard 6 – Engage in professional learning 

This assignment comprises the preparation of a fact sheet/brochure pertaining to a specific neurodevelopmental disorder (e.g., Autism Spectrum, ADHD, Specific Learning Disorders, Motor Coordination disorders). The choice of neurodevelopmental disorder is open, but it should be prepared for teachers, parents, students (e.g., with or without neurodevelopmental disorders), or allied health professionals. The fact sheet should utilise the knowledge you have developed pertaining to the identification of characteristics and/or signs and symptoms of the condition you have selected. Identify the key characteristics which impact academic and social functioning in school contexts, along with the risk and protective factors associated with the everyday challenges young people with such conditions experience. Finally, identify the reliable resources that are available for the population you are preparing the fact sheet for. This exercise shows students that in addition to having a comprehensive understanding of the learning difficulties that children and adolescents under their care experience, they should also be able to communicate this in a concise and understandable fashion to others. In preparing the fact sheet, students must first consider whether they would be attracted and engaged by it, if presented to them. They then need to consider the veracity of the information included on the fact sheet, whether it explains to the reader why the student behaves in the ways he/she does (i.e., key features of underlying theoretical motivations or mechanisms such as Theory of Mind, Weak Central Coherence, EF) and what can be undertaken to assist the student. Be particular and specific rather than containing general information. Ensure a critical engagement with the audience through visual presentation. Show an understanding of the academic and social challenges confronting students each day and the reliable avenues available to assist in remediating these.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 10 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 30 out of 50 (a mark of 5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
