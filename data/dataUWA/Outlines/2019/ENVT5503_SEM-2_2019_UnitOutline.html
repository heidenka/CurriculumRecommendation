Academic Information

Content This is an advanced unit linking interdisciplinary aspects of soil, groundwater, hydrogeochemistry and biological sciences to form an understanding of pollutant behaviour and of means to mitigate pollutant impact through remediation. The unit focuses on various types of point-source contamination problems (such as hydrocarbons, metal(loid)s), diffuse contamination problems (e.g., agricultural) as well as mining impacts on groundwater quality. A range of common chemical, physical and biological remediation techniques are studied, including natural attenuation and (active) bioremediation. Students are introduced to numerical modelling of the key physical, hydrogeochemical and microbial processes that affect pollutant fate and remediation. Acquired skills are used to assess environmental risks and to plan remediation schemes. Where feasible, site visits are arranged to expose the students to projects at various stages of contamination, remediation and rehabilitation.

Unit Information

ENVT5503 is an advanced unit linking soil, water, (geo-)chemical and biological sciences in the context of assessing and remediating geogenic and anthropogenic pollution problems. The lecture material focuses on the chemical, physical and biological processes controlling contaminant release, contaminant movement, contaminant attenuation, environmental risks and remediation techniques. In addition, the regulatory framework governing contaminated site rehabilitation will be reviewed. The unit includes a numerical modelling component that will teach the students to understand and develop conceptual and numerical models of contaminant transport and remediation. The unit will introduce students to soil and groundwater remediation through a reiteration of basic chemical and hydrological processes in order to assess how contaminants move in the environment and the potential risks they pose.

Teaching and Learning Strategy

Assessment is conducted individually though students are encouraged to openly discuss problem solving methods and the science during tutorial sessions. We use a wide range of case studies to illustrate the application of theoretical concepts to the assessment and remediation of soil and groundwater. The unit will revise and reinforce fundamental concepts in subsurface hydrology, hydrogeochemistry and microbiology. Contact hours include discussion sessions (which may include presentations by guest speakers from industry or government agencies and tutorial exercises) and computer lab exercises. Students will also be expected to read widely and demonstrate the ability to collate and synthesise information from disparate sources. In the tutorials students will work on exercises designed to impart both a solid understanding and quantitative skills for working with contaminant biodegradation, remediation strategies, and assessing environmental risk. The theory and quantitative skills will be reinforced by a field trip and workshop at a case study site, which will be led by an environmental practitioner who has worked on the site.

Unit Structure

The core of the unit are weekly seminars on Monday afternoons, with a mixture of presentations and tutorial activities. Some weeks the seminar will include a guest speaker. In the later part of the course, part of the Monday session will include computer-based tutorials. 
A field trip is scheduled in to take place on Mon 26 Aug (preliminary date).  
For a three week period, students will attend additional Tues lectures by a guest speaker on the physical and biogeochemical fundamentals of organic contaminant hydrogeology.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 10 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 30 out of 50 (a mark of 5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
