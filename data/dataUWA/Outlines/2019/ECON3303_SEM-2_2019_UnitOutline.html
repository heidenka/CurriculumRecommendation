Unit Information

This unit emphasises the application of macroeconomic theory that is taught in Levels 1 and 2 units, to macroeconomic policy, encompassing policies governing the growth rate of the whole economy and the use of financial regulation, fiscal policy, and central banking to stabilise it along its growth path. It reviews growth models; national account and balance of payments account consumption, saving and investment; international trade and the real exchange rate; money and nominal exchange rates; applying the complete model to the analysis of macroeconomic shocks; enhancing the role of expectations—a more advanced model; macroeconomic policy responses and debates.

• Introduction
  -- This unit provides an advanced treatment of macroeconomics and the current practice of macroeconomic policy.

• Unit content
  -- The standard content of macroeconomics units is reviewed, including key concepts and analytical frameworks. The former include the measurement of macroeconomic variables and their behaviour in partial equilibrium environments, say,  how consumers make decisions on consumption, labour supply, and saving in a dynamic/intertemporal scenario given the interest rate and wage rate.  The latter consists of theories on growth and business cycles which provides general equilibrium frameworks organizing the macroeconomic variables to understand long-run growth and short-run fluctuations. The course has two features. The first is numerical implementation of macroeconomic models. The second is critical thinking on a wide range of macroeconomic issues. Prospective students can refer to Unit Outline and Reading List for details on the structure and contents of the course.

• Unit goal
  -- This unit is aimed to enhance students' understanding of standard macroeconomics concepts, theories and tools, and to develop their analytical skills. There are two main goals. First, in depth, the course unit emphasizes quantitative analysis. Participants will work with computer-based numerical exercises (with Microsoft Excel) which involve data collection, model estimation, and construction of macroeconomic “laboratories” to simulate and evaluate effects of monetary policies and fiscal policies.  It trains students to work out simple macroeconomic models with data.  Second, in breadth, it enables participants to follow modern macroeconomic literature in blogs, financial media, and professional journals, and to participate in macroeconomic policy debates. To meet the end, participants will be encouraged to read articles in magazine, newspapers, and academic journals.

• Educational principles and graduate attributes	
  -- Participants in this unit are encouraged to critically evaluate macroeconomic arguments that are put forward in the political arena and that can be found in the popular press. This experience enhances the ability to debate macroeconomic issues and policies in a consistent, reasoned way that emphasizes economic principles and facts from authoritative sources. At the same time, more effective written communication and presentation skills are fostered that can be used in many areas of applied economic and financial research. On completion of this unit, you should have:
    (1) A deeper grasp of the principles underlying macroeconomic behaviour, performance, and its measurement. The ability to apply analytical tools to economic policy formulation.
    (2) The ability to write both descriptive and analytical reports on the key issues facing macroeconomic policy. A clear understanding of the origins of current macroeconomic problems and policy choices.
    (3) To develop and assess the last two outcomes, assignments will be required that applies macroeconomic modelling to key policy evaluation.

• Learning  outcomes
  -- Understand the key issues in recent macroeconomic policy;
  -- Identify the theory needed to analyse these issues and then learn how to conduct analysis using very standard and intuitive macroeconomic models; 
  -- Demonstrate the ability to apply, analyse, synthesise and evaluate information from multiple sources to assess issues in macroeconomics that are relevant to both the private and public sectors;
  -- Demonstrate an ability to work effectively, independently and in a team situation.

Participation

* Participation in class, whether it be listening to a lecture or getting involved in other activities, is an important part of the learning process, therefore it is important that you attend classes. More formally, the University regulations state that ‘to complete a course or unit students shall attend prescribed classes, lectures, seminars and tutorials’. Where a student, due to exceptional circumstances, is unable to attend a scheduled class, they are required to obtain prior approval from the unit coordinator to be absent from that class. Any student absent from class without having had such absence approved by the unit coordinator may be referred to the faculty for advice and may be required to withdraw from the unit.
* For this unit in particular, attendance at tutorials is completely essential. Tutorial work is the engine room of learning, where participants actually discuss problem-set questions and do macroeconomic analysis. For this reason, to provide incentives to participate in every tutorial session, there are in-tutorial assessments randomised through the semester the results from which are not redeemable. Intending participants who know in advance that they are not available to attend all tutorial sessions should consider other units.
* Successful completion of this unit requires all tutorial work (including nine problem sets and five numerical exercises), a mid-term quiz, a group presentation, and a final examination. A good understanding of the tutorial work alone is sufficient for a strong performance in the unit, though more work on lecture material is generally needed to achieve very high marks.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 10 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 30 out of 50 (a mark of 5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.
