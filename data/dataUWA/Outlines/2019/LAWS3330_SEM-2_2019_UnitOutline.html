Unit Information

This unit examines critical theories of social identity and their relationships with the law. It covers feminist theories, theories of sexual and gender orientation and masculinities as well as theories of intersection, between race, gender, sexuality and other structural relations. The first part of the unit is a survey of approaches taken by general and legal theorists. The second part examines several areas of law and society from the perspective of these ideas. The areas may include: homicide (reforms to defences); law relating to the economic life of women (e.g. pay discrepancies and the 'glass ceiling'); reproductive technology laws; and internet regulation. The unit emphasises inquiry into ideas rather than the learning of legal doctrine. It extends written and oral communication skills with a focus on theoretical and analytical approaches. This is a seminar unit: face to face participation applies to all of the three hours per week and none of the classes are recorded. (Please note the information relating to the UWA 'Lecture Capture System' included in this Unit Outline refers to UWA lecture style units, and does not apply to this seminar unit.)
The course is designed to explore the nature of the social organisation of gender and some of the ways this is expressed through law. It will:
- Introduce students to the sources, breath and some of the history of feminist and gender theory and feminist legal scholarship
- Encourage an appreciation of the functions and value of theoretical, philosophical and historical enquiry
- Examine how gender relations are reflected in, and maintained by, specific laws and aspects of the legal system
- Further develop skills of analysis of written materials
- Further develop thinking skills and skills (written and oral) for clear communication and argument
The required material for each week, along with your discussions of them, is the foundation of the course so it is essential that you read the material each week. Some weeks you will write a “response paper”. The emphasis on required reading prior to the seminars is so that the whole group has a substantial, shared basis from which analysis and criticism of the material can proceed. The aim is to understand and analyse the positions taken by the authors of the material and to respond to them with well formulated (rather, formulating) ideas. Your ideas will, of course, be developed, and probably modified (or completely altered!), in the seminar discussions.
Readings will be indicated, and generally provided, through LMS. Anna Krien, Night Games (2013) is required reading for week 12 (so keep this in mind! Copies are available in the Library and at the Co-op Bookshop). There is no general text, though Rosemarie Tong and Tina Fernandes Botts, Feminist Thought (2013) is a useful text to consider along with the weekly prescribed readings.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

The basis of the course content is the set of prescribed readings for each week. Understanding of these readings will be supported by: (1) seminar discussions (Mondays) and tutorial investigations, on particular aspects of the readings; (2) the response papers, which require, in part, an analysis of readings selected by the student; (3) the "Diary of a Learner", a journal in which students reflect on the content of the course material; (4) Guest lecturers who will lecture on their areas of expertise; and (5) the Final Paper, which requires an analysis of an "event" by reference to the theoretical perspectives considered throughout the course. A focus of the course is on philosophical and intellectual-creative thinking. This focus will be supported by: (1) seminar discussions in which students will have opportunities to consider others' interpretations of texts, and to articulate their own; (2) response paper 2 and the Final Paper, which require a response to an article derived from the students own, lived experience; (3) the "Communities of Inquiry", which will form the basis of the tutorials. These are group inquiries that encourage collaborative and creative investigation into ideas, based on a 'stimulus text' but not limited to the text; and (3) the Diary of a Learner, which allows students to reflect on what supports their own intellectual development.

Unit Structure

There is a two hour seminar each week on Mondays (all students are in one group), and a one hour tutorial/seminar each week (the group is divided into two for this hour). The tutorial/seminars are based on "Communities of Inquiry". Monday seminars begin in week 1, tutorial/seminars (Monday or Wednesday) begin in week 2. Remember, none of them are recorded.

Participation

This course is designed to focus on participation and engagement. Thirty per cent of your overall mark is directly based on participation in both Monday seminars and Monday/Wednesday tutorial-seminars. (The 30 per cent comprises 10 per cent for participation in the three hours of classes each week, and 20 per cent is for your "Diary of a Learner" which is a reflection on those classes.) However, in addition, the written assessment pieces (the 'Response Papers' and the 'Final Paper') rely substantially on the skills developed in classes, in particular the "Community of Inquiry" skills on which we focus in the tutorial/seminar.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.

Special Considerations

If your study has been adversely affected by illness or other significant circumstances outside of your control, you can apply for special consideration. Significant circumstances may include but are not limited to:
