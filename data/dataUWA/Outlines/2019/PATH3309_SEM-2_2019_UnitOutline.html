Academic Information

Content This unit offers an advanced study in cellular and molecular changes responsible for the development and progression of human cancers. Special emphasis is given to the role of oncogenes and tumour suppressor genes in abnormal cell growth, programmed cell death, as well as mechanisms of tumour angiogenesis, invasion, metastasis and response to therapy. Several major types of human cancers are covered including those of the breast, colon and prostate. Hereditary cancer syndromes are also discussed. Practical aspects of the unit include techniques for investigation into the cellular and genetic changes occurring in human tumours.

Unit Information

This is a level 3 core unit in the Pathology & Laboratory Medicine major focusing on the cellular and molecular changes that underly the development of human cancers.

Students will be taught by a variety of cancer researchers and clinicians who are experts in their fields. 

Theoretical knowledge provided in lectures will be built upon in laboratory classes that teach core techniques used in the investigation of the cellular and genetic changes in human tumours and in tutorials that require students to apply their knowledge to a clinical human cancer scenario.

Teaching and Learning Strategy

PATH3309 is taught in a blended model comprising face-to-face lectures, tutorials and practical laboratory classes. This unit is taught using a variety of methods to accommodate as many of your individual learning styles as possible.

Unit Structure

Lectures: There will be 2-3 lectures per week throughout the semester. Supplementary readings will be recommended for most lectures and it is strongly recommended that you read these. 

Laboratory Classes: There are 3 lab sessions (weeks 3, 4 and 5) supported by pre- and post-lab tutorials (weeks 2 and 7). Due to student numbers, each class will be repeated twice. The pre-lab will explain what you will be doing in the lab classes and will teach you how to do important calculations that will be assessed in your lab reports. The post-lab is an informal tutorial where we will show you how to interpret and analyse your data/results that will be assessed in your final lab report.

Applied Cancer Pathology Tutorials: In the second half of the semester, we have prepared two applied pathology tutorials. One will be focused on melanoma (week 8) and the other on breast cancer (week 11). The content covered will be included in the final exam and these tutorials will be run by anatomical pathologists who are specialists in the respective areas. Please note that answers to the tutorial sessions will NOT be made available via LMS and will only be available in class.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 10 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 30 out of 50 (a mark of 5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
