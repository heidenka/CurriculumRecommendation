Unit Information

The natural world faces significant challenges in the modern era.  In this unit students look at the way that the environment is represented in textual forms, both on the page and on screen. The unit is particularly interested in contemporary scholarly debates in eco-criticism, animal studies and the post-human and how they attempt to address ecological crises of different kinds, but also the way that they see these crises as throwing into question the qualities of the human being.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

The teaching and learning strategy in this unit involves four main elements. The first to read or watch a selection of environmental texts. These will be introduced through lectures. The second is small group discussion which we will do in tutorials and we will use these opportunities to break the issues open in terms that make sense to us. The third is going into the world—the natural world, the man-made world, and the points of interface where these two things meet. Our fourth element is our own writing, both critical and creative. We will practice environmental writing.

Unit Structure

This unit requires attendance of one one-hour lecture per week (or listening online) and one two-hour tutorial (or in Week Three, the relevant activity, see below). It is expected that students will be prepared for all classes, having read the required material beforehand and completed any necessary activities. 

There will be one optional off-campus activities offered:

WEEK THREE - Tutorial Two will be replaced with a private tour of Kings Park's conservation and ecological science department, with poet and seed collector/curator Luke Sweedman. This will involve a tour of the conservation areas, the seed bank and the working laboratories, followed by a workshop conversation about how this work drives his poetry. Details will be forthcoming via the LMS. This workshop will replace the tutorial for that week. 

The Writing Exercise due on the 30/8/19 (Week Five) will involve a self-guided writing exercise (critical and creative options), where you will be asked to respond to Kings Park as a space. While this off-campus activity is not compulsory, it is recommended you try and attend. If you cannot, it is possible to complete and submit the exercise yourself.

Participation

Students are required to attend (or listen to) all lectures, and attend all tutorials. As the tutorials will not be recorded, they are mandatory in-person participation. 

Participation is graded as 20% of your final mark. This mark is made up of multiple components - active participation within all tutorials, the completion of an in-class presentation (during tutorial times), and evidence of thorough engagement with the unit materials, including readings and lectures. This mark will be assessed by your tutor over the course of the unit. 

Participation in class, whether it is listening to a lecture or getting involved in other activities, is an important part of the learning process. It is therefore important that you attend classes. More formally, the University regulations state that ‘to complete a course or unit students shall attend prescribed classes, lectures, seminars and tutorials’. Where a student, due to exceptional circumstances, is unable to attend a scheduled class, they are required to obtain prior approval of the unit coordinator to be absent from that class, or present evidence for special consideration. Any student absent from class without having had such absence approved by the unit coordinator may be referred to the faculty for advice and may be required to withdraw from the unit.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
