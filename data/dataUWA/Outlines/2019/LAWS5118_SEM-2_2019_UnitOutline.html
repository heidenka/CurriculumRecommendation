Academic Information

Content In this unit students learn the key aspects of family law; marriage and de facto relationships, separation and divorce, parentage, parenting orders relating to children, property orders and the alteration of property interests and financial support for spouses and children, before progressing to study selected topics in-depth including family violence, family law processes and ethical issues in the practice of family law. The unit develops students' ability to critically analyse family law cases and statutory principles. Students critically examine family law concepts and principles in the context of social, economic and psychological dimensions of family formation and relationship breakdown, including the impact of separation on children.

Teaching and Learning Strategy

The unit is taught by way of a three hour weekly seminar and activities that support the seminars and the assessment items. The seminars begin in Week 1, the week beginning Monday 29 July.  The class will be conducted through a combination of teacher led presentations, small group discussions, student case presentations and whole of class discussions based on pre-reading and online lectures and in-class activities.

The seminars are led by Robyn Carroll, with presentations by students and invited speakers. Student preparation and participation in the seminars contribute to a vibrant and active learning experience. With this in mind, you are expected to prepare for and be an active participant in the seminars. During weeks 3-8 students will make oral presentations on a family law case that is relevant to the topic that week.

Lecture Capture System is not available in this unit.   This unit is taught through interactive seminars. From time to time online short lectures will be available in LMS to assist your seminar preparation.

Unit Structure

The topics in this unit are presented in five modules over 12 weeks.
    Family Law in Australia (weeks 1-2)
    Parents and Children (weeks 3-5)
    Property and Financial Support (weeks 6-8)
    Marriage and De facto Relationships (week 9)
    Process, Practice and Ethics in Family Law (weeks 10-12) 

The first four modules introduce you to Australian family law with emphasis on the Family Law Act 1975 (Cth), parenting orders and orders for the alteration of interests in property. The early seminars aim to assist you to select a topic for your research paper. The final module will cover selected topics and introduce you to the practice of family law. Arrangements will be made for guest speakers to attend class during this module.

Assessment Item Details:

Oral Case Presentation (20%)
You are required to make an oral presentation of a decided case in class in a group of 3 or 4 students during one of the seminars in weeks 3-8. You will have approximately 12-15 minutes for your group presentation. (The amount of time will depend on final enrolment in the unit.) The cases will be selected by Robyn Carroll and will be drawn from the unit reading lists that will be made available on LMS during semester. 

This assessment item has two key aims:
1. to give you the opportunity to demonstrate your understanding of the legislation and case law principles in the context of a family court decision; and,
2. to give you the opportunity to make a group oral presentation to your peers and lecturer and obtain constructive feedback on your presentation. 
The formal assessment of the presentation will reflect both of these aims.

The allocation of cases and presentation dates will be advised in LMS by Week 3. Efforts will be made to accommodate student preferences although this will not always be possible. Further information about the oral case presentations, including the scope of the exercise and the assessment criteria, will be provided once the allocations have been made.
	
Reflection Journal (20%)
You are required to keep a journal of your reflections on the topics, material and content of the seminars and submit four journal entries by the due date (see above). This assessment item provides you with the opportunity to demonstrate your ability to engage in critical reflection. You will be assessed on the extent to which you demonstrate that you have reflected critically on the materials discussed in the seminars, the law, your learning experience and your thoughts and feelings about family law, practice and processes and how the law interacts with other disciplines and professions. 

In your reflection journal entries you might ask yourself:
• What did you learn about family law from the class?
• What did you learn about yourself, and other people, during the class?
• What thoughts were provoked by the materials you read for class or by any other materials you have read or heard on the topic of the class?
• How might what you have learned in the class be relevant to you in the future?

You can demonstrate critical reflection through:
• questioning your experience
• showing examples of your learning
• linking your learning and experience to other knowledge and experiences you have learned about in the unit
• integrating learning material into your reflection
• identifying and questioning your assumptions and perceptions
• acknowledging your emotions and your response to them
• showing how your learning might lead to change in your thinking, feelings and behaviour.

You are not expected to address all of these suggested questions or to incorporate all of these ways of demonstrating critical reflection in each journal entry. These are simply guides. (For further guidance see ‘Notes on Critical Reflection’ on LMS).

It will usually be apparent from your reflection that you understand the readings and the discussion in class. That said you are expected not to simply paraphrase or summarise what you have read and heard. Understanding of the materials is something that should be implicit in your reflections on them. If, however, you choose to reflect on something that you have not understood, that should also be made clear and relevant to your reflection.

You are encouraged to complete a journal entry every week. You are required to submit four journal entries on the seminars throughout the semester. You must submit one journal entry based on one of the seminars in Modules 2, 3 and 5. You may select any other seminar in the semester for your fourth submitted journal entry. The standard of written expression including grammar and spelling will be taken into account in marking.

Each journal entry is to be of no less than 300 and no more than 350 words (Total submission word limit  1,400). You are strongly advised to work on your journal as soon as possible after each seminar while your thoughts and experiences are still fresh and make a journal entry each week.

You are welcome to submit your first reflection journal entry to Robyn Carroll for brief feedback on whether you are on the right track. This reflection will not count as one of the four assessed journal entries. You can submit this entry via email. Robyn will return it to you before the next seminar.

Research Paper (60%)
You are required to submit a research paper of no more than 3,600 words, not including footnotes and a bibliography by the due date (see above). The research paper provides you with an opportunity to demonstrate your ability to research an area of family law in depth and write a substantial, original and analytical paper.

Time will be allocated in the seminar in Week 2 to discuss legal research skills and methods and to clarify what is expected of students in relation to the research paper. One of the law reference librarians has been invited to attend the class to provide information and advice on resources and search methods.
A list of research paper topics will be provided by Robyn Carroll by week 2 of semester. Alternatively if you wish to write on a topic of your own choice you must submit a brief outline (approx 250 words) of your research paper topic to Robyn Carroll by Monday 9 September (week 7). You are strongly encouraged to submit your outline before that date. You are welcome to discuss any proposed topic with Robyn at the end of any class, however, you must still submit the research outline document to gain the necessary formal approval. This outline must be submitted to Robyn Carroll as a word document via email. Robyn will advise you by email whether your outline is approved shortly after it has been received.

If you wish to write on a topic of your own choice that might overlap with a paper you have written for another unit you have taken in the JD or in previous studies please discuss this with Robyn Carroll before submitting an outline.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 10 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 30 out of 50 (a mark of 5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
