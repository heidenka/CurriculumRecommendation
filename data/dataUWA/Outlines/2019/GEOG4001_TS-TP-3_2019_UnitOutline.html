Academic Information

Content This unit examines the fundamental relationship between human population dynamics and development. It focuses in particular on the implications of rapid global population growth, and the patterns and processes of human migration. Students analyse key development issues and current responses associated with phenomena such as resource scarcity in the face of a rapidly growing (and increasingly urban-based) human population, gender inequality and its relationship to fertility/population growth, forced migration (e.g. the rise of political and environmental refugees), and the impacts of rural-urban migration and temporary labour migration in the global south. The unit also examines the threats and opportunities faced by mobile and nomadic peoples.

Teaching and Learning Strategy

This unit is student led. It has been developed to provide you with a robust framework for directing your own learning and relies on thorough preparation for class each week. The unit is delivered over 8 weeks (with a one week break following week 5) of intensive 5-hour ‘teaching blocks’ or seminars each Thursday. Each week’s seminar will be taught in an interactive manner with some traditional lecturing, a lot of group discussion and activities, and multi-media presentations. Because of the highly interactive nature of classroom activities, the lecture capture system is not used, so attendance is critical. Your participation in each teaching block will require that you come having completed the preparatory readings. Assessment tasks are structured to reflect this.

The seminars are designed to: delve deeper into and apply learning from the key readings; to teach you to be critically reflective about your understandings of population dynamics and development; and equip you with practical skills to be able to engage in development work and research. The unit is also designed to develop your written communication and peer learning/teaching skills, and to equip you with skills in producing the kinds of outputs that are commonly generated in the development industry (e.g. evaluation frameworks, policy briefs etc). Most seminars include one or more ‘practice-oriented activity’ that is designed to simulate these kinds of tasks.

Unit Structure

The unit is organised into three modules through which we will examine some of the key population ‘mega-trends’ of pressing interest and concern to development policy and practice today.  
1.	Population Growth and Composition – In this module we will examine the contemporary spatial and demographic dynamics of global population growth and various frameworks that have been adopted through time for understanding how population growth relates to key development issues such as resource consumption and scarcity.  In particular we will examine key theories and trends in global fertility and mortality and how these relate to development. We will also examine the key role that gender plays in debates about policies and approaches for managing human population growth to support sustainable development. 
2.	Labour Migration – Arguably, the most significant population ‘mega-trend’ today is the scale and scope of human population mobility. In this module we explore global migration associated with labour and livelihoods in the Global South, and how these movements relate to the dynamics of poverty, underdevelopment, and inequality on an international scale. In particular, we will analyse temporary labour migration dynamics schemes and their current status as official policy approaches to international aid and development programs in the Asia Pacific region. We will also examine the drivers of rural-urban migration in the Global South and its role in contributing to another contemporary population ‘mega-trend’: rapid urbanisation. 
3.	Population Displacement – Migration exists on a continuum of forced to voluntary movement, and in the last five years, much global attention has been directed at the ‘forced’ end of the migration spectrum. We live in a world where there are now more refugees, asylum seekers and Internally Displaced Peoples than at any time since the United Nations High Commission for Refugees (UNHRC) was first established. In this final module of the unit, we turn our attention to examine their experiences. We will examine the political and environmental drivers of disasters, as well as the expanding reach of major development projects, that all generate human displacement. We will also analyse existing policy responses to the needs and aspirations of displaced people groups.  Finally, we will explore the contested migration experiences of mobile cultures (including Indigenous peoples, herding pastoralists, and peripatetic nomads). Globally, these mobile cultures experience similar forms of marginalisation and exclusion within their nation states as a consequence of mainstream development objectives.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 10 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 30 out of 50 (a mark of 5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
