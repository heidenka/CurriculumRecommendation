Academic Information

Content Much work in contemporary political theory, together with much real-world political contestation, has to do with questions of social justice. This course surveys the most influential accounts of the nature of social justice developed over the past half-century. It also considers their application to a series of real-world controversies, including the rights of the disabled, private education, redistributive taxation, global poverty, immigration, freedom of speech, gender equality, pornography, civil disobedience, and democracy.

Teaching and Learning Strategy

Teaching and learning in this unit consists of: (a) a single two-hour lecture per week, (b) a single one-hour tutorial per week, and (c) reading to be completed in advance of the relevant tutorials. Attendance at lectures is optional (though encouraged!), and lectures will be recorded and made available via the LMS. Attendance at tutorials is compulsory (see below), and the content of the tutorials lags one week behind the lectures (e.g. the week 6 tutorials will focus on the material from the lectures in week 5).

Unit Structure

See the unit schedule, below.

Note that I have put the readings for each week under the 'preparation' heading. However, I will not expect students to have done the reading in advance of the lecture. Rather, students will be expected to do the reading upon which the lecture is based in advance of the following week's tutorial. With respect to week 4, for example, students will be expected to attend or listen to the lecture on relational egalitarianism, and then read Elizabeth Anderson's (excellent!) 'What is the Point of Equality?' in time to discuss both the lecture and the reading in the week 5 tutorial.

Note also that there will be two weeks in which lectures will be pre-recorded and posted to the LMS, and tutorials will not take place - week 3 (the week beginning 12/8/2019), and week 8 (the week beginning 16/9/2019).

Participation

As noted above, 30% of your final grade for the course is devoted to in-tutorial assessment. This comprises two components - tutorial participation, and tutorial quizzes. One-third of this grade (or 10/30) will be for tutorial participation. The remaining two-thirds (or 20/30) will be for the tutorial quizzes.

Tutorial Participation
Attendance will be taken at all tutorials throughout the semester. Students must attend 80% of all tutorials. Failure to attend 80% of tutorials (absent special consideration) will result in a mark of 0 for tutorial participation. Tutorial participation marks will be awarded for contribution to class discussion, not for mere attendance. Tutorials will commence in week 3. Note that there will also be no tutorial in week 8.

Tutorial Quizzes
In-class, closed-book, multiple choice quizzes will be conducted in each tutorial (starting in week 3). Just as with the content of the tutorials, these quizzes will be based on the content of the previous week's readings and lecture (e.g. the tutorial quiz in week 6 will be based on week 5's readings and lecture). At the completion of the course, I will calculate a mark out of 20 for each student on the basis of their best 7 tests only.

Both these forms of assessment are intended to give an incentive to students to do the readings and participate in class discussion. They are also intended to reward those students who engage consistently in the course throughout the semester. Students who keep up with the readings and stay engaged in the course will not find it difficult to do well in these assessments.

Referencing Requirements

In your essays, you must provide pinpoint (i.e. page-specific) references for all sources you discuss.There is no particular style of referencing required. However, you must use a recognised style (e.g. APA, AGLC, Harvard, Chicago), and use it consistently throughout your essay. You must also provide a bibliography with full details of the works discussed. References and bibliographies do not count towards the word count for your essay.

Texts

Students are required to purchase a copy of Will Kymlicka's 'Contemporary Political Philosophy: An Introduction' (Second Edition, 2002), Oxford, UK, Oxford University Press. Other readings will be made available via the LMS.

Other

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 10 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 30 out of 50 (a mark of 5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "acting with the values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching and research". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
