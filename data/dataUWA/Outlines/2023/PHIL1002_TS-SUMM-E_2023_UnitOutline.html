Academic Information

Content The fundamental questions each human being faces are—What should I believe? What should I do? Asking those questions implies that some answers are better than others and the better answers are those supported by better reasons. Answering them requires the ability to distinguish good reasons for believing or doing things from bad ones. In this unit, students acquire the concepts and develop the skills involved in analysing, creating and evaluating arguments for and against beliefs and actions. They learn to apply these skills to real cases from philosophy and other disciplines. Because the ability to think and argue clearly and convincingly is central not just to philosophy but to university studies in general, a wide variety of material is studied, and many students find the unit’s content of immense value later on, both within the University and later in employment and their subsequent areas of specialisation.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

This unit is taught in 'intensive mode'. The teaching period spans five weeks, four weeks of learning followed by an exam in the fifth week. The teaching and learning in this unit follows a 'flipped classroom' model, where students work through the unit content independently through the LMS and then attend a two-hour workshop once per week during which the unit content is clarified and applied in practice.

Unit Structure

The unit is taught intensively. Nine Modules of content are available through LMS. There are four two-hour workshops, one per week from week 1 to week 4. Students are expected to arrive at the first workshop having already worked through at least the first two modules of online content.
Workshops
Weekly workshops begin in week 1 (week beginning Monday the 9th of January). The face-to-face workshops are on Thursdays, the online workshop on Friday. Please enrol in a workshop through Class Allocation System (CAS). Workshop attendance is compulsory and is marked. If you have a difficulty attending a workshop please email me to work out an alternative.
Independent Learning
Each week you will have the following to do: 
-preparatory reading from the text book
-working through the weekly learning modules on LMS (this will include watching lecture videos, reading lecture notes, and working through the lecture comprehension exercises)
-attending a two-hour workshop (in weeks 1, 2, 3, and 4)
-completing an assignment (in weeks 1, 2, 3, and 4)
-checking your answers to the previous week's assignment and consulting answers to any uncompleted exercises from the workshops. Assessment Details
The assessment components for PHIL1002 are:
(1) two 60-minute take-home tests (30%)
(2) four workshop participation assignments (20%)
(3) a single 2-hour examination in week 5 (50%).
(1) Tests: The first test, if completed with a serious attempt at every question and handed in on time, will be awarded 10% of the total for the unit regardless of whether the answers are correct (that is, 2% is awarded for each question given a serious attempt). The second test is worth 20% of the total for the unit and will be marked like the exam. The due dates for the tests are shown in the unit schedule. Extensions are not normally granted for the tests. The tests will be submitted through LMS.
(2) Workshop participation assignments: The mark for workshop participation assignments (20%) will come from the student’s submission of four weekly assignments. The due dates for the assignments are shown in the unit schedule. The assignments will be submitted through LMS.
(3) Exam: The exam is worth 50% of the final grade and will be similar in format to the tests; doing it will be like doing both at once.
Overall Mark:
Subject to the necessary condition (a) below the Philosophy Examiners will recommend to the Faculty that a student who obtains an overall percentage of at least 50 in any first year unit be awarded a pass in that unit.
(a) In order to pass a first year unit a student must normally obtain a percentage of at least 50 in any semester examination.
Assessment Standards and Appeals:
The pass grades used nation-wide are High Distinction (HD: 80–100%), Distinction (D: 70–79%), Credit (CR: 60–69%) and Pass (P: 50–59%). Descriptors for these grades can be found on LMS.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "honest, ethical and responsible scholarship". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
