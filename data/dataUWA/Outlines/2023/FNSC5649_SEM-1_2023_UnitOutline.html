Teaching contingency plan (In response to Covid19 restrictions)

Q: What will happen to the unit in the event of lockdown, masking or stringent social distancing?

A: lectures and some tutorial sessions will be moved online with labs/practical sessions deferred (see below).

Q: What will happen about practical and/or laboratory activates that cannot be undertaken remotely or online?

A: these will be block taught at the end of the semester and/or when appropriate advice allows us to do so safely following relevant Government mandates. Final examination dates may need to be moved accordingly to allow appropriate revision/preparation time.

Q: What will happen to the unit in the event of staff absence due to COVID-related reasons?

A: Where possible we will use replacement lecturer(s) and demonstrators. If this is not possible, we will move to an online delivery (where possible) and defer other activities (see above).

Q: What the implications for students in the unit are if they get COVID or have to self-isolate due to close contact?

A: Students are required to apply for special consideration following the directives established by the relevant student office. Any student having to isolate during testing or for other reasons will have access to streamed lectures via TEAMS (by prior arrangement with the unit coordinator). The relevant materials for missed practical session(s) will be provided at an alternative time.

Academic Information

Content This unit is taken over two successive semesters and parts 1 and 2 must be completed to fulfil the requirements of the unit. Students can commence the unit in either semester 1 or semester 2. The unit is a component of the Master of Forensic Science Anthropology (72580). It is designed to provide students with practical experience in independent scientific study through the completion of a literature-based project of direct relevance to forensic anthropology. Students develop scientific writing skills by compiling a substantial scholarly dissertation on their research topic. The dissertation must be analytical but it is not essential that the student collects the original data, therefore the following strategies may be employed: (1) experimental design; (2) statistical treatment of data from other studies; (3) a compilation of information from other studies; (4) meta-analytical procedures; or (5) profiling of case studies.

Unit Information

This unit is taken over two successive semesters and Parts I and II must be completed to fulfil the requirements of the unit. This unit is designed to provide students with practical experience in independent scientific study through the completion of a literature-based project of direct relevance to forensic anthropology. Students develop scientific writing skills by compiling a substantial scholarly dissertation on their research topic. 
The scope of the Master of Forensic Anthropology dissertation is more limited than that for a thesis because it represents only one quarter of the requirements for the degree. Whereas a thesis must have an approved research program with original data collection, the dissertation generally describes and analyses a particular topic that is outlined in a formal proposal that must be approved. The dissertation must be analytical, but it is not essential that the candidate has collected the original data.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

This unit is designed to develop a higher capacity for independent learning and research, concurrent with enhanced problem-solving, critical thinking, and communication skills (both written and oral). It is very important that you have effective time management skills in order to meet the strict submission deadlines for the assessments in this unit. You will have regular meetings with a supervisor to help you complete achieve the milestones required in this unit. 

Unit Structure

You are required in Week One to identify a supervisor and arrange a regular day and time for weekly meetings that is suitable to you both. The purpose of those meetings is for you to work with your supervisor to develop a dissertation topic and research proposal (see below), seek guidance on how to structure your dissertation and obtain feedback on written drafts. It is your responsibility to prepare for those meetings; for example, if you wish to have feedback on written work, then it must be submitted at least 1-week prior (supervisor will confirm specific requirements). If you cannot attend your scheduled meeting then you must let your supervisor know in advance (where possible); the same courtesy is expected from your supervisor, who will be responsible for arranging an alternate meeting (if required).

Other

The total length of a Master of Forensic Anthropology dissertation must be between 12,000 to 18,000 words (maximum) and may not exceed 100 pages. The Centre will provide a template for an appropriate title page and dissertation declaration. The abstract should summarise the appropriate headings, aims, scope and conclusion of the dissertation in no more than 300 words. The dissertation will encompass at least five chapters, three of which encompass the critical literature review relevant to the defined aims. You will consult with your supervisor to determine chapter purpose and required content. 


Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "honest, ethical and responsible scholarship". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
