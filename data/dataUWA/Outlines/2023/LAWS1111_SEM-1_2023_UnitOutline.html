Unit Information

This unit introduces students to the nature of law, and the relationship between law and justice. It examines the way that law operates in Australia and across the world's major legal systems, drawing on both Australian and international perspectives. The unit seeks to empower and enable students to assess the strengths and weaknesses of law as a means of regulating society in the context of competing and conflicting interests.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

This unit is taught through a combination of face-to-face lectures and tutorials.
Lectures
There is one two-hour lecture per week. Lectures are face-to-face (unless they fall on a public holiday, in which case they will be pre-recorded). Each lecture is different and will not be repeated. PowerPoint slides from lectures will be made available on LMS. All lectures will also be recorded and made available in accordance with UWA policy. 
The main purpose of each lecture will be to introduce and explain the basics of a subject within the unit. The lecture content presumes students are new to the topic, with no more prior knowledge about the subject than anyone else who takes an intelligent interest in the world around them. Accordingly, you can treat the lectures as a starting point. This said, you will find the lectures  more useful if you have done some of the readings beforehand (available through the Unit Readings tab on LMS, or on the Tutorial Sheet for the following week).
As there is no prescribed textbook for this unit, you will find it difficult to follow the progress of the unit if you do not attend the lecture every week.   
Tutorials 
There will be 11 tutorials across the semester. Tutorials commence in Week 2.  These are face-to-face small group sessions which are not recorded. The purpose of tutorials is to develop the ideas presented in the previous week's lecture. You will find it difficult to do well in the unit if you do not attend your assigned tutorial regularly.
Tutorial enrolment is via CAS: https://www.cas.uwa.edu.au/student. Tutorial times and locations can be seen on CAS and the UWA Timetable website.
Prior to each tutorial, you should complete the prescribed readings and tutorial questions on the Tutorial Sheet for that week (available on LMS). These readings (also available through the Unit Readings tab on LMS) will develop your knowledge of that topic. Tutorial questions are important practice for thinking about key issues likely to arise in the assessments.
Tutorials provide you with the opportunity to check progress, to sort out any problems in understanding the material, to discuss and develop ideas about the subject with your peers and tutor, and to prepare for and receive feedback on the assessments. The Tutorial Sheets contain further reading/viewing links to expand your knowledge of the topics discussed. Additional links and videos may be posted on LMS throughout the semester.

Unit Structure

As noted above, this unit involves one two-hour lecture and one 45 minute tutorial per week.  Attendance at all lectures and tutorials is strongly encouraged.
Your participation and achievement in this unit will be assessed in three ways.  The first (worth 15% of your final grade) is an online quiz on the AGLC referencing system, to be completed through LMS by the beginning of Week 4.  The second (25%) is an independent research essay, to be submitted through LMS during the mid-semester study week.  Please note that the use of unattributed ChatGPT – or any other non-detectable software – will be treated as academic misconduct. The final assessment is an exam (60%), held during the standard post-semester examination period.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "honest, ethical and responsible scholarship". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
