Teaching contingency plan (In response to Covid19 restrictions)

As this is a practicum unit, in the event of COVID-19 restrictions we will likely move to delivery of services via telehealth. Pausing some placement activities for a period of time may be required. Contingency planning will need to occur on a case-by-case basis via consultation between the unit coordinator, clinic director and student. Weekly clinic meetings and case presentations/topic discussions can be flexible and will be rescheduled or moved online as needed.

This unit is the first practicum unit for postgraduate clinical psychology students. The aim is to provide an introduction to the practice of psychotherapy, and to offer students supervised training and experience in interviewing, assessment, as well as individual and group therapeutic interventions. The unit introduces students to the key aspects of professional practice and includes consideration of the ethical guidelines and legal responsibilities that relate to clinical practice. The unit also introduces students to assessment, basic counselling skills, interviewing techniques, therapeutic interventions, note taking, report writing and the clinical supervision process. Students engage in supervised clinical client contact, clinic meetings and workshops.
As well as working with individual psychotherapy clients, trainees will be provided with the opportunity to work within a range of groups/modules. This may include group therapy (e.g. emotion regulation/management, insomnia, etc) and the Clinical Neuropsychology Clinic. Please refer to the Placement Guide for additional details.
Students are completing their professional training in clinical psychology and are required to pass placement evaluation reports and submit logbooks of their activities to meet placement competencies as per APAC requirements. As this is an UP/UF unit, the weighting of the assessments is irrelevant.
This Unit includes a five week module: Ethics & Professional Practice: PREPARATION FOR PLACEMENT (taught by Associate Professer Carmela Pestell) which prepares provisional psychologists (in clinical psychology and neuropsychology) by providing an introduction to the key aspects of professional practice and includes consideration of the ethical guidelines and legal responsibilities that relate to clinical practice. The unit also introduces students to basic counselling skills and interviewing techniques. Trainees will also be taught a decision making model to aid in their application of the APS Code of Ethics and Practice Guidelines. Students will gain an increased understanding of their professional responsibilities, as well as the primary role of AHPRA and the Psychology Board of Australia.
LEARNING OBJECTIVES
Students who complete this module: 1) will be familiar with the national registration act and other relevant legislation; 2) will be familiar with the APS code of ethics and how it guides clinical practice; 3) be able to apply contemporary ethical decision making models when confronted with ethical dilemmas in clinical practice; 4) will have an understanding of the basic elements of effective therapeutic relationships; 5) will be aware of the basic principles of effective interviewing and be able to conduct a clinical interview.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

Students are provided the opportunity to be supervised in their first exposure to clinical psychology practice at an internal placement within the UWA, School of Psychological Science, Robin Winkler Clinic. This involves face to face experiential learning in the clinic (inclusive of telehealth as required). This internal placement provides students with the opportunity to accrue APAC required direct client and client-related hours, and demonstrate core APAC clinical competencies, both of which are required as part of this accredited program.
In addition, a range of learning and teaching strategies are used to support students. These include i) Problem-Based Learning: that is, real-world problems within a cross-cultural setting are used as the vehicle to promote student learning of concepts and principles. For example, students work in small groups to generate case formulation, assessment, and treatment plans for de-identified case examples (clinical vignettes) based on actual cases during class. They are taught several contemporary ethical decision-making models and are required to apply them to these vignettes via small and large group class discussions, and by completing online quizzes. ii) Evidence-Based Learning. For example, students are taught an evidence-based approach to risk assessment, with current best clinical practices and health department requirements outlined. Trainees are required to show inter-leaved practice via role-playing a range of clinical scenarios and complete relevant empirical and theoretical readings from a cross-cultural perspective..iii) Simulated Learning is achieved in several ways. For instance, students are provided with clinical assessment tools and therapeutic interventions. These are discussed, questionnaires are completed and scored, and activities are role-played in small groups, video and class demonstrations; or with clients. During clinic meetings, students participate in a mock team meeting in which client issues are discussed within a confidential setting.  iv) Student-centred learning.  Students are also required to complete study guides and quizzes as learning activities. This provides the opportunity to receive feedback on content or ask questions about concepts they did not understand. v) Active student participation: All lectures encourage class reflection and discussion of in-class activities, and the quizzes and additional reading.

ASSESSMENT
Formal placement evaluation reports will also be conducted by Supervisors at the completion of each of two individual psychotherapy rotations, and at the completion of each module (approx 4-5) that students have participated in over the entire course of internal placement (i.e., PSYC5678/9). It is also expected that students have developed competencies administering the WAIS-IV, WMS-IV and WISC-V during the internal placement.
To pass this unit, it is expected that students receive an overall pass on the Supervisor Placement Report for every practicum activity (i.e., each individual therapy rotation and each group/module) and meet required placement hours (i.e., being on track to meet 160 direct client contact hours and 400 total placement hours by the end of internal placement as indicated in the electronic SONIA internal placement logbook). If a student is not on track to pass a practicum activity this will be raised by their supervisor during mid-review, so they have the opportunity to rectify the identified difficulties. Should a student not pass any one practicum activity/module on internal placement, an appropriate course of action for managing the situation will be determined by the Internal Placement Coordinator in consultation with the Robin Winkler Clinic Director and Program Director. Failing one practicum activity/module may be open to remediation depending on circumstance, however a student cannot fail two or more practicum activities/modules and pass the internal placement unit. See the Clinical Psychology & Clinical Neuropsychology Practicum Guide for more information. 
There are quizzes to be completed for each preparation for placement lecture and for some workshops.
There is also an online RiskAware module which must be completed by 30 June 2023.  Risk Aware - Enhancing students' clinical competence in risky environments (www.riskaware.education) is an online education program that enables students to identify and manage placement related risk. Risk Aware consists of: a) Seven online learning modules that provide an academic and theoretical context for student learning; and, b) Authentic simulation‐based learning experiences that allow students to apply their theoretical knowledge and practice newly acquired skills. The seven modules and their content were determined by a literature review, stakeholder interviews and student surveys. The Risk Aware platform also includes downloadable resources, such as the Safe Placement Guide. This resource was designed to guide conversations between higher education providers and their industry partners, promoting student safety through open conversations about risk.

Participation

It is compulsory that students be punctual and attend all coursework and other specified training activities unless extenuating circumstances (such as illness or significant personal crisis) prevent them from doing so. Where students cannot attend (or have not attended) the full required activities or classes, they are expected to notify the unit coordinator to provide an adequate explanation and documentation (such as a medical certificate in the event of illness) explaining their absence or lateness. A failure to attend without adequate explanation and documented evidence of illness or other impeding factors beyond one’s personal control will result in failing the unit in which the class/activity was missed. Note that even where an adequate explanation and documented evidence are supplied, a student will still fail the unit if he/she has not attended a sufficient proportion of the unit. A “sufficient proportion of the unit” is defined as at least 80%.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "honest, ethical and responsible scholarship". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
