Academic Information

Content This unit introduces the concept of property by considering questions regarding 'what is property?' and 'what things are capable of being the object of property rights?' It explains the importance of possession in property law and considers this in the context of real and personal property. It identifies and explains fundamental principles of land law including the physical dimensions of real property; the doctrine of tenure and estates; and aboriginal rights to land at common law. Students also explore the different ways in which proprietary interests in real and personal property may be created and transferred including by consent; without consent and by the operation of general equitable principles. The unit concludes with an examination of the priority rules to resolve disputes between competing interests in land and the law of co-ownership. The unit fosters a critical and analytical approach to examining property law principles. Where appropriate, it explores the social, political, economic and historical context of, and the ethical issues that arise in, property law. Students advance the core legal skills of problem solving; statutory interpretation; effective oral communication skills; and clear, concise and persuasive written legal arguments.

Unit Information

An important aspect of the Property unit in 2023 are the different perspectives of, and relationships to, property law.  Specifically Indigenous peoples’ laws and legal systems and how they relate to settler-state laws.  This will be particularly relevant in our early discussions on the meaning of 'property' and in the topics 'Doctrine of tenure and estates' and 'Indigenous peoples’ relationships to country and settler-state land laws'.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

The tuition in this unit is 3 lectures per week and a two-hour fortnightly tutorial. The assessment includes: class participation; a written assignment; and a final open book exam. Each method of assessment is designed to enable students to achieve the stated outcomes in this unit. The class participation mark is directed to fostering an understanding of the context of property law, particularly in light of Indigenous peoples' perspectives, laws and legal systems, and also the fundamental Common Law property law principles and Western Australian legislation. The tutorials also hone students' legal problem solving skills and critical thinking about property law issues. The tutorial participation mark encourages students to prepare for tutorial discussions and fosters oral communication skills. The assignment is directed to promoting students' critical thinking and writing skills, including writing in a clear, concise and persuasive manner. The final exam requires students to reflect on the whole semester's work, excluding material already covered by the assignment. As with the assignment, the exam promotes clear, concise and persuasive writing about property law and in addition, encourages effective time management skills.

Participation

The Objectives of the Participation Mark are to Promote:
• engagement, includes active listening, 
• preparation,
• understanding,
• reflection,
• responsible tutorial membership; and
• quality of contribution to the tutorial.
Engagement will most obviously be in speaking but a student might also be engaged whilst listening. This will be important to note for students who are by nature quieter. Engagement may be demonstrated by attentive listening to other contributors and/or responding directly to student contributions. Active listening includes eye contact, and/or body language demonstrating focus on the contributor, for example, agreement by nodding. 
Preparation might be evident in notes the student has come with or in the quality of answers and comments in discussion.
Understanding of the issues raised in tutorials will most commonly be demonstrated though a student’s overall contribution to the tutorial discussion, including small group discussions within a tutorial, whether by way of answering questions, asking questions, making observations, or listening and responding to other students.
Reflection on the issues (and preparation) might be evident in the student drawing on material outside the material directly related to the tutorial problem, perhaps from other topics in the course and in questions asked during (or even after) the tutorial.
Responsible tutorial membership refers to the respect given to other student’s comments and refraining from attempting to dominate the discussion.
Quality refers to participation being about the quality not necessarily the quantity of contributions (where ‘quality’ means considered and reflective contributions – you are encouraged to make mistakes!). The aim of including this component is to encourage unselfconscious discussion amongst students, not an atmosphere of assessment.
In order to encourage and facilitate student engagement and participation, the use of electronic devices is generally not recommended because it limits the tutor's capacity to assess engagement.
The Assessment Criteria for Tutorial Participation:
HD (80% or above) - Student is well prepared for every tutorial; demonstrates good understanding of and insightful and/or original reflection on the issues raised in tutorials throughout the semester; critically engages with the subject matter of each tutorial; and consistently demonstrates responsible tutorial membership.
D (70% - 79%) - Student is well prepared for most tutorials; demonstrates good understanding and reflection on the issues raised in tutorials throughout most of the semester; is engaged with the subject matter of most tutorials; and consistently demonstrates responsible tutorial membership
C (60% - 69%) - Student is well prepared for most tutorials; demonstrates developing understanding of and sound reflection on the issues raised in tutorials throughout most of the semester; is engaged with the subject matter of most tutorials; and demonstrates responsible tutorial membership throughout most of the semester
P (50% - 59%) - Student is not well prepared for tutorials throughout most of the semester; demonstrates little understanding of reflection on the issues raised by the tutorials; is not engaged with the subject matter of most tutorials; and shows little responsible tutorial membership
F (below 50%) - Student is not well prepared for any tutorials; demonstrates little or no understanding of and reflection on the issues raised by the tutorials; is not engaged with the subject matter of any tutorials; and does not demonstrate responsible tutorial membership.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "honest, ethical and responsible scholarship". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
