Academic Information

Content We live in an increasingly multi-cultural world where there are many ways of 'being human'. At the same time, all people share some experiences in common: all learn, share and communicate beliefs, create and maintain social relationships, adapt to their environments, and make a living through particular modes of production. This unit explores the world's rich diversity of human experience and practice, looking at cross-cultural examples of it in domains such as religion, identity, subsistence, social inequality and communication. It also considers the variety of ways that anthropologists and sociologists conduct their research.

Unit Information

This unit is UWA's Introduction to Anthropology. In this unit, we will be exploring what it means to be human, how anthropologists try to understand social and cultural life, and what professional opportunities Anthropology leads to. We discuss humans shared capacities, and look at the wide variety of ways that these are realised through diverse ways of life. Drawing on examples from across Australia and around the world, 'Being Human' explores how ordinary people organise their lives, interact through cooperation and conflict, develop and share ideas and values, and identify themselves and others according to various social and cultural categories. In this unit, we are concerned with both culture and society, and the relationship between these ways of understanding what it is to be human.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

LECTURES
This unit includes a weekly, two-hour lecture held on Mondays from 3pm to 5pm in the Social Sciences Lecture Theatre (SSCI G130).
These lectures introduce and explain important concepts and examples which will be vital to help you understand the readings, tutorial activities and assignments. They are also an opportunity to ask questions directly of the unit coordinator. For Albany students, and those who miss a lecture, recordings will be available from the Lecture Recordings link. The lecture slides will also be available in the topic folders.
The first lecture is in Week 1 - on Monday, February 27 at 3pm.
TUTORIALS
The weekly tutorial discussions are a very helpful learning activity for this unit. These are organised as 45 minute sessions, coordinated by a tutor, addressing the topic from the lecture the previous week. It is very important that you try to attend these tutorials having completed the assigned readings and tutorial questions. They are a chance for you to ask questions, to discuss ideas, and to actively learn in the unit.
Tutorials begin in Week 2 of the semester, but these first tutorials address the material from 'Topic 1'. Do your best to complete the readings and the tutorial activity sheet for this topic in preparation for the first tutorial.
READINGS
Each week, you will be expected   to complete a set of readings, as listed in the topic folders. Tutorial discussions will centre around engagement with these readings, which will usually comprise 1-3 book chapters, or academic journal articles, and will in some weeks be supplemented by documentary films. You can find these resources through the Unit Readings link under 'Student Tools'.
For the first 8 weeks, this will include reading through the book In Search of Respect: Selling Crack in El Barrio by Philippe Bourgois. This classic work of urban ethnography examines the lives of drug dealers in a Puerto Rican neighbourhood in East Harlem, which we will be using to illustrate and explore the different dimensions of society and culture that we address through the unit. While specific chapters are associated with particular topics, you are encouraged to read ahead. However, please be aware that this book contains content which some readers may find distressing, including accounts of interpersonal violence, including physical abuse of children. Chapter 5 includes a graphic account of sexual violence and rape. If you would prefer not to read this content, then please speak to your tutorial leader, who will assign you an alterantive book to read instead. You will not be penalised in any way for changing book. The book can also serve as one of the major ethnographic examples that you can use in writing your research essay. (We will discuss the meanings of 'ethnography' in more detail in Topic 2; for now, 'an ethnography' can be defined as an extended social scientific study of a particular social or cultural group, and especially the written product of such a study).
The second edition of: In Search of Respect is available to read in digital format through the UWA library.

Participation

The weekly tutorial discussions are a vital learning activity for this unit; these are organised as 45 minute sessions, coordinated by a tutor, addressing the topic from the lecture the previous week. It is very important that you try to attend these tutorials having completed the assigned readings and tutorial questions (which are available for each Topic on the LMS site). Tutorials are a helpful component of the unit, as an aid to your learning.  However, keep in mind that within the tutorials, we don't expect you to have complete mastery of the material - tutorials are an opportunity to ask questions, especially about concepts or material that you are unsure about. If you didn't understand something, most likely there are other students who didn't quite get it either, and you should be able to use the tutorials to work together towards a better understanding. With this in mind, tutorials are more about asking questions, and discussiing your perspectives with others, rather than about coming up with all the 'right answers'.

Referencing Requirements

It is important to properly acknowledge your sources. In your research essay for this unit, you must properly reference your sources - clearly indicate when you are quoting directly, include citations for all sources that you quote, paraphrase, or otherwise reference, and include a complete reference list comprising all and only those sources that you have cited. Follow the APA citation style as described in the UWA library APA guide (https://guides.library.uwa.edu.au/apa (https://guides.library.uwa.edu.au/apa). Please note that you should include page numbers in your in-text citations when paraphrasing or quoting specific passages from written sources. Refer to the assignment rubrics for more information, available on the LMS site.

Late submission of Assignments
A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.
For example,  if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 30 out of 50 (a mark of 5 is deducted per day).
If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.
If an assignment is graded Pass/ Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;
