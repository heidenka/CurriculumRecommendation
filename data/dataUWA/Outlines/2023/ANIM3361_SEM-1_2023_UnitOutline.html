Academic Information

Content This unit analyses the way animal populations fluctuate and use resources in space and time. It considers how individuals within populations interact with each other and with individuals in populations of other species, including those interactions that contribute to the determination of the biodiversity apparent in communities today. It covers population parameters such as age structure, birth and death rates and how these are used to construct life tables and population models. Theories and empirical evidence as to how biodiversity has developed over time and is currently maintained are considered along with discussion of how biodiversity contributes to ecosystem function and how that biodiversity should be managed and conserved.

Unit Information

This unit covers advanced topics in animal population and community ecology. It covers the way animal populations fluctuate and use resources in space and time. It considers how individuals within populations interact with each other and with individuals in populations of other species, including those interactions that contribute to the determination of the biodiversity apparent in communities today. It covers population parameters such as age structure, birth and death rates and how these are used to construct life tables and population models. Theories and empirical evidence underpinning community assembly processes are discussed. It covers the origin and maintenance of biodiversity, along with discussion of how biodiversity contributes to ecosystem function and how that biodiversity should be managed and conserved.
Learning outcomes in ANIM3361: students are able to (1) acquire a knowledge of population parameters and use them to produce population models; (2) understand how models are used to manage and conserve animal populations; (3) understand how populations interact with populations of the same and different species and model those responses; (4) use statistics to analyse data either collected during practical sessions or provided from field experiments; (5) articulate the main characteristics of communities and the divergent theories on how diversity is generated and maintained; (6) be able to apply theories of community organisation to conservation and management; (7) use multivariate techniques to analyse community data; and (8) critically assess recent and relevant literature.
Our expectations of students taking this unit are that they:
1. attend all laboratory sessions (face-to-face, when possible).  This is compulsory and attendance will be noted.
2. attend all scheduled face-to-face seminars and listen to pre-recorded sessions on LMS within 24 hours of the scheduled seminar time.
Read at least ONE of the textbook accounts, plus any assigned reading on the lecture topic in conjunction with a review of your notes from the lecture.
Attempt all the self-assessment exercises and check their answers against the solutions posted on LMS.
Complete all the assignments and submit them by the due date.
We also assume that for every contact hour (five per week) for this unit you will spend an equivalent amount of time on private revision, reading and study and completing assignments.  Students who follow these guidelines should have no trouble passing the unit.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

Lectures/seminars are recorded, either before or during the allocated time slot.  Lecture/Seminars are scheduled in a 2hr block on Tuesday 9-10 and 10-11am (see the 'unit schedule' on LMS and regular announcements each week on which seminars are face-to-face or online-only). Note that in the second half of the semester, the community ecology seminars will have an assessment component for group presentation and individual contribution to discussions, and face-to-face attendance is compulsory.
Practical sessions are compulsory and involve hands-on participation (face-to-face, when possible). This includes both laboratory and computer-based exercises.  Some practical activities are assessed in-class.  Online classes may involve scheduled 'Teams' sessions.
There is one three-hour field excursion during lab time in Week 9 of semester (May 3rd) and one field activity to be completed in your own time.

Unit Structure

There are two lecture/seminars each week scheduled in a 2hr block on Tuesday 9-10 and 10-11am, in the Jennifer Arnold Lecture Theatre (JALT) in the Zoology Building.  Students must attend one three-hour practical session per week, either at 10am on Wednesday morning or 2pm on Wednesday afternoon in JALT.  Five of these laboratory sessions are held in the Botany annex computer lab. Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
