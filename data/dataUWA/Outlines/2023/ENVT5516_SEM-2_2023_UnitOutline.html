Unit Information

UNIT SYNOPSIS
This outline provides information for students studying ENVT5516 - Environmental Monitoring and Sensors in the School of Agriculture and Environment in the second semester of 2022.
We are currently on the precipice of an exponential expansion of environmental data. Sensors for a wide range of variables relevant to environmental quality have been emerging alongside interest in sensor networks and centralised data repositories to study environmental change. These are increasingly being applied by government agencies, consultants and interest groups as part of environmental research and management activities. Students will develop skills in the design, deployment and data analysis associated with sensing infrastructure. We use a range of platforms to measure atmospheric, soil and water environments, to experience first-hand the challenges of using sensor systems, including their strategic deployment in real field environments, and the subsequent analysis and post-processing of data.
UNIT GOAL
The aim of this unit is to develop practitioners with spatial and analytical skills to measure climate, land and water using sensors to tackle environmental policy and management issues.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

STUDENT LEARNING OUTCOMES
(1) Understand how to create an environmental monitoring plan to address a real world environmental problem.
(2) Configure and operate data acquisition systems and associated sensors for measuring environmental conditions, specifically for atmospheric, soil and water systems.
(3) Evaluate how data are collected, managed, and techniques for post-collection data analytics and presentation.
(4) Recommend relevant variables for measurement within common applications, including suggestions for deployment location, frequency and time period.
(5) Analyse drone technology and methods for processing imagery from drone-based technology.
It is anticipated that after successfully completing this unit students will:
A. Be able to develop an environmental monitoring plan to address a real world environmental problems.
B. Be able to design and configure and operate data acquisition systems and associated sensors for environmental monitoring, specifically for atmospheric, soil and water systems.
C. Be able to critically evaluate how data is collected, managed, and be able to visualise data using various analytics and presentation techniques.
D. Be able to suggest relevant variables for measurement including suggestions for deployment location, frequency and time period within common applications of atmospheric, soil and water systems.
E. Be able to process and analyse drone imagery to inform environmental monitoring programs.

Referencing Requirements

REFERENCING
When completing academic work it is essential that students refer to the sources of their information within the text. Complete details of these works should be arranged alphabetically in a reference list at the end of the essay. The Faculty prefers the use of either the Harvard or the APA reference style. The style chosen must be used consistently throughout any document. These may be accessed at through the UWA Library at http://www.is.uwa.edu.au/information-resources/guides
Text Referencing
There are standard procedures for referring to authors and their work, for example:
1. Smith and Brown (2006) supported the interpretation….
2. … when recognising two inseparable criteria of experience; continuity and interaction (Clandinin & Connelly 2000).
3. When referring to a specific page or pages of a reference:
Labaree (2006 p. 17), (Labaree 2006 p.17), Labaree (2006 pp.17-18)
4. When referring to more than one work by the same author(s):
Fink and Brayman (2004, 2006)
5. When referring to more than one work in the same year:
Hargreaves (2005a), Hargreaves (2005b)
6. When there are two authors:
(Tilbury & Wortman 2003); Tilbury and Wortman (2003)
7. When there are three or more authors:
(Leithwood et al., 1999)
Note: in the reference list, multiple authors should be listed in full.
8. When referring to a publication that has no specific author, but which was written by an organization: (UNESCO 1992).
Reference List
All work referenced in the text must also be included (in alphabetical order by author) in the reference list at the end of the assignment. This also applies to websites but cautious use of websites is recommended.
1. Book:
Diamond, J 2005, Guns, germs and steel: A short history of everybody for the last 13,000 years, Vintage, London.
2. Article/Chapter in a book:
Fink, D 2000, ‘The attrition of educational change over time: The case of ‘innovative’, ‘model’, ‘lighthouse’ schools’, in The sharp edge of educational change: Teaching, leading and the realities of reform, Eds N Bascia & A Hargreaves, Falmer Press, London, pp. 29-51.
3. Article in a journal:
Hargreaves, A 2005, ‘Leadership succession’, The Educational Forum, vol. 69, no. 2, pp.163-174.
4. Government publication:
Department of Environment and Heritage 1999, Today shapes tomorrow: Environmental education for a sustainable future, Environment Australia, Canberra.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;
