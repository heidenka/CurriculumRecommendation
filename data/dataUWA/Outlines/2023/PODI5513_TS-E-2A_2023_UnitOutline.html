Academic Information

Content This unit develops a patient centred approach to the investigation and management of common foot pathologies. Students undertake tuition in the selection of elective podiatric surgical procedures, the principles of internal fixation and post-operative care of the surgical patient. The diagnosis and management of post-operative complications is considered in a multidisciplinary context.

Unit Information

This unit focuses on theoretical knowledge of common podiatric surgical procedures with aims for students to be able to
understand indications, contraindications, surgical techniques, peri-operative management as well as accurate diagnosis
and classification of conditions via medical imaging. Case studies are implemented to further progress students with their
therapeutic application skills when managing surgical patients. Concepts of internal/external fixation as well as wound and
bone healing are also explored for students to be able to apply this as they progress through the course.
Students are able to (1) demonstrate knowledge of common podiatric surgical procedures, their indications,
contraindications, techniques and peri-operative management of soft tissue, fore-,mid- and hind-foot deformities.; (2)
demonstrate knowledge of appropriate clinical assessments and investigations required for common podiatric surgical
procedures of fore-,mid- and hind-foot deformities.; (3) demonstrate appropriate choice and interpretation of medical
imaging to diagnose and stage common podiatric conditions; (4) demonstrate knowledge of the diagnosis and management
of peri-operative complications, both local and systemic in the elective podiatric surgical patient.; (5) demonstrate
knowledge of therapeutic drug prescibing relevant to management of the podiatric surgical patient; and (6) demonstrate
knowledge of internal and external fixation concepts, implant selection, osteogenesis and wound healing..
Students must abide by the Professional Behaviour Procedure set out in the experiential work-place learning environments.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

This unit has been designed to accommodate all  students as students have varying levels of knowledge in podiatric medicine. Using student centred learning and teaching strategies such as lectures, interactive practical session, cadaver labs, case-based learning, socratic seminars, and collaborative learning the unit engages students to promote deeper learning. This unit builds on the skills obtained in DPS Year 1 units – as seen by the developing learning outcomes scaffolded in the DPS program through the use of Blooms taxonomy. PODI5513 aims for students to continue to develop the overarching inherent qualities required of a podiatric surgeon to be a competent and safe surgeon able to develop critical thinking, undertake self-reflection, as an ethical practitioner, communicator, collaborator, advocate and lifelong learner. The combination of theoretical and practical aspects of this unit is to help you get the basic skills required to approach more advanced clinical skills required as a podiatric surgeon. This unit will provide foundational skills.

Participation

Attendance
All sessions are important learning experiences and therefore attendance is expected. All lectures delivered by guest lecturers are compulsory. Students must arrive in time to sign-in prior to the session commencing. All tutorials, laboratories, workshops, site visits, e-learning etc. are compulsory.
If a student is unable to attend a session due to illness or for any other exceptional circumstance, the Unit Coordinator must be contacted as soon as practicable. Absences of more than one day due to illness will require a medical certificate to be presented to the Academic Services Officer (admin-podiatry@uwa.edu.au) within seven days. Failure to meet this requirement or provide a valid explanation may/will result in action according to the FMDHS Professional Behaviour Assessment, available on the Podiatric Medicine Community LMS page. Medical certificates must be provided by a medical doctor or surgeon. Medical certificates provided by pharmacists will not be accepted.
If a student wishes to be away for any period during their course, it is the student’s responsibility to apply for Short Leave. It is also the student’s responsibility to inform their Unit Coordinators and/or clinical teachers and tutors about any possible absences, so they are aware of the approved leave. It is the student’s responsibility make arrangements to catch up with any work missed, including any assessments. This is a condition of short leave being approved. Students can apply for short leave of 1 or 2 days by emailing the Head of the Discipline of Podiatry, clearly stating the following:
• Full Name and Student ID
• Year level
• Dates of absence
• Detailed reason for absence
• Any assessments that may be affected by the leave
• Whether this has been discussed this with Unit Coordinators
Students will be given a response in writing as to whether the requested leave has been approved or not. Please note that short leave has the capability to inconvenience supervisors/unit coordinators greatly if there are assessments due during the period of leave. Please consider this when applying for short leave and decide whether the proposed absence is more important than the assessment.
If 3-10 days is required, any leave request must go to the Head of the Discipline of Podiatry and the Manager of Student Services and Engagement. A maximum of 10 days is allowed per calendar year.
Absence for Religious Reasons
The University supports students who are required to be absent for Religious Reasons. If a student is aware of certain dates, they will not be able to attend classes/assessments, it is their responsibility to let the Unit Coordinator and the Head of the Discipline know before the absence occurs.The University has an over-arching plan for the management of student interaction in the event of public health disasters such as pandemics (ie: COVID-19). Key to this plan is the individual discipline plans for distance learning, impacts of health department orders, and emergency enforcement of public health and safety.
In the event of students being unable to attend campus physically due to pandemic restrictions, alternate plans will be advised in relation to lectures, tutorials, laboratory education sessions and assessments. The impost of time available for clinical practicum and work integrated learning clinical placements will be managed in accordance with University and external accreditation bodies (ie: AHPRA) to ensure students complete sufficient clinical hours to be considered clinically competent to practice in accordance with the registering bodies.
LMS will be the primary communication for changes to course content as impacted by pandemics.
Students are encouraged to maintain consistent vigilance on their University emails for any directions made by the University executive, in response to Federal or State legislation or emergency regulations (ie: Health Worker (Restrictions on Access) Directions).
Lateness
Students who are consistently late to teaching sessions (more than 10 minutes) will be marked as late and an explanation will be sought from the student. Your fellow students and teachers appreciate punctuality which is part of professional behaviour and courtesy. Multiple late arrivals will be managed according to the SAH and PMSD Professional Behaviour Assessment. Late arrival at lectures is also discourteous and unprofessional. If you are unavoidably late, it is important that you enter a teaching session with minimal disruption.
Adherence to rostered session times
It is critical that where a student is assigned to attend a particular session at a particular time, only that session may be attended. Students are not to attend non-assigned sessions for their convenience. Students doing so will be subject to the SAH and PMSD Professional Behaviour Assessment process.
Patient confidentiality
As podiatry students you are of course not only free to discuss your experiences with each other, but you are actually encouraged to do so. Some teaching may involve discussion of clinical cases. Do not discuss these where you may be overheard by the public. DO NOT upload information or images regarding clinical cases to social media sites. It is important to remember that any information you learn about a patient should be regarded as a matter of professional confidence.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
