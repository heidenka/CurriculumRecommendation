Unit Information

Placements are recognised as an essential component in the training of professional psychologists. APAC standards require post-graduate Psychology students to complete 1,000 hours of placement activity in order to be eligible to register as a Psychologist at the completion of their degree.
Placements provide students with opportunities to develop their skills as a psychologist in professional settings under the guidance and support of more experienced organisational psychologists. This helps the student to:
• Build professional competence as an Organisational Psychologist in a work context.
• Develop a range of core and specialist competencies in the field of Organisational Psychology.
• Understand real work environments, scenarios, requirements and constraints.
• Integrate theories and academic training into practice.
• Develop a critical appreciation of the roles and functions of Organisational Psychologists across a variety of organisations. Build important industry networks and contacts.
• Develop critical skillsets relating to employability and working effectively in organisations.
Secondary benefits of placement activity relate to increasing student employability.
As such, the placement program not only aims to provide students with practical work experiences, but to also help you develop the personal attributes, self-awareness, and other key employability skills (e.g., such as interpersonal skills, self-reflection, etc.) which will assist your success in the workplace. This is achieved through a range of different activities throughout your placement journey including skills workshops and individual career development and coaching. On completion of the placement program, students will be able to demonstrate behaviour consistent with a beginning level of independent practice as an organisational psychologist.
There are a series of Group Skills Workshops scheduled for each cohort. These workshops will begin in October or November of your first year and will conclude in September of your second year.
Each workshop will be run in a two-part format- the first part will include learning underpinning theory and practice, and the second part will include practice and discussion of these skills in a supervised small group format. You will also have the opportunity to discuss issues and progress within your placement with your peers and to receive guidance and support.
The Group Skills Workshops have been carefully designed to be progressive and focus on relevant skill development at key parts of the placement journey. Workshops will follow the themes of:
• Wellbeing and Self Care (WSC),
• Interpersonal Skills Development {ISD), and
• Professional Skills Development (PSD).
All sessions are counted as group board approved supervision and as such they are all mandatory and need to be completed in order. While we expect
the content to remain as planned, there might be slight variation in topics delivered based on our assessment of training needs.
Whether you are currently on placement or are in-between placements, you are required to attend these meetings. Preparatory and post-meeting work is part of the process and we expect you will have completed this before attending the meetings.
Group skills workshops will run in the PaW laboratory or Myers Meeting Room (Floor 1, 238.lllSPS). The schedule for the group skills workshops for 2022-23 will be communicated to you during PSYC5547. Sessions are held on a monthly basis, with a break in January and July.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

LEARNING METHODOLOGY
This unit is run flexibly, structured to facilitate maximum discussion. During class you will be encouraged to:
• Contribute to the development of a psychologically safe learning environment;
• Maintain confidentiality;
• Contribute to class and group discussions through questioning and presenting ideas;
• Apply methods, models, learning to problem solving case scenarios; and
• Clearly and convincingly present your findings and ideas in oral and written form.
TEACHING AND LEARNING STRATEGIES
Classes are designed to be interactive and applied. Content presented is designed to support knowledge, skills or ability development as an organisational psychologist, this includes skills in employability as well as in professional practice. All classes will be held in face-to-face format. In situations where we must revert to on line mode, relevant adjustments will be made and will be communicated to you as required. Please note there is no capacity to facilitate a hybrid session (i.e., simultaneous online and face to face).

Participation

Attendance
Engagement in the class sessions is essential in this unit. It is expected that you will be actively involved in class activities and discussions and will prepare for class by doing the assigned pre-work as specified on LMS. It is the responsibility of all students to contribute to the learning environment of the unit.
Master of Industrial and Organisational Psychology (MIOP) students, please note that the Australian Psychology Accreditation Council (APAC) requires that students enrolled in an APAC accredited program undertake sufficient training to offer the opportunity for them to achieve the required skills and competencies. It is compulsory that students enrolled in an APAC accredited program be punctual and attend all coursework and other specified training activities unless extenuating circumstances (such as illness or significant personal crisis) prevent them from doing so. Where students cannot attend (or have not attended) the full required activities or classes, they are expected to notify the unit coordinator or placement coordinator to provide an adequate explanation and documentation (such as a medical certificate in the event of illness) explaining their absence or lateness. A failure to attend without adequate explanation and documented evidence of illness or other impeding factors beyond one's personal control will result in failing the unit in which the class/activity was missed. Note that even where an adequate explanation and documented evidence are supplied, a student will still fail the unit if he/she has not attended a sufficient proportion of the coursework unit (i.e., at least 75% of the unit). If absences or lateness are numerous or prolonged, the student will be directed at the discretion of the unit coordinator or placement coordinator to discuss with the Program Director to make a plan to address the issue(s). Given the current UWA Health Advice to stay home if unwell for any reason, for 2023 a minimum of 75% attendance in every coursework unit will be applied in consideration of the extraordinary circumstance in which we are now living.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "honest, ethical and responsible scholarship". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.
