Academic Information

Content This is an introductory unit covering the fundamentals of soil science in the context of agriculture, environmental science and geography. In field, practical and lecture classes the unit covers soil description and morphology (pedology); soil formation (pedogenesis); detailed soil biological, chemical and physical processes and properties, including an introduction to the cycling of water and nutrients in soils; plus soil acidification and salinity. It addresses the consequences of soil and landscape properties for environmental management, and land uses such as agriculture, horticulture, habitation and infrastructure.

Unit Information

Welcome to ‘Soil Science’. The following information is for students studying ENVT2236 Soil Science in the School of Agriculture and Environment in first semester of 2023.
This is an introductory unit covering the fundamentals of soil science in the context of agriculture, environmental science and geography. Soil science is the study of the chemical, physical, and biological properties of the Earth’s surface that acts as a habitat for flora and fauna. This includes soil formation (pedogenesis) and soil description (pedology). Often soil properties are studied in relation to the use and management of soils.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

This unit is delivered on-campus (Albany and Crawley) as a series of online recordings and and face-to-face practicals from Weeks 1–12 Semester 1, and includes a one-day compulsory field trip on Saturday 11 March (07:30 – 17:00).
The unit commences with an introduction to soil description, mapping and soil formation (pedogenesis) through lectures, practicals, and a field trip. This is followed by a series of modules examining soil physics, soil chemistry and soil biology. You will gain an understanding of cycling of water and nutrients in soils, plus discuss contemporary issues in soil science such as soil acidification, salinity, soil carbon sequestration and greenhouse gas emissions. Through lectures and practicals you will discuss the consequences of soil and landscape properties for environmental management, and land uses such as agriculture, horticulture, and habitation.

Participation

Participation in class, such as listening and asking questions in your  laboratory and field trip activities, is an important part of the learning process. It is therefore important that you attend the review the online recordings and attend practicals in this course. The unit coordinator’s and lecturers’ role is one of facilitation and you are primarily responsible for your own learning.
Attendance at practicals is essential so that you can get assistance with completing calculations and interpreting your laboratory findings. We strongly recommend you use your practical classes effectively throughout the semester to work towards your written assignments, and to avoid having to compile and interpret your data at the last minute.

Referencing Requirements

When completing academic work it is essential that students refer to the sources of their information within the text. Complete details of these works should be arranged alphabetically in a reference list at the end of the assignment. UWA prefers the use of the APA reference style. Details of this style can be accessed through the UWA Library at https://guides.library.uwa.edu.au/apa.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "honest, ethical and responsible scholarship". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
