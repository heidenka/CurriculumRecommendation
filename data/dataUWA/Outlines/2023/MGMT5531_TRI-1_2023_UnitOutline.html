Academic Information

Content This unit provides a framework for understanding the challenges and barriers that are facing healthcare organisations. The unit provides an overview of how healthcare institutions are organised and managed including primary care centres, acute care hospitals and post-acute care facilities, public payers and insurers, medical devices firms, and biotech and pharmaceutical firms. Emerging trends in healthcare including digitisations, delivery system, telemedicine, smart-phone app-based diagnosis and monitoring and globalisation of healthcare delivery are changing the competitive landscape. The strategic management of healthcare organisation unit is an advanced subject within the MBA program, offered to students who wish to pursue their career in the healthcare industry or to enhance the strategic skills for students who are already involved in the healthcare industry. The learnings in this unit are focused primarily on healthcare organisations, and we believe these learnings are also applicable to not-for-profit organisations and will help students who would like to build their career in the non-profit organisational setting.

Unit Information

Introduction
Welcome to the Strategic Management and Innovation in Health, one of the clusters of units within the health and leadership management specialisation of your MBA/Graduate Certificate in Health Leadership and Management course.  This unit provides you with a strategic framework for understanding the challenges and barriers that are being faced by healthcare organisations. 
What makes the healthcare market so challenging compared to other industries? The answer to this question is not simple.  This program is designed for participants to understand the complexities of the healthcare industry and the healthcare delivery system. The program content is helpful to participants from various healthcare organisations ranging from the department of health, public, private, not-for-profit, and solo/group practice primary healthcare operators, who want to enhance their strategic thinking and strategic planning skills and to develop cost-effective healthcare delivery services of their organisations.
The primary concern for the health industry stakeholders is how to contain and manage the rising cost, while not compromising patient safety and quality of care. Policymakers, regulators, providers, funders, pharmaceuticals, and medical device firms play a significant role in shaping the industry in the interest of providing affordable and quality healthcare to the community. All major economies around the world are struggling to balance the demand-side growth driven by ageing populations, chronic disease conditions, and supply-side constraints of increasing costs. The expenditure growth is driven by several factors, such as the greater use of lifesaving drugs, managing chronic diseases, the rising prices of new pharmaceutical drugs, the application of new technologies, and the use of advanced medical devices. 
The Economic Intelligence Unit estimates the global expenditure on healthcare to be more than US$10 trillion by 2021, plus the associated costs due to the current global pandemic.  In Australia, the healthcare industry is a 4th significant contributor to GDP and employs about 14% of the workforce. Australia’s healthcare spending in 2018-19 was equal to A$187 billion and expected to grow considerably in the coming decades. The increasing cost will have a tremendous impact on the national healthcare budget and healthcare delivery. Healthcare leaders must understand the changes that are taking place in their environment. They must see into the future, create a new vision for success, and be prepared to make significant improvements to deliver cost-effective value-based healthcare to patients, and other stakeholders. 
Therefore, healthcare leaders must understand the changes that are taking place in their environment. They must see into the future, create a new vision for success, and be prepared to make significant improvements to deliver value-based health care to patients, and other stakeholders. Therefore, strategic thinking within healthcare organisations is even more critical now and in the future.
This unit is valuable to students/participants who would like to pursue a career in the healthcare sector and improve the strategic thinking skills of those already involved in healthcare. In particular, those involved with long-term strategic planning, policy-making, healthcare providers and funders, pharmaceutical drugs, and medical device manufacturing. This program is designed from the perspective of the CEO, General Manager, and Clinical and Operations Manager who is required to make appropriate strategic decisions. The topic is of interest to all levels of healthcare management, including physicians, nurses, policy analysts, and as well as staff and advisers. The learnings from this program assist the participants who have, or are moving into, a position of strategic responsibility.
Unit content
This unit takes a general, integrating perspective and is concerned with setting the future strategic direction of healthcare organisations. The most important outcome of undertaking this unit is to learn strategic management concepts and consider how healthcare managers/leaders can think and act strategically.
This unit is based on a combination of theoretical concepts and the use of case studies to improve the strategic thinking capabilities of students.  Several topics are considered:  What is healthcare?; What is a public and healthcare policy, and what policy has an impact on healthcare organisations?; How does strategy happen within healthcare organisations?; How to analyse service area environmental forces and develop a sustainable competitive advantage; the importance of public and private funders; the impact of disruptive technologies on healthcare delivery models; value-based healthcare delivery; innovation and technological advances in pharmaceuticals and medical technology; leadership and managing strategic change.
Unit goals
The most important outcome is that you can think of the complexities surrounding healthcare organisations and act strategically.  Overall, the course aims to improve the practice of management, i.e. your ability to manage a variety of strategic and operational situations within the healthcare settings.  These situations may be complicated, and you should be able to cope with a considerable amount of ambiguity, including the constant shifting of healthcare policies.  The course aims to introduce students to:
•	Understand the importance of stakeholders and how they interact with healthcare organisations.
•	Learn how to formulate public health policy and its implications on various stakeholders and healthcare organisations.
•	Understand the concepts and practices of healthcare providers within its service area: service area strategic analyses and strategy formulation.
•	Analysis of the healthcare service area environment and to identify the long-term trends and potentially disruptive changes.
•	Able to position the firm competitively within its service area.
•	Learn how to develop a sustainable competitive advantage based on a value-based healthcare delivery model.
•	The critical aspects of innovation in health, including value-based delivery, design thinking, innovation in pharmaceutical drugs and medical technology/devices; understanding the approval process for use that includes advancing the product from the research table to bed-side, various phases of clinical trials, and pricing of new technology.
•	Understand the digitisation of the healthcare system and value creation, including electronic medical records, and the changes that are taking place in access to physicians and specialists. 
•	Healthcare leadership in setting the strategic direction of the organisation.
The unit perspective is that of the entire organisation, usually as seen from the position of a general manager/clinical and operations manager and/or managing director/CEO. The topic is also of interest to all levels of management including physicians, nurses, analysts, policymakers, and as well as staff and advisers. These people are affected by and in turn, influence the way the firms deliver healthcare services and position themselves in their service area. 
With a more active approach, you will learn far more than a few theories and some interesting case studies, and this will serve you well in understanding the strategic management of healthcare organisations.  Therefore, you are required to pre-read and prepare well for seminar sessions, attend and participate actively in those sessions, hand in assignments on time, and do your fair share of work in team/group projects.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

The combination of applied and conceptual learning in this unit provides students with the knowledge and skills required to manage ‘real world’ health care organisations issues.  This unit integrates theory with practice by drawing on a range of sources such as lectures, textbook chapters and journal article readings, case study analysis, and videos.  These will be supported by individual and group assignments and class activities that are designed to apply and practice concepts in a safe environment.
Learning outcomes
The course lends itself to the discussion and analysis of practical cases as well as theoretical concepts. An essential feature of the course is an emphasis on perspective and developing one's point of view. Students are expected to spend an average of 6-8 hours individually and in their groups preparing for each class session.  Successful learning in the course depends very much on you reading the material, adequately preparing the cases, and having a constructive discussion of issues in your groups and the class.  To paraphrase Frances Bacon: “reading makes a person full, writing makes a person precise, and debate makes a person prepared.”
You will have to deal with quite several different ideas – sometimes they agree with each other, sometimes they don't.  You will be given the concepts/frameworks and support to help you to integrate the different perspectives you will come across. But it is also essential that you exercise your mind to make sense of or accept, these differences in your way so that you develop your thinking and views.
This course uses a variety of learning methods including case analyses, and classroom group discussions of both theory and practical application. Therefore, attendance and participation in class sessions are essential parts of the learning process and the functioning of the course.
The course consists of 12 sessions; each session is about 3.0 hours. The unit structure consists of:
1)	A number of case studies,
2)	A selection of a few important journal articles/readings supplement to any strategic management textbook, and
3)	A collection of important concepts/theories from the textbook chapters.
On completion of this unit, you should be able to:
•	Have a deeper understanding of the healthcare sector and the importance of various stakeholders in shaping the healthcare system of the nation.
•	Have a deeper understanding of strategy theory specific to healthcare organisations.
•	Have an experience in the application of strategy theory/concepts and your work experience in several case studies and readings.
A critical task is to identify which questions to ask rather than to seek answers to questions put by someone else. Throughout the course, in-class analysis of cases, in deciding on topics for assignments, and in evaluating theoretical concepts, students must frame their questions wherever possible.  In this unit, you will be provided with the opportunity to:
1)	Critically evaluate the issues that healthcare organisations face.
2)	Apply relevant strategic theory/concept to various health business contexts.
3)	Demonstrate effective communication skills to influence and relate to multiple stakeholders across a range of business contexts.
4)	Develop competencies to work more effectively in teams through the completion of a group/team project relating to your selected health business context.
5)	Understand the healthcare system, including the importance of stakeholders, policies, and competitive threats that organisations face.
6)	Apply research skills to investigate current and future issues confronting healthcare organisations.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "honest, ethical and responsible scholarship". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.
