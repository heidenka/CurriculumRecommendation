Unit Information

The primary purpose of this unit is to teach you about consumers and their behaviours. You will learn many factors that influence consumers' behaviours. By understanding consumers' behaviours, you will be able to develop a better marketing strategy. The by-product of this learning experience is that you will learn to be a better, savvier consumer. Even if you are not considering (which we will change by the end of this unit) a career in marketing (i.e., only if you do not want to get the most interesting jobs, travel to destinations around the world, and have more fun), you will find that this unit applies to almost all aspects of your everyday life.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

The goal of the unit is to introduce you to consumer behaviour, a fundamental aspect of any business. This will be achieved through a combination of workshops and assessments. There will be 3 x 2 hour workshop sessions per week. Please refer to the Class Allocation System (CAS - https://www.cas.uwa.edu.au/) page for the workshop timetable. 
The assigned text provides background and examples that prepare you for the online interactive lectures and tutorial sessions. Thus, it is imperative that you read the assigned readings (from the text, slides, etc.) before each workshop.

Unit Structure

This unit is structured around the model of consumer decision making process, and will focus on consumers as individual decision makers. We begin with an overview of consumer behaviour and marketing strategy. We then examine internal influences (i.e., perception, learning, memory, motivation, personality, emotion and attitude) that affect the decision making. We continue with examining the consumer decision making process, starting from problem recognition to post-purchase, in more detail. We also examine external influences (e.g., situational influence, group influence, culture) on the process. We also learn how marketers use this information to inform and persuade consumers. A series of weekly activities are designed to help you integrate the course material and better understand your own and other consumers decision making process.

Participation

Participation, whether it is listening to a lecture or getting involved in other activities, is an important part of the learning process, therefore it is important that you attend all workshops. 
More formally, the University regulations state that to complete a course or unit students shall attend prescribed classes. Where a student, due to exceptional circumstances, is unable to attend a scheduled class, they are required to obtain prior approval of the unit coordinator to be absent from that class. 
Any student absent from class without having had such absence approved by the unit coordinator may be referred to the faculty for advice and may be required to withdraw from the unit.

Referencing Requirements

It is important that the referencing of any sources used in your written work is done properly, if only to substantiate the points you are making in your assignment or project. The Harvard style is the preferred unless otherwise stated by the unit coordinator. There are some notes for guidance which have been prepared by the library staff: 'Citing your Sources Harvard Style' (http://guides.library.uwa.edu.au/harvard).
The EndNote software package is a really good system for building up a database of references. Not everyone will want to invest the time in using this system but you should consider it if you intend to build up resource materials or plan to undertake extensive research in a particular area. The library staff have also developed a tutoring package: A Quick Guide to Using EndNote which provides the basics for using EndNote with an essay (http://guides.library.uwa.edu.au/endnoteAcknowledging).

Late submission of Assignments
A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.
For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).
If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.
If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course.
