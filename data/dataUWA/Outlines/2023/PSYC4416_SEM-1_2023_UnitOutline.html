Unit Information

What is it that makes Psychology a science, and can all the various approaches to investigating human behaviour in Psychology be considered scientific? What are the relative merits of these different approaches? What are their limitations? How does psychology as a science make cumulative progress? These are the key questions that we will consider over the course of this unit.
This goal of this unit is to foster the development of high-level, critical thinking skills and a richer understanding of the assumptions that have historically informed and currently underlie modern psychological theory, research and practice. The ability to “step back”, examine one’s underlying assumptions and consider different perspectives is a critical skill, that is valued not just in a psychologist, but across many fields and by many employers. This unit will help you develop these skills.
It should be stressed that this unit will be quite different to many others you have taken in psychology so far. The goal in this unit is not simply to remember lectures and readings, and show your understanding of them. Instead, you will be asked to step back from the specific information and theories that you have been learning over the years, to examine the various dominant approaches to the study of psychology into which those theories fall. As such, the unit will cover a range of prominent approaches to scientific investigation in psychology and the fundamental assumptions that underpin these.
We will highlight arguments and counter-arguments from prominent researchers defending and attacking different approaches to psychological research. Your task is not to find a “correct” approach---there is no one right approach!---but to understand the arguments for and against certain approaches, and the theoretical, historical, and social contexts in which they have been made. In this way the unit will have some parallels to ‘debating’ in that we hope you will actively take on these different perspectives as proponent, detractor, and also moderator to develop an understanding of the assumptions and motivations underlying these different approaches, and their strengths and limitations.
You bring three years of experience with psychological theories and practice to the table, and we will draw on your expertise during discussions. One of our goals is to try and formalise some of the intuitions you've picked up over your undergraduate course, but have never been explicitly taught (and maybe never even given any thought to).
The unit comprises a number of seminars in which we will describe the broad principles and assumptions underlying a number of different approaches to the study of human behaviour. The seminar format means that we will actively encourage discussion and contribution from the group. The discussion sessions are designed to encourage synthesis, comparison, and contrasting of the different approaches discussed, and hopefully provoke debate and reflection on the scientific nature of these approaches, including their strengths, weaknesses and utility. You will also take part in online discussions, as these will assist you in your coursework, in which you will be explicitly required to consider different sides of an argument or issue.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

The primary mode of teaching is through discussion-based seminars. In most seminars the seminar leader will give a lecture, will guide some discussion in the class and include some small group discussion with your peers. Please note that involvement in these discussions facilitates collaborative learning.
In some cases the lecturer will expect that you have completed one or more readings prior to the seminar. Reading requirements for each individual seminar will be communicated to the students beforehand (generally with one week's notice). All readings are available under the 'Readings' tab.
Additional learning will take place in an online discussion forum. In the first 5 weeks of semester, you will be expected to take part in discussions in an online forum set up by the Unit Coordinator on LMS. The purpose of this online discussion is to discuss topics relevant to the seminars outside the class, in your own time. This will give you thinking time to digest ideas and link them with your existing knowledge. The discussion will form a core part of the preparation for the coursework, but will be assessed separately.

Unit Structure

There will be twelve seminar sessions, involving a mixture of lecture content and class discussions. These will be complemented by discussion in an online forum (on LMS) in the first five weeks.  
You will receive Formative feedback from either your peers or teachers via their responses to your contributions to in-class discussions and the online forum discussions.

Participation

Attendance of the seminars in person and engagement in seminar discussions is strongly encouraged, but is not a unit requirement and is not assessed.
Contributions to the online forum discussion on the dialogue topics are worth 10% of the total unit mark. The marking scheme for participation in the online forum will be provided in class in the first two weeks, and will be placed on LMS. The assessment of online forum activity will close at 11.45 pm on Friday March 31, to ensure discussion takes place in sufficient time before the due date for the dialogue assignment.  The discussion forum will remain open for the entire semester for you to freely use, but any contributions after the assessment cut-off will not be counted towards your participation mark.

Referencing Requirements

1. Online Discussion Forum - references not required. 
2. Dialogue Assignment - The assignment format (Dialogue) is not described in the APA Manual. You should still cite your sources in the text and include a reference list. The reference list should follow APA 7th-prescribed format. However, your in-text citations do not need to follow APA guidelines (though APA format is perfectly acceptable). In-text citations can be more casual and can be in any form you choose but should be unambiguous, i.e., the citation should allow a reader to easily identify which reference in the reference list is being referred to.
3. Final Assessment - APA 7th-prescribed format  (including in-text citations and reference list)

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.

Academic conduct

Academic Integrity is defined in the University Policy on Academic Conduct as "honest, ethical and responsible scholarship". UWA expects the highest degree of academic conduct from all students.

Penalties for breach of academic conduct vary according to seriousness of the case, and may include the requirement to do further work or repeat work; deduction of marks; the award of zero marks for the assessment; failure of one or more units; suspension from a course of study; exclusion from the University; non-conferral of a degree, diploma or other award to which the student would otherwise have been entitled.

Students should familiarise themselves with the information and resources available regarding academic conduct and ethical scholarship at UWA.
