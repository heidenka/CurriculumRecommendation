Academic Information

Content This unit provides an overview of the law relating to land use planning in Western Australia, including the environmental review of planning instruments. The legislation that provides the framework for land use planning will be explored together with key legal concepts which inform and shape planning practice. The unit will trace the historical development of land use controls and the emergence of planning and environmental laws in Western Australia. Seminal cases will be introduced and discussed. The role of policy in land use planning and decision-making will be explored.

Unit Information

The unit is designed for students who wish to develop an awareness of, or practice, planning law in Western Australia.  The unit involves a close examination of the history of planning in this State, its regulatory framework, statutory processes and relevant principles.   The principles that inform land compensation for acquisition and injurious affection will also be explored.

Lecture Capture System

In accordance with UWA’s policy on lecture capture all lectures* in enabled venues will be recorded and made available to stream and download by enrolled students via the Lecture Capture System (LCS) for the duration of the unit (including Supplementary and Deferred Examinations period).

* Please note that in some limited circumstances lectures may not be recorded due to:  ethical and/or security related issues; the use of commercially sensitive material; the use of third-party copyright teaching material; or culturally sensitive content. Students will be advised, in advance, when this applies.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

The unit teaching plan will consist of face-to-face lectures delivered over a one-week intensive course from 6 to 10 February 2023.  The lecture will involve much discussion with students and there is, therefore, an expectation that students contribute to that discussion.

Unit Structure

The unit will be taught via 10 lectures over 5 days.  Each day will involve two lectures (each generally three hours duration with breaks).  The morning lecture will commence at 9am with the afternoon lecture commencing at 1pm and concluding by 4 pm.  The lectures and discussions will explore the history of land use planning, the current statutory and regulatory framework, the statutory processes involved in planning and the relevant principles that have developed.  Seminal planning cases, including a number that have been determined by the House of Lords and the High Court of Australia, will be explained and discussed.

Participation

The lectures will involve much discussion and interaction with students.  Students are expected to participate in the discussions and these contributions will form 10% of the available marks in the unit.
Students will be awarded participation marks based on the following criteria:  
HD (8 to 10 marks) : Student shows excellent preparation for all lectures; exceptional standard of participation with contributions being clear, substantive and insightful; student is punctual, involved in discussion, and listens respectfully to others; student shows excellent command of subject matter with articulate, well-constructed arguments.
D (7 to 7.5 marks): Student is well prepared; contributions to class discussion are clear, relevant and well considered; student is punctual, involved in discussion, and listens respectfully to others; student shows good understanding of subject matter and arguments are informed and thoughtful.
CR (6 to 6.5 marks) : Student is adequately prepared for all lectures; attends and is punctual, shows interest, contributes to discussion and contributions are generally correct and mostly clear.
P (5 to 5.5 marks): Student is not adequately prepared for most lectures; attends; contributions to discussion are mostly adequate but reflect little understanding of the issues raised.
F (0 to 4.5) : Student attends but may be late, shows little or no interest, contributions or preparation are inadequate; or student does not attend.

Late submission of Assignments

A penalty of 5 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment item is deducted per day for the first 7 days (including weekends and public holidays) after which the assignment is not accepted. Each 24-hour block is recorded from the time the assignment is due.

For example, if an assignment is late by three days and was given 45 out of a possible mark of 50, you would receive a mark of 37.5 out of 50 (a mark of 2.5 is deducted per day).

If there are a number of tasks within an assessment item, the late penalty may be applied to the whole assessment item after all tasks have been completed.

If an assignment is graded Pass/Fail, failure to submit the assignment in time may result in the student not being permitted to take the final exam at the end of the semester or a teaching period and being unable to progress to the next level of the course or to graduate in the case where it is the final unit of the course;

Penalty for exceeding word limit

Where a submitted assignment exceeds the word limit, a penalty of 1 per cent of the total mark allocated for the assessment task applies for each 1 per cent in excess of the word limit, or the marking ceases once the word limit is reached.
