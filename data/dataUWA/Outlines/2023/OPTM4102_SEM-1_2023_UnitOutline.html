Unit Information

Ocular anatomy and physiology underpin the core component of this unit.
In this unit, students develop an understanding of ocular anatomy and are introduced to the basic physiological processes that underlie vision. The unit builds on a basic knowledge of cell biology and introduces concepts of developmental biology as they pertain to ocular structures. Initially, students will cover the organ systems of the body that enables normal eye function. They will then identify the cell types that collectively form the eye and consider how the anatomical structure of the eye provides the basis for the function of the eye. Subsequent classes will introduce students to the anatomy and basic physiology of ocular structures and the structure and purpose of the blood ocular barriers.
Learning Outcomes
At the time of successful completion of the unit, the student should be able to:
1.	Develop a working knowledge of the systemic determinants of ocular health and disease, including haemodynamics, liver and kidney function.
2.	Describe the anatomical structures and physiological functions that enable various visual processes.
3.	Relate the gross anatomy and physiology of the eye, orbit and its adnexa, its network of vascular supplies and ophthalmic innervation to evidence-based nature of the practice of optometry.
4.	Demonstrate an understanding of the molecular events that underpin neural control and key physiological processes in the eye.
5.	Discuss the cellular and subcellular components that both integrate and segregate the eye with and from the rest of the body.

Learning And Teaching Strategy

This unit comprises pre-recorded lectures, face-to-face tutorials, small group activities, practical classes, peer-assisted learning, seminars and reflection.  It is recommended that the prescribed online lecture material be viewed before attending tutorials. Tutorials will consist of group discussions with academics on foundation topics required for progression in the Optometry course. Practicals consist of dry laboratory exercise and are hands-on. The seminar will be a team-based learning environment that will facilitate peer-to-peer learning and communication skills. The assessments are designed to prepare students for a career in Optometry as well as the demands of the Optometry course. A reflective component is included to develop a means for you to monitor and better understand your knowledge and understanding. Reflection is essential for deeper learning and for self-awareness that is required for professional growth.

Unit Structure

OPTM4102 is delivered in a blended-learning environment, which links pre-recorded lecture material with face-to-face tutorials, seminars (flipped classroom format) and practicals. The materials for the unit will be provided on a weekly basis in LMS.
In 2023, lecturers and teaching staff will facilitate and support fully-online sessions in the instance that face-to-face sessions are not able to be conducted.
Students will, on average, spend 300 hours a semester undertaking the teaching, learning and assessment activities associated with this unit. This includes:
• Up to 3 hours of pre-recorded lectures each week.
• 5 x 2 hour practical classes covering anatomy and physiology topics.
• 21 x 2 hour seminars/flipped classrooms consolidating and integrating learning from physiological optics with learning from research lectures, including quizzes undertaken in class.
• 4 hours of other assessments given to students to complete within a week timeline.
• Students are expected to dedicate a further 12 hours a week to private study of subject matter contained in this unit, including weekly readings, private revision and completing take-home assignments and assessments.
All contents delivered in this unit will be examinable in the final examination.

Attendance is an important learning experience for all students and therefore attendance is expected at all sessions. All flipped class, practical and tutorial sessions are compulsory.
